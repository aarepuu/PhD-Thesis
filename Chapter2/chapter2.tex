\epigraph{With data collection, `The sooner the better' is always the best answer.}{--- \textup{Marissa Mayer}}

This chapter describes the iterative user-centred design process, development and deployment of SenseMyStreet (SMS), a sensor commissioning toolkit that has been set up together with Urban Observatory (UO), the UKÕs largest urban data sensing network. The toolkit enables citizens and communities to use and commission data from scientific-grade environmental monitors, determining where they will be located on their streets and collecting evidence to inform local issues. A strong case can be made for evidence-based decision-making by using data as a resource to make educated and informed decisions. However, there seems to be a disjoin between the people who are affected by these decisions and their access to tools and resources to collect data in order to provide the needed evidence for change. To truly democratise this process and for citizens to become active producers and makers of data, there is a need for new mechanisms of citizen data production. This chapter begins by explaining the motivations for establishing a sensor commissioning toolkit for the community arising from critiques in the literature and the current state of the art of citizen sensing. In addition, it also outlines the context of the study, documents the process of designing and setting up the toolkit, provides an overview of the technical specifications, and describes the development and deployments carried out by the author in collaboration with technicians from the UO. Furthermore, an analysis of the use of the toolkit and its associated platforms and technologies is provided. The toolkit continues to operate as part of UO at Newcastle University, enabling communities and citizens to gather data relevant to issues important to them at a hyper-local scale.

\section{Motivations}
\label{sec:sms_mod}
This section expands on the literature and related work presented in Chapter \ref{chap:literature} surrounding active citizen participation and production of data in the smart city, with a specific focus on work related to environmental data in citizen sensing and how such data is currently generated; who has access to it and in what way; how it can be used for community problem-solving activities; and how the generation, access and use of this data may be democratised in the future.

\subsection{Sensing the Urban Environment}
Monitoring the urban environment is an important part of figuring out how the city operates and how people are influencing the city as a whole through their everyday activities. The collected data from the city can be useful for understanding these complex processes, but it can also be used for monitoring and evidencing the changes implemented in the city. Motivations for monitoring could be, for example, creating a digital twin of a city \citep{bolton_gemini_2018}, understanding city soundscapes \citep{adams_sustainable_2006} or air quality \citep{department_for_environment_air_2018} to inform policies around planning and infrastructure changes in the city, or building a smart city analytics tools for smart service delivery \citep{puiu_citypulse:_2016}.

\subsubsection{The Tools}
\label{sub:sensetools}
The affordances of ICT and IoT have enabled a new wave of smart city sensing carried out by citizens. These initiatives can be connected to a research effort or a citizen science project \citep{haklay_citizen_2013}, but they can also be initiated by individuals or groups of people using some type of citizen sensing toolkit (Table \ref{table:citizen_kits}) or commercially available monitoring device, examples of which could be devices put in oneÕs own house to monitor the indoor environment\footnote{\url{https://foobot.io}}\footnote{\url{https://www.airthings.com}} or kits that people could use outside and publish data to a bigger cloud platform\footnote{\url{https://www.netatmo.com/en-gb/weather/weatherstatio}}. These smart home devices are helpful in increasing self-knowledge or understanding, thus optimising and improving one's living space. Using these devices for making a home `smart' can often be quite expensive, and usually such devices do not provide any opportunities for customisation. However, with the increasing popularity of the open-source hardware movement and the availability of micro-controllers such as Arduino, RasberryPi and MicroBit, people (those with the capacity or a particular skill set) can now build their own sensors to use in citizen sensing. Although people can build their own kits using off-the-shelf components\footnote{\url{https://wonderfulengineering.com/10-best-raspberry-pi-sensor-kits/}}, plug and play citizen sensing kits are already available. Table \ref{table:citizen_kits} provides an overview of popular devices for citizens carrying out environmental monitoring and reviews their openness and ability in regard to getting the larger community involved.

These sensor kits have found usage in science education, but because of their low barriers to entry, such devices are particularly good for involving people in gathering data about the environment to spark discussions around environmental issues with the community. There are multiple crowdsourced monitoring projects for citizen sensing around the world, where volunteers use low-cost sensing equipment to collect data about environmental issues and then use this data to advocate for positive change, examples of which are non-profit groups like GASP\footnote{\url{https://gasp-pgh.org}} and INHALE\footnote{\url{http://www.inhaleproject.ca}}, which recruit people to participate in local air quality monitoring; AirCasting\footnote{\url{http://aircasting.org}}, which provides a full platform for air quality monitoring, mapping and exploring; and university initiatives such as London Air\footnote{\url{http://www.londonair.org.uk}}, which provide up-do-date information about regional air quality. Industry organisations have also come together in projects such as HAMNAIR\footnote{\url{http://www.hamnair.ca}} and Aclima partnering up with Google to instrument Street View cars with monitors and publish data on the Google Maps platform to provide a new source of accessible environmental data \citep{aclima_aclima_2018}.

\subsubsection{The Approaches}
A lot of these projects, however, come from already-framed agendas, similar to citizen science projects, which are sometimes presented to people simply as `have a go'. \citet[p. 384]{hakken_social_2003} is critical of these kind of approaches, stating that real constructive learning is replaced by participation in the development of scientific knowledge. Although contributing to scientific knowledge and open resources is important for advancing human knowledge, it does not provide individual citizens with the power and choice to create knowledge around issues important to them. As a response, new types of citizen sensing and community investigation tools are surfacing that are more focused on citizen-led projects and enabling citizens to come up with their own problems, conduct investigations and create knowledge to act upon. Examples of such tools are Open Data Kit (ODK) \citep{hartung_open_2010}, EpiCollect \citep{aanensen_epicollect:_2009}, Sensr \citep{kim_sensr:_2013}, wq.io \citep{sheppard_wq:_2012}, CitSci.org \citep{newman_art_2011} and DisCoPar \citep{zaman_discopar:_2015}, which enable people to propose their own investigations through a reusable mobile or web application that can be reconfigured and deployed to a specific project. \citet{garbett_app_2016} have taken this one step further and set up a commissioning framework called App Movement, where citizens can co-create dedicated applications for their community investigations and data collection. Furthermore, research around smart cities has started to look into issues of spacial inequality and inclusiveness, with projects such as Spatial Inequality and The Smart City \citep{franklin_spatial_2019} leading the way. It is important to understand and identify these risks and their impacts on citizens when implementing smart city technology in order not to create sensor deserts and instead nurture a smart city of all citizens. Clearly, the city needs to serve the needs of all its citizens and not just a select few. Collecting data about the city is just a small part of this -- there is also a need for mechanisms that help incorporate the wishes and desires of citizens into the picture of how a city operates.
\newline

\noindent
Indeed, it seems that the majority of drivers for carrying out city-scale monitoring come from institutional actors, and such projects are mainly run by experts who make assumptions about the population and the issues important to them \citep{townsend_smart_2013,tenney_data-driven_2016}. Furthermore, citizens sensing projects are often disregarded by decision-makers because of a lack of trust in the data quality or accountability of the process -- having the perspective that people are simply going around using `toys' \citep{gabrys_just_2018}. It seems that while this is partly to do with citizens not having the resources to acquire the equipment for monitoring equivalent to that used by the city, it also relates to a lack of mechanisms that would enable citizens to access already existing resources to conduct their own investigations.

\subsection{Data and the City}
Cities around the world are working hard to implement their sensor networks not only to collect data but also to pull other sources of data (e.g. administrative data, official statistics, operational data, social media data, and location-based data) together into one place to answer questions about the city. In addition to using that data in decision-making and governance, cities are also publishing the data for others through data dashboards \citep{kitchin_real-time_2014}, for example, CityDashboard London\footnote{\url{http://citydashboard.org/london}}, City of Los Angeles Hub\footnote{\url{http://geohub.lacity.org}}, City of Sydney Dashboard\footnote{\url{http://citydashboard.be.unsw.edu.au}} and Smart CEI Moncloa in Madrid\footnote{\url{https://ceiboard.dit.upm.es/dashboard}}. It seems that a prerequisite for starting to call a city `smart' is at least having one smart city interface \citep{al-hader_smart_2009,al-hader_soa_2009}. However, people have criticised this by pointing out that having access to ubiquitous technologies and fast broadband is not the only thing that will make the city smart -- human factors also play a big role in making the city smart \citep{nam_smart_2011}. The city might have many infrastructures in place for collecting data about its operations and population, but it needs an engaged public to make use of that data \citep{mcmillan_data_2016}.

Whether data in the city is generated through citizen science or crowdsourcing efforts, research, or governmental initiatives, the way it is presented to people largely depends on the purpose of generation -- whether it is for public awareness, policy making, research or civic engagement. In order to figure out what purpose city data serves, it needs to be examined from the perspective of its potential users. Looking at these features helps to understand some of the motivations for people to engage with city data. With non-expert systems, there is a clear emphasis on features that put data in a form that gives people immediate benefits (e.g. providing daily levels of pollutants), linking it to health and quality of life, in addition to importance also being given to personal place awareness through making links with places of importance (e.g. schools, nurseries, and shopping areas) and activities that people engage in there (e.g. storytelling). Non-expert systems also try to give people already processed data (e.g. air quality indexes and summaries and readings represented through emojis and popular icons) rather than fine-grain readings from the sensors.

There are vast quantities of data being generated from sensing the city, and this data is increasingly becoming available to people through different platforms. However, the question of what citizens can do with those datasets remains. Looking back to the ladder of participation of \citet{arnstein_ladder_1969} (Chapter \ref{chap:literature} in Figure \ref{fig:ladder}), it seems that most of the current ways of projecting city data to non-expert citizens are still at the \textit{tokenism} phase. It is becoming more evident that issue-led approaches are having a greater impact on the lives of citizens than those implemented through organisational strategies. Examples of such projects are the Bristol Approach, initiated by \citet{balestrini_city_2017}, which takes a people- and issue-led approach to tackle community issues through capacity building and usage of sensor technology, and Public Lab\footnote{\url{https://publiclab.org}}, which brings together community groups, researchers and technologist to develop low-cost solutions and tools for exploring local issues. This is also evident in Citizen Science, where platforms like SciStarter\footnote{\url{https://scistarter.com}} enable citizen scientist to form community networks around issues important to them; in this sense, instead of just volunteering their time or acting as `citizen sensors' \citep{goodchild_citizens_2007}, they can engage with other citizen scientists and scientists working on different projects. To help citizens move up on the participation ladder, research needs to look into ways that data can be used by citizens to improve their personal and community lives, a good starting point for which may be understanding and helping communities voice the issues impacting on their neighbourhoods and examining ways that data and technology could act as resources for exploring and tackling those issues at the hyper-local scale.

\subsection{Data for Community Problem-Solving Activities}
It is becoming apparent that an important prerequisite for using data by communities is to identify the needs of the community through bringing common concerns to the surface \citep{marres_issues_2007}. The literature review in Chapter \ref{chap:literature} Section \ref{sub:com_net} looked at different ways that groups, CoPs \citep{lave_situated_1991} and \textit{publics} \citep{dewey_public_1954} form and come together around issues to achieve common goals. Furthermore, it was pointed out that a mix of different groups are often referred to as \textit{community networks}, which support interactions between people in different groups, help articulate shared concerns and values, facilitate information gathering and build shared knowledge \citep{carroll_trajectory_2003}.

Although community networks move away from organisational ties and issues take precedence, organisations or community-based institutions \citep{carroll_trajectory_2003} often organically form around issues of importance. This is particularly prevalent with regard to environmental justice, where groups of individuals (from different professional and non-professional backgrounds) come together to fight issues linked to climate change and sustainability, e.g. an international NPO such as Friends of the Earth\footnote{\url{https://friendsoftheearth.uk/}}, who campaign for climate change, nature conservation and environmental protection, or more localised citizens' groups like Group Against Smog (GAS) in Pennsylvania, US and SPACE for Gosforth in Newcastle, UK, who are campaigning for healthier, safer and sustainable environments. These groups are examples of potential active users of city data and are often already actively leveraging existing data to raise awareness for community problem-solving activities.

Nowadays, the wider availability of the internet and ICT means that community networks are able to form quicker and mobilise better. Digital tools and network capabilities are enabling people to participate in community life even when they cannot attend face-to-face meetings. The internet has provided people with mediums for communications and for new networks to surface, e.g. posting information to public websites and blogs, sharing content on social media platforms like Twitter and engaging people in discussions around local issues on FB groups. In addition to the popularity of social media platforms and online communities \citep{crivellaro_pool_2014,afzalan_planning_2015}, the formation of community networks is also supported by increasing the push to create digital cities, digital twins \citep{bolton_gemini_2018} and digital communities \citep{carroll_strengthening_2018}. \citet{carroll_trajectory_2003} make a distinction between community networks and network communities, in that the latter is limited only to having interactions on the internet. However, they acknowledge that the availability of different technologies (i.e. community network resources) is changing the way community networks and \textit{proximate communities}, which associate with common activities and places in the community, operate. Furthermore, with the increase of civic-minded \citep{olivier_digital_2015,vlachokyriakos_digital_2016} approaches to service provisioning, more resources are becoming available for communities to conduct and help engage people in inquiry-driven investigations, example of which are online-resources such as Beautiful Rising\footnote{\url{https://beautifulrising.org/}}, which provides resources for activist groups to voice their concerns, and Digital Civics Toolkit\footnote{\url{https://www.digitalcivicstoolkit.org}}, which aims to help educators engage youth in exploring civic issues and the capabilities of digital tools for political participation. Although ICT provides more ways to increase communication and exploration of common issues, it can also inhibit participation by creating network communities limited to people who have access to these online digital services, resulting in a bigger \textit{`digital divide'} \citep{gurstein_effective_2003}. Additionally, there is often more tangible value generated from in situ and informal social encounters where information exchange in happening. \citet{carroll_trajectory_2003} point out that the social context helps to concentrate focus and make the activities more goal-oriented. People are more likely to commit to responsibilities and make something of vital importance happen because success and failure in community-led projects largely depend on individual initiative-taking \citep{carroll_participatory_2007}. However, how things are actually done with regard to the responsibilities to make something happen is face-to-face and in small core groups of communities that have combined people with different skills.
\newline

\noindent
Multiple concerns have surfaced around the way city data is currently generated, made available and used by communities to raise attention to and address local issues. In this sense, community groups are often limited in terms of the tools and resources they can access and use to participate in decision-making processes, and they are often dependent on professional services and solutions provided by governments and experts. This means that somebody else might be controlling the knowledge-making processes around these issues. On the other hand, while there are already examples of groups actively using the data, for example environmental justice, there may be more groups with different agendas and skills who are yet to benefit from the city data. There is a need for mechanisms that support different community groups and individuals coming together to form networks, consisting of multiple stakeholders at different levels of expertise and also power structures. Furthermore, there is a need to create a synergy of online-offline infrastructures and participation methods in order to address inequality issues around participation and local decision-making using data.

\section{Study Context}
\label{sec:sms-contex}
The following sections will provide an overview of the context within which this research was conducted, i.e. the particularities of the context in terms of the challenges of and opportunities for the research.

\subsection{Effects of Urbanisation}
\label{sub:urbanisation}
Every citizen needs their space to live, work, play and get around for their everyday activities. However, with more than half the world's population now living in cities, this is becoming increasingly more difficult. It is projected that the rate of urbanisation will increase to the point that about 68\% \citep{ritchie_urbanization_2018} of the world's population will live in densely populated urban environments by the year 2050. As space in the centre becomes increasingly scarce, cities become dispersed and more people inhabit the outskirts of urban areas. Although people live on the edges of towns and cities, they often work or have to travel because of work to the centres. According to the UK government's Department of Transport, the total number of vehicle registrations in the UK has increased every year since the Second World War, with an average growth of 630 thousand per year\footnote{\url{https://www.gov.uk/government/statistics/vehicle-licensing-statistics-2018}}. This increased volume of traffic and people commuting to city centres consequently creates problems of congestion and air quality, which means that cities are faced with a multifaceted problem (i.e. `wicked problem') requiring collaboration from policymakers, city architects, transportation and city planners, public health officials, and also citizens.

In July 2017, the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (DEFRA) and the Department for Transport announced a plan to tackle roadside nitrogen dioxide concentrations in the UK, a direct response to the illegal levels of air pollutants in 29 of the local authorities in the UK\footnote{\url{https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/air-quality-plan-for-nitrogen-dioxide-no2-in-uk-2017}}, among which were Gateshead Metropolitan Borough, North Tyneside and Newcastle in the north east of England, the focus areas of this case study. The local authorities have had to come up with a combined task force to develop proposals and solutions to tackle these illegal levels of air pollutants. 
Newcastle and Gateshead, separated only by the River Tyne and connected via multiple bridges, are both old coal mining areas, and with North Tyneside and its connection to the North Sea, the area also has strong ties to shipbuilding. Although both of these industries are not active anymore, they are still part of peopleÕs historical identities, places and spaces. Newcastle is the biggest city in the north east of England, with an area of 44 square miles and a population of 300,196, averaging an age of 37 years, with 58.3 per cent of households owning or having access to a car (according to the 2011 Census). As new developments are taking place and more people are moving into the area, the roads need to cope with increased amounts of traffic.

There are already two 'Air Quality Management Zones' in Newcastle, which have been under constant monitoring since 1995 and the release of the Environmental Act\footnote{\url{http://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1995/25/contents}}. Similar to other cities and towns in the UK, the area is undergoing an urban shift to create an environment for modal shift and multi-modal transportation. Alongside attempts to improve public transportation networks, there is a particular focus on improving cycling infrastructure and encouraging cycling as a means of transport in the area. To date, the local council (i.e. Newcastle Council) has taken steps to promote alternative means of travel and has invested in infrastructure for active travel through the UK Government's Cycling and Walking Investment Strategy (CWIS)\footnote{\url{https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/cycling-and-walking-investment-strategy}}. Despite these measures, the problems still persist, and there have been discussions around implementing more drastic measures, such as closing roads and implementing Clean Air Zones. Even if these kinds of measures are put in place, with the growing urbanisation trend, the estimation for zero emissions journeys by 2050 seem unrealistic. It became clear quite early on that the only way to tackle issues surrounding air quality was to tackle the sources, which means a wider engagement with citizens to increase public awareness around the issue and working together to come up with viable solutions for getting around the city for everyone.

\subsection{The Air We Breathe}
\label{sub:breathe}
According to the 2016 report `Every breath we take - The lifelong impact of air pollution' published by the Royal College of Physicians and the Royal College of Paediatrics and Child Health\footnote{\url{https://www.rcplondon.ac.uk/projects/outputs/every-breath-we-take-lifelong-impact-air-pollution}}, outdoor pollution is linked to around 40,000 deaths each year in the United Kingdom. In addition to explaining the repercussions of bad air quality on people's health, the report also provides recommendations for action and research for national and local
government, business and industry, schools and the National Health Service (NHS), as well as communities and individual citizens. Among the recommendations for tackling air pollution as an individual were the following: \textit{`be aware of the air quality where you live and harness technology to stay informed and monitor air pollution effectively'}. Having access to this kind of information locally could not only help raise awareness around the issue, but could also help people make better decisions about their travel choices. However, not everyone has the means to monitor air pollution `effectively', even less so in their local neighbourhoods.

Newcastle hosts one of the six national urban observatories in the United Kingdom\footnote{\url{https://gtr.ukri.org/projects?ref=EP/R013411/1}}. Each city takes a slightly different viewpoint, but all are focused on environmental monitoring and data collection in some way or another. Newcastle's UO\footnote{\url{https://urbanobservatory.ac.uk}} aims to provide a resource for understanding the impact of the city and the complex interactions within it through sensing and data collection. The UO operates as openly and transparently as possible to provide the best public access to environmental data from the city and to do so in such a way that promotes information being used by a wide range of stakeholders -- environmental scientists, city officials, universities and businesses -- but also regular citizens to promote initiatives that involve community-led investigations. The UO's data portal\footnote{\url{https://newcastle.urbanobservatory.ac.uk}} has had over five million downloads from when it was initially set up. Despite the best efforts of the project to make data accessible and usable for everyone, the data were mainly used by individuals or groups of expert users, thus not living up to the observatoryÕs slogan Ð `Data for Everyone'. Having the UO already established served as an excellent opportunity to investigate ways we could open such resources up for citizens and community use, helping to understand whether the public actually cares about environmental data or if there is a need for a different approach towards citizen engagement. 
\newline

\noindent
In order to obtain a better understanding of the challenges in this context and use the available opportunities, a more participatory approach was taken to explore ideas of active citizen participation in the smart city with the data produced by the UO. The aim of the research was to come up with mechanisms to democratise data production and use in the smart city by enabling citizens to collect and explore data relevant to the issues important to them at the hyper-local scale.

\subsection{Study Design}
The motivations outlined in Section \ref{sec:sms_mod} within the context of local issues of increasing urbanisation which affect peoples' health and how they get around in the city (Section \ref{sub:urbanisation} and \ref{sub:breathe}), and the potential availability of resources which could be used to shed light on those issues, opened up an opportunity for research to understand how could data production be democratised and used to better engage communities affected by these issues.

The case study followed the overall methodology of the research conducted in this thesis (see Section \ref{sec:research-approach}), which uses participatory action design research, aiming to understand the communities needs through engagement activities and user-centred design processes to come up with digital tools and processes to support people led investigations and advocacy efforts. The initial interest of the community was explored in a 2.5 hour workshop (Section \ref{sub:pinsonamap}) where two activities were run in order to explore current practices of advocacy groups, communities' data needs and technologies which would support collection and effective use of that data for civic advocacy and action. The workshop was recorded and transcribed for further analysis. Transcriptions and written material from the workshop were analysed and coded drawing on \citet{braun_using_2006}, to identify themes around concerns and flesh out initial design ideas for the usages of data by communities.

\subsubsection{Configuring Participants}
\label{sub:config_par}
One of the areas of Newcastle already had an established community action group who were advocating for changes to city infrastructure to promote walking and cycling. It consisted of people of different demographics, skills and backgrounds. The activities of the group included lobbing and raising awareness around the effects of air quality and the infrastructure changes being implemented in the city. The main communication of ideas from the group was done through public meetings and by publishing articles on a blog form to the members of the general public. They were also actively using the data produced by some of the sensors deployed by the UO at Newcastle University. From the initial meetings with the group initiators followed a 2-year ethnographic engagement with walking and cycling advocates in the area. Ethnographic work helped to understand the exciting practices of the groups and how they have leveraged the data and technology in their advocacy work. The core members of the action group became the key people for making connections with the wider community. In addition to having meetings with the group organisers, the author also attended group's annual meetings where they planned their focus and activities for the year. Reflecting on the ethnographic work and conversations had with the members of the group, there seemed to be a lot of interest in using data in as resource for advocacy and action. Furthermore, there were public funds for infrastructure changes allocated for public proposals and people were keen to gather evidence about air quality and traffic movements in their local areas to use as a basis for backing up their proposed changes.

From these reflections an initial workshop (Section \ref{sub:pinsonamap}) was designed to explore further ways to promote usage of data for raising awareness about the issues in local neighbourhoods and for civic advocacy and action. The initial workshop participants were recruited by snowball sampling through the existing advocacy group members. Overall 22 participants took part in the workshop activities. The workshop did not only provide a deeper insight to people's perceptions of data and its uses for civic advocacy, it also helped broaden the participation and make connections with a group of interested residents in another part of the city (2 miles from Newcastle city centre) which had a scarcity of hyper-local environmental data. The area itself has historically seen as heavily populated by students, which now is now shifting more towards residential with bigger proportion of young professionals and families settling down in the neighbourhood.

These efforts manifested in taking part of group bike rides, organising community meetings, raising awareness in the community (e.g., by engaging local schools, churches and talking at community events) and having active social media presents. Four participants were involved in forums like healthy streets board which discusses transport, health, air quality; and cycling stakeholder forum which brings together people who have an interest in cycling in the city. \subsubsection{Design Process}
The case study consists of two parts: (1) the design, development, deployment and analysis of the community commissioning toolkit usage; and (2) the evaluation of use and value of the commissioned sensor data by the community (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}). The first part of the case study, which is described in this chapter, focuses on the setting up of the sensor commissioning toolkit through working together with a group of residents who are advocating for active travel and infrastructure changes in their local area and the city. This was conducted through an 8-month iterative design process which consisted of: building relationships and understanding the community; gathering design requirements and responding with technologies that could help people better generate data; and assessing the use of the tools and processes by the community. The process is reported through ethnographic engagement with a advocacy group which involved regular meetings, emails and discussions of Facebook group; observations at group organised public events and meetings around the issues of concern; and collected statistics about the use of different digital tools of the toolkit.

This part of the case study analysis is mainly focused on the small sample of participants in a particular advocacy group which limits the findings to be generalised to the whole population. Additionally the particular group involved in the design of the toolkit consisted of people who were professionals in their respective areas and had a very good knowledge of the issues they engaged with. However, outside this group more people have signed up through the infrastructures that have been set up as a result of this research. The success of the project and the interest from the community is also illustrated by the fact that additional organised advocacy groups have been created based on the model of this one. Particularly using data and digital tools that have been designed through the project to raise awareness and push for positive change in the community. Although the issue of air quality took precedent in the case study and was definitely one of the key drivers for the local authority and the citizens involved in the study, it is not the sole focus of the commissioning toolkit, and the research around active citizen participation and data production in the smart city.


\section{Designing Community Resources}
\label{sec:design_sms}
Following section will describe the process of gathering requirements for setting up the community sensor commissioning toolkit. A five month engagement involving a workshop, community meetings, focus groups and ethnographic work resulted in the first prototype of the SMS. Work done is presented in a chronological order to better document the design process.

\subsection{Design Workshop}
\label{sub:pinsonamap}
A community workshop called ``Beyond Pins On A Map'' was run by the research team\footnote{The research team consisted of Thomas Maskell and the author. Through collaborative effort workshop activities were created, conducted and analysed to help understand the community data needs.} in spring 2017 around how people can collect meaningful data about their everyday journeys, take ownership and use the data in advocating for positive changes in local communities. Workshop engaged with 22 local residents from different parts of Newcastle who took part in two activities to explore the opportunities and challenges of using data to have positive change in the community. The workshop already had a previously identified focus of air quality, and pedestrian and cycling safety (Section \ref{sub:urbanisation}, \ref{sub:breathe}, \ref{sub:config_par})

The first activity looked at some challenging scenarios that were related to collecting data about air pollution and safety. That included speculating on the uses of low cost citizen sensing (Section \ref{sub:sensetools}) equipment and bespoke solutions \citep{maskell_spokespeople:_2018} to gather data from peoples everyday commutes, and thinking about the city datasets already published by the UO. In groups, people explored issues such as privacy, reliability of the data and how other people not involved in the study might trust the data that has been collected. There were lively discussions going around the tables which were captured and transcribed for further analysis. The Second task prompted participants to think about how could that data be used to advocate for better spaces for people to spend time in and move through. In groups, people looked at the issue in hand using examples ranging from art installations, to activist campaigns to inspire them. Groups then brainstormed ideas about what might work in their neighbourhood with the resources they can have and fed back a shortlist of their chosen tactics. Building on Beautiful Rising\footnote{\url{https://beautifulrising.org}}, material was made for the workshop to guide the design process. All the materials produced by the participants were archived, accompanied by audio recordings and photographs of the workshop.

Workshop findings revealed that people were interested in environmental data, but often from places that it was not available and there were no known plans from the city officials or decision-makers to collect it from. Further analysis also showed that although people feel a bit uneasy about the accuracy of the data (collected from low cost citizen sensing devices described in Section \ref{sub:sensetools}), they thought that it is more about how things are framed, and that having something out there for people to discuss is better than not having anything. The figures from the published report about the effects of air pollution and its consequences to people's health (Section \ref{sub:breathe}) can feel remote to citizens just going about their everyday activities in the city. However everyone in the community is affected by it in some way or another. In order to tackle the issue it had to be made visible within the community by the people living there. There was a consensus among the participants that the issue needs local people to come together not only to collect data but also to make sense of it and to decide how it might be used to take action to make local streets more liveable places.

After the initial design workshop the author was approached by residents from an area couple of miles from the centre of the city (Section \ref{sub:config_par}). There had been multiple articles in the press lately about the air quality in cities around the world and that had sparked discussions among residents in different community meetings. One of the more proactive resident had also acquired a diffusion tube\footnote{\url{https://laqm.defra.gov.uk/diffusion-tubes/diffusion-tubes.html}} for monitoring air quality and had fitted it near their house. It had provided some concerning results, raising questions and desire to find out more. Some of the residents from that group also attended the initial design workshop. In contrast to the active lobbing group mentioned previously who were using data from the UO, this group did not have sensors in their local area and there was a lack of information to share with the community. Furthermore, according to the group the DEFRA air quality model\footnote{\url{https://uk-air.defra.gov.uk/research/air-quality-modelling}} showed that the pollutant levels were in accordance with the legal limits and the area was not part of the two Air Quality Management Zones in the city, which meant that there was no known plans from the government to start doing monitoring in the area. However people who used the spaces daily, pointed out that they are seeing issues raising with the infrastructure changes and increased volumes of traffic. People were also looking at the increasing high readings of pollutants in other areas of the city which had monitors and that led to a small group of residents getting together to looking at the topic in more detail. Residents expressed particular concerns about the effects of harmful pollutant to children and their developing lungs. This together with increased levels of through traffic trying to get to the city centre made residence worried about the lack of space for active travel and other infrastructure funding not going towards making their local streets healthier and safer for everyone to get a round.


\subsection{Surfacing and Mapping Community Concerns}
The initial design workshop findings provided indicative evidence of community interest and also contacts of a community keen on getting involved with the co-design process. Furthermore, were available funds from the UO to acquire environmental monitoring equipment, however up to this point there was no commissioning toolkit in place and manage this shared resource, nor there was a clear view of in which places and the sort of environmental indicators that people are interested in measuring. Together with the research team\footnote{The research team of the Urban Observatory consisted of Prof. Philip James, Dr. Jennine Jonczyk, Neil Harris, Dr. Luke Smith, David Pearson and later Richard Turland.} from the UO, we came up with a set of potential monitors that could be acquired or re-purposed, provided there was a interest from the people. These included scientific grade air quality, noise and traffic monitors that could be located on lamp-posts by UO engineers, and hand-held environmental monitors that people could use themselves on commutes.

In order to explore these avenues and to get an idea of what people are particularly interested in measuring, a meeting was set up with the group of residents (Table \ref{table:sms_par}). At the meeting the group gave a bit of background of the area, talked about the areas of concern and their desire for data to help residents make better decisions and advocate for change. To make the conversation more related to the community, a printed out map of the area in question was used. After the group learned about the environmental indicators that could be potentially monitored using a selection of scientific grade sensors, they collaborated around the map (see Figure \ref{fig:areasofconcern}) to point out the areas of concern and marked the potential locations for different environmental monitors. 

Having no data available from that particular area, the residents had to rely on their perception and local knowledge when deciding on which are the most important places that need investigating through environmental sensing. Through discussion, people had to agree on where to focus their effort and use the limited resources to gather environmental data about the area. As five of the participants were parents whose children go to the local schools the focus was on areas where young people might be exposed to harmful effects of roadside traffic pollution. This focus has been a reoccurring theme with almost all community groups engaged through the project. Advocates are trying to voice the concern of the ones who might not be able to do it themselves (e.g., young children, elderly, people with reduced mobility) but often are the ones who are affected by the issue the most. Although, this type of engagement activity worked well in a focus group setting, it needed everyone to come together in a physical space and needed support from the researcher to facilitate the mapping. Reflecting on the engagements, it seemed that in order for this process to scale, and enable a broader participation from the community, it needed to take the form of an online participation system.

\subsection{Planning Community Investigation}
\label{sub:com_plan}
The first set of meetings and engagements with the community group helped identify places in the neighbourhood and the issues that people wanted to explore in those places. Next steps for the community group was to come up with an action plan. After the initial meetings and conversations with the community group, they came up with a proposal called \textit{``Air Pollution in Heaton - The desire for data to help residents make decisions''} which consisted of:
\begin{enumerate*}[label=\itshape\alph*\upshape)]
\item Background;
\item Areas of concern;
\item A desire for data;
\item Action Plan;
\item Areas for fixed monitors;
\item Areas for hand-held monitoring.
\end{enumerate*}

People were driven by the fact that there was no data available from the area to make an informed statement about the air quality. The first aim for people was to establish a baseline on the level of pollution in the area. This document also set out a clear action plan for doing that by collecting the data, but also using those data for advocacy and activities for local benefits. The following is a statement from the air pollution bid submitted by the residents.

\begin{quote}
\textit{``The hope is that the data collected from these monitors will fill in the current gaps in air quality data for the area and provide a good base line for decisions of further research around measures designed to improve air quality.''} \citep{space_for_heaton_air_2017}
\end{quote}

At this point there was no fixed process in place which would set the criteria of how resources are would be allocated to the people, nor was there any mechanism of transparency. The back and forth conversations between the the author and the community group that guided the creation of the proposal were not documented and stayed within the close group. In addition to the issue of transparency there was also no opportunity for others to learn from these processes. Although the exercise of creating an action plan (i.e., proposal) was a good for setting the agenda for the community-led investigation the knowledge shared and obtained in the process stayed with the specific community group. This knowledge however could potentially be leveraged by another community group and lessons learned from it would help improve the process of the commissioning. Reflecting on the process of community developing data collection plan, there seemed to be a need to design a mechanism that encourages wider participation of the community through active discussions and feedback loops.

\subsection{Initial Community Investigation}
\label{sub:com_invest}
In addition to enabling people commission environmental monitors from the UO and get them deployed in their community, the people also wanted to be more actively involved in the investigation. This meant that there was a need for equipment that people themselves could use on their commutes to work or taking their children to school. By doing that, people could not only get an individual's perspective and an idea of personal exposure, which links to the health impacts, but also cover more ground in the neighbourhood and provide a good basis for decisions of further investigation around air quality in the area. Although these sensors would provide a snapshot of data, they would still provide a basis for discussions and decision making for the residents. In a way this part of the process was less linked advocacy and raising awareness, and more about self-knowledge and taking ownership of the investigation of the community issues. The main concerns pointed out by the members of the groups were linked to the increased volumes of traffic, having impacts to people's safety and air quality. Through the UO project at Newcastle University a high precision hand-held particulate monitor\footnote{\url{https://www.palas.de/en/product/fidasfrog}} was then purchased and loaned out to the community group to start investigating the air quality in the area. At this point all the communication with the advocacy group were done through a face-to-face meetings or through an email correspondence. This also included the scheduling of the sensor handover and return to the UO. This meant that there was always reliance of the researcher to make connections with community groups and coordinate the sensor loans. This surfaced another design challenge and requirement for the technology. This stage of the investigation was highly dependent on the contributions of people. After they got the sensor and a brief introduction of how to use it, they needed to figure out the monitoring schedule (Section \ref{sub:com_plan}), time allocation and logistics around exchanging the monitor within the group to meet the monitoring schedule. People were given an overview of data collection methods, sampling, variability of different indicators and robustness of the data, but the actual monitoring plan was intentionally left for them to set out. An additional reason for promoting community-led investigation was the emphasis on peoples experiences of living and moving around the area which provided local tacit knowledge not obtained by the search team. These experiences were also important for understanding the issues and different perspectives around them. In order to share these perspectives there was a need for a mechanism that would enable to capture them, in addition to the sensor readings from the hand-held sensor.

\noindent
This case study was conducted through user-centred design process where the designed technologies were direct responses to the requirements that surfaced from engaging with the people. A number of requirements for the design of the technology was surfaced from the initial engagement with the community group around local issues. However, because of the participatory nature of the research, the main focus of was not only on acquiring and providing people artefacts (i.e., the environmental monitors) that would give them capabilities to do community-led investigations, but on the whole design process and the \textit{coupled technologies} which were considered as a way of creating knowledge, expanding community networks and helping to build capacity of the community. This approach and strategies linked to it have also echoed from scholars working on civic engagement \citep{asad_tap_2017} and participatory design \citep{vines_configuring_2013,binder_design_2011}. 

\section{SenseMyStreet Toolkit}
\label{sec:sms-toolkit}
From the beginning the research, the focus and emphasis was put on supporting initiatives were citizens are involved in problem definition, data collection and analysis; hence taking the \textit{extreme citizen science} \citep{haklay_citizen_2013} approach and helping people to achieve more control \citep{arnstein_ladder_1969}. 
The findings from the initial engagements, conversations with the community members, observations and the design workshop and the process of designing for community resources (Section \ref{sec:design_sms}) all fed into the first prototype of the SMS. When designing the toolkit the aim was not to develop stacks more of digital technologies because there were already an abundance of smart city technologies available, put to also look at how existing technologies could be re-configured to be put to use by citizens for community problem solving activities. Through the development and use of participatory GIS and mapping, leveraging environmental sensing equipment, the models of community commissioning \citet{garbett_designing_2017} and HCI for geospatial technologies; the first prototype of sensor commissioning toolkit was built. Following sections describe the process (Section \ref{sub:sms_process}) and the implementation of digital technologies (Section \ref{sub:sms_impl}) linked to the first prototype. The toolkit is continued to be used by communities and is constantly involving, however for this case study analysis the focus is on the first prototype.

\label{sub:sms_process}

\item Plan and propose;
\item Get the facts;
\item Data for everyone.

Each step of is broken down to smaller activities to give better details of the whole process of issue identification, citizen data collection and sensor commissioning.

This might have been something that an individual was concerned in particularly, or something that a group of residents might advocate for, nevertheless these issues were almost always connected to places in the community and something that could potentially nourish a creation of a community network. The back and forth communication between the group and researcher, and planning and organising of sensor handover was replaced with automated systems that people could access online. To extend the exploration of the flagged issues, the toolkit enabled people do investigate them further through conducting environmental monitoring with hand-held monitors. \subsubsection{Get The Facts}
After the community had consolidated their discussions into a proposal and it was approved by everyone who participated, it moved to the next stage of scheduling the deployment with the Urban Observatory. This step of the toolkit did not introduce any additional technologies to the process, but relied on the information and scheduling obtained from the proposals created by the community group. Once deployment was agreed, engineers at UO scheduled it in with the overall deployment plan. From this point forward the responsibility of data collection was handed over from the community to the researchers and engineers at UO, which meant that they had to guarantee a successful deployment and deliver best possible quality of the data possible. Initially the maximum deployment time was one month, but because the monitors needed some time to settle, the deployment time was extended up to 2 months. However, community could re-apply for another deployment in the future once the resources were available again.

\subsection{The Implementation}
\label{sub:sms_impl}
The first prototype of the SMS toolkit consisted of four main development stacks: SenseMyStreet platform, automated sensor loan scheduling system, hand-held monitoring tools, SenseMyStreet Proposals platform; and some external services (Figure \ref{fig:sms-stack}). The SenseMyStreet platform consisted of a web based application written in PHP leveraging \href{https://make.place}{make.place}, a re-configurable GIS platform developed in Open Lab at Newcastle University (based on Silverstripe framework), and a RESTful JSON API for communicating with the MYSQL database. The main website had multiple re-configurable elements and was deployed using Docker, which made the application scalable and easily deployable on virtual or physical infrastructure. Software included re-configurable features such as: configurable geographical surveys; API to access the survey data programmatically; commenting and voting on survey responses; full Content Management System (CMS) to input content and design the look of the website; configurable dynamic filtering interface; and theme customisation using Docker variables. These features provided additional ways of integrating data with other systems and platforms in the SMS ecosystem (Section \ref{sub:data-flow}). The SenseMyStreet website provided all the information about the project, its resources (i.e., available environmental monitors), description of the sensor commissioning process (Section \ref{sub:sms_process}) and contact details for the research team. Website also acted as on-boarding component for the SMS toolkit: where people could create account on the website, learn about the project and its resources, and start flagging areas of concern using the participatory mapping tool on the platform. Furthermore, platform users could explore previously identified areas and have a discussion around the issues with the community.

\begin{figure}[ht!]
\centering
 \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{sms-stack}
  \caption[SenseMyStreet Toolkit Architecture]{SenseMyStreet Toolkit Architecture}
  \label{fig:sms-stack}
\end{figure}

The automated sensor loan scheduling system consisted of monitor loan forms\footnote{\url{https://goo.gl/forms/7A9eVtB4KwyuM0AA2}} and an automatic monitor loan scheduler\footnote{\url{https://goo.gl/14wrgM}} and deployment calendar\footnote{\url{https://calendar.google.com/calendar/b/1?cid=c2Vuc2VteXN0cmVldEBnbWFpbC5jb20}} built using Google Apps Scripting. The sensor scheduling system automatically allocated resources and schedules hand-held sensor handovers to the community, while also automatically publishing the list of available resources and the deployment calendar. This enabled people to get notifications of when the resource becomes available and when they can expect to receive a monitor to start doing their investigations. Furthermore, this provided a one place for coordination of resources which was always up to date.

The hand-held monitoring tools consisted of hand-held monitors; SenseMyStreet mobile application\footnote{\url{https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=uk.sensemystreet}} (based on SpokesPeople App \citep{maskell_spokespeople:_2018}) with and a blue-tooth Flick\footnote{\url{https://flic.io}} button integration to control the functionality on the application; automatic sensor data uploader (using Google Drive); data parser\footnote{\url{https://github.com/aarepuu/fidasfrogparser}} written in Python (using Pandas library) for parsing hand-held sensor data and merging with GPS readings; and Carto\footnote{\url{https://sensemystreet.carto.com}} maps for visualising data from hand-held monitoring devices. Hand-held monitors were loaned out with easy to use instructions (Appendix \ref{app:fidas-guide}) which were developed through the process of community investigation (Section \ref{sub:com_invest}). The additional mobile application enabled people to record their monitoring journeys, insert textual comments and recording of audio snippets while they were doing monitoring and also reflect upon their journey once finished. The system had mechanism to automatically send hand-held sensor readings, GPS and personal reflections to the cloud from the sensor commutes once the devices were connected to the Internet. Furthermore, having data available online would enable to build a pipeline where the readings get automatically parsed, processed and visualised.

The SenseMyStreet Proposals platform was a web based Node.JS application leveraging Ideaboard\footnote{\url{https://ideaboard.co.uk}}, a re-configurable online collaboration and feedback platform developed in Open Lab at Newcastle University (based on Vue.JS framework), and RESTful JSON API for communicating with the MongoDB database. People could use the platform to create an account and start collaborating around proposals for fixed monitors. The system enabled people to have discussions around issues and ideas using modern social media functionalities such as online chatting and discussion boards, media sharing, polling, video recording and calling, collaborative whiteboards and online forums. Furthermore, platform provided sharing functionalities for other social media platforms (e.g., Twitter, Facebook, LinkedIn) feeds to get wider community involved with the proposals.

Additionally, there were several external services used to add functionality or support the SMS toolkit. Amazon Web Services (AWS) for deploying two web based platforms and guaranteeing the data backups (using S3 storage) on the systems. SendGrid email delivery service for sending out email notification for subscribed users on different SMS systems. Google Maps API for mapping functionalities on the SenseMyStreet web platform's GIS system, Google Analytics to provide metrics on user visits, and Google reCAPTCHA for protecting against malicious automated software activity. Lastly, Firebase for syncing data between the mobile application and the SenseMyStreet web platform's API.

\subsection{Collected Data}
This section reports on the usage data of SMS and observed interactions with it across different platforms and tools linked to the toolkit. Participants included in this reporting are everyone who took part in community engagement activities, the design of the toolkit or who engaged with any of the toolkits' digital platforms. An overview of the submitted issues are given with their geographical division in relation to the Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD)\footnote{\url{https://www.gov.uk/government/statistics/english-indices-of-deprivation-2015}}. Additionally, this analysis provides an overview of activities for different groups and individuals who took part in the case study and engaged with the digital systems. 

The SMS toolkit enables the collection of metrics from three different bespoke user facing platforms: SenseMyStreet website, automated sensor loan scheduling system, and SenseMyStreet Proposals website. Each system uses its own technology (Section \ref{sub:sms_impl}) to collect statistics about the engagement:
\begin{itemize}
    \item SenseMyStreet website allows reporting on the number of people signed up and reported issues and provide locations and information about inserted issues;
    \item Automated sensor loan scheduling system allows reporting on the number of people applied for doing hand-held sensing and their advocacy group association;
    \item SenseMyStreet Proposals website allows reporting on number of proposals created and the groups that were involved;
\end{itemize}
In addition, there were Google Analytics provided metrics collected from both websites (SenseMyStreet and SenseMyStreet Proposals) and additional external third-party platforms in the SMS toolkit such as Carto Maps\footnote{\url{https://sensemystreet.carto.com}} which provides the number of views on each map, however they were excluded from the analysis on the basis of their lack of use in this particular evaluation.

Although the data generated might become useful in the future, for example when there would be an infrastructure changes implemented in the area, however without any promotion or intended use by citizens, it does not serve the efforts off effective use of data by community for civic participation, advocacy and action.

\section{Discussion}
The data that is being produced about the way city operates and how people interact with it, allows us to see the city through a completely different lens. This is also changing the way that citizens interact with the city, how they express their concerns and participate in decision-making processes. An integral part of this is the integration of civic technologies that aim to engage people to participate in these processes. This section discusses how the commissioning infrastructures such as SMS toolkit could open up new opportunities for more active citizen data production and civic participation in the smart city.

\subsection{New Models of Active Citizen Participation}
The literature review of the thesis (see Chapter \ref{chap:literature}) and motivation of this study (Section \ref{sec:sms_mod}) covered a variety of different technologies and modes of participation (i.e., passive, active) that aim to engage people in all matters of civic life. Furthermore, scholars in design and HCI have also started to distinguish between designing for immediate use or a response to a particular inquiry and designing for future unseen uses \citep{dantec_infrastructuring_2013,ehn_participation_2008,bjorgvinsson_participatory_2010}. This is what is referred to as \textit{infrastructuring}, which essentially means using design practices to support capacity building in the community. 

However designing a commissioning platforms situates itself somewhere in between, where the aim is to develop a generic infrastructure which at the same time could be appropriated by people in their pursuit of issues. For example systems like App Movement where people come together to leverage technological infrastructure to commission bespoke location based mobile applications \citep{garbett_app_2016}. Although it is packaged as a finished platform or toolkit (i.e., product or a thing), it is driven by a participatory design process, facilitates the discovery of issues and also promotes the creation of publics \citep{dewey_public_1954}. This contradicts what \citet{ehn_participation_2008} has suggested, however similar findings where discovered from a community project around the design and use of Community Resource Messenger (CRM) system for homeless communities living in urban areas \citep{dantec_infrastructuring_2013}. Although the CRM system was designed as a response to a practical need of a population, it still facilitated the creation of publics an their attachments around issues. Community commissioning platforms such as App Movement and SenseMyStreet do not shy away from being designed as a purposeful systems, however built for uses which are defined engaging with it through issues of concern.

The SMS toolkit and the process of sensor commissioning had successfully provided mechanisms to carry out a citizen-led deployment of environmental monitors and helped generate data about issues that are important to communities. The toolkit had also created a situation were data is demand-driven rather than the usual open data portal's ideology -- ``If we put data out there, people will use it''. Furthermore, taking things online enabled to scale the commissioning process and extend the participation through different forms of communications for people unable, or unwilling, to engage in face-to-face talk. For example taking the proposal creation online provided a way for people who could not make or were not aware of the community meetings to participate in the discussion and get their voices represented. Additionally it also helped to document the process for transparency and reproduction, sharing the knowledge with other groups who might want to do their own investigations and submit a proposal of their own. Proposals were also looked over by expert professionals at the UO who could give community guidance how to get most out of the deployment in terms of data quality and placements of the fixed monitors.

Indeed, the technology is changing the way we understand infrastructuring. Commissioning of resources and provisioning of infrastructuring through platforms like SMS, certainly proposes a new set of questions of the way infrastructuring ought to be configured. Commissioning platforms have the ability to scale up or streamline the processes of infrastructuring, but the same time have preserve the components of participatory design for constituting publics to develop attachments to act upon \citep{marres_issues_2007, dantec_infrastructuring_2013}. Within this somewhat generic system for engagement, it is important however to recognise the diverse nature of these formed publics and provide different levels of support for taking actions.

\subsection{Physical Infrastructure and Infrastructuring}
Additionally, there needs to be a distinction between providing physical infrastructure and infrastructuring engagement around it. With the case of SMS, the aim was to help people access the physical resources of the smart city to engage in exploration of local issues. Hence, the question is can the act of using or deploying the sensors be seen as a successful effort of infrastructuring? Availability of physical tools (i.e., environmental monitors) can provide support for community action through enabling people to participate in the creation of new sources of data which did not exist before. So in some regards, certainly, commissioned senors helped generate data about the local community and to inform issues of concern in the neighbourhood. Furthermore, in some cases it enabled people to voice their concerns by making them more visible to others through appropriating of the toolkit resources. However the availability of physical infrastructure and the act of participating itself did not facilitate the creation or by itself was not sufficient enough for forming the publics \citep{dewey_public_1954} needed for moving towards citizens taking action. It needed to be integrated with activities supporting the usage of these physical things, or in other words it needed infrastructuring around the physical resources. The SMS toolkit approached this through a four-step exploration process which facilitated framing of issues, gathering support and develop attachments. Issue discovery was built into the system through participatory GIS and citizen sensing to align data generation with particular concerns, and to help form publics around those issues. This aligns with \citet{dantec_infrastructuring_2013} and \citet{marres_issues_2007} ideas of infrastructuring where socio-technical processes and resources are put in place to support imagined futures.

However, without going through the process, the physical infrastructure did not necessarily provide effective use of the resources. This was evident in couple of cases where people signed up to borrow hand-held monitors without defining the issue (i.e., mapping it through the platform) they are interested in (by skipping the first step of the toolkit). Some of them did not collect the monitors and some just tested the monitors for a brief while, but that was the extent of those explorations. It is not to say however that self discovery and learning about technology are something that are not useful for framing issues. When reflecting on the process of commissioning, it seems that getting the commissioned sensors deployed is actually marking a start of additional activities that relate to effective use of data by citizens. Similarly as pointed out by \cite{dantec_infrastructuring_2013}:

\begin{quote}
    \textit{``[...] infrastructuring comes as a result of the re-configurations that occur around and with a technological intervention; that is, the deployment of the technology is a beginning, not an end.''} \citep[p .249]{dantec_infrastructuring_2013}
\end{quote}

It has also been documented that ownership plays an important role in infrastructuring, because of the way it steers people towards future action \citep{dantec_infrastructuring_2013}. Weather it is taking ownership and building attachments to issues \citep{disalvo_local_2009,marres_issues_2007,asad_tap_2017} or taking ownership of the designed technology \citep{disalvo_towards_2009}. The SMS toolkit worked well for helping people to take ownership of the issues by publicly posting them and enabling people to attach personal opinions on the matter to them. Taking ownership of the designed technology (i.e., toolkit and all the resources) however appeared to be much more multi faceted. Commissioning systems are usually designed as a generic platforms or system that connect multiple platforms and bespoke sub-systems. Latter is referred to as design `appropriation' \citep{dix_designing_2007} or `unplatformed' design \citet{lambton-howard_whatfutures_2019} approach, which implies the use of online platforms for purposes that they where not initially designed for. The SMS toolkit could be considered as an unplatformed system because of its use of multiple configurable platforms like MakePlace, Ideaboard, Carto, and Google Drive, Forms, Calendar, mobile app and Scripting (with the addition of physical infrastructure in the form of scientific grade environmental monitors). However, a hidden part of the toolkit were also the communication channels (e.g., Facebook, Twitter, Blogs) which groups used to exchange ideas and share information. These channels often already had an existing community which could be integrated into the system. It would have been impossible for the community to take ownership of those tools and technologies, however people could take ownership of the resources generated as a result of the process. Putting it in \citet{dantec_infrastructuring_2013}'s terms, people taking ownership of the data generated and knowledge created through the commissioned monitors and their role in shaping future actions.

As designers and particularly as engineers of these technologies for participation and civic engagement, there is often a desire to come up or respond to people's requirements with a particular solution that addresses the issue. In case of a commissioning toolkit, it can still be a product or a useful system, however it has to facilitate infrastructuring. Designing provisioning of resources through infrastructuring are more likely to support capacity building (in terms of skills and knowledge) that would benefit the community in a long run and provide a recurring and sustained participation around issues of concern.

\subsection{Active Citizen Data Production}
There is an ongoing debate around data ownership in regards of its generation and rights to access\footnote{\url{https://royalsociety.org/-/media/policy/projects/data-governance/data-ownership-rights-and-controls-October-2018.pdf}}. This also extends to the idea of by law data has no owner, the collection of data does. However, if the data are commissioned by the community, does it means that the community own those data? The SenseMySteet project and the Urban Observatory who deployed the fixed monitors, both work in the spirit of transparency and openness, which means that the data is made publicly available using open licences. If data is in the public domain who does it belong to and can the community take ownership of those data?
 
The act of citizen data generation was actually a two stage process: (1) citizen sensing using hand-held monitors (2) and commissioning environmental sensor data from the UO. In the first stage people engaged in the data collection themselves by using borrowed hand-held monitors to learn more about the issue and what goes into doing environmental monitoring -- what are the issues with the technologies, the uncertainties with the readings and the value of the data. This could have helped the community to get a personal perspective of the issue and enabled them to take ownership of the issue in hand. Enabling citizens access the smart city tools that are already used by the city officials and scientist responded to the cirques of citizen sensing projects, which are often discarded because the types of sensors used by the public is considered to be `toys' \citep{gabrys_just_2018}. In the second stage people commissioned stationary monitors to be placed in their neighbourhood by the Urban Observatory.
 
Having two types of data generation presented itself to be kind of double edge sword. On one hand having people doing their own investigations helped them better understand and frame the issues. The data collected by people using hand-held monitors on their commutes were not used in advocacy efforts in the community, it were predominantly used by the groups themselves to explore the issues and target particular areas for prioritising. Furthermore, because there were no official regulations how do conduct the monitoring the research team could not guarantee the rigorousness and transparency of the processes. For people it was easier to take full responsibility and ownership of that data because they invested their time in it and it was important to them to get the best data out of the process for future actions.

However, the second stage of data generation was carried out by professionals at the UO. This meant that the community commissioned monitors got the same attention and went throw the same transparent deployment process as they would if the data was collected for scientific research or policy purposes. Additionally, having the data for the whole city accessible in one place helped avoid creating another `data silo' and also enabled people to compare the sensor readings from their area to other areas giving them an idea how does their neighbourhood match up to others. When communities handed over the task of data collection to the research team at UO, they put a lot of trust in the process, however often it meant that they also handed over the responsibilities for appropriating the collected data. This was evident with some groups that participated in the study where they commissioned the sensors to provide data for the community without having a particular aim to use it themselves in the future:

\begin{quote}
    \textit{``Air Pollution Monitors along Brunton Lane near the roundabouts would be beneficial to everyone.''} (posted on SenseMyStreet Proposals platform)
\end{quote}

Unfortunately this meant that once the stationary monitor was installed the group stopped interacting with the toolkit because there were no planned next steps with the data. Although the commissioned sensor data was published on the UO city data portal, available for everyone, it was mainly up to the citizens to make use of the data and turn it into activities for local benefit. The toolkit had created an opportunity for communities to access resources to investigate local issues, however generating and making data available is only the first step of its effective use by the community. The initial aim of the case study to come up with mechanisms to democratise data production and use in the smart city got responded through the design, development and deployment of community sensor commissioning toolkit. The indicative findings from the toolkit usage suggest that there is an importance of planned action and establishing a strong community network. It is not only important for keeping up the engagement with the toolkit, but also for sharing skills, increasing social capital and building up community knowledge. There is a need for identifying the underlying relations that help constitute networks as supporting infrastructure for proximate communities. Furthermore, these should be also integrated into the design of the digital tools and processes to promote creation of these networks, specially for communities that are placed in a disadvantage position. However the utility of this infrastructure to act as a catalyst for civic advocacy and action needed to be assessed through an evaluation of its use by the communities. Hence, the extended analysis of the case study will be presented in the following chapter which gives an in depth chronological breakdown of activities of the initially engaged group of residents' (Section \ref{sub:config_par} and \ref{sec:design_sms}) using the commissioned sensor data for advocating for changes in their community.

\section{Summary}
This chapter described the design, development, deployments and analysis of SenseMyStreet (SMS), a sensor commissioning toolkit for communities that enables people use scientific environmental sensing equipment to investigate local issues and commission environmental sensors from the Urban Observatory; placing them in the neighbourhood to gather data relevant to issues of the community at a hyper-local scale. The chapter described in detail the iterative design process of setting up SMS and giving overview of the processes and digital technologies linked to operating a sustainable sensor commissioning toolkit. Furthermore, a detailed description of user interactions on the platforms where given and how data is exchanged within the SMS ecosystem. Finally, a analysis of the toolkit usage was provided to report on the ways that people engaged with the technologies. An extended analysis of how different groups engaged with the digital tools of the toolkit was also provided to understand the key factors that influence the effective use of the toolkit. The purpose of this step was to get an overview of the usage of the system, but also to develop a conceptual understanding for the analysis of the case study for the following chapter. The next chapter provides an analysis of the use of the data by a particular advocacy group to further develop an understanding of effective use of data by communities.
