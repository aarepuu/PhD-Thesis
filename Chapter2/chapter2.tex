\section{Introduction}
A strong case has been made for evidence based decision-making by using data as resource to make educated and informed decisions. However, there is a disjoint between the people who are affected by these decisions and them also having access to tools and resources to collect data in order to provide the needed evidence for change. To investigate how data collected through a sensor commissioning toolkit can support citizen evidencing local concerns, and to understand the processes linked to operating a sustainable and transparent sensor commissioning toolkit, a number of deployments and engagement activities have been carried out in Newcastle Upon Tyne and surrounding areas over the period of three years. This Chapter describes the iterative design process and deployments of SenseMyStreet, a project that has been set up together with Urban Observatory (UO) – UK’s largest urban data sensing network. Sensor commissioning toolkit enables citizens and communities to commission environmental monitors and determine where they will be located on their streets, collecting evidence to inform local issues. SenseMyStreet toolkit was officially launched in the summer of 2017. Since then over 60 people have signed up, 7 groups of people have done environmental sensing with hand-held monitors loaned from the toolkit in their commutes to explore issues in their local area and three communities have commissioned stationary sensors to be deployed in their neighbourhood. Through constant engagement activities with the communities and feedback from online systems, the toolkit went through an iterative design process to involve into an operational citizen sensor commissioning toolkit.

% mention amount of sensor we have in the toolkit??

This chapter explains the motivation for establishing sensor commissioning toolkit for the community, sums up the process of designing and setting up the toolkit, and the development and deployments carried out by the author collaborating with the technicians from the Urban Observatory. This is followed by the evaluation of the use of the toolkit by the communities. The data used in the analysis will include the fieldwork conducted by the author which studied different groups using the toolkit and appropriating the data gathered from the environmental monitors and the interactions recorded through the digital systems. The results from this project will be used to analyse the methods used in the design process and the overall challenges and opportunities associated with developing a transparent sensor commissioning toolkit, which allows people to collect, share, explore and purpose data collected using high precision environmental sensors. Furthermore, the toolkit continues to operate as part of Urban Observatory at Newcastle University to give communities and citizens the power to gather data relevant to issues that are important to them at a very local scale.

\subsection{Sensing the Urban Environment}
Monitoring the urban environment is an important part of figuring out how the city operates and how are people influencing the city as a whole with their everyday lives. Collected data from the city can be a useful tool to understanding these complex processes, but also monitoring and evidencing the changes implemented. Motivations for doing monitoring can be for example creating a digital twin of a city \citep{bolton_gemini_2018}, understanding city soundscapes \citep{adams_sustainable_2006} or air quality \citep{department_for_environment_air_2018} to inform polices around planning and infrastructure changes in the city, or to build a smart city analytics tools for smart service delivery \citep{puiu_citypulse:_2016}.


The affordances of ICT and IoT have enabled a new wave of smart city sensing which is done by the citizens. They can be connected to research effort or a citizen science project \citep{haklay_citizen_2013}, but often they are individuals or groups of people using some type of citizen sensing toolkits (see Table \ref{table:citizen_kits}) or commercially available monitoring devices. Latter devices are what people can put in their own houses, for example to monitor indoor environment\footnote{\url{https://foobot.io}}\footnote{\url{https://www.airthings.com}}, but also kits that people could use outside and publish data to a bigger cloud platform\footnote{\url{https://www.netatmo.com/en-gb/weather/weatherstatio}}. These smart home devices are helpful for increasing self knowledge or understanding, optimising and improving one's living spaces. Using these devices for making a home smart can often be quite expensive and usually they do not provide any opportunities for customisation. However with the increasing popularity of open source hardware movement and the availability of micro-controllers such as Arduino, RasberryPi and MicroBit, people can now build their own sensors to use in citizen sensing. Although people can build their own kits using off the shelf components\footnote{\url{https://wonderfulengineering.com/10-best-raspberry-pi-sensor-kits/}} there are already available plug and play citizen sensing kits. Table \ref{table:citizen_kits} gives an overview of popular devices for citizens for doing environmental monitoring and reviews their openness and ability to get the larger community involved.

These sensor kits have found usage in science education but because of their low barriers to entry, they are particularly good for people to get involved in gathering data about the environment to spark discussions around environmental issues with the community. There are multiple crowdsourced monitoring projects for citizen action around the world where volunteers use low cost sensing equipment to collect data about environment issues and then use those data to advocate for positive change. Non-profit groups like GASP\footnote{\url{https://gasp-pgh.org}} and INHALE\footnote{\url{http://www.inhaleproject.ca}} who recruit people to participate in local air quality monitoring or AirCasting\footnote{\url{http://aircasting.org}} which provides a full platform for air quality monitoring, mapping and exploring; university initiatives such as London Air\footnote{\url{http://www.londonair.org.uk}} which provide up-do date information about regional air quality. Industry is also getting together in projects like HAMNAIR\footnote{\url{http://www.hamnair.ca}} and Aclima partnering up with Google to instrument Street View cars with monitors and publish data on Google Maps platform providing a new source of accessible environmental data \citep{aclima_aclima_2018}.

A lot of these projects however come from already framed agendas similarly to citizen science projects, which sometimes are presented to people seductively to just `have a go'. \citet[p. 384]{hakken_social_2003} is critical of these kind of approaches stating that real constructive learning is replaced with participation in the development of scientific knowledge. Although contributing to scientific knowledge and open resources is important to advance the knowledge of humans, it does not give the individual citizens the power and choice to create knowledge around issues that are important to them. As a response to this, new type of citizen sensing and community investigation tools are surfacing that are more focused on citizen-led projects and enabling citizens to come up with their own problems, conduct investigation and create knowledge to act upon. Examples of such tools are Open Data Kit (ODK) \citep{hartung_open_2010}, EpiCollect \citet{aanensen_epicollect:_2009}, Sensr \citep{kim_sensr:_2013}, wq.io \citep{sheppard_wq:_2012}, CitSci.org \citep{newman_art_2011} and DisCoPar \citep{zaman_discopar:_2015} which enable people propose their own investigations through reusable mobile or web app that can be reconfigured and deployed to specific project. \citet{garbett_app_2016} has taken this one step further and set up a commissioning framework called App Movement where citizens can co-create dedicated applications for their community investigation and data collection. 

Majority of drivers however for doing city scale monitoring come from institutional actors and are ran by experts making assumptions about the population and issues that are important to them \citep{townsend_smart_2013}. Citizens sensing projects are often disregarded by the decision makers, because of lack of trust in the data quality or accountability of the process -- having a perspective that these are people going around using `toys'. It is partly to do with citizens not having the resources to acquire the equipment for monitoring that is equivalent to the ones used by the city, but also the lack of mechanisms that would enable citizens to access already existing resources to conduct their own investigations. Research around smart cities have started to look into issues of spacial inequality and inclusiveness with projects such Spatial Inequality and the Smart City \citep{franklin_spatial_2019} leading the way. It is important to understand and identify these risks and their impacts for citizens, when implementing smart city technology in order to create sensor deserts and to create a smart city of all the citizens. The city needs to serve the needs of all its citizens, not only the select few. Collecting data about the city is just a small part that. There is need for mechanisms that help incorporate the wishes and desires of citizens into the picture of how a city operates.

\subsection{Data and the City}
Cities around the world are working hard on implementing their sensor networks do collect data, but also trying to pull other sources of data (e.g., administrative data, official statistics, operational data, social media, LBS data) together into one place to answer questions about the city. In addition to using that data in decision-making and governance, cities are also pushing that data out for others through data dashboards \citep{kitchin_real-time_2014}. Dashboards such as CityDashboard London\footnote{\url{http://citydashboard.org/london/}}, City of Los Angeles Hub\footnote{\url{http://geohub.lacity.org/}}, City of Sydney Dashboard\footnote{\url{http://citydashboard.be.unsw.edu.au/}} and Smart CEI Moncloa in Madrid\footnote{\url{https://ceiboard.dit.upm.es/dashboard/}}. It seems that a prerequisite to start calling a city smart is at least having one smart city interface \citep{al-hader_smart_2009,al-hader_soa_2009}. Having access to ubiquitous technologies and fast broadband are not the only things that will make the city smart, human factors also play a big role in making the city smart \citep{nam_smart_2011}. City might have a lot of infrastructure in place collecting data about the city, its operations and population, but it needs an engaged public to make use of that data \citep{mcmillan_data_2016}.

Whether data in the city is generated through citizen science or crowd sourcing effort, research or through governmental initiatives, the way it is presented to people depends largely on the purpose of generation -- either it is for public awareness, policy making, research or civic engagement. In order to figure out what purpose does the city data serve, it needs to be looked into from the perspective of its potential users. There are two distinguishable type of potential users of city data -- experts and non-experts. Looking at these features helps understand some of the motivations for people to engage with city data. With non-expert systems there is a clear emphasis on features that put data in a form that gives people immediate benefits (e.g., providing daily levels of pollutants) and links it to health and quality of life. There is also importance on personal and place-placed awareness through making links with places of importance (e.g, schools, nurseries, shopping areas) and activities that people engage in there (e.g., storytelling). Non-expert systems also try to give people already processed data (e.g., air quality indexes and summaries, readings represented through emojis and popular icons) rather than fine grain readings from the sensors.

\subsection{Effects of Urbanisation}

Everyone needs their space to live, work and get around doing their everyday activities. However with more than half of the world's population now living in cities it is becoming increasingly more difficult. It is estimated that the urbanisation will continue and almost 90\% of they world's population will live in densely populated urban environments by the year 2050. As the space in the centres become more of a deficit, cities become dispersed and more people inhabit the outskirts of urban areas. Although people live on the edges of downs and cities, they often work or have to travel because of work to the centres. According to UK government's department of transport the total number of vehicles in Great Britain has increased every year since the Second World War with an average growth of 640 thousand per year. This increased volumes of traffic and people commuting to the city centres consequently creates problems of congestion and air quality. This means that cities are faced with a multifaceted problem that requires the collaboration of policy-makers, city architects, transportation and city planners, public health officials, but also citizens.

\subsection{The Air We Breathe}

According to the 2016 report "Every breath we take - The lifelong impact of air pollution" published by the Royal College of Physicians and the Royal College of Paediatrics and Child Health, outdoor pollution is linked to around 40,000 yearly deaths in the United Kingdom. In addition to explaining the repercussions of bad air quality on people's health, the report also provides recommendations for action and research for national and local
government, business and industry, schools and the National Health Service (NHS), as well as communities and individuals citizens. Among the recommendations for tackling air pollution as an individual were: be aware of the air quality where you live and harness technology to stay informed and monitor air pollution effectively. Having access to this kind of information locally could not only help raise awareness around the issue, but also could help people make better decisions about their travel choices. However, what does it mean to monitor air pollution effectively and does everyone have the means to do that in their neighbourhood?

City of Newcastle upon Tyne hosts one of six national urban observatories in the United Kingdom [REF]. Each city take a slightly different viewpoint, but are all focused on environmental monitoring and data collection in some way or another. Newcastle's Urban Observatory\footnote{\url{http://urbanobservatory.ac.uk}} aims to provide a resource for understanding the impact of the city and the complex interactions in it through sensing and data collection. The observatory operates as openly and transparently as possible to provide the best public access to environmental information from the city and do it the way in which promotes that information being used by a wide range of stakeholders - environmental scientists, city officials, universities, businesses, but also including regular citizens to promote initiatives that involve community-led investigation. Observatory's data portal has had over 5 million data downloads from when it was initially set up. Despite of the best efforts of the project to make data accessible and usable for everyone the data were mainly used by individuals or groups of expert users thus not living up for the slogan of the observatory - "Data for Everyone". The questions that surfaced where whether the public actually cares about environmental data or is there a need for a different approach of citizen engagement?

\subsection{Approach}

Case study followed the overall methodology of the PhD research (see Section \ref{sec:research-approach}) which uses action research (Hayes 2011) aiming to understand the communities needs through engagement activities and user centred design processes to come up with tools and processes to support people led investigations and advocacy efforts.

The motivation for the study (see Section \ref{} and \ref{}) was the increasing urbanisation which affect peoples' health and how they get around in the city, and the availability of resources which could shed light on these processes and issues they rise. Although there was increasing availability of data about these issues there was not a lot of engagement from the people who were affected by these issues.

The Initial interest of the community was explored in a X hour workshop where two activities were run in order to explore current practices of advocacy groups, communities data needs and technologies which would support collection and effective use of that data for civic action and advocacy. The Workshop was recorded and transcribed for further analysis. Transcriptions and written material from the workshop were analysed and coded drawing on Braun and Clarke, to identify themes around concerns and flesh out initial design ideas for the toolkit.

The case study consists of two phases: (1) the designing of community commissioning toolkit and (2) the evaluation of its use by the community. The first part of the case study focuses on the setting up of the sensor commissioning toolkit through working together with a group of residents who are advocating for active travel and infrastructure changes in Newcastle Upon Tyne. This was a 8-month long iterative design process which consisted of understanding the community, gathering design requirements and responding with technologies that could help people better generate data and access and use data in their advocacy efforts. The process is reported through ethnographic engagement with the group which involves private meetings, observations at group organised public events and meetings around the issues of concern.
 
The second part of the case study focuses on the evaluation of the use of the toolkit in the wild. The analysis is conducted through four phases: (1) analysis of the usage and activities on digital platforms built to support the community commissioning of sensors, (2) analysis of the discussions of a specific advocacy group on Facebook (i.e., community discussion), (3) analysis of audio recorded reflections of the core members from the same group on their advocacy efforts and the role of data generated using the toolkit (i.e., community reflection) and (4) observations of groups and individuals who are purposing the data in their advocacy efforts and the impact it has had.

The data collected to report on the case study is however not definitive, as the toolkit is still operational, constantly evolving and being used by communities to gather data for exploring and evidencing issues in their neighbourhood.

\section{Designing Community Resources}

Following section will describe the process of gathering requirements for setting up the community sensor commissioning toolkit. A five month engagement involving a workshop, community meetings, focus groups and ongoing ethnographic work resulted into the first prototype of the commissioning toolkit. Work done is presented in a chronological order to better document the design process.

\subsection{Design Workshop}

A community workshop called "Beyond Pins On A Map" was run by the research team\footnote{The research team consisted of Thomas Maskell and Aare Puussaar. Together we designed the workshop activities, conducted and analysed the workshops to come up with the initial design requirements for the toolkit.} in spring 2017 around how people can collect meaningful data about their everyday journeys, take ownership and use the data in advocating for positive changes in local communities. Workshop engaged with 22 local residents from different parts of Newcastle upon Tyne who took part of two activities to explore the opportunities and challenges of using data to push for change.

The first activity looked at some challenging scenarios that were related to collecting data about air pollution and safety. In groups, people explored issues such as privacy, reliability of the data and how other people not involved in the study might trust the data that has been collected. There were lively discussions going around the tables which were captured and transcribed for further analysis. Second task prompted participants to think about how could that data be used to advocate for better spaces for people to spend time in and move through. In groups, people looked at the issue in hand using examples ranging from art installations, to activist campaigns to inspire them. Groups then brainstormed ideas about what might work in their neighbourhood with the resources they can have and fed back a shortlist of their chosen tactics. Building on Beautiful Rising\footnote{\url{https://beautifulrising.org}}, material was made for the workshop to guide the design process. All the materials produced by the participants were digitalised and archived, accompanied by audio recordings and photographs of the workshop.

Workshop revealed that people are interested in environmental data, but often from places that it is not available and there is no plans from the city officials or decision-makers to collect it. Further analysis also showed that although people feel a bit uneasy about the accuracy of the data, they thought that it is more about how things are framed, and that having something out there for people to discuss is better than not having anything. The figures from the published report about the effects of air pollution and its consequences to people's health can feel remote citizens just going about their everyday activities in the city. However everyone is affected by it and in order to tackle the issue it needs to be made visible within the community by the people living there. There was a consensus among the participants that the issue needs local people to come together not only to collect data but also to make sense of it and to decide how it might be used to take action to make local streets more liveable places.

After the initial design workshop I was approached by a small group of residents from an area couple of miles from the centre of the city. There had been multiple articles in the press lately about the air quality in cities around the world and that had sparked discussions among residents in different community meetings. One of the more proactive resident had also acquired diffusion tube and fitted it near their house. It had provided some concerning results, raising questions and desire to find out more. Some of the residents from that group also attended the initial design workshop. In contrast to the active lobbing group mentioned previously, this group did not have sensors in their local area and there was a lack of information to share with the community. Furthermore, according to the Department for Environment Food and Rural Affairs (DEFRA) air quality model, the pollutant levels were in accordance with the legal limits and it was not part of the two Air Quality Management Zones in the city, which meant that there was no active push from the government to start doing monitoring in the area. People were looking at the increasing high readings of pollutants in other areas of the city which had monitors and that led to a small group of residents getting together to looking at the topic in more detail. Residents expressed particular concerns about the effects of harmful pollutant to children and their developing lungs. This together with increased levels of through traffic trying to get to the city centre made residence worried about the lack of space for active travel and other infrastructure funding not going towards making their local streets healthier and safer for everyone to get a round.

\subsection{Mapping/Surfacing Community Concerns}

There was available funds from the Urban Observatory to acquire environmental monitoring equipment, however up to this point there was no toolkit in place and manage this shared resource, nor there was a clear view of where and the sort of environmental indicators that people are interested in measuring. Together with the research team\footnote{The research team of Urban Observatory consisted of Philip James, Jennine Jonczyk, David Pearson and later Richard Turland.} from Urban Observatory, we came up with a set of potential monitors we could acquire for the toolkit, provided there was a interest from the people. In order to explore these avenues and to get an idea of what people are interested in, a meeting was set up with the group of residents. At the meeting the group gave a bit of background of the area, talked about the areas of concern and their desire for data to help residents make better decisions and advocate for change. For making the conversation more related to the community, a printed out map of the area in question was used. After the group learned about the environmental indicator that can be potentially monitored using selection of scientific grade sensors, they collaborated around the map (see Figure \ref{fig:areasofconcern}) to point out the areas of concern and marked the potential locations for different environmental monitors. 

Having no data available from that area, people had to rely on their perception and local knowledge when deciding on where are the most important places that need investigating through environmental sensors. Through discussion, people had to agree on where to focus their effort and use the limited resources to gather environmental data. As five of the participants where parents whose children go to the local schools the focus was on areas where young people might be exposed to harmful effects of roadside traffic pollution. This focus has been a reoccurring theme with all community groups engaged through the project. Advocates are trying to voice the concern of the ones who might not be able to do it themselves (e.g., young children, elderly, people with reduced mobility) but often are the ones who are affected by the issue the most.

\subsection{Planning Community Investigation}

After the initial meetings with the community group, they came up with proposal called \textit{Air Pollution in Heaton - The desire for data to help residents make decisions} which consisted of:
\begin{enumerate*}[label=\itshape\alph*\upshape)]
\item Background;
\item Areas of concern;
\item A desire for data;
\item Action Plan;
\item Areas for fixed monitors;
\item Areas for hand-held monitoring.
\end{enumerate*}

People where driven by the fact that there was no data available from the area to make an informed statement about the air quality. The first aim for people was to establish a baseline on the level of pollution in the area. This document also set out a clear action plan for doing that by collecting the data, but also using those data for advocacy and activities for local benefits. The following is a statement from the air pollution bid submitted by the residents.

\begin{quote}
\textit{``The hope is that the data collected from these monitors will fill in the current gaps in air quality data for the area and provide a good base line for decisions of further research around measure designed to improve air quality.''} \citep{space_for_heaton_air_2017}
\end{quote}

At this point there was no fixed process in place which would set the criteria of how resources are given to the people, nor was there any mechanism of transparency. The back and forth conversations between the research team and the community group that guided the creation of the proposal where not documented and stayed withing the close group. In addition to the issue of transparency there was also no opportunity for others to learn from the process. Although the exercise of creating an action plan (i.e., proposal) was a good for setting the agenda for the community-led investigation the knowledge shared and obtained in the process stayed with the specific action group. This knowledge however could potentially be used by another community group and lessons learned from it would help improve the process of the commissioning. There was a need to design a mechanism that encourages wider participation through active discussions and feedback loops.

\subsection{Initial Community Investigation}

In addition to being able to commission monitors from the Urban Observatory to be installed in their community the people also wanted to be more actively involved in the investigation. This meant that there was a need for equipment that people can use on their commutes to work or taking their children to school. By doing that people could not only get an individual's perspective and an idea of personal exposure, which links to the health impacts, but also cover more ground in the neighbourhood and provide a good basis for decisions of further investigation around air quality in the area. Although these sensors would provide a snapshot of data, they would still provide a basis for discussions and decision making for the residents. In a way this part of the process is less liked advocacy, and more about self-knowledge and taking ownership of the investigation of issues.

The main concerns pointed out by the members of the advocacy groups where linked to the increased volumes of traffic, having impacts to people's safety and air quality. Through the Urban Observatory project at Newcastle University a high precision hand-held particulate monitor was purchased and loaned out to the community group to start investigating the air quality in the area. At this point all the communication with the advocacy group were done through a face to face meetings or through an email. This also included the scheduling of the sensor handover and return to the UO. This meant that there was always reliance of the researcher to make connections with advocacy groups and coordinate the sensor loans. This surfaced another design requirement for the toolkit.

Next stage of the investigation was highly dependent on the contributions of people. After they got the sensor and a brief introduction of how to use it, they needed to figure out the monitoring schedule, time allocation and logistics around exchanging the monitor within the group to meet the monitoring schedule. People were given an overview of data collection methods, sampling, variability of different indicators and robustness of the data, but the actual monitoring plan was intentionally left for them to set out. An additional reason for promoting community led investigation was the emphasis on peoples experiences of living and moving around the area which provided local tacit knowledge not obtained by the search team. These experiences where also important for understanding the issues and different perspectives around them which meant that there should be a way of capturing them additional to the sensor readings.

\section{SenseMyStreet Toolkit}
% Describe the process step by step (maybe use the actual tutorials made for people)

The findings from the initial engagements, conversations with the community members, observations and the design workshop and the process of designing for community commissioning all fed into the first prototyping of the technology. From the beginning the focus and emphasis was put on supporting initiatives where citizens are involved in problem definition, data collection and analysis hence taking the \textit{extreme citizen science} \citet{haklay_citizen_2013} approach. This case study was conducted through user-centred design process where the designed technologies were direct responses to the requirements that surfaced from engaging with the people. However, because of the participatory nature of the research, the main focus was not on the actual artefacts (i.e., the environmental monitors) that would provide people the capabilities to do community led investigations, but on the whole design process and the \textit{coupled technologies} which where considered as a way of creating knowledge and helping to build capacity within the community. This approach and strategies linked to it have also echoed from scholars working on civic engagement \citep{asad_tap_2017} and participatory design \citep{vines_configuring_2013,binder_design_2011}.The aim of the case study was not to develop stacks more of digital technology because there is already an abundance of smart city technologies available that needs to be put to use by the citizens. The focus with the study was more on the process which investigates the possibilities around re-configuring smart cities to be led by the real needs and desires of citizens, rather than dictated by available technologies and the assumptions of decision makers.

Through the development and use of participatory GIS and mapping, environmental sensing equipment and Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) for geospatial technologies, the first prototype of sensor commissioning toolkit was built. Following sections describe the prototype and digital technologies linked to it. Toolkit is still being used by people and is constantly involving, however for this case study the analysis focuses on the first prototype. The prototype consisted of four steps which each have their associated processes and technologies (see figure \ref{fig:4steps}): \begin{enumerate*}[label=\itshape\alph*\upshape)]
\item Tell us your issue;
\item Plan and propose;
\item A desire for data;
\item Get the facts;
\item Data for everyone.
\end{enumerate*}
SenseMyStreet toolkit consists of a website\footnote{\url{https://sensemystreet.uk}} with MakePlace mapping tool; scheduling system built using Google Forms, Calendar and Scripting (link); SenseMyStreet mobile application\footnote{\url{https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=uk.sensemystreet}}; data parser\footnote{\url{https://github.com/aarepuu/fidasfrogparser}} for parsing hand-held sensor data and merging with GPS reading; Carto\footnote{\url{https://sensemystreet.carto.com}} maps for visualising data from hand-held monitoring devices; Ideaboard\footnote{\url{https://sensemystreet.ideaboard.co.uk}} for collaborating around proposals for fixed monitoring.

\subsection{Tell Us Your Issue}

First step of the toolkit was to help people identify the issues that they are concerned with and want to explore in their neighbourhood. This might be something that an individual is concerned of particularly or something that a group might advocate for, nevertheless these issue were always connected to a place in the community and something that could nourish a creation of a community network. Similar process, as initial community meeting where people collaborated around a map, was mirrored on a website. SenseMyStreet\footnote{\url{https://sensemystreet.uk}} website was set up which provided all the information about the project, its resources, description of the commissioning process and contact details for the research team. To enable participatory mapping, the website also integrated Make Place\footnote{\url{https://make.place}} software, a geographical survey tool developed in Open Lab at Newcastle University. After creating an account on the website it enabled people to flag areas of concern (figure~\ref{fig:4steps}), explore already identified areas (figure~\ref{fig:4steps}) and have a discussion around the issues with the community (figure~\ref{fig:4steps}). Website was based on Silverstripe with configurable elements and was deployed using Docker. Make Place software included re-configurable features such as: configurable geographical surveys; Application Programming Interface (API) to access the survey data programmatically; commenting and voting on survey responses; full Content Managment System (CMS) to input content and design the look of the website; configurable dynamic filtering interface; theme customisation using Docker variables. These features provided additional ways of integrating data with other systems in the toolkit which will be discussed in later sections.

Following an investigation with a hand-held sensor people were presented with a set of data to reflect on. Additional to giving people the datasets they generated on their monitoring journeys, data were also visualised as average air quality readings on the map (see Figure \ref{fig:handheldmap}). Taking the proposal creation online provided a way for people who could not make the community meetings to participate in the discussion and get their voices represented. Additionally it also helped to document the process for transparency and reproduction, sharing the knowledge with other groups who might want to do their own investigations and submit a proposal of their own. Proposals were also looked over by professionals at the Urban Observatory who could give guidance how to get most out of the deployment in terms of data quality and placements of the monitors.

\subsection{Get the Facts}

After the community consolidated their discussions into a proposal and it was approved by everyone who participated, it moved to the next stage of scheduling the deployment with the Urban Observatory. This step of the toolkit did not introduce any additional technologies to the process, but relied on the information and scheduling obtained form the proposals created by the community groups. Once deployment was agreed, engineers at UO scheduled it in with the overall deployment plan. From this point forward the responsibility of data collection was handed over from the community to the researchers at UO, which meant that they had to guarantee a successful deployment and deliver best possible quality of the data possible. Initially the maximum deployment time was one month, but because the monitors needed some time to settle, the deployment time was extended up to 2 months. This did not mean however that the same group could not re-apply for the deployment of extension of the deployment. It all depended on the deployment queue and whether there was available resources.

\subsection{Data for Everyone}

Once the monitors where successfully deployed, people where notified and they gained access to data from their commissioned monitor. To avoid creating another data silo, the data collected from the commissioned sensors were made accessible in real time on UO data portal -- similarly to other monitors deployed in the city. People could use the portal to view the last readings, graph 24 hours or 7 days of readings and use data download functionality or the API to get data on any period of the deployment. Having the data for the whole city accessible in one place allowed people to also compare the sensor readings from their area to other areas giving them an idea how does their neighbourhood match up to others.

\subsection{Summary}

SenseMyStreet has enabled successfully carry out a citizen led deployment of environmental monitors and helped generate data about issues that are important to communities. Toolkit has also created a situation where data is demand-driven rather than the usual open data portal's ideology -- ``If we put data out there people will use it''. Although the toolkit has created an opportunity for communities to access resources to investigate local issues, generating and making data available is only the first step of its effective use by the community. The initial questions of how to provide local communities resources to take part in issue exploration in the city got answered through the design and development of community sensor commissioning toolkit. However the utility of this infrastructure to act as a catalyst for civic action needs to be assessed through an evaluation of its use by the communities.

\subsection{Analysis of FB Data}
The data represented in this section is retrieved through the Facebook (FB) graph API which consists of 265 parent-posts and 1092 comments and replies (a total of 1357 digital objects) that were posted on the group's page between April 2017 and July 2019.

A qualitative analysis of was conducted on the data using thematic analysis approach \citep{braun_using_2006}. In order to cluster the empirical data into codes, social cognitive theory (SCT) developed by Albert Bandura \citep{bandura_social_1977} were used. Theory states that learning and acquiring knowledge occurs by observations of others through social interactions and 
experiences. Bandura merges ideas from cognitive learning theory, which stresses the importance of psychological factors towards learning, and behavioural learning theory, which states that leaning is a direct response to an environmental stimulation, by suggesting that learning occurs in a social context with a dynamic and relational interactions of the \textit{person}, \textit{environment}, and \textit{behaviour}. These three factors where used to analyse the data in terms of each factors strength in advocating for change. This meant that each post discussing an advocacy effort (e.g., organising a community meeting, sending official response to consultations) was coded using its main factor of desired change (i.e., cognitive strategy). The strength was derived from an absolute number of advocacy efforts discussed linked to each factor, which is also analysed from the perspective of how data and sensor technologies were appropriated as tools for civic action. One advocacy effort was often liked to multiple strategies and at times to all of them. This is not surprising, because according to SCT, the factors are tied to each other in a reciprocal manner. Meaning they are in constant interaction with one another which in turn enables cognition to happen. However the factors have a power relationship between each at any given moment. This allowed to assign codes with weights to each data snippet liked to an advocacy effort individually.

To filter the data set and better analyse the interactions between the people related to the different cognitive strategies, the following rules where applied: (1) post has to have at least one reply; (2) post has to be replied by at least one person other than the original poster; (3) post cannot be just done for archival purposes (e.g., sharing a document or image); (4) posts has to be more than simple information requests related to the group admin. Initially, these rules included a minimal length for the post, however in closer inspection there were documents and files shared without a long textual description which prompted discussion and feedback within the group. Some examples where an email thread between a council worker and a group member, or sharing of a image of local newspaper posted through the letterbox. Data set also excludes `like' (i.g., ``thank you'', ``great'', ``good work'' and other confirmatory expressions) type messages, where people are agreeing with the poster or expressing their gratitude. Although this would be interesting data set to study on their own, in order to look at the dynamics of the group, it is out of the scope of this analysis. After applying these rules the data set left for the coding consisted of 193 posts and 647 comments and replies.


Additionally to reporting on the cognitive strategies and how they changed over time as more information became available, anonymized quotes from the data set are provided to illustrate the chronological order of the process and strategies linked to advocacy efforts. This reveals the main focus of advocacy efforts for the community group, how it has formed over time and how does data and technology play role as helping implement strategies for social cognitive learning prompting action and positive change in the community.

\subsection{Thematic Analysis}
This section makes use of the social cognitive action framework and reports on the process of citizen commissioning toolkit and its utility to act as a resource for people to support tackling local issues. Data used in this analysis originates from ongoing ethnographic work which includes field notes from meetings, audio recorded focus group with participants (n=3) reflecting on the overall process and setting the main strategies for changing the community.

Data collected through ethnographic work and using the analysis of the group activities on FB described in the previous section enabled to compile an informed view of the process of sensor commissioning for local communities. This was followed by a focus group where participants involved in the study reflected on the authors informed view by providing their feedback. Focus group was organised as fairly unstructured conversation. It started off with introduction, the processes engaged within the scope of the toolkit and the current status of the group in relation of utilising the data generated using the technology. This was followed by an open discussion in the group facilitated by the author providing occasional prompts to keep people on track. Social cognitive action framework was also used in the focus group with the participants to help reflect on the activities enabled by commissioning toolkit the set the focus and future strategies for effectively using the collected data in civic action.

The analysis shows that the existence of a community of practice (CoP) group is not essential for starting to engage with the toolkit. In many cases advocacy groups formed alongside participating in the activities of SenseMyStreet. However, it was paramount to identify the issue(s) within the community to focus on at specific location(s). Participant(s) who did not identify the issues or did not have a specific agenda in mind did not manage to start generating data from the monitors. There was one special case where an individual's advocacy efforts might have had negative consequences to their quality of life, which meant that the issue was dropped by the individual. However it could be picked up again if there is enough people who are interested in the issue and want to do something about it. This shows that there is a need for a CoP to emerge at some point in order for the successful use of the toolkit and the data it generates. The importance of community network is not only important for keeping up the engagement with the toolkit, it is also crucial for sharing skills and building up community knowledge.

Observations also showed that participant(s) who set a prerequisite action plan that would follow data generation, managed to make effective use of the data. This was prominent on both cases -- when people used hand-held monitors to collect measurements or using data generated through commissioned monitors. There where multiple cases where participant(s) engaged with the toolkit up to the point of getting fixed monitors being deployed, seeing that as the end results. Although the data generated might become useful in the future, when there will be infrastructure changes implemented in the area, however without any promotion or intended use by the community it does not serve advocacy efforts.

\section{Discussion}
SenseMyStreet has enabled successfully carry out a citizen led deployment of environmental monitors and helped generate data about issues that are important to communities. Toolkit has also created a situation where data is demand-driven rather than the usual open data portal's ideology -- ``If we put data out there people will use it''. Being able to access resources to investigate local issues helped people voice their concerns and make them more visible to others. However generating and making data available is only the first step of its effective use by the community. The findings show that process of data generation by communities became useful only when it was supported by additional strategies to purpose the data. The following section discusses how to design to help support the creation of strategies for data that would assert its use; how to appropriate data and digital tools to build and disseminate community knowledge; and how turn that knowledge into activities for local benefit.
