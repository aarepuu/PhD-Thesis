\begin{section}{Prelude}

In the era of the Information Age, libraries no longer hold the largest source of data accessible to humans. Computer systems store and help generate far more data than can be found at the world's largest library -- The Library of Congress. As a result of this, \textit{big data} -- either `too big' in terms of volume, `too fast' in terms of generation or limited processing window (i.e., persisting in state), or `too complex' in terms of structure or variety of sources -- is becoming the centre of our worlds, communities, and research. The term \textit{datum}, that refers to the singular unit of data, is rarely used anymore and seems to be gradually disappearing from our vocabulary. The plethora of data that now can be obtained by using new generation and mining methods, are becoming something that are essential in decision-making processes in organisational structures and in governance. Industry, being the early embraces of the big data, have been successfully using data-driven approaches in product development and market forecasting to make strategic decisions for many years now. In recent years, governments have also started to look into new methods and sources of data to accompany the traditional ways of polling their citizens by contacting censuses to inform polices. For example investing into \textit{smart city} infrastructure or using new techniques and methods accompanied with mobile phone data -- which permits to conduct spatial mobility analysis on national scale -- to extract early statistical indicators about the population. However there is a debate to be had, whether these methods hold up to the scientific rigorousness and whether they are actually working towards eradicating inequalities.

Furthermore, this surfeit of data also provides food for scholars from wide range of research disciplines; from technical domains like pervasive computing, datalogy or data science and urban studies, to human computer interaction and computer-supported collaborative work. Whether it concerns with techniques or methods for recording and performing mass analysis of human behaviour, or looking at complex human interactions mediated by computer supported systems, data have always a role to play in it. All, private and public sector, and research have recognised great potential in working with data to produce monetisable, scientific or social value. 

There is no denying that data creates opportunities, but it can also impose people its use. For example in extreme cases of \textit{datafication} in governance, where there is no need for engaging the public in any issue around the state because there is data that can simply be tallied up to enforce decisions. This means that all the intricacies, unique cultural peculiarities and imperfect organic ways people do things are discarded, and communities are boiled down to fabricated algorithmic identities.

But what happens to people in these communities? Do people just have to stay as subjects of this data or could this data be leveraged by them also to participate in these decision-making processes? There is a growing demand for data and evidence from the civil society when engaging in decision-making processes -- either at local or national level. Often there is lack of data for these groups to state their claims, but in some cases data is already generated. For example by the government conducting censuses and released the data as Governmental Open Data. However in many cases the value offers from that data are hidden from these community groups because of lack of access, know-how, skills or unevenly distributed resources. They are left to their own devices to make data work for them.

Either it is sparse data generated from a sensor attached to a lamppost in the city or dense data obtained through a thoroughly planned collection process, its efficiency is determined by its effectiveness to produce value. The ultimate goal after all for data is to help create actionable knowledge or \textit{wisdom} -- often defined as \textit{intelligence} or in business lingo as \textit{insights} -- which will ultimately helps us make better decisions. Having access to these vast amount of data puts humans at the adjacent possible to create new knowledge and wisdom. Although ubiquitous computing and other information technologies permit people to develop systems that collect, store and retrieve data and information in immense quantities and velocities, such systems will never be able to generate wisdom.
\begin{quote}
\textit{``It may well be that wisdom — which is essential for the pursuit of ideals or ultimately valued ends — is the characteristic that differentiates man from machines.''} \citep{ackoff_data_1999}
\end{quote}
In order to transform data into something valuable and actionable, people often refer to Data-Information-Knowledge-Wisdom (DIKW) pyramid, which original idea is credited to \citep{ackoff_data_1989}. \cite{ackoff_data_1989} states that data is raw material (\citealt[cf.]{tuomi_implications_1999,gitelman_raw_2013}) that needs to be moulded into information, knowledge and then wisdom. According to his knowledge pyramid, there is always more data than information, more information than knowledge and more wisdom than knowledge (\citealt[cf.]{jennex_revised_2013}). There have been different takes on the knowledge pyramid \citep{tuomi_implications_1999,jennex_revised_2013}. \cite{tuomi_implications_1999} state that data is not in fact the building block for the pyramid because it in not observed, collected or recorded in vacuum -- hence it can also not be a raw material. \cite{jennex_revised_2013} argue it is neither one or the other, but in fact both at the same time. They also distinguish that in learning processes the pyramid acts more like \cite{ackoff_data_1989}'s traditional and in organisational structures and knowledge making the pyramid \cite{tuomi_implications_1999} top-down model. These theories were born from fields such as business intelligence, systematic thinking, system sciences and engineering, and organisational memory management. However we are now in a completely different context where these organisational structure rules do not apply, but still we are working with data, so it is often natural for systems engineers and informaticians to bring the same methodology to accompany with the data.

Regardless of the the fact that computer systems can not create wisdom, they are still a crucial part of knowledge making process. They act a gatekeepers and collection mediators, enabling or restricting, people accessing or gathering necessary data to help generate new knowledge or enforce the already known. However a lot of the tools for generating, accessing, interpreting and making sense of data are still catered to professionals and often driven by governmental initiatives or commercial endeavours. This means that available tools for communities for using data in knowledge creation process are often constrained to the same organisational knowledge networking they come from. In order to use such data systems to support knowledge making in communities, the communities need to posses the similar knowledge base that the system developers or the data publishers. However, this hidden \textit{tacit knowledge} \citep{polanyi_tacit_2009} that engineers possess and embed into expert systems is very hard or nearly impossible to communicate to people who might benefit from using these systems. In order it to become \textit{focal knowledge} it needs to come from communities themselves by constructing or deconstructing data in context and in situ \citep{suchman_plans_1985}, and using tools and methods designed with people for those specific purposes.

By not recognising the social. We can’t ignore the social and just engineer or model intelligence, information and knowledge.
% Digital civics approaches

To respond to these challenges, new \textit{digital civics} \citep{olivier_digital_2015} approaches have emerged that recognise the importance of `social' in these processes. The affordance of internet of thing devices, and connected collaborative nature of the internet has enabled new models of knowledge production that promote participation and co-creation of information sources and services. Examples of such approaches are citizen sensing \citep{balestrini_city_2017,gabrys_programming_2014}, crowd sourcing for planning \citep{maskell_spokespeople:_2018,le_dantec_planning_2015} or even communities commissioning their own technologies to create a new data sources \citep{garbett_app_2016}. Projects like these have enabled citizens to generate alternative data sources to be used within the community to evidence issues and potentially challenge decisions. Although the data is now generated by the citizens themselves there is a considerable step from having the data and turning that data into actionable knowledge and activities for local benefit. This often involves working with multiple sets of data from different sources; accessing, cleaning, merging and interpreting them, and using visualisation techniques to tell a story - all which are activities that a technically literate and experienced data scientist would do.

The value of data and its usefulness for civil society to influence governance and decision-making is still unclear. Mainly because it is underutilised by communities due to lack of access, understanding and resources to support data science practices. These practices and skills which are essential for knowledge creation and value generation in this data saturated world. However not only enabling access to new and improved tools will solve the problem of inequality and democratise data science, it is also crucial for research to pursue deeper understandings and role of data in these contexts. Working together with communities, researchers need to identifying caps and barriers, and help increase data literacy in order to empower communities to create knowledge. This thesis seeks to do exactly that, by investigating how citizens can effectively use data in civic participation, advocacy and action. Through conducting two case studies, this thesis looked at how different communities use data for knowledge creation and in local action. 

Case study one engages people with smart city technology and gives people the power to use the technology on their own terms. The case study supports communities in collecting and commissioning collection of environmental data from scientific grade sensors by establishing a toolkit called SenseMyStreet. A transparent toolkit, that has been designed through a user-centred approach, enables people to generate their own data sources to raise awareness about local issues, use them in civic advocacy and influence local policy making processes. Enabling citizens to take control of the technology in the smart city has increased the usage of data collected from the city and has helped with the wider engagement of the public in civic issues. This case study illustrates the importance of citizen engagement in the smart city and helps identify needed processes and resources for effectively using that data. SenseMyStreet continues to being deployed and used by communities -- also being expanded to open up different routes for civic engagement, education and advocacy -- with processes put in place to achieve sustained model of engagement. Having routes for citizens to actively contribute to decision-making processes is changing the power relationships between citizens and decision-makers moving towards a truly inclusive smart city.

Second case study looks at the opportunities and challenges of using Open Governmental Data in community setting and for informing local decision-making processes. Although number of datasets that have been published are increasing by the day, the usage of that data still stays low or is contained within expert group of users. Looking at current state of available tools for interrogating open data issues of paywalls and lack of support for non experts are identified. Engaging with local community and taking a user-centred design approach, an online tool called Data:In Place has been built. Platform uses a visual map based querying system to help define requests for official data sources to help remove technical barriers and make interrogation of data possible for non experts.

Data might be out there, however to condense it into the form that it is accessible and usable by everyone is not a straight forward task due do because of; lack of clear documentation and technical support; corporatization of technology; and absence of unified data standard across the board. Thesis expands on these issues further through reflection and discussion on the lessons learned from designing and building systems for accessible data in Chapter 4.However making data accessible is only the tip of the iceberg of effectively using it in civic advocacy and action. Chapter 5 reflects on both case studies to understand the linked processes around effective use of data and relevant social capital needed turn it into activities for local benefit. 

Finally drawing on the literature the thesis reflects on the research conducted and the systems built for accessible data and their utility for communities hoping to instrument change, to propose a framework for effective use of data. Reflecting on case studies the framework helps to identify the key principles for successful engagement, contributing new understanding of the relationships between data, communities and knowledge networking and providing further design considerations for creators of technologies that aim to democratise data science practices use data for social good.

\begin{section}{Aims and Objectives}

This research explores the use of data within civil society and looks at how can people use data in knowledge making processes that contribute to activities for local benefit. The main aim of this thesis is: 
\begin{center}
\textbf{\textit{explore tools and methods for effective use of data in civic advocacy and action.}}
\end{center}

From this the underlying objectives are:
\begin{itemize}
\item[i.] (\textit{Theoretical objective}) Build up a theoretical model and vocabulary that helps understand and articulate concepts and important factors of \textit{data}, \textit{information}, \textit{knowledge} and \textit{wisdom} in the context of community knowledge making; that sits at the intersection of engineering (i.e., Information Theory), epistemology and social studies.

\item[ii.] (\textit{Technical objective}) Design and build tools that enable and promote the use of data in civic participation and advocacy. This involves understanding the context, culture, values and practices of different community groups working with data, in order to adequately respond to their needs and issues they are tackling. This also includes establish processes that will help build capacity within the community to help sustain the support for community groups working with data.

\item[iii.] (\textit{Conceptual objective}) Provide an understanding around the role of data in knowledge networking/creation/making at community level, that can be applied when designing and building tools for effective use of data in civic participation and advocacy. 
\end{itemize}

\begin{subsection}{Research Questions}

The following research questions are taken up to further study this area and address the aims and objectives of this thesis:
\begin{itemize}
  \item What is involved in citizens creating actionable knowledge using data?
  \item How do we build and configure tools and processes to support knowledge making and promote effective use of data in civic advocacy and action?
\end{itemize}
\end{subsection}

\begin{subsection}{Research Approach}
\label{sec:research-approach}
The availability of different datasets and capabilities of information technologies helps us re-think the role of data in civic participation and in complex sociotechnical processes. This provides opportunities to focus on digital technology and mechanisms that support human centred and alternative approaches. To answer the proposed research questions, after stating an argument that effective use of data in civic advocacy and action needs establishment of knowledge networks within communities, this thesis has adopted a case study approach to explore barriers and opportunities for knowledge making in different contexts and the technologies that support the foundation of \textit{knowledge society} \citep{hakken_social_2003}.

This thesis undertook a Participatory Action Research (PAR) approach manifesting through a user-centred design processes where iterative design and more engineering focused design sprints where applied. Research focused on the active involvement of people in the design processes; not only to identify their particular needs in order to respond with technical solutions, but also to encourage community-based inquires and activities that would drive the research forward. After each iteration there was a feedback loop from the community which dictated the next steps and refinement the developed technologies. This process involved frequent field visits, ethnographical work, engagements, focus groups and workshops with the communities; followed by design and development which resulted in technical tools and platforms. Throughout the case studies the following methodology (see figure \ref{fig:methodology}) was applied to study the role of data and technology in knowledge creation for meaningful civic participation and community decision-making:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Identifying the need for data (\textit{Discovery Phase}). Through conducting interviews and observations of existing community of practice to map out how they are using or not using data. 
  \item Making data and technology accessible (\textit{Design/Develop Phase}). Undertaking user centred design process to create tools and platforms that respond to the needs of the community -- either for data access or production. Starting with paper based methods and then moving to digital tools where appropriate.
  \item Meaningful presentation of data (\textit{Deploy Phase}). Ascertain what visualisations and formats of data are more accessible and useful for the sensemaking processes in different contexts and with different communities. 
  \item Taking collective action by purposing data (\textit{Sensemaking Phase}). Supporting processes of sensemaking and knowledge creation in local communities to use data for decision-making and developing new thinking and solutions.
  \item Assessing the outcomes of data use (\textit{Evaluation Phase}). Evaluating the use of data for improving civic participation and turning it to activities for local benefit.
\end{itemize}

The contexts and the communities with which the research was conducted, differed in terms of social, economic and cultural backgrounds; (i) local groups of activist residents in Newcastle Upon Tyne, UK leveraging environmental monitors to produce data for campaigning and local decision-making (ii) a neighbourhood-planning group in North Tyneside, UK exploring the use of data to inform the process of local policy-making, (iii) two charity organisations in Newcastle Upon Tyne and North Tyneside, UK reliant upon data to focus their work based on the needs of the community, and to illustrate their impact to extend their funding. Design process and the developed technology where directly derived from working with these communities and were translated into technical outputs by the researcher. Although the research was first heavily dependent on the engagement activities of the research team, the aim was to identify the processes and build the needed technology that would sustain the engagement without the lead from the researchers in longitudinal deployments. These included processes of guiding issue formulation, eliminating barriers of data access and production, enabling communities to take ownership of data and self organise to take action around issues. Having these technologies and processes implemented outside `the lab' in longitudinal deployments, and accessible online, enables us to study their use and adoption by different communities and understand how to refine them to remove barriers of engagement and work towards more inclusive technologies.

Hence the lens which the case studies have been analysed and reflected upon is mainly empirical and qualitative in nature, and focused on observations within the communities of practice; and consisting of both, communities and researchers reflections on the impact of created tools and platforms for enabling/empowering people to take action.

\begin{section}{Summary of Contributions}

The research undertaken during this thesis resulted in five contributions to the field of HCI and information engineering. In addition to published work in peer-reviewed scientific journals (see publications), the research also produced open-source tools and processes that are continued to being used and making a lasting impact for communities. The list of scientific contributions from the thesis are as follows: 

\begin{itemize}
\item Establishing a basis of effective use of data in communities and contributing new understanding of the relationships between data, communities and knowledge networking though the design and development of open-source data platforms.

\item\textit{SenseMyStreet}\footnote{\url{https://sensemystreet.uk}}, establishing a first sensor commissioning toolkit for communities that enables people to use scientific grade environmental monitors to produce data about their neighbourhood. (i) The design and development of SenseMyStreet toolkit; (ii) Evaluation of the toolkit and its tools through multiple deployments `in the wild' to determine the needed commissioning services for data production and the utility of that data to support local decision-making; (iii) Establishing a sustainable model for sensor commissioning.

\item\textit{Data:In Place}\footnote{\url{https://data-in.place}}, an open-source web platform which supports citizens in accessing, interpreting and making sense of open data for purpose of civic advocacy. The main contributions of Data:In Place: (i) The iterative design process of Data:In Place, from the perspective of community data needs and the barriers and opportunities for utilising open data; (ii) An online tool with novel map-based querying system for access official statistics about a place, helping interrogate the data, and enabling to map additional data sources and create data visualisations (see \ref{sec:datainplace}); (iii) Reflections on Data:In Place system, its utility and current limitations as a tool for civic advocacy; (iv) Design recommendations to inform future research of data systems for community knowledge making and civic advocacy.

\item A reflection on the process of building and deploying data platforms to support community knowledge creation for purposes of civic advocacy and action. 

\item Definition of a framework to understand the process of community knowledge making and linked processes for effective use of data that helps identify the gaps and needed resources to support data science practices within the civil society.

\end{itemize}

\end{section}

\begin{section}{Thesis Outline}

The remainder of this thesis consists of seven chapters that reflect upon two case studies and an extended study of outcomes to investigate what role does data play in civic participation, advocacy and action. Analysis of the iterative design process, development and deployment of two accessible data systems has provided insights to conclude with a new framework for effective use of data in civic participation and advocacy that pinpoints steps for purposeful use of data and sustained engagement with it around civic concerns.
\newline

Chapter 1 explores the relevant literature for this work. Use of data within communities and in civic action explores a wide range of disciplines from technical fields like sensing networks, pervasive computing and data systems to human computer interaction and computer-supported collaborative work. Purpose of this chapter is to point out the value of data in knowledge creation and different uses of data in decision-making, and the way citizens are involved or not involved in these data-driver processes.
\newline

Chapter 2 presents the iterative design, development and deployment of SenseMyStreet, a sensor commissioning toolkit for communities that enables people to use scientific grade environmental monitors to collect data about their neighbourhood. Chapter describes the evolution of the toolkit, the analysis of lessons learned from setting it up and the results from particular case study with group called SPACEforHeaton, where citizens are using the data that is being collected to evidence an issue and influence policies linked to their local areas.
\newline

Chapter 3 focuses on additional sources of data to promote civic advocacy and presents case study two, the design and iterative development process of Data:In Place, a online map based system for accessing and interpreting open data from governmental sources to support local decision-making and civic action.
\newline

Chapter 4 describes the technical, financial, political aspects involved for appropriating data and making data \textit{open}, \textit{accessible} and \textit{usable} for communities. Chapter touches on some of the points raised by \cite{gurstein_open_2011} around `effective data use', and involves the description of the researchers process and the difficulties faced when making data accessible and usable for communities.
\newline

Chapter 5 reflects on two case studies and goes deeper into the barriers and issues around data use and appropriation for local benefit. Through engagement activities and evaluation of the case studies, an understanding of relevant stakeholders and needed processes for effective use of data are identified.
\newline

Chapter 6 starts off with describing the role of data in the context of knowledge and value creation linked to complex decision-making processes. Moving away from the assumption that data is the `silver bullet', meaning if there is data information, knowledge and wisdom reveals itself, to identifying the key principles of knowledge creation from data that leads to action. Chapter concludes with a framework for effective use of data that helps understand the gaps and needed resources to promote data use within the civil society for actively participating in renewed democratic processes. 
\newline

Finally, Chapter 7 concludes and assesses the success of the PhD through the primary aims and goals. It points out the challenges and limitations with the built tools and processes, proposing routes to future research and suggestions for the sustainability of these developed solutions.

\end{section}
