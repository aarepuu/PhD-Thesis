\section{Prelude}
\subsection{Data, City and Citizens}
In the era of the Information Age, libraries no longer hold the largest source of data accessible to humans. Computer systems store and help generate far more data than can be found at the world's largest library -- The Library of Congress. As a result of this, \textit{big data} -- either `too big' in terms of volume, `too fast' in terms of generation or limited processing window (i.e., persisting in state), or `too complex' in terms of structure or variety of sources -- is becoming the centre of our worlds, communities, and research. The term \textit{datum}, that refers to the singular unit of data, is rarely used anymore and seems to be gradually disappearing from our vocabulary. The plethora of data that now can be obtained by using new generation and mining methods, are becoming something that are essential in decision-making processes in organisations and in governance. Industry, being the early embraces of the big data, have been successfully using data-driven approaches in product development and market forecasting to make strategic decisions for many years now. In recent years, governments have also started to look into new methods and sources of data to accompany the traditional ways of polling their citizens; by conducting censuses to inform polices. For example investing into \textit{smart city} infrastructure or using new techniques and methods accompanied with mobile phone data -- which permits to conduct spatial mobility analysis on national scale -- to extract early statistical indicators about the population. However there is a debate to be had, whether these methods hold up to the scientific rigorousness and whether they are actually working towards eradicating inequalities. 

For the algorithmic smart cities, citizens -- the people who inhabit, work and play in cities -- are seen as another source of data for management and control at scale. Citizens also participate in the smart city by contributing data -- either actively or passively -- using different services on their phones or computers. However, there might be a also room for a more active participation in the smart city, where citizens are active users of those data (i.e., smart citizen), taking part in decision making processes. This surfeit of data and the instrumentation of technologies that support it has provided food for scholars from a wide range of research disciplines; from technical domains like pervasive computing, datalogy or data science and urban studies, to human computer interaction and computer-supported collaborative work. Whether it concerns with techniques or methods for recording and performing mass analysis of human behaviour, or looking at complex human interactions mediated by computer supported systems, data have always a role to play in it. Indeed, private and public sector, and research, all have recognised great potential in working with data to produce monetisable, scientific or social value.

\subsection{Democratisation of Data}
Either it is sparse data generated from a sensor attached to a lamp post in the city; or dense data obtained through a thoroughly planned collection process, its efficiency is determined by its effectiveness to produce value. The ultimate goal for data is to aid people in creating \textit{actionable knowledge} or \textit{wisdom} -- often defined as \textit{intelligence} or in business lingo as \textit{insights} -- which will ultimately help people make better decisions.
Having access to these vast amount of data puts humans at the adjacent possible to create new knowledge and wisdom that can used in action. Although ubiquitous computing and other information technologies permit people to develop systems that collect, store and retrieve data and information in immense quantities and velocities, such systems will never be able to generate wisdom.
\begin{quote}
\textit{``It may well be that wisdom — which is essential for the pursuit of ideals or ultimately valued ends — is the characteristic that differentiates man from machines.''} \citep{ackoff_data_1999}
\end{quote}
\citet{ackoff_data_1989} put forward a Data-Information-Knowledge-Wisdom (DIKW) pyramid, a simplistic idea, which states that in order to transform data into actionable knowledge (i.e., wisdom) one has to go through all the steps of the pyramid from the bottom. \cite{ackoff_data_1989} states that data is raw material (\citealt[cf.]{tuomi_data_1999,gitelman_raw_2013}) that needs to be moulded into information, knowledge and then wisdom. According to his knowledge pyramid, there is always more data than information, more information than knowledge and more wisdom (i.e., knowledge that can be applied in action) than knowledge (\citealt[cf.]{jennex_revised_2013}). There have been different takes on the knowledge pyramid \citep{tuomi_data_1999,jennex_revised_2013}. \cite{tuomi_data_1999} state that data is not in fact the building block (or foundation) for the pyramid because it in not observed, collected or recorded in vacuum -- hence it can also not be a raw material. \cite{jennex_revised_2013} argue it is neither one or the other, but in fact both at the same time. Furthermore, they also point out that in learning processes the pyramid acts more like \cite{ackoff_data_1989}'s and in organisational structures and in knowledge making the pyramid acts as \cite{tuomi_data_1999}'s top-down model. These theories were born from fields such as business intelligence, systematic thinking, system sciences and engineering, and organisational memory management. However, we are now working with data in a completely different context where these organisational structure rules do not apply.

Regardless of the computer systems inability of creating wisdom (i.e., applied knowledge), they are still a crucial part of using data in action. 
%act a gatekeepers and collection mediators, enabling or restricting, people accessing or gathering necessary data to help generate new knowledge or enforce the already known.
However a lot of the tools for generating, accessing, interpreting and using data are still catered to professionals and often driven by governmental initiatives or commercial endeavours. This means that the format of data and available tools for communities for using data in knowledge making process and in action are often constrained to the same organisational knowledge networking \citep[ch. 2]{hakken_social_2003} they come from. In order to use such data and information systems to support knowledge making in communities, the communities need to possess the similar knowledge base that the system developers or the data publishers. However, this hidden \textit{tacit knowledge} \citep{polanyi_tacit_2009} that engineers possess and embed into expert systems is very hard or nearly impossible to communicate to people who might benefit from using these systems. This means that currently citizens are in need for support from professionals in order to access, interpret and use these datasets. Furthermore, it is easy to think of knowledge as being an individualistic thing in the head of an individual, but because a lot of the things in civic advocacy requires collective action, there is need for sharing of knowledge and the distribution of information. In order it to become \textit{focal knowledge} it needs to come from communities themselves by constructing and/or deconstructing data in context and in situ \citep{suchman_plans_1985} using tools and methods designed with people for those specific community purposes.

The value of data and its usefulness for citizens to influence governance and decision-making is still unclear. Mainly because it is underutilised by communities due to lack of access, understanding and resources to support data science practices. These practices and skills which are essential for knowledge creation and value generation in this data saturated world. However not only enabling access to new and improved tools will solve the problem of inequality and democratise data science, it is also crucial for research to pursue deeper understandings and role of data in these contexts. Working together with communities, researchers need to identifying caps and barriers, and help increase data literacy in order to help empower communities to create knowledge that they can put in action. This thesis seeks to do exactly that, by investigating how citizens can effectively use data in civic participation, advocacy and action.

\section{Aims and Objectives}
\hypertarget{objectives}{This research explores the use of data within civil society and looks at how citizens can use data in processes that contribute to activities for local benefit. The main aim of this thesis is}: 
\begin{center}
\textbf{\textit{to explore tools and methods for effective use of data by citizens for civic advocacy and action.}}
\end{center}

From this the underlying objectives are:
\begin{itemize}
\item[i.] (\textit{Conceptual objective}) Through a critical lens of engineering (i.e., Information Theory), epistemology and social studies, build up a conceptual model and understanding of ``what it means to make data usable for purposes of civic advocacy and action''.

\item[ii.] (\textit{Technical objective}) Design and build tools that enable and promote the use of data in civic participation and advocacy. This involves understanding the context, culture, values and practices of different community groups working with data, in order to adequately respond to their needs and issues they are tackling. This also includes establish processes that will help build capacity within the community to help sustain the support for community groups working with data.

\item[iii.] (\textit{Pragmatic objective}) Provide an model of practice for interactions with data in knowledge networking at community level that can be applied when designing and building tools for effective use of data by citizens for civic participation, advocacy and action. 
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Research Questions}

\hypertarget{questions}{The following research questions are taken up to further study this area and address the aims and objectives of this thesis}:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item What is involved in citizens creating actionable knowledge using data?
  \item How do we build and configure tools and processes to support knowledge making and enable effective use of data by citizens in civic advocacy and action?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Research Approach}
\label{sec:research-approach}
The availability of different datasets and capabilities of information technologies helps us re-think the role of data in civic participation and in complex sociotechnical processes. Furthermore, this provides opportunities to focus on digital technology and mechanisms that support human centred and alternative approaches. To answer the proposed research questions and to understand what makes data useful for communities, this thesis has adopted a case study approach to explore barriers and opportunities for effective use of data by citizens for civic participation, advocacy and action. Hence, the level of participation that this thesis is trying to support is very active, or even activist in nature, where citizens become active producers, consumers, and users of data for civic action.

The focus of the research conducted in thesis is \textit{transdisiplinary} \citep{ferreira_social_2020,madni_transdisciplinarity_2007} in nature, meaning at the same time the research is trying to address the engineering problem solving agenda as well as the community problem solving activities \citep{stillman_capability_2014}. The research in this thesis was conducted through Participatory Action Design Research (PADRE) \citep{haj-bolouri_introducing_2015,haj-bolouri_padre_2016} approach which manifested through a user-centred design processes where iterative design, and more engineering focused design sprints, where applied. Research focused on the active involvement of people in the design processes; not only to identify their particular needs in order to respond with technical solutions, but also to encourage community-based inquiries and activities that would drive the research forward. After each iteration there was a feedback loop from the community which dictated the next steps and refinement of the developed technologies. This process involved frequent field visits, ethnographic work, engagements, focus groups and workshops with the communities; followed by design and development which resulted in technical tools, platforms and research methods. Throughout the case studies the following methodology (Figure \ref{fig:methodology}) was applied to study the role of data and technology in knowledge creation for meaningful civic participation and community decision-making:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Identifying the need for data (\textit{Discovery Phase}). Through conducting interviews and observations of existing community or group of individuals, to map out how they are using or not using data.
  
  \item Making data and technology accessible (\textit{Design/Development Phase}). Undertaking user-centred design process to create tools and platforms that respond to the needs of the community -- either for data access or production. Starting with paper based methods and then moving to digital tools where appropriate.
  
  \item Meaningful presentation of data (\textit{Deployment Phase}). Ascertain what visualisations and formats of data are more accessible and useful for the sensemaking (i.e., interpretation and comprehension) processes in different contexts and with different communities.
  
  \item Taking collective action by purposing data (\textit{Sensemaking Phase}). Supporting processes of sensemaking and knowledge creation by local communities to use data for decision-making and for developing new thinking and solutions.
  
  \item Assessing the outcomes of data use (\textit{Evaluation Phase}). Evaluating the use of data for improving civic participation and turning it to activities for local benefit.
\end{itemize}

The contexts and the communities with which the research was conducted differed in terms of proficiency of data; existing community networks, links and capacity; social, economic and cultural backgrounds; social capital:

\begin{itemize}
    \item local groups of active residents in Newcastle Upon Tyne (Newcastle), UK leveraging environmental monitors to produce data for campaigning and local decision-making;
    \item a neighbourhood-planning group in North Tyneside, UK exploring the use of data to inform the process of local policy-making;
    \item two charity organisations in Newcastle and North Tyneside (North East), UK reliant upon data to focus their work based on the needs of the community, and to illustrate their impact to extend their funding.
\end{itemize}

Broadly these research efforts can be linked to different modes on active participation's though data: \textit{making or producing data}; \textit{finding or accessing data}; and lastly \textit{interpreting and using data}. The design process and the developed technologies where directly derived from working with these communities and were translated into technical outputs by the researcher. Although the research was first heavily dependent on the engagement activities of the research team, the aim was to identify the processes and build the needed technology that would sustain the engagement without the lead from the researchers in longitudinal deployments. Throughout the research there was a constant dissembling of the role of the professional data analyst (i.e., the thesis author); scoping the boundaries of how far can the expertise of the professional be removed, knowledge handed down to citizens, or what extent replaced by intelligent systems. These included experimenting with processes of guiding issue formulation, eliminating barriers of data access and production, enabling communities to take ownership of data and self organise to take action around issues. Furthermore, having these technologies and processes implemented outside `the lab' in longitudinal deployments, and accessible online, enables the study of their use and adoption by different communities and understand how to refine them to remove barriers of engagement and work towards more inclusive technologies that support citizens in taking action on local matters.

This thesis has embraced the messiness of society and of conducting research outside the lab environment (i.e., `in the wild') with a wide range of stakeholders, which gives no guarantee of successful outcomes because of the multiplicity of uncontrolled variables for success. Hence the lens which the case studies have been analysed and reflected upon is mainly empirical and qualitative in nature, and focused on observations within the communities; and consisting of both, communities and researchers reflections on the impact of created tools and platforms helping empower people to use data to take action.

\subsubsection{The Issue of The ``Black Box''}
The thesis is written and the research in this thesis is conducted from a perspective of an information engineer who has trained and practised as a data scientist working on big data analytics or over a decade. Working with big data over the years have led the author question the ethical preconditions of conducting such studies on passive datasets from the population. Furthermore, through a sympathetic reading of works from social and human sciences, and conducting research with real people `in the wild' have helped start informing new understandings of data and people that extend across disciplines. Although there is no surveys conducted of researchers, it appears that many of the people working in these social contexts are from a design or humanities backgrounds. This often means that there is a lack of in depth understanding how technology works, and it is often then seen as a ``black box'. That can foster resistance to technology and may be seen as giving a way control from the human to the machine. However, with the field of HCI we are seeing more and more people from mix disciplines crossing over and working together to develop new thinking around this issue. Similarly, the unique skill-set of the author enables to fully understand the technology and see ways that it can be used or reconfigured to help citizens achieve more control over local matters as opposed to giving it away.

\section{The Case Studies}

In order to explore the research questions and address the aims and objectives of the thesis in systematic way, a series of case studies were conducted as outlined below:
\newline

\noindent
\textit{Case Study I} (in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} and \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}) engages people with smart city technology and gives people the power to use the technology on their own terms to produce data. The case study supports communities in collecting and commissioning collection of environmental data from scientific grade sensors by establishing a toolkit called SenseMyStreet. The toolkit consisting of a GIS system for issue mapping, scheduling system of environmental monitor loans and deployments, mobile application for additional active citizen data collection, and a collaborative proposal platform to commission environmental sensor deployments and data; that has been designed through a user-centred approach, enables people to generate their own data sources to raise awareness about local issues, use them in civic advocacy and influence local policy making processes. Enabling citizens to take control of the technology in the smart city has increased the usage of data collected from the city and has helped with the wider engagement of the public in civic issues. This case study illustrates the importance of active citizen participation in the smart city and helps identify needed processes and resources for producing and effectively using that data. SenseMyStreet continues to being deployed and used by communities -- also being expanded to open up different routes for civic engagement, education and advocacy -- with processes put in place to achieve sustained model of engagement. Having routes for citizens to actively contribute to decision-making processes is changing the power relationships between citizens and decision-makers -- moving towards a truly inclusive smart city.
\newline

\noindent
\textit{Case Study II} (in Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) looks at the opportunities and challenges of accessing and using Open Governmental Data (OGD) in community setting and for informing local decision-making processes. Although number of datasets that have been published are increasing by the day, the usage of that data still stays low or is contained within expert group of users. Looking at current state of available tools for accessing and interrogating open data, issues of paywalls and lack of support for non experts users where identified. Engaging with local community and taking a user-centred design approach, an online tool called Data:In Place has been built. Data:In Place is a GIS system built using open-source database and web technologies such as PostgreSQL, Node and Angular JS, Leaflet interactive mapping framework and D3.js visualisation framework. Platform is built to utilise visual map based querying system to help define requests for official data sources connecting to their expert systems and retrieving data to be visualised on the map. Additionally, built in georeferencing enables users to automatically map their own data sources to reference with the official datasets. Furthermore, leveraging a open source visualisation library RawGraphs\footnote{\url{https://rawgraphs.io}} the platform enables to make simple downloadable graphs and charts. The case study aimed to address the information engineering problem of removing technical barriers to data access and interpretation, and make interrogation of open data possible for non experts, however it approached this issue from a community problem solving perspective.
\newline

\noindent
\textit{Case Study III} (in Chapter \ref{chap:cdi_in_action}) uses the new model of Community-Data Interaction (CDI) derived from the findings of previous two case studies and applies it in action. The Case Study III uses a dual approach which leverages the capabilities of technologies built in the Case Study II, and develops new training and guiding methods that help transfer some of the skills of data professionals to citizens. With this case study, the aim was to help citizens increase social capital and effectively use data for activities for local benefit.

\section{Summary of Contributions}
The research undertaken during this thesis resulted in five contributions to the field of Human-Computer Interaction (HCI), Information Systems (IS) engineering and Community Informatics (CI). In addition to published work in peer-reviewed scientific journals (see publications), the research also produced open-source tools (SenseMyStreet and Data:In Place platforms) and processes that are continued to being used and making a lasting impact for communities. The list of scientific contributions from the thesis are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
\item Establishing a understanding what makes data usable (i.e., effective use) for non expert citizens and communities, contributing new understanding of the relationships between data, communities and civic advocacy and action. This includes the deeper understanding of the role the expert professionals play in this.

\item\textit{SenseMyStreet}\footnote{\url{https://sensemystreet.uk}}, establishing a first sensor commissioning toolkit for communities that enables people to use scientific grade environmental monitors to produce data about their neighbourhood. (i) The design and development of SenseMyStreet toolkit (GIS system for issue identification, mobile application, automatic scheduling system, user tutorials, collaboration platform for sensor commissioning) (Section \ref{sec:sms-toolkit} in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}); (ii) Evaluation of the toolkit and its tools through multiple deployments `in the wild' to determine the needed commissioning services for data production and the utility of that data to support local decision-making; (iii) Establishing a sustainable model for sensor commissioning.


\item\textit{Data:In Place}\footnote{\url{https://data-in.place}}, an open-source web platform which supports citizens in accessing, interpreting and using open data for purposes of civic advocacy and action. The main contributions of Data:In Place: (i) The iterative design process of Data:In Place, from the community problem solving perspective; (ii) An online GIS tool with novel map-based querying system for accessing official statistics about a place from expert systems, helping interrogate the data, and enabling to map additional data sources and create data visualisations (Section \ref{sec:datainplace} in Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}); (iii) Reflections on Data:In Place system, its utility and current limitations as a tool for civic advocacy; (iv) Design recommendations to inform future research of data systems for community knowledge making and civic advocacy.

\item Extending the field of CI by presenting of a model of Community Data Interaction (CDI) to understand the process of community knowledge making for civic action through the effective use of data by citizens. Providing a vocabulary and ontology that helps identify the gaps, needed resources and capacities to support data science practices within the civil society.

\item Empirical findings of a practical use of CDI translated into technical implementations and research methods to support community knowledge creation using data for purposes of civic advocacy and action.

\end{itemize}

\section{Thesis Structure and Outline}
The remainder of this thesis consists of eight chapters that reports on three case studies with community groups outlined in Section \ref{sec:research-approach}. Figure \ref{fig:thesismap} presents a map and a timeline of the thesis read from left to right. Because of the nature of doing research `in the wild' and with communities a lot of the time the different research activities often start simultaneously, run concurrently, overlap, feed into each other and strand off. Figure illustrates how different case studies where contacted within a time-frame of three years. We can see that engagements for (1) Data:In Place (Case Study II in Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) started before (3) SenseMyStreet (Case Study I in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} and \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}), however they are reported in the thesis in a reverse order for purpose of having a clearer narrative. Furthermore, (2) the literature review was constantly being expanded and analysed in the light of new findings from the case studies. The reflection of the iterative design processes, development and deployment of the two actively engaging data systems (SenseMyStreet in Case Study I and Data:In Place in Case Study II) provided insights to conclude with a new model of Community Data Interaction (CDI) for effective use of data by citizens for civic participation, advocacy and action. The CDI model was then trialled (4) in action in the final case study (Chapter \ref{chap:cdi_in_action}) presenting empirical evidence for its value in purposeful use of data and sustained engagement with data around civic concerns. Additionally, first half of the research was more focused on answering the first \hyperlink{questions}{research question} and the second half dealt more with the second \hyperlink{questions}{research question}.

\noindent
Chapter \ref{chap:literature} (\textit{Literature Review}) explores the relevant literature for this work. The chapter defines a smart city and the role of citizens in it as active and passive data users. The purpose of this chapter is to present the additional level of participation for citizens in the smart city which enables them to become active users of data for participating in the decision making processes. Additionally, the chapter enables to understand what makes data usable for citizens as active participants.
\newline

\noindent
Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} (\textit{Case Study I - democratising data production}) presents the iterative design, development and deployment of SenseMyStreet, a sensor commissioning toolkit for communities that enables people to use scientific grade environmental monitors to collect data about their neighbourhood. The chapter describes the evolution of the toolkit, the analysis of lessons learned from setting it up.
\newline

\noindent
Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval} (\textit{Case Study I - data use in advocacy}) presents the evaluation of the use of the sensor commissioning toolkit by a group called SPACEforHeaton\footnote{\url{https://www.spaceforheaton.com}}, where citizens were using the data that were being collected to evidence an issue, raise awareness in their community and influence policies linked to their local areas.
\newline

\noindent
Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace} (\textit{Case Study II - democratising data access, interpretation and use}) focuses on access and interpretation of already existing sources of data for uses of community problem solving. It presents Case Study II, the design and iterative development process of Data:In Place, an online GIS system for accessing and interpreting open data from governmental sources to support local decision-making and civic action.
\newline

\noindent
Chapter \ref{chap:cdi} (\textit{CDI - a model of effective use of data}) reflects on the first two case studies and goes deeper into the barriers and issues around data use and appropriation for local benefit. That involves the description of the researchers process and the difficulties faced when aiming to make data accessible, usable and useful for communities. From this analysis a new model for community data interaction is presented that supports future research in community informatics and around effective uses of data by citizens for civic participation, advocacy and action.
\newline

\noindent
Chapter \ref{chap:cdi_in_action} (\textit{Case Study III - CDI in action}) puts the new CDI model into action through dual approach that involves the use of the platform developed in Case Study II (i.e., Data:In Place), additional technologies for deliberation (i.e., Ambit\footnote{\url{https://myambit.co.uk}}), and social facilitation methods that aim to increase social capital and help transfer some of the skills of data professionals to the local communities to effectively use data in action. 
\newline

\noindent
Chapter \ref{chap:discussion} (\textit{Discussion}) discusses and assesses the success of the research through the primary aims and objectives, and outlines their contributions to the wider field of research. The chapter reflects on the challenges and limitations with the built tools and processes, proposing routes to future research and suggestions for the sustainability of these developed solutions.
\newline

\noindent
Finally, Chapter \ref{chap:conclusions} (\textit{Conclusions}) presents a summary of contributions to multiple fields of HCI, IS engineering and CI, and concludes by positing responses to the research questions of the thesis.
