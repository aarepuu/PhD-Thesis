\begin{section}{Background}

Ubiquitous computing helps to generate a lot of data. Either it is sparse data generated from a sensor attached to lamppost in the city or dense data obtained through a thoroughly planned collection process, its efficiency is determined by its effectiveness to produce value. Data is becoming something that is essential in decision making processes in organisational structures and in governance. For example census or product development etc.
Although the the amounts of data generated is vast, there is often paucity of data about how to use it in a purposeful way.
There is no denying that data creates opportunities, but it can also impose people its use. For example in extreme cases of datafication, there is no need for engaging the public in any issue around the state because it can simply tally up the numbers.

The ultimate goal for data is to help create actionable knowledge or \textit{wisdom}, often defined as \textit{intelligence} or in business lingo as \textit{insights}, which will ultimately helps us make better decisions. Having access to these vast amount of data puts humans at the adjacent possible to create new knowledge and wisdom. Although ubiquitous computing and other information technologies permit people to develop systems that collect, store and retrieve data and information in immense quantities and velocities, such systems will never be able to generate wisdom.
\begin{quote}
\textit{``It may well be that wisdom — which is essential for the pursuit of ideals or ultimately valued ends — is the characteristic that differentiates man from machines.''} \citep{ackoff_data_1999}
\end{quote}
In order to transform data into something valuable and actionable, people often refer to Data-Information-Knowledge-Wisdom (DIKW) pyramid, which original idea is credited to \citep{ackoff_data_1989}. \cite{ackoff_data_1989} states that data is raw material that needs to be moulded into information, knowledge and then wisdom. According to his knowledge pyramid, there is always more data than information, more information than knowledge and more wisdom than knowledge. There have been different takes on the knowledge pyramid \citep{tuomi_implications_1999,jennex_revised_2013}. \cite{tuomi_implications_1999} state that data is not in fact the building block for the pyramid because it in not observed, collected or recorded in vacuum. Hence it can not also be a raw material. \cite{jennex_revised_2013} argue in fact that it is neither one or the other, but in fact both at the same time. They also distinguish that in learning processes the pyramid acts more like \cite{ackoff_data_1989}'s traditional and in organisational structures and knowledge making the pyramid \cite{tuomi_implications_1999} top-down model.

These theories are used in business intelligent, systematic thinking and engineering, organisational memory and knowledge creating/making
However we are now in a different context, but still working with data so its natural to bring the same methodology to with the data.

Regardless of the the fact that computer systems can not create wisdom, they are still a crucial part of knowledge making process. They act a gatekeepers and collection mediators, enabling or restricting, people accessing or gathering necessary data to help generate new knowledge or enforce the already known. However a lot of the tools for generating, accessing, interpreting and making sense of data are still catered to professionals and often driven by governmental initiatives or commercial endeavours. This means that available tools for communities for using data in knowledge creation process are often constrained to the same organisational knowledge networking they come from. In order to use such data systems to support knowledge making in communities, the communities need to posses the similar knowledge base that the system developers or the data publishers. This is also referred to as tacit knowledge which is hard or nearly impossible to communicate. Hidden tacit knowledge that engineers put in to expert system that people who use it may not possess or can not acquire \citep{tuomi_implications_1999}. In order it to become focal knowledge it needs to come from communities themselves by constructing or deconstructing data in context and in situ \citep{suchman_plans_1985}.

\cite{hakken_social_2003} also points to the shortcomings of expert systems not adequately dealing with the social, contextual and cultural dynamics at the centre of knowledge networking. 
By not recognising the social. We can’t ignore the social and just engineer or model intelligence, information and knowledge.
This also means that data needs to be constructed or deconstructed in context an in situation.

\begin{subsection}{Research Questions}
This research explores the use of data within civil society and looks at how can people transform data into activities for local benefit. The following research questions are taken up to further study this area:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item What is involved in citizens creating actionable knowledge (from data)?
  \item How do we configure tools and processes to support knowledge making and promote effective use of data in civic advocacy and action?
\end{enumerate}

\begin{section}{Thesis Outline}

The remainder of this thesis consists of seven chapters that reflect upon two case studies and an extended study of outcomes to investigate what role does data play in civic participation, advocacy and action. Analysis of the iterative design process, development and deployment of two accessible data systems has provided insights to conclude with a new framework for effective use of data in civic participation and advocacy that pinpoints steps for purposeful use of data and sustained engagement with it around civic concerns.
\newline

Chapter 1 explores the relevant literature for this work. Use of data within communities and in civic action explores a wide range of disciplines from technical fields like sensing networks, pervasive computing and data systems to human computer interaction and computer-supported collaborative work. Purpose of this chapter is to point out the value of data in knowledge creation and different uses of data in decision-making, and the way citizens are involved or not involved in these data-driver processes.
\newline

Chapter 2 describes the overall approach and methodology for conducting the research and the lens which the case studies are analysed and reflected upon.
\newline

Chapter 3 presents the iterative design, development and deployment of SenseMyStreet, a sensor commissioning toolkit for communities that enables people to use scientific grade environmental monitors to collect data about their neighbourhood. Chapter describes the evolution of the toolkit, the analysis of lessons learned from setting it up and the results from particular case study with group called SPACEforHeaton, where citizens are using the data that is being collected to evidence an issue and influence policies linked to their local areas. 
% should I include all the groups? It seems that groups who had an aim for the data will be successful.
\newline

Chapter 4 focuses on additional sources of data to promote civic advocacy and presents case study two, the design and iterative development process of Data:In Place, a online map based system for accessing and interpreting open data from governmental sources to support local decision-making and civic action.
\newline

Chapter 5 reflects on two case studies and goes deeper into the barriers and issues around data use and appropriation for local benefit. Through engagement activities and evaluation of the case studies, an understanding of relevant stakeholders and needed processes for effective use of data are identified.
\newline

Chapter 6 starts off with describing the role of data in the context of knowledge and value creation linked to complex decision-making processes. Moving away from the assumption that data is the 'silver bullet', meaning if there is data information, knowledge and wisdom reveals itself, to identifying the key principles of knowledge creation from data that leads to action. Chapter concludes with a framework for effective use of data that helps understand the gaps and needed resources to promote data use within the civil society for actively participating in renewed democratic processes. 
\newline

Finally, Chapter 7 concludes and assesses the success of the PhD through the primary aims and goals. It points out the challenges and limitations with the built tools and processes, proposing routes to future research and suggestions for the sustainability of these developed solutions.
