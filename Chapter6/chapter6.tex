This chapter focuses on understanding the challenges of making data \textit{useful} for the community and methods that would help guide people through the whole process of effective use of data to support civic advocacy and action. This includes empowering people to better articulate their questions for data and helping them to come up with strategies to implement to make use of data.

\section{Introduction}

\kylenote{This is more like context and motivation for the study}
Developing and deploying tools that promote better \textit{access}, \textit{comprehension} and \textit{usability} of data, does not mean that the data will find its way into actionable results. Data itself is not evident and inherently useful to a person or group who are able to access it. Both case studies, SenseMyStreet and Data:In Place, the technologies built aimed to make data more accessible by anchoring with place and local context for people to make sense of it. With SenseMyStreet it was the case of democratising \textit{data production} and with Data:In Place it was the case of democratising \textit{data access}. Both studies made good strive towards helping people access and explore different datasets on their own terms. However it might have also created an overload of choice for people, without helping people to come up with questions, queries and strategies for data use. From the case studies it became evident that it is not only up to engineers to build better tools and interfaces for data, there are also other, more human factors, that need to be explored to combine a more holistic approach for effective usage of data in civic action.

Extending on the previous case studies, this chapter discusses what makes people go from ``I want to see all the data'' to ``I want to see data that relates to a question I have'', hence providing data an actionable purpose. Building from the previous chapter, this chapter explores how we might start linking data on the web with questions that people have. Through the use of dual approach, this case study investigated use of digital technologies and cognitive processes involved in using data to support civic action in complex sociotechnical settings. This was done through the process of running Data Consultations in focus groups with key stakeholders from a non-profit organisation and through leveraging Right Question Institute's (RQI)\footnote{https://rightquestion.org/} Question Formulation Technique (QFT) in a community workshop setting. With each engagement a combination of digital tools with supporting methods where trialled. This chapter presents empirical evidence about the effectiveness of these methods to define actionable steps towards purposing data and enables to start mapping out the supported processes needed for effective use of data in advocacy and action. Processes that have often been neglected by designers and information engineers building automated information technologies (AIT) for data discovery. Additionally this chapter reflects on the role that data professional or data scientist play in enabling and supporting communities in using data in action.

\section{Making A Formal Data Request}
\kylenote{This should contain breadcrumbs to chapter before. Also a lot of this can go to the previus chapter because it is open challenges.}
With the Freedom of Information Act, a new mechanism was set up for people to access data held by public authorities. Enabling everyone who wanted, to request data from public authorities by submitting a Freedom of Information (FOI) request. FOI can be looked as a formal query for data, where a person or an organisation deposits a question to the data provider (i.e., public authority). Submitting an FOI request can be a lengthy and complicated process often requiring knowledge regarding legal processes. In order to streamline these processes MySociety came up with a online platform called What They Know\footnote{\url{https://www.whatdotheyknow.com}}, that helps people to submit their requests through a web form. This has made the process of submission much easier, however the actual requesting of data has not improved. In order to get the data needed, one needs to know about the existence of some unpublished document or dataset that is being requested. Apart form using the platform to explore previous FOI requests, there is no way one can make sure that particular data is not yet published somewhere else. As long as there is no unified `Web of Data` that describes all the existing data on the internet and makes it universally accessible to any service or client, this remains an issue. Previous chapter touched on technical and organisational challenges of making the `Semantic Web` a reality.

Another scenario which can be encountered, is when a request is formulated as a direct question without an indication to particular requested resource. In that case somebody has to make the association between the question and a particular dataset (i.e., describe the relationship). Now we have reached to the same problem that was described in the previous chapter: \textit{the lack of links between questions and abstract datasets that might bear the answer}. The ways that these connections are made are largely unknown and often require specialist skills with combination of tacit knowledge. This means either understanding the organisational structures of a public authority or knowing the initial purpose for the data generation.

\section{Redesign of Data:In Place (General Intervention)}
\label{sec:data-in-place_redesing}

The initial prototype of Data:In Place was built (see Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) to enable community groups and charities to access and make sense of data related to their local area. The design and development of the platform was largely driven by the questions and issues of a specific public, and the datasets that were first integrated into the system were a direct response to issues and questions posited by the local Neighbourhood Planning Group at their meetings. Furthermore, additional datasets were added through engagement with local charities and looking into ways their work can be targeted and evidenced. In later stage of design of the platform a \textit{Data Request Feature} was added to the system that enabled people to put in requests for datasets digitally without having to meet face-to-face with a researcher making it a more generic for everyone to request adding datasets. Although this solved the issue physicality, it did not make it easier for people to compile these requests or for the researcher to reply to them. All these issues added to the core issue of these open datasets being underutilised by communities. Data:In place platform was facing the same issues that were pointed out in the case of What Do They Know website.

Working on the dual approach's \textit{general interventions} meant improving the platform to help unpack the challenges around connecting peoples questions and issues to abstract datasets. Additional features where implemented into the platform that automatically added contextual data to requests with the aim of improving data responses to peoples issues and questions. These took form of \textit{anchored data requests} and \textit{data challenges} which will be described in following subsections.

\subsection{Anchoring Data Requests}
\label{sub:anchour_requests}
Data:In Place case study illustrated that to make data accessible and usable to people it needs to be contextualised through place, issues and situated activities people engage in those places. These can be seen as anchors for data that make abstract datasets more comprehensible for people. However using anchoring can also be beneficial when requesting for new datasets to be added. Providing that contextual information to better understand the context where data needs to fit.

The platform's \textit{Data Request} form provided people text entries for describing the issue they are exploring, linking the actual dataset or public authority who might posses the data and describing the issue in with a story (see Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}). This works well if a person has an idea about what type of data they are looking for and where to access it. Then it was a case of connecting the database with the map based querying of Data:In Place. Furthermore, if the particular data provider uses standardised vocabularies and descriptions of the data (such as json-schema) exposed through an API, these processes can be further automated. However if a person had no idea what data might be useful for exploring a particular local issue it makes much harder to find those datasets to add to the platform. Previous work undertaken in the PhD on developing Data:In Place platform (see Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) within the context of Neighbourhood Plan illustrated that people had difficulty making connection between particular issues and datasets that might inform them. They had an understanding that there might be some data available, making a statement that ``the council has the data'', however what type or format they where in was unknown to the community. Working closely with the community and attending their neighborhood plan meetings, enabled the author to make these connections because of the added context obtained from these meetings.

To improve data requests on the Data:In Place platform and add additional context alongside with the submissions, a metadata collection was implemented that included map \textit{boundary data} (see Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) drawn by the request submitter, submitter \textit{geographical location} (provided it was allowed by the person) and the \textit{explored datasets} within the submission session. This additional metadata that was automatically attached to the requests, provided some of the context that would have been originally obtained through the community engagements, but was missing when people only interacted with the system and could potentially help respond to the request with more accurate dataset.

\subsection{Data Challenges}
\label{subs:data_challenge}

An issue that is was encountered in both of the previous case studies (see Chapters \ref{chap:sensemystreet} and \ref{chap:datainplace}) was lack of actions that for using the data once it was obtained. It was often an issue of not possessing the skills needed to work with the data in order to transform it into something usable (see Data:In Place Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}), however it was also the lack of planning or mapping out routes to actions that would be of a local benefit. This meant developing attachments to the data and committing to activities that would use it. These issues are tightly coupled to building community capacity and reaching out to supporting networks for additional support and skills. Some of these processes could be automated, however it was still evident that in some cases people would be needing support form professionals.

As a response, a \textit{Data Challenges} feature was added to the platform that enabled people post challenges about \textit{finding} or \textit{accessing}, \textit{producing}, \textit{making sense} of data and \textit{taking action} with data. This served two purposes: firstly to serve as a plan for taking action with data and secondly a way to increase the capacity and widening the community network by reaching out to people externally.

\section{Targeted Engagements}
\label{sec:targeted_engagements}

Additionally to focusing on the technical implementations by adding features onto the Data:In Place platform, a parallel more targeted approach was also taken to aid people in making data useful for their purposes. This consisted of engagements with different groups of people interested in using data in community settings to explore or evidence issues and take action. This was done through guiding people in positing better questions for data and assisting in creation of steps for actions based on the data. In addition to working with these communities and providing them professional support directly, the aim was also to develop methods and resources that could be used to help others people outside these communities to use the new features of Data:In Place platform and find actionable use for data.

\subsection{Hypothesis Setting}
\label{sub:hypotheses}

In order to know what types of data is needed or might become useful for a particular case, there needs to be a clear aim for the investigation. Case studies described in two previous chapters, although both working with different communities and datasets, had a recurring criteria for successful use of data in action -- data found use in civic action only when people knew ahead or planned out what they are looking for in the data. Simply put when they were asking the right questions from the data. Having a predefined agenda for the use of data turned out to be particularly important for community groups who where hoping to use data for civic action. However, setting that agenda was a difficult cognitive task which often required knowledge of professional data analyst. In a sense it was a ``chicken and egg'' dilemma which needed further investigation in order to depict the variables that influence the process of discovery and queues that would aid people coming up with these agendas.

A common practice in scientific research projects is to first start with hypotheses that the project is trying to prove (alternative hypothesis) or disprove (null hypothesis). This is approach is used in used in variety of scientific communities and fields from engineering to social sciences which forces to come up with set of statements to explain a phenomenon or process. Hypothesis setting was one of the approaches that was trialled in the case study to help people, who where not professional data analysts, to come up with statements for discovering and making use of data.

\subsection{Question Formulation Technique}
\label{sec:qft}

The scientific hypothesis approach or scientific approach may work in professional settings, with people who are familiar with these concepts. However, this thesis aims to help truly democratise these practices and make them work in community contexts where the scientific approach is not commonplace and most probably would not work. To make the process more inclusive and not serve the values of some stakeholders over others, there was a need to look at additional alternative approaches. The closest things to hypotheses are questions, which people are asking every day. The difference is that these are unknown and highly depended upon the context and the way they are formulated.

Challenge was to come up with questions and issues that can be mapped to and explored by abstract datasets. There was need for a better process of guiding people in asking questions that can be explored and answered using data. Fortunately there was already an existing process called Question Formulation Technique (QFT) developed by the Right Question Institute (RQI)\footnote{\url{https://rightquestion.org}} initially to assist parents in better participating in their children's education process to prevent school dropouts. Furthermore, RQI’s methods have been now used in variety of context and settings such as education, health and social care, community-based organisations, and in local democracy. In general the aim of the RQI strategies is to ``help all individuals learn how to ask better questions, participate more effectively in decisions, advocate for themselves, their families, and communities, and hold decision-makers accountable on all levels of democracy'' [REF]. QFT strategy in particular, is a guided step-by-step process to help people formulate, improve and use their own questions by applying multiple ways of thinking: (i) divergent thinking - linear learning and following instructions, (ii) convergent thinking - generating own problems and questions, and (iii) metacognitive thinking - reflecting on their own cognitive processes. Detailed process of the QFT is summarised on Figure \ref{fig:QFT}.

\subsection{Challenge Creation}
% This section describes how can we move from questions to challenges and plan out actions for data.

Asking the right questions might help people better \textit{access}, \textit{interpret} and \textit{make sense} the data used in decision making processes and perhaps even collect their own data to inform an issue, however to effectively participate in these processes using the same data, people need to be able to come up with plans or strategies for civic action that \textit{makes use} of these datasets. A more in depth approach had to be taken to explore and map out the steps that people need to take to move from questions and statements to planning out actions for data. Creating plans for action needed to integrate questions and statements as the starting point and get people thinking about the practical steps that needed to be taken to answer them. This also included figuring out what type of data might be useful and in what form and what kind of resources are needed to make it happen. This guiding process was called \textit{challenge creation} to link it up with the general intervention approach of submitting data challenges (see Section \ref{subs:data_challenge}).

The next section describes the study design taken to investigate the effectiveness of these approaches accompanied with the general intervention strategies described in Section \ref{sec:data-in-place_redesing}. This includes planning data consultation focus groups for hypothesis setting, leveraging Community Conversational (see Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) tabletop game for mapping and reflecting on issues, creating a modified approach of QFT method to help people come up with questions that can be answered by exploring different datasets, and integrating challenge creation guides with QFT to be trialled in community setting. The aim of applying these methods in a workshop setting with specific population was to enable people make better use of Data:In Place added features for data use (see Section \ref{sec:data-in-place_redesing}) that where part of the general interventions of the dual approach.

\subsubsection{Challenge Creation in Community Action}

In this context a \textit{data science challenge} falls under categories such as data access, collection, data processing and analysis, augmentation and visualisation and contextualisation. Starting point for the challenge creation activity where the top priority questions (three questions) that the group chose in the previous activity. Activity itself was set up for people to transform these questions into challenges for data, through thinking about what data and other resources (e.g., technology, people, skills) are needed. People where given additional worksheets ((see Appendix \ref{}) that had prompts on them, helping people think about different aspects of creating a \textit{data challenge}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item What do we already know?
    \item What data/resources do we need to answer these questions:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item What is out there/what exists?
        \item How to get access to it?
        \item Do we need to collect something ourselves?
    \end{itemize}
    \item How to turn the data into something useful?
    \item How to make data or the product of data actionable?
\end{itemize}

 Participants needed to think and document things that they already know, resources and data needed and how could they access them, and what can they do themselves and what kind of help they need externally. Similarly to Data Consultations people also had to think about how would they then bring that data into life through visualisations and how they use these visualisations to take action and advocate for change in the community. This was done through an open discussion within the groups where participants also used provided worksheets to sketch down their ideas. The final step of data challenges where for the participants to document their challenges in Data:In Place platform using the data challenges functionality (see Section \ref{subs:data_challenge}).
 
 Some of those challenges could be taken up by participants themselves without needing any external help, but some of those challenges need help from for example data analyst or policy maker. However the challenge creators would still be involved as the owners of those challenges, providing local insight and collaborating with the people who take up the challenges. Finally the participants are given an opportunity to be involved with the project going forward and indicate whether they want to collaborate or take up any of the challenges. They could do so providing information about skills that contribute and their preferred communication channel (e.g., email, post, face-to-face, Facebook, WhatsApp, Video calls etc).

\subsubsection{Hypotheses Generation}
\kylenote{Have better titles/themes that actually describe the content. Use the process as titles.}
% complexity of hypothesis
% Questions 
% assumptions, local knowledge, domain knowledge
% the unknown
The hypothesis approach required participants to come up with a null hypothesis (HO) and two alternative hypotheses (H2, H2) as the initial part of the data consultations (see Appendix \ref{app:data_consultations}). Hypotheses are the preferred way of conducting experiments or investigations in the scientific community at large. However, what was observed straight away was that the participants found it difficult to come up with well formed hypotheses, and they fell back on asking simple questions which was more natural to them. For example trying to come up with H2, the program officer (PO) deposited following:
\begin{quote}
    ``Has the wellbeing improved of residents in the city, I suppose, or people that have interacted with the campaign somehow.'' (PO)
\end{quote}
To which the outreach officer (OO) added:
\begin{quote}
    ``What is their mental and physical wellbeing like now? [...and] What is it like after we’ve finished?'' (OO)
\end{quote}
Although all of these were valid questions, they were not in a form of a hypothesis and lacked details for starting the investigation. Simple details like time period, place and population, but also the specific measure that of wellbeing for example that can be looked at using data. In order to move into the next phase of the consolidation there was a need to narrow down these details. Through the process of guiding questioning from the author, the participants were able to come up with all three hypotheses (see Appendix \ref{app:data_consultations}).

What also surfaced was the fact that hypotheses where often derived through assumptions or were linked to local and domain knowledge obtained in years of doing work in the community. Here is an example of the learning and skills manager (SM) narrowing down potential metrics to investigate among school performance measures relating to charity's work.
\begin{quote}
    ``I can't imagine we'd have much impact on maths, but I suppose you could argue that if they're improving their attendance'' (SM)
\end{quote}
Or another example of local knowledge from the OO:
\begin{quote}
    ``[...] a hypothetical example. If we know that, if we already know [people's health in one part of a city] is great, it’s perfectly fine. Let’s go target [other part of the city], for example, instead.'' (OO)
\end{quote}
This shows that \citet{ackoff_data_1999}'s proposed data-information-knowledge-wisdom (DIKW) hierarchy does not apply here. Data is not put into use to reveal new knowledge, it used to enforce arguments that are already known. It is also notable here that these snippets of local knowledge were essential for coming up hypotheses that would provide actionable insight for the charity and retain the ownership of investigation results.

\subsubsection{Data and Other Resources}

When discussing  different datasets that might be used to investigate the proposed hypothesis, the participants where convinced that data must exists somewhere amongst the official open data sources:
\begin{quote}
``I know the data is probably out there. Attendance, homework… Attendance, especially, will definitely be out there, we’ll definitely be able to get hold of. Behaviour and homework will probably be recorded, but might just be kept to the individual schools.'' (OO)
\end{quote}
Additionally there where organisations that participants where aware which where specially set up for collecting that type of data:
\begin{quote}
    ``Maybe it’s an Ofsted thing, Ofsted might have hold of all these things because Ofsted go in and they scrutinise schools and they look through all the paperwork.'' (OO)
\end{quote}
Knowing that there might be data available however did not help them access and use it. Participants felt overwhelmed with the amount of effort and expert knowledge which is needed to find the datasets that would be interest to them. SM shared experiences trying to gather information for funding bids and navigating all that data: 
\begin{quote}
    ``How do you find it and how do you access it and how do we then understand some of them? I’ve looked at data sets before trying to find information for bids and they’re just a nightmare. Some of the stuff you get from the Department of Education is horrendous. For some of the stuff you need do need to be a data scientist to unpick it.'' (SM)  
\end{quote}

However, putting down concrete hypotheses made participants think about how they operate as a charity and whether they should also try to collect some of the data, hence reflecting on own practices and trying to get data that is more related to them. Current practices of the organisation was limited to couple of surveys that they asked the teachers to fill in, but thinking into the future participants started to consider new datasets that link to their hypotheses.
\begin{quote}
    ``It’s something that we could record when we’re in schools. If we’re looking at the first hypothesis [H1], where we’re saying that [anon program] improves these things, we can definitely ask these schools, "Can we have your attendance information, your behaviour information, and your homework information while we’ve been here?"[...] There might be a standardised way in which we collect that or we ask the schools to give us that data.'' (OO)
\end{quote}
Also thinking about how to standardise these practices across the different program the organisation is running and perhaps also across the charity sector to promote data sharing and collaboration. Participants were also conscious of not loading the responsibility of this to the teachers who are already overworked and were leaning towards automated systems and processes.
\begin{quote}
    ``If there is some sort of online portal that all schools have to upload to, then great. [...] The teachers don’t want any more work. If they need to start telling us behaviour, attendance, and homework of each single child in the school that’s a big pain.'' (OO)
\end{quote}
% Quate
Hypotheses helped participants to think about the bigger picture and all the different data sources that are needed. For example, in order to compare and relate the data that the organisation produces to the schools that they do not engage with, they would also need data about the general population.
\begin{quote}
    ``I’m just thinking, for the second hypothesis, obviously we can’t go in and ask a school for that if they don’t want to buy into our primary schools package in the first place. That second hypothesis would have to be collected from data that’s already out there.'' (OO)
\end{quote}
Which also made the participant reflect back to original rationale of the project: 
\begin{quote}
    ``I suppose my original idea, with data and place, was, “How can I compare what I capture to the general population?” If I know the measures of the general population, I can relate my data to there and say that my programme is working, it’s great.'' (OO)
\end{quote}
The statement above illustrates that there are notable distinctions between \textit{open data}, \textit{accessible data} and \textit{data in context}. The latter two should be the preferred forms of data and the ones that could potentially be used in an actionable way.

\subsubsection{Visualisation of Data}
\label{sec:data_visualisation}
% identity is important
% types of visualisations depend on the audience
Data could provide the charity confidence that they are doing good work and having an impact in the community, but in order to show that to others it needs to be curated. Participants felt that the types of visualisations that are needed would depend on the target audience and purpose. There are differences in the way you communicate these concepts to different people. For example for official documents the participants would need numbers and statistics, but for engaging the public they would need ``easily digestible infographics''.
\begin{quote}
    ``I think if you had taken away- Obviously, I know we discussed who to show the data to. You’ve got certain people which you’d want to show a bar chart to. They’re probably the stakeholders and the people who are funding the project, for official documents. People who are more visual, it might go nicely into presentations and things for schools and showing teachers.'' (OO)
\end{quote}
It was important to the participants that the visualisations would have all the facts, but would be communicated in a clear way: ``Like I say, something that people look at and they go, 'Ah.' They can instantly see what it is, anyone can understand it, and it’s got the facts.'' (PM).
Probably the most important aspect of the visualisation for the participants was that it needs to incorporate the identity of the organisation and the things they feel strongly about, ``So it’s identifiable that it’s us that’s presenting that infographic, not just anyone else.'' (PO). During the consultation activities, participants used the worksheets (see Appendix \ref{app:data_consultations}) to give an idea how it would look like. Here is OO commenting on the sketched visualisation:
\begin{quote}
    ``I think it’s quite nice to incorporate the [anon sports] club into a lot of this information that we portray. It might be the amount of seats in the stadium or it might be using a percentage of the [anon sports] pitch which has been… Taking the data, or taking the stats, and putting it into something that’s [anon sports] related and digestible.'' (OO) 
\end{quote}

Setting hypotheses, thinking about data and resources needed to investigate them and types of outputs people expected, created a basis for the second consultation. In order to investigate the hypothesis some of the pre-work had to be done by data professional. These included finding the relevant data sources, pre-processing them (if needed) and making them accessible thought the Data:In Place platform's map based query system. These tasks could have been fully automated through data requests built in to the system, or done by any data professionals leveraging the collaborative open source system, however for the purposes of this case study it was done by the author of the thesis who acted as data consultant for the charity. Also having been working closely with the organisation for long period of time, a level of trust had been established which enabled to share some of the operational data needed to carry out the investigation.

\subsubsection{Issue Mapping}
Issue mapping through the use of Community Conversational (CC) provided people an open space to bring any issue onto the table. Participants where asked to use prompt cards (see Figure \ref{fig:ccv2_cards}) to provide more information about issues that where flagged in previous sessions (see Chapter \ref{sec:datainplace}) or add completely new issues to the map. Although people had the freedom to do so, throughout the longitudinal engagement with the local community there where particular set of issues that kept coming up and were being identified by the community. These were linked to things like lack of facilities for kids and green spaces that people in the neighbourhood could enjoy. Participants turned back to discussing those issues, from which some of them were also marked in a previous Community Action Day where CC was used. The utility of tools such Data:In Place or Ambit (the technology behind CC) where not only to document these processes for further use, but to also provide anchoring to these issues by situating them. 

Issues that people where identifying where mainly observations about different places in the community, which they wanted something to be done about. As mentioned before, many of the issues where linked to underused fields or green areas that had been neglected and in need of maintenance. Such as:
\begin{quote}
    ``It’s on your left side and down the road, isn’t it. 
	Where Ronsons was and then they got all the derelict ground.''
\end{quote}
    , or in another place in the area
\begin{quote}
    ``You got a lot of area out there on [anon] Road, haven’t you, that’s just like lying derelict.''
\end{quote}	

Participants felt that these spaces could be utilised better, for example providing activities for young people who did not have any facilities:
\begin{quote}
    ``Nothing, nothing. Absolutely nothing. Sometimes it does get used for kids drinking on the field, hiding in the bushes, so forth. So, I think a lot more could be done with that area, there. They could have a lot more for the kids to play on there. ''
\end{quote}
Although participants where noticing these issues in the community they felt that others, for example the local government, need to do something about it. There where also ideas of establishing new housing and businesses or allotments on those ``no man’s lands'', however participants shifted the actions back to the local council. 
\begin{quote}
    `` I mean, the council could put into plan a cheaper rent, for people to start their own businesses, or anything like that.''
\end{quote}    
    , with echoing similar attitudes from the another group
\begin{quote}
    ``Isn’t that because the council have a long list of people who would like an allotment and the council are so slow at informing the people when the allotments become available.''
\end{quote}
Using the prompt cards and taking turns flagging and discussing issues, both groups ended up with set of marked issues on the map. The activity was set up in a way that people could express support or agreement with others through the 'second that' prompt cards, loaning itself to help filter out issues that are most important to people and preparing for the next activity of prioritisation.

\subsubsection{Issue Focus}
To move onto exploring an issue, participants had to prioritise flagged issues by discussing amongst each other and listening back recording of other people's opinions, finally deciding on which issue is the most important to them. The mechanics of the CC already helped bring some of the issues forward which multiple people felt important for improving the community.

As mentioned before and example focus of `rock climbing` (chosen by the thesis author) was provided to guide participants through the next steps of the process and the question formulation. However, having already gone around the map in the first activity and identifying issues provided participants a better, more anchored focus. One group took ownership of an issue regarding lack of facilities for children, ``[We] want to focus on [anon] Way, because there is nothing there for the kids'', and other group focused on green areas and wanted to ``Re-purpose [anon] View playing fields'' where half of it was used by a local football club. Once the focus was agreed, each group used Data:In Place platform's mapping function to document their focus an make it visible to the other group. This provided each group an issue to focus on, and location to anchoring it, before moving on to exploring it further through questions.

\subsubsection{Question Formulation}
At this point participants had identified and flagged issues, listened other people opinions on them (both live and recorded), discussed within the group and had chosen a focus for their investigation. They had to then use that focus and apply it within QFT that was modified for (see Section \ref{sub:qft_action}). 

When people started asking questions about their issue they where not only constraint to what they could see in those places or ``what is it used for currently''. People had a more exploratory attitude, promoting curiosity and discovery, and coming up with questions like

\begin{quote}
    
    ``What would we like to see there?''
    
    ``How much would it cost?'' and ``Where would the money come from''
    
    ``I wonder if there are any people who have already seen the places, what have you seen in the parks that you think we’d love to have that?''
    
    ``Would a sports day be -- I don’t want to say popular -- community sports day be something you’d value?''
    
    ``Also, how much would it cost to maintain? It might be like an ongoing thing.''
\end{quote}

Asking questions about the issue also shifted the focus from `what the council can do' to `what can the people themselves or the community do':
\begin{quote}
       ``First one would be what can \textbf{we} do with a bit of grass?''
       
       ``How would \textbf{we} go about changing it? The likes of who would \textbf{we} have to see?''
       
       ``We can only try. If \textbf{we} get the funding on it.''
\end{quote}
When people worked on their questions according to the QFT method (for example changing the openness of them), they also reflected on their own role as community leaders or spokespeople for the community. Perhaps using the same questions to give voice to the wider community, not and get more opinions forward:
\begin{quote}
    ``Actually between them two and these two [questions], what I’ve changed is you’re not giving people the option on what they would want there as in what- what was it then we changed, number four? We’re talking about the green area behind a specific thing and to me it would be a park but on my first question what would you like to see there? Now that gives everybody and anybody a chance to say what I would like a bike park, skate park, this park, that park. My next question is would a park benefit your children?''
    
    ``So, I probably shouldn't even use the word ‘park’, because that limits it a bit, doesn’t it?''
    
    ``How do we go about changing from what it is now to what we want it to be? Or what the community want it to be? It’s not what we want it to be, is it?''
\end{quote}

Additionally, questions sparked discussions around the way participants could figure out the needs of the wider community, make them visible to everyone and get community involved in delivering these changes for the neighbourhood. These points where the main considerations when prioritising questions for investigation and challenge creation for both groups:
\begin{quote}
    ``So, our priority questions were, what are the options for the use of the space? Quite broad. What is lacking in terms of the existing local provision, facilities? Then, what is the space already used for?''
    
    ``Our three questions were, because we would like to see more things on there for kids, so our three questions were; how do we get the community involved and maintain their involvement? Because if not everybody wants it then it is not plausible really, is it? It’s got to be everyone on board. Our second question was; how do we go about changing the space to make it what the community want to see? Our third question was; who would fund this?''
\end{quote}
Applying QFT on the issue focus set the groups up to have concrete questions at the end of the process which set the tone for the challenge creation activity.

\subsubsection{Challenge Creation}
% who own the land
% what are current uses
Challenge creation aimed to break down each question using the prompts provided with the worksheets. Because both groups where looking at green areas, it was important to people to figure out who owns those lands first. People always assumed that that land is owned by the council, but nobody knew that for certain. This was a clear challenge in terms of \textit{data access} to obtain the information of land ownership.
\begin{quote}
    "I always thought, “Who owns the deeds of that land?” You know, because obviously I think they should be... [owned by council]. Then, of course, the football team come into it, but owns that last 50 percent of the field or whatever is left of it. Is it council or is private land?''
    
    ``So, local knowledge, council. Presumably, do the football club own their little patch?''
    
    ``Yes, it’s finding out the ownership, yes? So, what I was saying about the idea of finding out what the options are for how to use it.''
\end{quote}
Another challenge that both groups identified was to figure out current uses of those two spaces:  ``I wonder if the idea of the, what’s the space already used for? That’s quite a key question, isn’t it really?''. People had some ideas of the uses based on the observations of ``there is walking access, [...] there are a lot of dog owners that use it [the paths on fields]'' and ``there is a metal seat there, and there are metal goalposts, and a hoop [...] kids do use that [...], but sometimes it is full of glass and that''. Additionally when asked if there were any data about it, participants said that nothing that they were ``aware of''.

In terms of data challenges, this was a challenge for \textit{data production}. This could have been done by observation or by compiling all the local knowledge of the workshop participants, but people thought this could be a good opportunity to collect data from the wider community -- people's current uses of the fields and their ideas for future. This was a challenge that came with immediate actions and people themselves could take it up, rather proposing it to someone externally.
\begin{quote}
    ``Right. To collect the data, or what people think, we would do a questionnaire. We could go door knocking in the local area. Yes [to figure out what people think], and in general if people would be interested and that.''
    
    ``Actually, I think you would have to put it into a different thingy. Not what would you want to see there? Because you could have one person wanting that, that and that. If you said, “Would you be interested in a park being put on?” It would then be a closed question, as in yes or no. Then your piece of paper would just be two. Or it could be three. You might have someone who says, “Maybe.” (Laughter) Then you would only have two, and then it’s pros and cons, isn’t it? Yes and no.''
    
    ``Do more people want a park than not?''
    
    ``So, I think the collecting stuff ourselves thing, that’s going to be what people would want to see it used for, isn’t it and what they’re already doing. So, there is potential there for collecting data from existing usage and that, potentially.''
\end{quote}
This would also extend to not only collecting the current uses of the fields, but also getting people involved in thinking about what can be done with the spaces.
\begin{quote}
 ``So, as in, what do they know that’s elsewhere, that they’d like to have on their doorstep [...]''
 
 ``I know this sounds like Eden, you know that big project down in Cornwall? A local communities thing called the Eden Communities, that help local communities to repurpose green spaces and that sort of thing or to organise themselves.''
\end{quote}
This also sparked conversations around compiling data of things people do in their free time and figuring what are the current available past time facilities in the area or neighbouring areas, creating multiple challenges for data in terms of \textit{data access} and \textit{data production}. 
\begin{quote}
    ``We could do with all this, knowing what the council provisions [...and] a little bit of just local knowledge that we could use there.''
    
    ``Yes, so actually even knowing that exists there is going to be useful, for just knowing what the options might be for knowing about the other facilities [...]''

    ``There was one over the, I think it was Station Road in Newcastle. If you come off the [crossroad], you go down over the bank, there’s actually a massive park which has everything, obviously swings for kids and I think one looks like a full-sized basketball -- a tennis court, whatever, but they have full-sized court there, which is -- but I don’t know if it’s still there or not, but it looks like there’s a park for kids, swings [...]''
    
    ``I can't remember what information is in the census, because you know you can get the anonymised data from the census from 2011, whether that would have anything about what the pastimes... I don't know if that’s in there. You don’t happen to know?''
\end{quote}

Data challenges did not only focus on the first part of getting the data (i.e., data access and production), but also got the participants thinking about challenges that would follow once the data is obtained or collected. These challenges where linked to \textit{data visualisations} which prompted people to think how can they turn that data into something useful. Participants thought that it would be best to present that data back to the community back to the community to gather support and get feedback on ideas. 
\begin{quote}
    ``I think you could put it into a leaflet, we could put one through the doors or if you go on the computer, then that’s all that data on there.''
    
    ``With the computer or just talk, have a chat sort of thing.''
\end{quote}
That would potentially include data collected from the community using the questionnaires which are process as ``[...] information in simple numbers, or graphs``, ``then you would probably put them into categories. Would people want it as this, this or this'' and merged with data from official statistics ``to give a simple answer everyone can understand''.
\begin{quote}
    ``The questions would... What would you like to see on the field? What would you like to see the field used for, blah, blah, blah? When the questions are all done, stacked up.''

A hundred people wanted a...’ So, there could be some graphical representation of [statistics].''
    
	``Could we also put in something there about the census pastime stuff as well? So, you know, we’ve got 300 people from the [X place], who love to cycle.

Perhaps the biggest challenge that people identified was, how to fund the development of these spaces and where would they start looking for those funds.
\begin{quote}
    ``Well, it’s got to be the big one, isn’t it? Where would the funding come from? Where would the money come from?''

\end{quote}
Participants where not only thinking how they could convince the local government to fund these changes, but perhaps there where alternative ways to fund it, looking how they could collaborate with other organisations in the community and even outside of it
\begin{quote}
    ``Maybe get some businesses on board. There are local businesses around here. There are B\&Qs. There is GTF. Actually, there are also another two community centres. There is the [anon organisation] and the thingy, so maybe they could help.''

\end{quote}
For the last activity participants where brainstorming actions they can take with the data. Their initial idea was to compile all the data and the seek feedback from the community to build support:
\begin{quote}
    ``Yes, I mean, so in terms of making the information actionable, are we going to say to people from this leaflet, “Give us a tick to say, ‘Yes, we’ll want to do that,’ or a cross to say, ‘No, I don’t want to do that.’”''
    
    ``Yes, or do we say, “Just come along to the meeting and we’ll talking about it as a meeting,” and a present a much more concrete ideas about things.''
    
    ``Getting all the community together to brainstorm something like that. I know you can’t keep everybody happy.''
    
    ``I guess, in this exercise, we’re assuming that if we’re doing this, there’s a certain level of buy-in, we’re happy, as people, doing this to galvanise a bit of... You know, if we’re talking about doing something with here, we’re happy to lead a bit on saying, “Come and get behind this, we’re going to do something with it.”''
\end{quote}
and then taking that information to the local government:
\begin{quote}
    ``You would have to get a meeting with the council [..] we would have to contact the council, wouldn’t we?
    You would take your findings from that to your council.

    ``If everybody agrees then you go to council and just say, “This is what we’d like to happen.”''

    ``[saying to the council] `This is what the community wants. We have done that door-to-door. We have done whatever is needed'...''

    ``We sort of show them the data, do we, to say that this has got community backing?''
    
    ``Yes. Ask the council if there is anything they can do, even if it’s half.''
\end{quote}

\subsection{Combined Approach to (Information) Inquiry}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Documenting Processes
    \item Reflecting on own practices
    \item Digital trails
    \item Visual identity (the social context)
    \item Simple stats
\end{itemize}
\citet{hakken_social_2003} points out that informatics, being an engineering discipline, the main aim for it is to design automated information technologies (AIT) that work. This often lends itself to building abstractions that distance from the social and/or contextual dimensions that are important to knowledge making processes. Using an approach that facilitates the functionalities of digital tools and data, enables not only understand these dimension, but also helps accommodate the use of AIT to already existing social contexts. In the case of the participants in the study, these where linked to the historical relationships between residents and decision makers or current practices of the local community organisations. These contexts became part of the process of inquiry, helping better surface issues and making people reflect on their own practices by thinking of the ways things have been done before and could be done in the future. Digital tools that accompanied these processes acted both as enablers and historians of sensemaking by creating digital trails of people's interactions. Studies in HCI and participatory design have demonstrated the benefits of capturing different voices and situated understandings, enabling multiplicity of narratives and social interactions to surface \citep{manuel_participatory_2017,rainey_gabber:_2019,bartindale_our_2019}. In many ways the processes engaged in data consultations and community action day served as means to surface relevant context to interpret and make use of abstract open datasets available through the Data:In Place. Although Data:In Place and its functionalities where built generic to enable better access and exploration open datasets, the appropriation of them were highly related to the communities using it and their identities. That related to the datasets needed and relevant to the issues that people where faced with, but also the way data odd to be represented to include the visual identity as social context. For the charity these where things that associated them with the local sports team and for the neighbourhood planning group these where things that represented the community identity, making data relatable to the local residents. However, this context needed to be surfaced by people and intertwined with the data and the formal representations of it.

As information and knowledge is situated and constraint to interpretations \citep{suchman_plans_1985}, it makes difficult to use information systems to convey that needed context. According to \citet{devlin_infosense:_2001} however it is impossible to embed context into information -- it should be extracted from information, at most we can do is to provide indicating of what context might be. This formal abstraction has been criticised by people in social informatics because of its contradictory to the social understanding of information and knowledge \citep{hakken_social_2003}. However both sides agree that in order to recover knowledge from representations of information a social interaction is needed. Whilst face-to-face interactions are the preferred way of knowledge networking, alternative approaches such as embedded context in representation of AITs and supporting knowledge making processes around it should be considered.

On social readings \citet{hakken_social_2003} provides understanding of why such approaches are more likely to have desired outcomes. He states that ``different understandings of functionality in software would provide a better key to developing a more satisfactory approach to the problem of mediating knowledge networking via AIT'' and ``goal should be to find AIT-mediated forms of knowledge representation that functionally equal the sociality of existing knowledge networking'' \citep[p.156]{hakken_social_2003}. Making the main goal for AIT system to implement ways that enable to encode us much context into formal representation of data as possible to support knowledge creation and action. He calls these systems \textit{applied social informatics of knowledge (ASIK)} systems which combine ideas from User Participation in Systems Development or Participatory Design (PD) (Dahlbom and Mathiessen) and Computer Supported Collaborative (or Cooperative) Work (CSCW) (Winograd and Flores), acknowledging that most of the work take place in groups. 

Drawing on \citet{hakken_social_2003} and others in PD and CSCW [REFs], an instance (or perhaps a manifestation of authors experiences and PD process) ASIK was also designed and built in this particular case study following a longitudinal engagement with stakeholders from the local community. Moving away from purely information engineering approaches to social informatics enabled to take a more holistic approach to how data is accessed, interpreted and made use of. The findings suggest that the combined approach using Data:In Place with QFT and Challenge Creation helped facilitate the process of contextualising data and provided conditions for actionable knowledge to be created. The process of inquiry often surfaced that data what was needed, was not as complicated as initially perceived, and through contextualising it could be presented through simple visualisations that people could do themselves or using Data:In Place platform. Additionally, the future directions of the platform might consider ways that knowledge networking can be expanded by brokering the communications between different stakeholders, both professional and non-professional.

Unfortunately in lot of cases (also illustrated by the case studies of this thesis) these observations do not necessarily always follow any actions. The reasons for this might be linked to things like lack of underestanding of the issue, complecities of decision making processes that bring upon or instrument change, lack of community support or perhaps lack of evidence. Although data might be `out there' or there are ways to generate it (for example using toolkit described in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}), people often feel powerless and suffer from an inability to take actions. Early in the process of defining issues, people were pointing out places (using Data:In Place platform and functionalities of Ambit) in the community that they thought someone needs to do something about. Whether it was connected to bringing the issue into light in the eyes of the wider community or even `rolling up sleeves' to put in the work themselves. That also extended to better underestanding of what data (and other resources) are needed and the actions that would follow. Promoting actions that extended from getting and underestanding the data to what was equally important: how that data became interpreted and presented to others. Shifting the end goal from \textit{getting access} to the data to \textit{making data actionable}. This also challenges the ways that citizen participation through data should be perceived, recognising broader interactions and the extent of how data could be used by communities.

Digital technology and affordances of data has created new opportunities to engage in civic participation. New channels of communication could provide a stronger (and more diverse) voice to citizens and support them in active participation in democracy. Appropriation of data as a means of engagement and participation can take many different forms. Research in participatory design, CSCW and HCI has examples of work done in building tools for people to interact with data and use data to communicate with others. System such as X and Y. 
\mynote{find examples}
Similarly, information engineering has examples of big data analytics systems built to algorithmically harness data from citizens to streamline civic participation \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016}. The tendencies are that these two modes -- active and passive, or direct and indirect -- are kept separately from each other because their origination from different disciplines, difficulties around mathematical representations of context or deeper ontological conflicts. Furthermore, there have been criticism towards big data analytics and the way open data advocates portray big data as providing citizens information and resources for capacity building and empowerment, but actually sifting towards datafication and instead serving the needs of citizens (particularly marginal and vulnerable) they are catering to agendas driven by large industry \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016,chandler_world_2015,michael_toward_2016}.

It was made clear from early on the levels of participation that the work in this thesis supports (see Chapter \ref{chap:intro}). Through the course of the case studies the aim was to explore ways data can be made usable to support civic advocacy. This was reflected by the designed digital tools and the processes that supported this type of \textit{active engagement} around data. In addition to the technical and conceptual issues around big data analytics systems, there is a fundamental issue that inhibits their effective use in civic advocacy. They mainly support interactions that are individual rather than group or community focused. It can be noted, that when people talk about public engagement or interactions within the wider concept of data and smart cities, it is often referred to as citizen engagement or citizen participation in the city, however these interactions are always connected to (either directly or indirectly) a wider social context. The way people learn, form understandings, make decision and decide to act on them are always linked to the environmental and social contexts [REF]. This also applies to interacting, interpreting and making use of data. Hence interactions with data and systems that mediate them, should be taken as `shared interactions` instead of individual, specially in the context of communities using data to support civic advocacy and action. That means providing resources and support that fosters the community and provides conditions sufficient for translating data into activities for local benefit \citet{gurstein_effective_2003}. Although some of the skills and resources could be outsourced, such as data science skills, it is also required for the community to take ownership of the issues and tap into local resources and skills to develop a strong community network. These realisations also echoed from the participants who knew that handful of community advocates are not enough to take action, it needs to have the backing of the whole community to instrument change. Going forward, it is essential for social informatics systems and big data systems to integrate mechanisms for community interaction, feedback and collaboration built upon contextualisation of abstract datasets.

Case studies have shown that the wealth of networks [REF] also plays a huge role in using data to support civic action. People who are marginalised or living in areas that are more deprived are less likely to `tap into` resources and infrastructures that enable them to use data in any meaningful way. However working together with these communities through methods that include direct interventions opens up new opportunities for appropriating digital tools and creating a social ecosystem capable of having an positive impact on the community.
