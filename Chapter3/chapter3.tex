\epigraph{Never doubt that a small group of thoughtful, committed citizens can change the world: indeed, it’s the only thing that ever has.}{--- \textup{Margaret Mead}}

This chapter is part of the Case Study I and focuses on the evaluation of the effective use of SenseMyStreet toolkit `in the wild' by communities. The SenseMyStreet sensor commissioning toolkit's process, which was described in detail in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}, consisted of four main parts: 
\begin{enumerate*}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item Identify the issues;
\item Plan and propose;
\item Get the facts;
\item Data for everyone.
\end{enumerate*}
Citizen engagement and civic participation have become an important intersection of studies across engineering, science and technology studies and participatory design. As more technologies are being integrated into the city, design researchers and system developers are exploring new ways to enable citizens to participate in the formulation of issues and taking part in the decision-making processes. Setting up SenseMyStreet toolkit (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}) illustrated how user-centred participatory design processes can be used to engage people in the discovery of local issues using scientific environmental sensor technology. The toolkit did not only aim to help empower citizens to investigate issues that are relevant to them, but it also tried to engage the wider public with the work of the Urban Observatory and the bigger concept of a smart city by helping to get people's voices into the city datasets. Extending on the findings on the toolkit usage by communities presented in the previous chapter, this chapter focuses on understanding engagements with the toolkit by a particular community group that was involved in the initial design of the toolkit (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} Section \ref{sec:design_sms}). Evaluation was conducted over a period of two years and reported in two phases: (1) analysis of the discussions of a specific advocacy group on Facebook (i.e., community discussions), and (2) analysis of audio recorded reflections of the core members from the same group on their advocacy efforts and the role of data generated using the toolkit (i.e., community reflection). Through these, it was possible to analyse the engagement of the community in different stages of the toolkit process and present findings on how the community responded in each step of the process, how they made use of the data, and how the focus of the group changed over time when new challenges surfaced or opportunities became available.

\section{The Evaluation Design}
The evaluation of the SenseMyStreet toolkit was conducted over a period of two years (Figure \ref{fig:sms-timeline}). Drawing on the findings of toolkit usage presented in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} the evaluation was done in two phases using different data sources and methods:

\begin{itemize}
\item[PH I.] (\textit{community discussions}) Looked at people's discussions around using the toolkit for civic advocacy and action. How people perceived the tools, appropriated them and used the data that the tools generate in their advocacy efforts. This phase of the evaluation focused on the discussions between people in an community group involved in the design of the toolkit (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} Section \ref{sec:design_sms}), and analysed posts, comments and replies made on a private Facebook (FB) group which the researcher was given access to. The coding guide used for analysing the empirical data was derived inductively from the initial findings of the toolkit usage presented in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} and from the fieldwork conducted by the author.
\item[PH II.] (\textit{community reflections}) Focused on the participants reflections on the SenseMyStreet toolkit, collected through a focus group with the key stakeholders from the same community group studied in the PH I. It provides a qualitative analysis on the transcribed focus group data using thematic analysis approach \citep{braun_using_2006} with confirmatory content included from the FB data analysed in the PH I.
\end{itemize}

Lastly the evaluation includes an overview of the \textit{community outcomes} from the project. These are presented through example uses of the data, generated through the use of the system and the outcomes of peoples' advocacy efforts using that data.

\section{Data Collection and Analysis}

This section describes the different datasets included in each phase of the evaluation and how they were collected and analysed. Each subsequent phase of the analysis builds on the insights learned from the previous phase building up a detailed evaluation of the case study.

\subsection[Community Discussions]{Community Discussions: Phase I}

About the same time when the first design activities for the SenseMyStreet toolkit kicked off, a group of residents (n=8) in a local community (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} Section \ref{sub:config_par}) started a private FB group to exchange information, discuss local developments and organise their advocacy activities. The same people were the core group involved in the design and development of the SenseMyStreet toolkit described in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}. The author was added to the Facebook group first to exchange information and coordinate the deployments, however retrospectively was given permission to use the discussions in the analysis of the case study.

The data used in the first phase of the evaluation was retrieved using the Facebook graph API consisting of 265 parent-posts and 1092 comments and replies (a total of 1357 digital objects) that were posted on the group's page between April 2017 and July 2019. Taking a contextual design inquiry approach \citep{beyer_contextual_1997} through the analysis of the toolkit usage presented in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}, and the fieldwork conducted by the author which studied different groups using the toolkit and appropriating the data generated using the environmental monitors -- three key factors were inductively derived that influenced the use of the toolkit and the data it provided. These factors where \textit{personal}, \textit{environmental} and \textit{behavioural} (Figure \ref{fig:key-factors}). Similar factors have been used to describe social learning according to the social cognitive theory which was originally developed by \citet{bandura_social_1977}. These three factors where used to analyse and cluster the empirical data in terms of each factor's importance in a particular advocacy effort (Figure \ref{table:outcomes}). Each post discussing an advocacy effort was assigned a code according to the main factor of influence. The importance was derived from an absolute number of advocacy efforts discussed linked to each factor, which was analysed from the perspective of how data and sensor technologies were appropriated as tools for civic action. However, an advocacy effort was often be linked to multiple factors and at times to all of the three. This is not surprising, because the factors are tied to each other in a reciprocal manner. Meaning they are in a constant interaction with one another which in turn enables learning, creation of knowledge creation and action to happen. However the factors have a power relationship between them at any given moment. This allowed to assign codes with weights to each data snippet linked to an advocacy effort individually. The weights of the codes were the following: the strongest factor got 1, one after that got 0.3, and last one 0.1 (if connected to all three). Furthermore, to filter the dataset and better analyse the interactions between people related to the different factors of influence, the following four rules were applied:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item posts must have at least one reply;
    \item posts must be replied to by at least one person other than the original poster;
    \item posts must not be created for archival purposes (e.g., sharing a document or an image);
    \item posts must be more than simple information request related to the group administration activities (e.g., adding a member, sharing contacts);
\end{enumerate}
Initially, these rules included a minimal length for the post, however in closer inspection there were documents and files shared without a long textual description which prompted discussion and feedback within the group. Some examples were an email thread between a council worker and a group member, or sharing of an image of local newspaper, posted through the letterbox of one of the members. Both of these started a discussion and subsequent actions were taken by the group. However, the dataset excluded `like' type messages (e.g., ``thank you'', ``great'', ``good work'' and other confirmatory expressions), where people were agreeing with the original poster or expressing their gratitude. Although this would be an interesting dataset to study on its own, for example to look at the dynamics of the group, it was out of the scope of this particular analysis. After applying these rules, the dataset left for the coding consisted of 193 posts and 647 comments and replies. Additionally to the reporting on the key factors of influence for advocacy efforts and how they changed over time, chronological events and anonymised quotes from the dataset are provided to exemplify the activities linked to advocacy efforts. This reveals the main focus of advocacy efforts for the community group, how it has formed over time and what role did the sensor commissioning toolkit play in implementing strategies for civic advocacy and action by the community. Although, the the factors of influence could be derived from the previous findings and in relation to social cognitive theory to code the data, there was little understanding of the relationships between the factors in the context of citizens using technologies and data in advocating for changes. The aim of this analysis is to get a better understanding of what are the relationships between the factors and what role they play to the process of civic advocacy and action.

\subsection[Community Reflections]{Community Reflections: Phase II}

Deductively applying the factors of influence on data in the previous phase of the analysis, helped to gain insights and enabled the author to compile an informed view of the process of sensor commissioning by local communities. The second phase of the evaluation built on that and seeked feedback from the participants on the authors informed view. The data used in this analysis originates from audio recorded focus group with participants (n=3) who were the key members of the community group. The focus group was organised as fairly unstructured conversations, where participants were reflecting on the overall process of the community commissioning through the SenseMyStreet toolkit and the group's main strategies for advocating for positive change in the community. It started off with an introduction, the processes they engaged within the scope of the toolkit, initial strategies of the group and the current status of the group in relation of utilising the data generated using the toolkit. This was followed by an open discussion in the group facilitated by the author providing occasional prompts to keep people on track: \textit{``What is the agenda we have?''}, \textit{``What could we potentially do with the data?''}, \textit{``What we going to actually do with the data?''}, \textit{``How we going to do it and who is going to do it -- the action plan?''}. Audio recorded focus group was fully transcribed by the author and a qualitative analysis was conducted on the data using thematic analysis approach \citep{braun_using_2006}. The key factors of influence (Figure \ref{fig:key-factors} were applied to code the focus group data in order to gain understanding of the ways that people purposed the sensor commissioning toolkit as a resource for community problem solving activities and for exploring local issues. Additional confirmatory content were also pulled from the previous analysis (i.e., posts and comments from PH I) to saturate the data. Under each theme the sensor commissioning toolkit and its functionalities are reflected upon and reported using anonymised quotes from the transcripts to illustrate how the capabilities of the toolkit can be appropriated for citizen-led advocacy and action.

\subsection{Analysis of Facebook Data}

To better analyse and represent the findings of the coded FB data they were visualised by the author using the project timeline (Figure \ref{fig:sms-timeline}). Figure \ref{fig:advocacy_efforts} presents the timeline graph of coded FB data in terms of the key factors of influence -- \textit{personal}, \textit{behavioural}, \textit{environmental}. The graph shows the evolution of different strategies linked to the key factors of influence (Figure \ref{fig:key-factors}) over a period of two years. Additionally three types of events are marked on the timeline: activities and events related to \colorbox{blue!20}{SenseMyStreet toolkit}, activities and actions of the \colorbox{green!20}{advocacy group}, and the events and \colorbox{red!20}{changes in the community}. Furthermore sections of the the graph are highlighted (using dashed line), where there is a surge of focused activities or where the power relationships between factors of influence changed.

As highlighted on the graph (05/27 -- 07/17), initially the main factor of influence was related to \textit{personal} -- experimenting, experiencing and forming a good understanding of the situation personally - meaning that people where intrinsically motivated to start taking action. This also came out from the initial meeting with group, where people stated that the immediate concern is to establish a knowledge base for the group to understand the issues, the severity of them and to start collecting the data to help make decisions. Similarly, this was also brought out as a major motivator in the action plan (i.e., `Air Pollution Bid') and in the FB discussion:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``Firstly, there doesn't seem to be any information about the air quality in [the area] that is readily available to members of the public. I think it would be good to establish a baseline on the level of pollution within [the area], particularly along the main roads.''} (posted on 06/06/2017)
\end{quote}
Furthermore, once the group received the hand-held monitors for doing initial monitors the had to learn about how the monitors works, how they would collect the data and make sense of it. However, once people got the hang of it and could understand the data, they started to think about how to present it to the wider community:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``We need to start on some explanation to go alongside the plots explaining what's being measured and what the results mean before putting it on our site. We'll need to explain particle pollution and safe limits too but we can talk about that on Thursday.''} (posted on 17/10/2017)
\end{quote}
The second highlight (10/17) on the graph shows how the main factor of influence changed from \textit{personal} to \textit{behavioural} about the time that data, such as hand-held monitoring and local traffic data, become available to publish to the wider community. Furthermore, the group started their website and Twitter account to publish that data, and to engage with the local community online and organised a door knocking to do the same offline. Simultaneously, there were also multiple things happening in the community: an opposition group or counterpublic \citep{warner_publics_2005} emerged, local consultations for infrastructure changes started, and there was media exposure for the group and the project. While the importance of the \textit{behavioural} factor was steadily growing in the advocacy efforts, the group also started to focus more on the \textit{environmental} factor. This was related to actively participating local consultations for built environment change, but also speaking at public meetings organised by local council about the issues in the neighbourhood and the data the group has collected.

The next major leap in advocacy efforts, as highlighted by the third dashed line (01/28), occurred about the time when community commissioned monitors got deployed by the UO in the neighbourhood and the data was made available for everyone on the UO's city data portal. This presented an opportunity to publish that data to the community using the group's online channels, however again people had to inform a better understanding of the issue by working together with the researcher and the team at UO and making sense of the sensor data. Hence, the group had discussions around posting an update to the community without going into the great detail prematurely:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``Good dispassionate set out of the position [group members name]. It seems odd not to put in any `editorial' even if just saying what we (SPACE) want and saying that the Coast Rd level looks like it might be in excess of legal limit assuming other months were similar. Also mention its the only part of [the are] in AQMA [Air Quality Management Zone]. We could say we are concerned and will continue to monitor and share with Ward Councillors, Schools and parents.''} (posted on 09/01/2019)
\end{quote}
The graph also shows a period of no activity in the middle of the evaluation, starting from the fourth highlight, which refers to the period when a longer baseline data was obtained to help inform a better view of the air pollution issue. As illustrated by the events on the graph (02/18 -- 05/18), the group used that time to grate relationships with local residents, schools, businesses and other advocacy groups from the city, to expand their community links and social capital. Furthermore, the availability of the commissioned sensor data on the official city data portal made it visible to other stakeholders in the city, which meant that that data could be potentially used to advocate for policy and built environment changes. The graph shows how the influence of the \textit{environmental} factor had become more predominant compared to the \textit{personal} over that period.

The fifth to sixth highlight (02/18 -- 11/18) on the graph shows the issues with the sensor technology which were flagged by the community and the researcher to the UO. Working together with the people and the research team at the UO, the issues got resolved by changing out the faulty sensors. Following that, there is a series of activities and actions linked to both (but not equally) \textit{environmental} and \textit{behavioural} factors of influence, showing a strong link between these two. The final highlight (06/19) on the graph shows the launch of the automated Twitter bot (Tweetbot) which was designed by the group and built by one of the members. This enabled the group to publish daily summaries from the commissioned sensors to the community online, and print out graphs to take them to face-to-face community events or public meetings.

\subsection{Thematic Analysis}
\label{sub:comm_ref}

This section presents the thematic analysis of transcribed audio data from the focus group with three key stakeholders from the advocacy group (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} Table \ref{table:sms_par}). The aim for the focus group was to seek feedback from the advocacy group for the informed view of the author on advocacy efforts and commissioning process established (and the uses of data) to the observations and FB data analysis in the previous section.

\subsubsection{Personal Motivations}

The initial motivator for the local people was a lack of information about the issues concerning them. When the author was first approached by the residents after the initial design workshop, that was the main reason why people wanted to start doing environmental monitoring in their area \ref{chap:sensemystreet}. This was also reflected by the participants in the focus group:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``Well [we've] got to remember why we started it in the first place, which was to just find out where we were really, wasn’t it? See if things were good, bad, or getting worst or getting better.''} (P2)
\end{quote}

There was some data available on higher levels from the governmental agencies (e.g., DEFRA modelled data) and already established baselines from other parts of the city, but not at the specific locations and the granularity that would confirm or refute the concerns of residents. Residents were finding hard to contextualise the national and city wide discussions without having any local information about those issues:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``I think the, we were aware that there were couple of city council run monitoring sites you know. Sort of on the Cradlewell bypass and the Civic Centre, but the scale of those is fairly large and having the ability of zoom in to street by street level, you know sort of neighbourhood level was really interesting.''} (P3)
\end{quote}

Although there were already people who where intrinsically motivated and came together because they wanted to do something about these issues, there was not enough hyper-local data available in order to build up a strong informed view of the issue. The community was relying on peoples personal experiences of living in the area, and their view was informed by stories and often anecdotal evidence. This also meant that it was hard for them to advice their fellow residents and the wider community on matters of concern:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``I mean it reflected the concern that various residence were included had about air pollution in the city or part of the city [in] particular. Um and not knowing whether it was good, bad or indifferent. Not knowing where there were problems lay and knowing how to advice people who talk to us or we relate to, you know about whether there is a problem and where there’s a problem and what, if anything, could be done about it.''} (P1)
\end{quote}

Furthermore, there were available statistics about about traffic in the area from one of the city wide consultations, but there was no data about what was it doing to peoples health and well being:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``I think what it also reflected, [is] the fact that congestion in our part of the city, has over the last five or ten years increased substantially. Lots of standing vehicles, the rush hour, ``so called rush hour'' is getting longer – both in morning and the afternoon. And there was concern what that increase in traffic is actually doing in terms of the health and welfare of people, particularly younger and older people.''} (P1)
\end{quote}
Additionally, the were national and city scale changes happening (e.g., clean air zones and transportation infrastructure changes) that could impact the local area and how people get around in the neighbourhood. The group was interested in establishing a baseline or a benchmark before the changes:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{`` And I think obviously they [the city council] model it from DEFRA about the [Anon] Road being one of the air pollution monitoring zones, I think one of the modelling from DEFRA is pointing towards that is being a problem area. And what we didn't want to happen is that the council propose to route more traffic through [the area], where people live, to solve the pollution on the [Anon] Road. You kinda really wanna benchmark what is going on before they start doing something you know. Because these are essentially residential areas you know.''} (P3)
\end{quote}

Most of the starting motivators and the focus for efforts were driven by personal agendas of motivated individuals wanting to establish an understanding of the current situation of the issues in the area. These findings where also confirmed by the FB analysis which showed how the activities (e.g., doing monitoring with hand-held monitors) and discussions on FB were connected to \textit{personal} factor of influence. 

\subsubsection{Environmental and Behavioural}

Although a lot of motivations for gathering local data for the group were connected to getting an accurate picture of the situation (e.g., ``establishing local knowledge base''), data was also seen by the group as a tool for raising awareness. The FB analysis showed how the \textit{behavioural} factors started gaining more importance as the time went on and new data became available, which was also confirmed by participants in the focus group reflecting on the process:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``And actually to me, absolutely candid, I think there was sort of another agenda as well. And that all us actually believe in using the city to travel around by means other than private car. And the worsening air pollution problem was seen by us – we’re not pleased by it – but it actually gave us an opportunity to talk to people and to encourage people to adopt more sustainable means of travel. Because doing anything else was chocking us all! So it was useful – unfortunate – but useful tool to use to get over to people the need to travel more sustainability for their own good and everybody else’s.''} (P1)
\end{quote}

The local data the commissioning toolkit enabled people to collect was seen as a useful tool for not only helping to change social norms, but also perhaps nudging the community to change their behaviour:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``I mean the thing with, the difficulty with air pollution is that you cannot see it, that is why we need the data and need to think of a way [of] making that accessible to people. And to link it to how they are getting about, and to a possibility of changes in the area and beyond, improving the air quality situation whilst also improving the sort of health and lifestyles in general.''} (P2)
\end{quote}

Moving from community investigations with the hand-held monitors to getting stationary monitors commissioned and deployed by the UO opened new possibilities for the group. Instead of having a snapshot of data, the commissioned monitors provided constant flow of data from a particular place in the community that was chosen by the people. That made people think about new possibilities to evidence issues with the built infrastructure and try to show cause and effect:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``[...] sometimes you see I think that says it takes the travel journey is 15 minutes to get through. So is the pollution worst during the 15 minutes journey time or, you know? Can you draw a link between, you know? And that is all you are trying to do is to demonstrate that air pollution is worst when there are more vehicles standing and/or travelling on that route as the traffic count goes up. And you are trying [to] kind of draw connections to, it you know.''} (P3)
\end{quote}
Also using that data to correlate with events that are happening in the built environment and to help understand the changes in the community and their impacts people and how people get. People felt that this additional data could be used to draw attention to how environmental changes influence the data: 
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``[A] lot of those events are quite easy to find out about aren’t they. But I mean it is finding the data about that window is quite difficult isn’t it. And it would good at some point to be able to perhaps, umm build up a list of things linked to the data. Say what, “well I looked at the data for yesterday, and also noticed this was happening”, so that it’s recorded for the future so you can sort of say “oh well so was that related to this”. And you know in few weeks’ time the [Anon] Road might be shut again and you can say it’s the same pattern there.''} (P2)
\end{quote}

People in the focus group agreed that it is important that the data is reliable and representing the situation as it actually is: \textit{``But we need to keep getting reliable data. Reliable and trustworthy.''} (P2). Although, is was important to have consistent stream of high reliable data, they also noted that they personally would not be using the high frequency data, because they would not be the people who would start engineers solutions and infrastructure changes in the city. Most importantly the participants wanted the reassurance from the engineers at the UO and researcher, that the data is the highest possible quality and the processes of collection and calibration would be transparent to them. Furthermore, peoples attachments were also shown from their investments in helping to solve the problems with sensors when they occurred on the first deployment. Although people saw data as a useful tool for evidencing and advocating infrastructure changes, but because complexities of those lengthy processes people started to see more benefit in helping to make data accessible for the community:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``But that seems to me to be giving us clues if we were trying [to] actually engineer a solution. And I don’t thing we are trying to do that, yeah. I am completely with you, in saying we want to see the data – but can we find a way of expressing the data in a way in which anybody would understand and would be meaningful.''} (P1)
\end{quote}

Furthermore, participants where thinking of ideas of how we could put out data in simple digestible forms or as pervasive information for the community to make use and adjust their behaviour accordingly:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``I was just thinking. Sorry, from my point of view though asking about the datasets and how easy it is to manipulate them to produce [a] certain sort of information set. It almost like it is trying to, there is a sweet shop of information and if you don’t know what that is it’s very hard to ask for exactly what it is you want you know. You are looking at things like the DEFRA daily air quality index. So that’s…is a mix isn’t it. So daily air quality index and that takes nitrogen dioxide, sulphur dioxide, ozone, pm2,5 and pm10 and then maps them into a chart. It comes up with a colour: like purple – don’t breathe, green – everything is fine, and everywhere in between you know. And you wander if there was a script that pulls – and that is based on a forecast for the next five days – so you know Newcastle is green for the next 5 days, because we have strong winds coming, you know. But you’re wondering [...], if there was a computer program that would just getting those five variables and then mapping them and then just producing a cell you know, that was a colour. And say: “today's daily air index quality in Heaton is red, green”..''} (P3)
\end{quote}

They also felt that in order for the community as a whole to start chaining their behaviour there is need for chaining the social environment and having the data available and in front of people all the time:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``And ultimately it will be that we have an app on our phones that actually gives us, everyday the colour and those there interested press the button and you get an explanation is the what the reading is. I remember. I probably said this to you before what cooked me up from all of this was the about 8 years ago going to China, and being a appalled about the air pollution. And finding that there were some apps that still that long ago, in China, that gave you real time data about the pollution. And now there are hundreds [of apps] in China and we don’t have any really.''} (P1)
\end{quote}

For participants ideally, the data would have been something that people would could check like they check their social media feed. It needed to be embedding into people everyday busy lives to start having the desired effect:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``The trouble is that it need someone to do something that is reasonably polished, you can spend a next year saying to people: “what do you want, do you want this”. What you need is something that works fairly reliably and is easy to use, people will start using it and go “uh look at the air pollution today”. People have busy lives, most people aren't slightest interested in this on to day to day bases because it doesn’t visibly, physically affect your day. Um, but nether that reading lots of tweets about nothing, but I still do that ((laughing)). If I got an app that distracts me for something than I will look at it. But I will not gonna put any thought into how it gets there, it need someone who has access to it and just do it. You know.''} (P2)
\end{quote}

Additionally, participants saw ways of getting through to different demographics for example by engaging the local schools to point out these resources to them:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``Can you point chilli road primary school to this data, with some confidence, that you can say to the school that you can now find out what is the pollution outside your school. What you do with that data as a school is up to you. You can’t you know, you can educate the kids to a certain degree you can try inform the parents you know. But they are primary school kids there is a conversation to be started around air quality but that’s probably you know.''} (P3)
\end{quote}
This was also confirmed by the activities of the group that where analysed in FB data analysis, were the group had meetings with local schools, organisations and businesses to draw their attention to issues and provide them updates about the data and community investigations. All of this was the groups larger plan to start chaining the way people look at the built environment and get around in the neighbourhood doing their everyday activities. Here the participant was giving ideas of how could they present this data to the community, which was later written up by them as a blog post on the group's website:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``So if you are able to say that, during an average week the levels of pollution at this are. And if that period of collecting the data was long enough, then it would actually average out all the little bits like the congestion, the wind, etc. And you would be able to give an average reading (at nine?) of a typical day at any one of our points in Heaton. And I think that would be an incredibly powerful bit of data, because if the average levels like say 9 in the morning when kids are walking to school – quarter to 9 or half 8 or whatever – or 4 o clock were actually massively higher than the legal limit or getting close to legal limit then that is a really powerful story. I think [we've] got to try and show very simple stories with very complicated data.''} (P1)
\end{quote}

The findings from the community reflections further illustrate the close relationship of environmental and behavioural factors in advocacy effort of the group. Furthermore, the point of getting sensors commissioned in the neighbourhood and working out what type of data they provide, and what can be done with it, the group had exhausted the personal factors of influence for the advocacy efforts. Although, the personal factors did not completely disappear from the picture and they where still connected (perhaps more loosely) to other two factors of influence.

\subsection{Multi-faceted Approach}
By engaging in investigation of local issues and the commissioning process, the group became more aware their different roles as community advocates and how they had to operate on different levels having multiple hats on. Firstly they saw themselves as a group who helps provide local information to the community about linked to the city wide issues, however not providing solutions or policies because the council needs to take the lead on that:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``I think, because the city council are coming out with this public relations campaign on dirty air and people are going to be asking questions about “what’s it like for me”. We are then going to be in the position where we can say, “this is what it actually is for you”. And then the city council – “and watch this space” – the city council is coming up with proposals how to deal with cleaning the air in the city. And at least then you are on to some information is to how bad it is and why is it important. I don’t think we should get to heavily – personally – to heavily into the policy arena at this stage. Because I think it’s up to the council to actually drive – they got more modelling than we got – we are just people who are concerned. .''} (P1)
\end{quote}

Secondly, being the `watchdogs' for the changes and keeping eye on the data to see if built environment changes from the local government has had a positive or negative effect to the community. Also updating the community if there is any changes that could effect their health personally or the way that they get around:

\begin{quote}
    \textit{``But we need to able to, you know that gives you some evidence to say the air quality is this. And anything new that is proposed in the future, you can say “is it likely to impact this” positively or negatively. If something does change, you can say “has it, has it done any”. It is not that take a day and hour and it’s done, this is a just a start you know.''} (P2)
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
    \textit{``Is this story simply, even simpler than that. “Space For Heaton have established a baseline pollution level for some of our streets. And will be watching to see what will happen with this and informing you as residence”. You know, whether it could be that, “this is how this comes up, compares with the legal limit”.''} (P1)
\end{quote}

Finally, the participants came to the conclusion that all factors are important and they will keep working on all, however the main focus for their advocacy efforts and also to the main utility of the data is its usefulness for helping to change peoples opinions:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``Well I can tell you what my thinking about this is. We’ve campaigned unsuccessfully changing the built environment, building cycle ways etc, etc. and OK, things get done. But very slowly, very incrementally. The same time all this is happening, actually things get worse. So the net gain is actually minus. You know we are making, we are not getting anywhere with it. So the conclusion I come to and I think a lot of other people [have] come to, is actually you got to start changing people’s opinions. So it’s a “hearts and minds” campaign. So I think when what are we doing about action here, actually not within a margin. We’re gonna continue to take some action to support city council in developing cycle route like up [Anon] Road etc, but actually we should be concentrating on how to get people to understand that there is an issue here that needs action and we are local people who are helping to articulate that for all.''} (P1)
\end{quote}

Here the participant expressed similar statements that came out from the FB analysis in the previous chapter, where the main factor (i.e., the focus) of influence for the advocacy efforts of the group was \textit{behavioural}. However, all the other factors still remained prevalent and instrumental for aiding the focus of behavioural change of the community. The findings from the FB analysis and the community reflections indicated that there was a need for closer inspection of the relationships between each factor of influence in relation to citizens advocating for changes in the community.

\section{Discussion}
Through the evaluation process new understandings of how people used the SenseMyStreet toolkit and the commissioned data. The findings showed that the effective use of the toolkit was highly dependent on the actions of self motivated people of the group, however it was also heavily influenced by key factors of personal, behavioural and environmental. By mapping out the advocacy efforts of the group in relation to community changes over time surfaced new understanding how the key factors link to each other and to the domains of desired change in relation to community problem solving activities. This section presents those understandings through a Citizens Advocacy Framework (CAF) (Figure \ref{fig:advocacy_framework}) and provides a discussion of the elements linked to the framework through the findings, providing an understanding of how might we build mechanisms for support effective uses of commissioning resources in the future.

\subsection[Citizen Advocacy Framework]{Citizen Advocacy Framework: Towards Understanding Civic Advocacy}

As mentioned previously, the factors of influence (Figure \ref{fig:key-factors}) were closely linked with one another and focusing on or being effected by one, could have an effect on the others. Graphing out the relationships between the factors of influence \ref{fig:advocacy_efforts}, analysing the community reflections \ref{sub:comm_ref} and looking at the community outcomes \ref{table:outcomes} revealed new findings about the ways people used toolkit for advocacy. A more focused lens was needed to analyse the outcomes of the advocacy efforts, determine the relationships between the factors of influence, and reflect and discuss the practices of the advocacy group appropriating the toolkit and the data. Rephrasing the core question of the evaluation from \textit{``How citizens make effective use of SenseMyStreet toolkit?''} to more encompassing \textit{``How are citizens taking action on local issues?''}, enabled the author to derive more specific categories which are simultaneously liked to multiple factors of influence. Figure \ref{fig:advocacy_framework} shows the derived CAF framework which incorporates factors of influence, but is more catered towards effective use of data and technologies by citizens for advocacy and action, and to create knowledge and positive change in the community. The findings of the study and outcomes indicate that changing \textit{policy} and the \textit{built environment} are leaning towards influencing physical space and for helping to instrumenting that change, \textit{social environment} and \textit{behaviour change} have aspects of influencing the social norms, attitudes, behavioural trends, self-efficiency and even personal attitudes. Similarly to key factors of influence, the individual parts of the CAF cannot be influenced in isolation. They are dynamically interlinked with each other and each action taken influences the whole action network. This is exemplified on Figure \ref{fig:advocacy_framework} by the different combinations of the factors of influence on the links between the elements. For example the correlation between the \textit{behavioural} \textit{environment} factor shown on the graph. However when setting a focus for the advocating efforts, it is possible to direct the action towards particular part of the CAF making that the strongest variable in the action network. Introducing the CAF will not only provide aid in discussing and reflecting on the processes of SenseMyStreet and the effective use of it by citizens, but it also provides support for setting focus for the future advocacy efforts of communities. Identifying the key focus points for an advocacy group, enables designers better explore digital technologies that could support citizens taking action on local issues.

\subsection[Issues, Attachments and Ownership]{Framing Issues, Developing Personal Attachments and Taking Ownership}
Researchers working in participatory design and particularly focused on community centred project have documented the importance of issues and developing attachments to them which help constitute publics \citep{dewey_public_2016} and create spaces for action \citep{asad_tap_2017,disalvo_neighborhood_2008,dantec_infrastructuring_2013,jenkins_object-oriented_2016}. Mostly this work focuses on using participatory design for infrastructuring that helps come up with technical responses to issues of a formed public. In the case of commissioning systems the technology itself acts as both -- a technical solution and socio-technical mechanism for infrastructuring (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}). These are systems like App Movement \citep{garbett_app_2016}, but also SenseMyStreet, which was designed to be a community-led urban sensing toolkit that would act as a catalyst for grass-roots civic action. That is not to say that they have not been designed to work on particular issues and publics. First interaction with SenseMyStreet starts with \textit{identifying an issue} on a map, using the GIS mapping functionalities on the project's website. This will set the tone for further activities in terms of framing the issue -- what type of issue it is and where does it occur. Furthermore, the \textit{place} has an important role to play in this too, as its an important part of hyper-local advocacy efforts, and essential for constituting new publics around issues, or expressing and developing personal attachments of existing publics. 

In the case of the advocacy group, a huge driver for them initially was to educate themselves about the issue, which then helped them to frame future actions. Intrinsic motivations for understanding the data were major factors for its use for the advocacy group. The findings from the FB posts reveal that initial motivations for people were connected to \textit{personal}: figuring out the current policies, how the use the hand-held monitors and how the make sense of the commissioned data. Once the required knowledge was obtained by the group and the personal attitudes formed they could start focusing on the other strategies linked to advocacy involving the domains of desired change (Table \ref{table:outcomes}). When reflecting on the SenseMyStreet process with the participants at the focus group, and looking at the data produced by the commissioned monitors, participants had a better sense of the figures because they had gone through a self discovery in the first step (i.e., Identify the issues) of the process involving hand-held monitoring. This argument is also supported by the findings of the usage data of the toolkit (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} Section \ref{sec:sms_usage}). Having a clear view of what they had (in terms of the current data and its value) and what they would expect to get from the commissioned monitors in the future, enabled the group to choose strategies and future actions accordingly. Enabling people to imagine the desired futures through the processes linked to the toolkit supported its value to act as a process for infrastructuring \citep{dantec_infrastructuring_2013} towards civic advocacy and citizen action. 

Furthermore, although there was a handover of responsibilities in the third step (i.e., Get the facts) of the process, the participants were also actively involved in the deployment processes of the commissioned sensors by prompting the researchers for updates. The findings indicate that when the technical difficulties with the monitors on the first deployment emerged, it seemed that the group felt that they were accountable and wanted to see that the issues get resolved. The group worked closely with the research team at the UO, keeping an eye on the readings to detect inconsistencies and get faulty sensors replaced. This also meant that some of the planned advocacy activities were put on hold by the group, and the focus and the responsibilities of the group shifted -- holding back on presenting the data to the community before it is validated. The ability to adapt to situations and dynamically shift responsibilities, often due to externals influences and community changes, and work around problems that might occur, have show to be strong benefits for infrastructuring projects \citet{dantec_infrastructuring_2013}. Additionally, as it was their efforts that got the monitors out there, it seemed that they felt that it was their responsibility to make something out of that data and provide it to the community in a more digestible form. 

Research in Community Informatics (CI) has shown that community taking ownership for the final outcome of the project is also essential for the sustainability of the initiative \citep{merkel_participatory_2004,carroll_participatory_2007}. However, there is a difference between actually taking ownership of the technologies and data (i.e., maintaining and sustaining the actual tools and infrastructure), and having a \textit{sense of ownership} over the investigations and issues (i.e., community initiatives). Analysis of the different groups that engaged with the SenseMyStreet toolkit shows, that when the group takes ownership of the community data and develops attachments, it will promote its further use in civic advocacy and action. All communities are different however, with different issues and attachments and all need their own approaches, thus there is a role for infrastructuring and technologies to play here to facilitate the creation of these bespoke methods of action.

\subsection{Plans of Action}
The SenseMyStreet toolkit has successfully helped to carry out a citizen-led deployment of sensors, creating an opportunity for citizens to access resources that were not available them before. However that was not only aim for the toolkit. It also aimed to help citizens put that data into use for activities for local benefit. The analysis of the SenseMyStreet usage suggested that the process of data generation was benefiting the community when it was supported by additional strategies to purpose the data. Meaning that data generation had to be connected to a specific goal -- either it was personal discovery and self knowledge, or for the purposes of advocating for changing the community. Although the commissioning platform was designed as a product that would facilitate infrastructuring, it was still largely up to the community to take it further from data production; to informing issues and exploring ways the data can be put into use to instrument change in the community.

The findings revealed that plans for actions often preceded the actual data production by citizens. When participants in the focus group reflected on the process of commissioning and talked about their motivations for getting involved in the project, they pointed out a set of strategies that they had for the data before it was available. Strategies that could be tailored and implemented depending on what is possible to get out of the data. However when it comes to infrastructuring, not only attachments are dynamic \citep{dantec_infrastructuring_2013}, but so are the strategies for actions. As shown from engaging participants in commissioning of environmental data, they could shift in focus from \textit{personal} to \textit{behavioural} to \textit{environmental} or the other way around depending on the affortance's of data. Initial strategies could end up not working at all in the long run, however having a set of questions, ideas and plans for actions gave people a specific goal to work towards. Findings from the analysis of FB data also illustrated that planning ahead enabled people to have much more focused advocacy efforts when the data became available. However, based on the analysis of FB data and the community reflections it seemed that there was need for an adjacent possible in terms of data and resource for people to actually be able to take any action. The planning of action was good, but data was also needed to take those actions. Additionally, there is a clear trend of actions being more focused when there was new data or new information available that the group could share with the community.

Furthermore, there was also things happening in the community that were out of the groups', and the toolikit's, control. These could be positive, such as opportunities to provide opinions and data for public consultations, or negative, such as an opposition group emerging, from the perspective of group advocacy efforts. It often made sense for the group to act accordingly to community changes -- directing their efforts or linking to some sort of already ongoing activity or event. Such events are illustrated on Figure \ref{fig:advocacy_efforts} as \textit{Community Changes} -- local and national media interest, local consultation, local elections, city wide events etc. Participants reflected that their initial aims were to take advantage of opportunities to influence the polices and built environment (e.g., public meetings and local consultations), however as time passed they realised that that will take more time because of the official processes in place. Although the group worked closely with the council to help consult on infrastructure changes they also did not feel that they where in the position of proposing solutions for the issues. This was also evident from the findings of the focus group with participants around reflecting on the uses of data. They did not feel that the data will help them engineer particular solutions, however they seemed themselves as concerned residents who want to expose the issues by expressing them through data in a way it would be understood by all members of the community. Hence their focus shifted more to influencing the social environment and coming up with strategies for changing behaviour of local people. This did not mean that other activities completely stopped, which is also illustrated by the connected nature of environmental and behavioural factors revealed from the FB data analysis. The group's ability to change strategies when there was an opportunity to influence another domain of change, for example when a new consultation was opened up for citizen feedback or when a public event was organised by the local council. Latter was used by the group as a platform for speaking out about the commissioned data and issues. This illustrated the importance of having capacities and resources (and large social capital) to respond quickly to the community changes.


\subsection{Forming Networks Through Community Links}
The importance of publics \citep{dewey_public_1954} in framing the issues should not be underestimated. Across the deployments it was clear that the existence or creation of publics where essential for the use of commissioned data. Often people living in one area were having similar opinions or concerns on matters, however they were not vocalised by anyone. Having a group of people that could formulate issues and seek to generate data through the infrastructure provided by the commissioning toolkit to back them up helped to voice the issues for the community. This type of collective representation of the community helped in turn to get more people behind of the issues and make them more prominent in the society and the eyes of decision makers.

The author's years of experience in working around data has revealed that ``everyone wants data until you give it to them'' -- meaning it takes much more effort to make data accessible and understandable. Data science skills and training are required to transform data into a useful form and into something that the community can use in their advocacy efforts. Rows and rows of sensor readings in a spreadsheet just do not work as being useful for the community. Although there is a huge representation issue with the data, does not mean that it should not be available for the public. Another importance aspect that was discovered in designing and evaluating SenseMyStreet, was the powerful effect of community links. This comes back to building capacity, however not only extending the skills and knowledge of immediate community but also reaching out to others outside the formed public (i.e., social capacity). Establishing links within the community and through collaborating with external actors can help alleviate some of these issues with data. Additionally, having the people involved in the processes of data production through infrastructuring enables us to break down barriers for data use by linking it to peoples concerns and helping to developing plans for action that could be sustained in the future.

The advocacy groups which were formed though the use of SenseMyStreet (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} and current chapter) started making links with each other. Although they were all living in separate areas of the city, having their own particular issues and attachments bounded to places, they where exchanging knowledge and resources, and learning from each others experiences of using data as a tool for advocacy. For example sharing of pledges for electoral candidates to publicly sign or ideas of how to present local data for residents (Table \ref{table:outcomes}). Formation of these links where not explicitly facilitated through the commissioning infrastructure, however they organically emerged once people started working on strategies on using data. During the focus group, when people were coming up with ideas for future strategies with the data, being the younger group, they often draw examples from more established advocacy groups in the city. As more data about the community was generated thought the commissioned monitors, the representatives from different advocacy groups started having regular meeting with each other to discuss the strategies and develop joint thinking for establishing combined voice of the people of the whole city. Including showing up to community events and council meetings as a combined Safe Pedestrian and Cycling Environment (SPACE) advocacy group. Groups did not only share strategies, they also started sharing actual technical solutions developed as a result of strategies for using the data. As reported in the community reflection, a big focus for the participants was to surface the issue and build knowledge of the community. The proposal of having a automatic system that gives the community daily information about up to date readings from commissioned local monitors, was later realised by one of the members in the advocacy group. The group set up a Tweetbot that tweets daily about the last 7 day average readings from their local sensor. Later the group also helped to set up a similar Tweetbot for the other advocacy groups. 

Links between advocacy groups definitely became a major factor in coming up with strategies for the use of community data. However links between advocacy groups were not the only connections that prompted the use of data within these groups. Also links with local authority helped advocacy groups to focus their efforts. As outline in the previous chapter (Section \ref{sub:config_par}), some of the participants were part of forums (e.g., Healthy Streets Board, Cycling Stakeholder Forum) within the local council. Often the groups had insider information about the next steps of the local council in terms of upcoming campaigns, consultations, infrastructure changes and policies. That enabled groups to come up with timely information snippets and explanations for the data and processes for community. Including using the data generated by the monitors to compile an informed view of issues. Advocacy groups often posted blogs with explanations of data, linked to things that are happening in the community or in the city at large. This relates back to the \textup{insider} and \textit{outsider} strategies linked to civic advocacy \citep{asad_tap_2017}. In a way the groups knew their capabilities and limitations of what they can achieve with the data, but also they where trying to reach out and link up with external groups and individuals who had knowledge or skills in other domains. Whether it was different advocacy groups, professionals in the university or the local authority. Similar situation played out in the study conducted by \citet{disalvo_towards_2009} where local community, facing regulatory difficulties of getting their radio broadcast off the ground, decided to sought help of professionals who would help them get it on the air. Although the group increased their reliance on others they where still attached to the issues and felt responsibility for the community to get the project out there. This echos the commitments of the advocacy groups who took part in SenseMyStreet and helped generated data for the community. They felt accountable and wanted to see that data being made visible and accessible by the community. These different ways of creating links and coming up with solutions to accommodate issues could be also looked at through a lens of \textit{collaboration patterns} \citep{moor_collaboration_2009} which emerged from engaging with the commissioning toolkit. According to \citet{moor_collaboration_2009} there are five different collaboration patterns: \textit{goal}, \textit{information}, \textit{communication}, \textit{task} and \textit{meta}-patterns. SenseMyStreet has provided practical examples for different patterns of collaboration though the analysis and evaluation of the toolkit which provide important insight for future research in Community Informatics (CI).

For some communities however establishing these links was easier than for others. Whether they had more social capital, already existing links and connections they could call upon or sometimes just by luck and timing. For example showing up to workshop or neighbourhood forum and meeting people interested in exploring similar issues. There have been successful attempts of using technology to accumulate that social capital. For example in the case of App Movement, a community commissioning platform for mobile technologies, used campaigning as a mechanism to help constitute the public prior to initiating the collaborative design process for the community technology \citep{garbett_app_2016}. Initially, similar mechanisms where considered for the design of SenseMyStreet toolkit. However there was a worry that the platform would start prioritising someones issues over others and the one who has the most `friends' will get the resources. Instead toolkit was made inclusive for everyone who had a issue that they wanted to explore, either it was a group of people or an individual. That proved to be the right move because in some cases the investigation was initiated by an individual first, in time when there was already some data available, more people joined hence forming a public \citep{dewey_public_1954}. This was also supported by the infrastructures of SensMyStreet technologies which enabled voting of and commenting of issues on SenseMyStreet website, and collaborating around proposals on SenseMyStreet Proposals website (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}). Infrastructuring around SenseMyStreet still did not provide a guarantee whether a deployment of monitors from the toolkit was followed by informed actions from the community. Although the toolkit supported collaborations and formulation of publics and links though infrastructuring, it was still largely up to the people involved to come up with strategies for acting upon the generated data. 

\section{Conclusions}
First prototype of the toolkit combined multiple tools and digital platforms to enable people to access scientific grade environmental monitors and use them to explore issues in their neighbourhood. Through the process of engaging people in commissioning collection of environmental data a lot of new opportunities for collaboration and community building surfaced. However also multiple challenges raised around the readiness of IoT and smart city technology, representation of data to serve the needs of the community and their ability to use data to take local action. Indeed, the current infrastructuring around SenseMyStreet worked well for groups that had additional capacity already existing within their community. Whether it manifested in needed skills or external links to resources or information. However, these links started informing as more groups started to surface and become vocal. Furthermore, the complex configuration of tools and technologies of SenseMyStreet (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}) worked as temporary infrastructure because of the constant support and orchestration from the researcher and the UO which was invisible for the participants. In some regard, SenseMyStreet stayed true to the promise of Ubicomp \citep{weiser_computer_1991} vision, which refers to seamless interactions with computers in the background responding to human interactions and intents. Going forward, there needs to be considerations of they way these tools can be transferred or taken over from the research to make them sustainable in the future. However, the value of this case study was not only to show how to configure and build tools for community commissioning, it also illustrated how th smart city could be made more accessible and usable by communities though human-centred design and active citizen engagements. Future re-configurations of the toolkit might consider building in mechanisms for information exchange with the smart city and making it easier to establish these links. This does not only include using technology to help connect between different groups, but also making better links between various platforms (including the ones already existing in smart city outside the toolkit) to connect people in one community in order to form publics \citep{dewey_public_1954}. With that in mind, future developments of community commissioning and CI systems should consider not only providing resources and access to collect data, but also work more on facilitation and training to enable communities to make effective use of those resources. These ideas are further explored in Chapter \ref{fig:cdi}, which provides a combined reflection and analysis of the two case studies (SenseMyStreet in current chapter and Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}, and Data:In Place in Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) from the perspective of effective use of data by communities for civic participation, advocacy and action.

\section{Summary}
This chapter provided an evaluation of SenseMyStreet from the perspective of effective use of commissioning resources by communities. This involved looking at a particular group's engagement (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} Section \ref{sub:config_par}) with the toolkit and how they appropriated the data that was produced through the commissioning process. Evaluation was done in two parts: (1) chronological analysing of the textual content of a FB group using key factors of influence (i.e., environmental, personal, behavioural), and (2) analysing transcribed audio from a focus group interview with key group members. Additionally the activities of the community using the data were presented through \textit{community outcomes}. As a result Citizen Advocacy Framework (CAF) was posited that enables researchers do discuss activities of civic advocacy and action, and help communities plan and focus their activities for changes for local benefit. From the initial launch of the toolkit there was a lot of interest from people to get involved, and people seem to care about their streets and wanted to investigate issues in the community. Having infrastructuring and infrastructure for people to use, provided new forms of active citizen participation and civic action that is driven by communities. Enabling citizens to help provide data as part of the smart city for the community, could help inform issues and also challenge uninformed decision in the neighbourhood. However, the case study findings also highlighted the importance of community links and social capital for making effective use of commissioning resources. Despite that, having evidence to point to that informs an issue in the community, has given people a voice to advocate for change and helped more people to become vocal. While this case study focused mainly on the issue of air quality, which was framed by the motivations and through the community research, there is no reason to assume that the issues will remain static. New areas of concerns might rise in the future from the community, that could be explored and evidenced through environmental sensing and the use of SenseMyStreet.
