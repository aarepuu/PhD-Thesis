This chapter is part of the Case Study I and focuses on the evaluation of the effective use of SenseMyStreet toolkit `in the wild' by communities. The SenseMyStreet sensor commissioning toolkit's process, which was described in detail in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}, consisted of four main parts: 
\begin{enumerate*}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item Identify the issues;
\item Plan and propose;
\item Get the facts;
\item Data for everyone.
\end{enumerate*}
Citizen engagement and civic participation have become an important intersection of studies across engineering, science and technology studies and participatory design. As more technologies are being integrated into the city, design researchers and system developers are exploring new ways to enable citizens to participate in the formulation of issues and taking part in the decision-making processes. Setting up SenseMyStreet toolkit (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}) illustrated how user-centred participatory design processes can be used to engage people in the discovery of local issues using scientific environmental sensor technology. The toolkit did not only aim to help empower citizens to investigate issues that are relevant to them, but it also tried to engage the wider public with the work of the Urban Observatory and the bigger concept of a smart city by helping to get people's voices into the city datasets. Extending on the findings on the toolkit usage by communities presented in the previous chapter, this chapter focuses on understanding engagements with the toolkit by a particular community group that was involved in the initial design of the toolkit (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} Section \ref{sec:design_sms}). Evaluation was conducted over a period of two years and reported in two phases: (1) analysis of the discussions of a specific advocacy group on Facebook (i.e., community discussions), and (2) analysis of audio recorded reflections of the core members from the same group on their advocacy efforts and the role of data generated using the toolkit (i.e., community reflection). Through these, it was possible to analyse the engagement of the community in different stages of the toolkit process and present findings on how the community responded in each step of the process, how they made use of the data, and how the focus of the group changed over time when new challenges surfaced or opportunities became available.

\section{Evaluation Design}

The evaluation of the SenseMyStreet toolkit was conducted over a period of two years (Figure \ref{fig:sms-timeline}). Drawing on the findings of toolkit usage presented in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} the evaluation was done in two phases using different data sources and methods:

\begin{itemize}

\item[PHI.] (\textit{community discussions}) Looked at people's discussions around using the toolkit for civic advocacy and action. How people perceived the tools, appropriated them and used the data that the tools generate in their advocacy efforts. This phase of the evaluation focused on the discussions between people in an community group involved in the design of the toolkit (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} Section \ref{sec:design_sms}), and analysed posts, comments and replies made on a private Facebook (FB) group which the researcher was given access to. The coding guide used for analysing the empirical data was derived inductively from the initial findings of the toolkit usage presented in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} and from the fieldwork conducted by the author. 

\item[PHII.] (\textit{community reflections}) Focused on the participants reflections on the SenseMyStreet toolkit, collected through a focus group with the key stakeholders from the same community group studied in PHI. It provides a qualitative analysis on the transcribed focus group data using thematic analysis approach \citep{braun_using_2006} with confirmatory content included from the FB data analysed in the PHI.
\end{itemize}

Lastly the evaluation includes an overview of the \textit{community outcomes} from the project. These are presented through example uses of the data, generated through the use of the system and the outcomes of peoples' advocacy efforts using that data.

\subsection{Community Discussions (PHI)}

About the same time when the first design activities for the toolkit kicked off, a group of residents (n=8) in a local community (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} Section \ref{sub:config_par}) started a private FB group to exchange information, discuss local developments and organise advocacy activities. The same people were the core group involved in the design and development of the SenseMyStreet toolkit described in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}. The author was added to the Facebook group first to exchange information and coordinate the deployments, however retrospectively was given permission to use the discussions in the analysis of the case study.

\mynote{Maybe make a table of the impacts with examples}
The data used in the first phase of the evaluation was retrieved through the Facebook graph API consisting of 265 parent-posts and 1092 comments and replies (a total of 1357 digital objects) that were posted on the group's page between April 2017 and July 2019. Taking a contextual design inquiry approach \citep{beyer_contextual_1997} through the analysis of the toolkit usage presented in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}, and the fieldwork conducted by the author which studied different groups using the toolkit and appropriating the data generated using the environmental monitors; three key factors were inductively derived that determined the use of the toolkit and the data it provided. These factors where \textit{personal}, \textit{environmental} and \textit{behavioural} (Figure \ref{fig:key-factors}). Similar factors have been used to describe social learning according to the social cognitive theory which was originally developed by \citet{bandura_social_1977}. These three factors where used to analyse and cluster the empirical data in terms of each factor's importance in a particular advocacy effort (e.g., organising a community meeting, sending official response to consultations, publishing commissioned sensor data). Each post discussing an advocacy effort was assigned code according to the main factor of influence. The importance was derived from an absolute number of advocacy efforts discussed linked to each factor, which was also analysed from the perspective of how data and sensor technologies were appropriated as tools for civic action. However, an advocacy effort could often be linked to multiple factors and at times to all of the three. This is not surprising, because the factors are tied to each other in a reciprocal manner. Meaning they are in a constant interaction with one another which in turn enables learning and knowledge creation to happen. However the factors have a power relationship between them at any given moment. This allowed to assign codes with weights to each data snippet linked to an advocacy effort individually. The weights of the codes were the following: the strongest factor got 1, one after that got 0.3, and last one 0.1. Furthermore, to filter the data set and better analyse the interactions between the people related to the different factors of influence, the following four rules were applied:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item post has to have at least one reply;
    \item post has to be replied by at least one person other than the original poster;
    \item post cannot be just done for archival purposes (e.g., sharing a document or an image);
    \item post has to be more than simple information request related to the group administration activities;
\end{enumerate}
Initially, these rules included a minimal length for the post, however in closer inspection there were documents and files shared without a long textual description which prompted discussion and feedback within the group. Some examples were an email thread between a council worker and a group member, or sharing of an image of local newspaper, posted through the letterbox. Both of these started a discussion and subsequent actions were taken by the group. However, the dataset excluded `like' type messages (e.g., ``thank you'', ``great'', ``good work'' and other confirmatory expressions), where people were agreeing with the poster or expressing their gratitude. Although this would be interesting dataset to study on its own, for example to look at the dynamics of the group, it was out of the scope of this particular analysis. After applying these rules the dataset left for the coding consisted of 193 posts and 647 comments and replies. Additionally to the reporting on the key factors of influence for advocacy efforts and how they changed over time, chronological events and anonymized quotes from the dataset are provided to exemplify the activities linked to advocacy efforts. This reveals the main focus of advocacy efforts for the community group, how it has formed over time and what role did the sensor commissioning toolkit play in implementing strategies for civic advocacy and action by the community.

\subsection{Community Reflections (PHII)}
% Reflections

Deductively applying the factors of influence on data in the previous phase of the analysis, helped to gain insights and enabled the author to compile an informed view of the process of sensor commissioning by local communities. Second phase of the evaluation built on that and seeked feedback from the participants on the authors informed view. The data used in this analysis originates from audio recorded focus group with participants (n=3) who were the key members of the community group involved in the design and the initial use of the toolkit (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} Section \ref{sec:design_sms}). The focus group was organised as fairly unstructured conversations, where participants were reflecting on the overall process of the community commissioning through the SenseMyStreet toolkit and the main strategies for advocating for positive change in the community. It started off with introduction, the processes they engaged within the scope of the toolkit, initial strategies of the group and the current status of the group in relation of utilising the data generated using the toolkit. This was followed by an open discussion in the group facilitated by the author providing occasional prompts to keep people on track. Audio recorded focus group was fully transcribed by the author and a qualitative analysis was conducted on the data using thematic analysis approach \citep{braun_using_2006}. The key factors of influence (Figure \ref{fig:key-factors} were applied to code the focus group data in order to gain understanding of the ways that people purposed the sensor commissioning toolkit as a resource for community problem solving activities and for exploring local issues. Additional confirmatory content were also pulled from the previous analysis (i.e., post and comments from PHI) to saturate the data. Under each theme the sensor commissioning toolkit and its functionalities were reflected upon and reported, using anonymised quotes from the transcripts to illustrate how the capabilities of the toolkit can be appropriated for citizen led action.

\subsection{Analysis of Facebook Data}

Figure \ref{fig:advocacy_efforts} presents a timeline graph of coded FB data in terms of the key factors of influence -- \textit{personal}, \textit{behavioural}, \textit{environmental}. The graph shows the evolution of different strategies linked to the key factors of influence (Figure \ref{fig:key-factors}) over a period of two years. Additionally three types of events are marked on the timeline: activities and events related to SenseMyStreet toolkit, activities and actions of the advocacy group, and the events and changes happening in the community. Furthermore sections are the graph are highlighted where there is a surge of focused activities or where the power relationships between factors of influence changed.

% initial focus is on personal, educating one self
% highly motivated individuals
The graph shows that initially the main factor of influence was related to \textit{personal} -- experimenting, experiencing and forming a good understanding of the situation personally - meaning that people where intrinsically motivated to start taking action. This also came out from the initial meetings with where people stated that the immediate concern is to establish a knowledge base for the group to understand the issues, the severity of them and to start collecting the data to help make decisions. Similarly, this was also brought out as a major motivator in the action plan (i.e., `Air Pollution Bid') and in the FB discussion:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``Firstly, there doesn't seem to be any information about the air quality in [the area] that is readily available to members of the public. I think it would be good to establish a baseline on the level of pollution within [the area], particularly along the main roads.''} (posted on 06/06/2017)
\end{quote}
Furthermore, once the group received the hand-held monitors for doing initial monitors the had to learn about how the monitors works, how they would collect the data and make sense of it. However, once people got the hang of it and could understand the data, they started to think about how to present it to the wider community:
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``We need to start on some explanation to go alongside the plots explaining what's being measured and what the results mean before putting it on our site. We'll need to explain particle pollution and safe limits too but we can talk about that on Thursday.''}(posted on 17/10/2017)
\end{quote}
Graph on Figure \ref{fig:advocacy_efforts} shows how the main factor of influence changed from \textit{personal} to \textit{behavioural} about the time that there was data, such as hand-held monitoring and local traffic data, available to publish. Additionally, the group started their website and Twitter to publish that data, and to engage with the local community online and organised a door knocking to do the same offline. About the same there where multiple things happening in the community: an opposition group or counterpublic \citep{warner_publics_2005} emerged, local consultations for infrastructure changes started, and there was media exposure for the group and the project. While the importance of the \textit{behavioural} factor was steadily increasing in the advocacy efforts, the group also started to focus more on the \textit{environmental} factor. This was related to actively participating local consultations for built environment change, but also speaking in public meetings organised by local council about the issues in the neighbourhood and the data the group has collected.

The next major leap in advocacy efforts accrued about the time when community commissioned monitors got deployed by the UO in the neighbourhood and the data was published on city data portal. However people again had to get an understanding of that data by working together with the researcher and the team at UO. Graph also shows a period of no activity in the middle, which refers to the period when baseline data was obtained to help inform a better view of the air pollution issue.

\subsection{Social Cognitive Action Framework}

As mentioned before the factors are very closely linked with one another and focusing on one will have an effect on the others also. There is need for a new framework to better analyse the outcomes of the advocacy efforts, determine the actuators for human cognition, and reflect on the practices of the advocacy group. Looking at the core question of ``How are citizens taking action on local issues?'' enables to derive more specific categories which are simultaneously liked to multiple variables of social cognition. Figure \ref{fig:action_framework} shows the derived framework which incorporates social cognitive theory, but is more catered towards action to promote learning, knowledge creation and change. Although it is clear that the left two are more leaning towards influencing physical environmental for instrumenting that change, there are aspects of influencing the social norms, behavioural trends and even self-efficiency in almost all of these. Similarly to social cognitive theory's factors, the action framework's individual parts cannot be influenced in isolation. They are dynamically interlinked with each other and each action taken influences the whole action network. However when setting the focus it is possible to direct the action towards particular part of the framework making that the strongest variable in the action network.

Introducing social cognitive action framework will not only provide aid in assessing and reflecting on the processes, but it also provides scaffolding for setting focus for the future advocacy efforts of the group. Identifying key focus points for an advocacy group, enables designers better explore digital technologies that might support citizens taking action on local issues.

As cities adopt more technologies and they are being embedded in the physical fabric of the city itself, it will drastically change the way decisions are made in the city. The data that is being produced about the way city operates and how people interact with it, allows us to see the city through a completely different lens. This is also changing the way that citizens interact with the city, how they express their concerns and participate in decision-making processes. Integral part of this is the integration of civic technologies that aim to engage people to participate in these processes. The literature review of the thesis (see Chapter \ref{chap:literature}) covered a variety of different technologies and modes of participation (i.e., passive, active) that aim to engage people in all matters of civic life. Whether they are aiming for large scale participation [REF], drying to improve city services by harnessing the power of the crowd [REF], or using more participatory approach to promote active participation, curiosity and involvement of people in the design of these technologies [REF]. In the case of latter, scholars in design and HCI also distinguish between designing for immediate use or a response to a particular inquiry and designing for future unseen uses \citep{dantec_infrastructuring_2013,ehn_participation_2008,bjorgvinsson_participatory_2010}. This is what is referred to as infrastructuring, which essentially means using design practices to support capacity building in the community. 
However designing a commissioning platforms situates itself somewhere in between, where the aim is to develop a generic infrastructure which at the same time could be appropriated by people in their pursuit of issues. For example systems like App Movement where people come together to leverage technological infrastructure to commission bespoke location based mobile applications \citep{garbett_app_2016}. Although it is packaged as a finished platform or toolkit (i.e., product or a thing), it is driven by a participatory design process, facilities the discovery of issues and also promotes the creation of publics [REF]. This contradicts what \citet{ehn_participation_2008} has suggested, however similar findings where discovered from a community project around the design and use of Community Resource Messenger (CRM) system for homeless communities living in urban areas [REF]. Although the system was designed as a response to a practical need of a population, it still facilitated the creation of publics an their attachments around issues. Community commissioning platforms do not shy away from being designed as a purposeful systems, however built for uses which are defined engaging with it through issues of concern.

Technology is changing the way we understand infrastructuring. Commissioning of resources and provisioning of infrastructuring through platform like this certainly proposes a new set of questions of the way infrastructuring ought to be configured. Commissioning platform have the ability to scale up or streamline the processes of infrastructuring, but the same time have preserve the components of participatory design for constituting publics to develop attachments to act upon \citep{marres_issues_2007, dantec_infrastructuring_2013}. Within this somewhat generic system for engagement, it is important however to recognise the diverse nature of these formed publics and provide different levels of support for taking actions.

\subsection{Physical Infrastructure and Infrastructuring}

% building solutions/platforms/objects
% unplatforming
% maybe talk about this in the end

There needs to be a distinction between providing physical infrastructure and infrastructuring engagement around it. With the case of sensor commissioning toolkit the aim was to help people access the physical resources of the smart city to engage in exploration of local issues. Can the act of using or deploying the sensors be seen as a successful effort of infrastructuring? Availability of physical tools (i.e., environmental monitors) can provide support for community action through enabling people to participate in creation of new sources of data which did not exist before. So in some regards, certainly, commissioned senors helped generate data about the local community and to inform issues of concern in the neighbourhood. Furthermore, in some cases it enabled people to voice their concerns by making them more visible to others through appropriating of the toolkit resources. However the availability of physical infrastructure and the act of participating itself did not facilitate the creation or by itself was not sufficient enough for forming the publics needed for taking action. It needed to be integrated with activities supporting the usage of these physical things, or in other words it needed infrastructuring around the physical resources. SenseMyStreet toolkit approached this through a four-step exploration process which facilitated framing of issues, gathering support and develop attachments. Issue discovery was built into the system through participatory GIS and citizen sensing to align data generation with particular concerns, and to help form publics around those issues. This aligns with \citet{dantec_infrastructuring_2013} and \citet{marres_issues_2007} ideas of infrastructuring where socio-technical processes and resources are put in place to support imagined futures.

Without going through the process, the physical infrastructure did not provide any particular use. This was evident in couple of cases where people signed up to borrow hand-held monitors without defining the issue they are interested in (by skipping the first step of the toolkit). Some of them did not collect the monitors and some just tested the monitors for a brief while, but that was the extent of those explorations. It is not to say however that self discovery and learning about technology are something that are not useful for framing issues. In the case of the formed advocacy group, the huge driver for them was to foremost educate themselves about the issue, which then helped them to frame future actions. Intrinsic motivations for understanding the data where major factors for its use for the advocacy groups. When reflecting on the process with the participants and looking at the data produced by the stationary monitors, participants could make sense of the figures because they had gone through a self discovery in the first step (i.e., Tell us your issue) of the process. Having a clear view of what they had (in terms of the current data) and what they would expect to get from the monitors in the future, enabled the group to choose strategies and future actions accordingly. Enabling people to imagine the desired futures through the processes linked to community commissioning supports its value to act as a process for infrastructuring [REF]. 

When reflecting on the process of commissioning and discussing strategies for the data, participants stressed the fact that getting the monitors deployed actually marks a start of their advocacy efforts not the end result. Similarly as pointed out by \cite{dantec_infrastructuring_2013}:

\begin{quote}
    ``infrastructuring comes as a result of the reconfigurations that occur around and with a technological intervention; that is, the deployment of the technology is a beginning, not an end.'' \citep[p .249]{dantec_infrastructuring_2013}
\end{quote}

As designers and particularly as engineers of these technologies for participation and civic engagement, there is often a desire to come up or respond to people's requirements with a particular solution that addresses the issue. In case of a commissioning toolkit, it can still be a product or a useful system, however it has to facilitate infrastructuring. Designing provisioning of resources through infrastructuring are more likely to support capacity building (in terms of skills and constituted publics) that would benefit the community in a long run and provide a recurring and sustained participation around issues of concern.

\subsection{Forming Networks Through Community Links}
% + people care about environment, people feel empowered, more space groups informed (more people are becoming vocal)
% people needed something to get behind
% facilitating the formation of publics
The importance of publics in framing the issues should not be underestimated. Across the deployments it was clear that the existence or creation of publics where essential for the use of data. Often people living in one area where having similar opinions or concerns on matters, however they were not vocalised by anyone. Having a community of practice that could formulate issues and seek to generate data through the infrastructure provided by the commissioning toolkit to back them up helped to voice the issues. This type of collective representation of the community helped in turn to get more people behind of the issues and make them more prominent in the society and the eyes of decision makers.

% Supporting other groups with knowledge and learning form experiences of others (younger groups reached out to already established groups) and where taking there example when providing information/interventions
Another importance aspect that was discovered in designing SenseMyStreet for the communities, was the powerful effect of community links. This comes back to building capacity, however not only extending the skills and knowledge of immediate community but also reaching out to others outside the formed public. The advocacy groups which where formed though the use of SenseMyStreet started making links with each other. Although they were all living in separate areas of the city, having their own particular issues and attachments, they where exchanging knowledge and resources, and learning from each others experiences of using data as a tool for advocacy. For example sharing of pledges for electoral candidates to publicly sign or ideas how to present local data for residents. As more data about the community was generated thought the commissioned monitors, the representatives from different advocacy groups started having regular meeting with each other to discuss the strategies and develop joint thinking for establishing combined voice of the people of the whole city. Including showing up to community events and council meetings as a unified Safe Pedestrian and Cycling Environment (SPACE) advocacy group. 

Formation of these links where not explicitly facilitated through the commissioning infrastructure, however they organically emerged once people started working on strategies on using data. During the focus group when people where coming up with ideas for future strategies with the data, being the younger group, they often draw examples from established advocacy group.

% talk about sharing the twitter bot
Groups did not only share strategies, they also started sharing actual technical solutions developed as a result of strategies for using the data. As reported in the community reflection, a big focus for the participants was to surface the issue and build knowledge of the community. The proposal of having a automatic system that gives the community daily information about up to date readings from commissioned local monitors, was later realised by one of the members in the advocacy group. The group set up a Twitter Bot that tweets daily about the last 7 day average readings from their local sensor. Later the group also helped to set up a similar twitter bot for the other advocacy group.

Links between advocacy group definitely became a major factor in coming up with strategies for the use of community data. However links between advocacy groups were not the only connections that prompted the use of data within these groups. Also links with local authority helped advocacy groups to focus their efforts. As mentioned before, some of the participants where part of forums (e.g., Healthy Streets Board, Cycling Stakeholder Forum) within the local council. Often the groups had insider information about the next steps of the local council in terms of upcoming campaigns, consultations, infrastructure changes and policies. That enabled groups to come up with timely information snippets and explanations for the data and processes for community. Including using the data generated by the monitors to compile a informed view of issues. Advocacy groups often posted blogs with explanations of data, linked to things that are happening in the community or in the city at large.

% talk about links with authority figures and how they had insight information to direct their advocacy efforts when something was happening
Although groups worked closely with the council to help consult on infrastructure changes they did not seems that they where in the position of proposing solutions for the issues. This was also evident from the discussions with participants around reflecting on the uses of data. They did not feel that the data will help them engineer particular solutions, however they seemed themselves as concerned residents who want to expose the issues by expressing them through data in a way it would be understood by the members of community.

% community links - when a group realises its capabilities and limitation it reaches out and seeks skills from external groups and individuals in these domains. However group still takes ownership of what they have done and wants to see it through.
In a way the groups new their capabilities and limitations of what they can achieve with the data, but also they where trying to reach out and link up with external groups and individuals who had knowledge or skill in these domains. Whether it was different advocacy groups, professionals in the university or the local authority. Similar situation played out in the study conducted by \citet{disalvo_towards_2009} where local community, facing regulatory difficulties of getting their radio broadcast off the ground, decided to sought help of professionals who would help them get it on the air. Although the group increased their reliance on others they where still attached to the issues and felt responsibility for the community to get the project out there. This echos the commitments of the advocacy groups who took part in SenseMyStreet and helped generated data for the community. They felt accountable and wanted to see that data being made visible and accessible for the community. 

% some groups had existing links which they could use
For some communities however establishing these links was easier than for others. Whether they had more social capital, already existing links and connections they could call upon or sometimes just by luck and timing. For example showing up to workshop or neighbourhood forum and meeting people with similar issues. There have been successful attempts of using technology to accumulate that social capital. For example App Movement, a community commissioning platform for mobile technologies, used campaigning as a mechanism to help constitute the public prior to initiating the collaborative design process for the community technology \citep{garbett_app_2016}. Initially, similar mechanisms where considered for the design of SenseMyStreet toolkit. However there was a worry that the platform will start prioritising someones issues over others and the one who has the most `friends' will get the resources. Instead toolkit was made inclusive for everyone who has a issue that they want to explore, either it is a group of people or an individual. That proved to be the right move because some cases investigation was initiated by an individual first and in time when there was already some data available more people joined hence forming a public. This was also supported by the infrastructures of SensMyStreet technologies which enabled voting of and commenting of issues on SenseMyStreet website, and collaborating around proposals on SenseMyStreet Ideaboard. Infrastructuring around SenseMyStreet still did not provide a guarantee whether a deployment of monitors from the toolkit was followed by informed actions from the community. Although the toolkit supported collaborations and formulation of publics and links though infrastructuring, it was still largely up to the people involved to come up with strategies for acting upon the generated data.

\subsection{Taking ownership}
% framing issues or developing attachments
Researchers working in participatory design and particularly focused on community centred project, have documented the importance of issues and developing attachments to them which help constitute publics and create spaces for action [REF]. Mostly this work focuses on using participatory design for infrastructuring that helps come up with technical responses to issues of a formed public. In case of commissioning systems the technology itself acts as both -- a technical solution and socio-technical mechanism for infrastructuring. These are systems like App Movement \citep{garbett_app_2016} and SenseMyStreet, which was designed to be a community led urban sensing toolkit that would act as a catalyst for grass-roots civic action.

That is not to say that they have been designed to work on particular issues and publics. First interaction with SenseMyStreet start with \textit{flagging an issue} on a map, using the toolkit's website. This will set the tone for further activities in terms of framing the issue -- what type of issue it is and where does it occur. The `place' has an important roll to play too, as that is essential for constituting a new public around the issue or expressing and developing attachments of an existing public. 

It has also been documented that ownership plays an important role in infrastructuring, because of the way it steers people towards future action [REF]. Weather it is taking ownership and building attachments to issues [REF] or taking ownership of the designed technology [REF]. SenseMyStreet toolkit worked well for helping people to take ownership of the issues by publicly posting them and enabling people to attach personal opinions on the matter to them. Taking ownership of the designed technology (i.e., toolkit and all the resources) however appeared to be much more faceted.
\kylenote{Unplatforming originally comes from A. Dicks. The way it should actually be interpreted is 'system appropriation', going to an already existing community on some platform and integrating that into the process of engagement}
Commissioning systems are usually designed as a generic platform or system that decouples together multiple platforms or sub-systems. Latter is referred to as `unplatforming' which implies the use of online platforms for purposes that they where not initially designed for [REF]. SenseMyStreet toolkit can be refereed to as an unplatformed system because of its use of multiple platforms like MakePlace, Ideaboard, Carto, and Google Drive, Forms, Calendar and Scripting. With addition to the physical infrastructure in form of scientific grade environmental monitors. It would have been impossible for the community to take ownership of those tools and technologies. However people could take ownership of the resources generated as a result of the process. Putting it in \citet{dantec_infrastructuring_2013}'s terms, people taking ownership of the data generated and knowledge created through the commissioned monitors and their role in shaping future actions.
% Data has no owner, collections of data do, so the first person in the chain to aggregate data is the owner by default

There is an ongoing debate around data ownership in regards of its generation and rights to access. This also extends to the idea of by law data has no owner, the collection of data does. However, when the collection of data is commissioned by the community does the community own that data then? Do make things even more complicated, SenseMyStreet project has two levels of data generations. One that people engage in themselves by using borrowed hand-held monitors, and the other when people commission stationary monitors to be placed in their neighbourhood. SenseMySteet project and Urban Observatory who deploys the monitors, both work in the spirit of transparency and openness, which means that the data is made publicly available using open licences. If data is in the public domain who does it belong to and who will take ownership of the data?

Having two types of data generation presented itself to be kind of double edge sword. On one hand having people doing their own investigations helped them better understand and frame the issues. The data collected by people using hand-held monitors on their commutes were not used in advocacy efforts in the community, it were predominantly used by the groups themselves to explore the issues and target particular areas for prioritising. Furthermore, because there where no official regulations how do conduct the monitoring the research team could not guarantee the rigorousness and transparency of the processes. For people it was easier to full responsibility and ownership of that data because they invested their time in it and it was important to them to get the best data out of the process for future actions.

The second stage of data generation was carried out by professionals at the Urban Observatory. This meant that the community commissioned monitors got the same attention and went trough the same transparent deployment process as they would if the data was collected for scientific research or policy purposes. When communities handed over the task of data collection to the researchers, they put a lot of trust on the process, however often it meant that they also handed over the responsibilities for appropriating the collected data. This was evident with some groups that participated in the study where they commissioned the sensors to provide data for the community without having a particular aim to use it themselves in the future:

% quote template
% get the quote form ideaboard
\begin{quote}
\textit{``Air Pollution Monitors along Brunton Lane near the roundabouts would be beneficial to everyone.''} (SenseMyStreet Ideaboard)
\end{quote}

Unfortunately this meant that once the stationary monitor was installed the group stopped interacting with the toolkit because there were no planned next steps with the data. In contrast, the participants interviewed in the focus group were actively involved in the deployment processes by prompting the researchers for updates. When there were technical difficulties with the monitors on the first deployment, the group also felt that they are accountable and wanted to see that the issues are resolved. People worked closely with the research team keeping an eye on the readings to detect inconsistencies and get faulty sensors replaced. It was their effort that got the monitors out there, so they felt that it was their responsibility to make something out of that data and provide it to the people in a more digestible form. Ability to adapt and dynamically shift responsibilities, often to externals actors, to work around problems that might occur has been show to be strong benefit of infrastructuring projects \citet{dantec_infrastructuring_2013}.

Research in community informatics has shown that community taking ownership for the final outcome of the project is essential for the sustainability of the initiative \citep{merkel_participatory_2004,carroll_participatory_2007}. Analysis of the groups that engaged with the SenseMyStreet toolkit shows that when the group who initiated the process of commissioning takes ownership of the community data, it will promote its further use in civic action. All communities are different, with different issues and attachments and all need their own approaches, thus there is a role for infrastructuring to play here to facilitate the creation of these bespoke methods of action.

\subsection{Plans of Action}
SenseMyStreet project successfully helped to carry out citizen led deployment of sensors, enabling people access resources that where not available them before. However that was not the end of the story, the question is how to take that data and put it into use for activities for local benefit?

The analysis suggests that the process of data generation was benefiting the community when it was supported by additional strategies to purpose the data. Data generation had to be connected to a specific goal, either it was personal discovery and self knowledge or for the purposes of changing the community. Although the commissioning platform was designed as a product that would facilitate infrastructuring, it was up to the community to take it further from data production to informing issues and exploring ways the data can be put into use to promote change in the community. It was evident that plans for actions actually preceded data. When participants reflected on the process of commissioning and talked about their motivations for getting involved in the project, they pointed out a set of strategies that they had for the data before it existed. Strategies that could be tailored and implemented depending on what is possible to get out of the data. When it comes to infrastructuring, not only attachments are dynamic \citep{dantec_infrastructuring_2013} but so are the strategies for actions. As learned from engaging participants in commissioning, they could shift in focus from \textit{personal} to \textit{behavioural} to \textit{environmental} or the other way around depending on the accordance's of data. Initial strategies could end up not working at all in the long run, however having a set of questions, ideas and plans for actions gave people a specific goal to work towards. 

Years of experience in working around data has revealed that ``everyone wants data until you give it to them'' -- meaning it takes much more effort to make data accessible and understandable. Data science skills and training are required to transform data into a useful form and into something that community can use in their advocacy efforts. Rows and rows of sensor readings in a spreadsheet just do not work. Although there is a huge representation issue with the data does not mean that it should not be available for the public. Establishing links within the community and through collaborating with external actors can help alleviate some of these issues with data. Additionally, having the people involved in the processes of data production through infrastructuring enables to break down barriers for data use by linking it to peoples concerns and helping to developing plans for action that could be sustained in the future.
