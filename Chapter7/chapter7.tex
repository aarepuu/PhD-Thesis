\epigraph{For these issues to be successfully addressed through the use of ICTs, attention will need to be paid not simply to ``access'' but also to an entire range of supports for ``effective use''.}{--- \textup{Michael Gurstein}}

The primary aim of this research has been to explore tools and methods for effective use of data by citizens in civic advocacy and action. Taking a participatory action design research approach, investigations were conducted through the design, development, deployment and analysis of two Community Informatics (CI) platforms (SenseMyStreet in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} and Data:In Place in Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) for effective use of data, which are being further sustained though research facilities and continue to be used by communities. By reflecting on the iterative design processes and the use of these systems by communities, enabled to produce a new model for shared data interactions as Community-Data Interaction (CDI) that could help designers discuss and design for effective use of data by communities. This chapter begins by giving a brief overview of the research and revisiting the initial objectives presented in the \hyperlink{objectives}{Introduction}, to see how the research has responded to these objectives and contributes to the wider research field. Furthermore, the sustainability of the solutions resulted from the research is discussed and how this work can inform further research in HCI, CI and wider fields.

\section{Overview}

Advances in ICT and an increase of Smart city technologies being deployed have made it possible to use data for measuring, control, optimisation and management of cities, but also to mediate and support citizen participation. This participation can take different forms depending on the level of engagement from the citizens. At the far end of the spectrum there is passive participation through data that supports the algorithmic smart city agenda (i.e., citizens as source of data). However at the other end there are smart citizens -- citizens as active producers, consumers and users of data. These latter modes are supported and advanced by citizen sensing technologies, open movements (e.g., open knowledge, open source, open data) and new ways of engaging in and creating community networks. These different modes of participation, highlighted in Chapter \ref{chap:literature}, can help citizens get more engaged and in some cases use and produce more data, however there is more work needed in order for that data to become useful for the community. Furthermore, the data in question is often big data at the scale of cities, which is inherently complex, large in scale or velocity. To work with such data requires specialised tools together with skills and knowledge of expert data professional (i.e., data scientists) who ultimately make that data useful for people. However these skills of information processing and cognitive processes take years of practice and training, which puts it outside the grasp of most individuals and communities. The challenge is then to understand how does these skills become transferred either to ordinary citizens or into a system that automates those processes for them. The field of CI has emerged to respond to these challenges, however it is a relatively new field that has still gaps and lack of empirical evidence how information systems could be utilised by communities in practice. This thesis explored these challenges through the design and development of CI tools (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} and Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}), and proposed a new approach to effective use of data that aimed to utilise technologies in all aspects of Community-Data Interaction (CDI) (Chapter \ref{chap:cdi}). Furthermore, the research went a step further and provided an example of a practical use of the CDI model (Chapter \ref{chap:cdi_in_action}) in a community context. With that the research did not only design and build the types of tools that non-professionals would need to interact with data, it extended beyond and looked at methods of \textit{social facilitation} that is needed for effective use of data by communities.

\subsubsection{The Limitations of CDI for Community Informatics Research}

The research in this thesis was conducted through in `in the wild' \citep{rogers_interaction_2011,taylor_leaving_2013} deployments with particular communities, dealing with explicit issues in specific locations, at a certain point in time -- which makes the repeatability (or reproducibility) of this research virtually impossible. Furthermore, the research in this thesis was highly dependent on the efforts of the author who positioned himself as part of the communities, providing expert skills and knowledge, and becoming one the supporting capacities for them. This in turn enabled to better understand the context and identify the key elements that contribute to the democratisation of data science practices and effective use of data by communities. However, by learning from the processes documented by this research and applying the CDI model on their own practices, researchers would be able to produce valuable outcomes in similar contexts.

\section{Approaches to Community Informatics Research}

This section looks back on the aims and objectives of the research conducted in this thesis, reflecting on the contributions of the work and pointing out the contribution of the thesis within the wider research field.


\subsection{Open/Available, Accessible, Comprehensible, Usable and Useful Data}
\begin{quote}
    (\textit{Conceptual objective}) Through a critical lens of engineering (i.e., Information Theory), epistemology and social studies, build up a conceptual model and understanding of ``what it means to make data usable for purposes of civic advocacy and action''.
\end{quote}

\noindent 
The majority of critiques around the use of data by citizens mainly point out the lack of resources, access, knowledge and skills to interact with expert systems that are used to produce or make data available -- referring to the statement of data in principle is `open' for everyone to use, which is not true in many cases. For example, this is not true around active citizen data production and accessing open datasets such as Open Govermental Data (OGD). For the vision of a \textit{citizen as an active data user}, put forward in Chapter \ref{chap:literature}, these barriers needed to be overcome for this positioning to become a reality. Hence, the initial investigations of the research looked into ways that data production can be democratised through establishing of commissioning toolkit (see Case Study I in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}); and how available (i.e., open) data can become accessible, comprehensible and usable data for citizens, when its contextualised using a CI system (see Case Study II in Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}). One could argue, that in terms of the dual agenda of the CI \citep{stillman_community_2009}, these problems related more to the information systems (or engineering) problem solving agendas, which deal with the technical design of information technologies and systems. However, the user-centred integrative design process that was undertaken in the design of the technical solution, was dictated by community problem solving activities (e.g., the second agenda of CI). These efforts resulted in a development of SenseMyStreet (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}), the first sensor commissioning toolkit for communities that enables people to use and to commission data from scientific grade environmental monitors from their local neighbourhoods; and Data:In Place (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}), an open-source platform which facilitates accessing and making sense of open data from expert systems by communities. These reflected the different layers of challenges of democratising practices around data; (i) data needs to be accessible, (ii) data needs to be comprehensible, (ii) data needs to be usable, (iii) data needs to be useful; which the research in this thesis has systematically worked through. 

Although the systems built addressed the dual agenda of the CI, working on both the technical and the social design of information systems, the implementations on their own did not make the data useful for communities. Reflecting on the first two case studies of the thesis (see Chapter \ref{chap:cdi}) it became evident that \textit{usefulness} is not actually a property of data. Essentially all data can be useful, however usefulness is actually a property of the person or the interaction. People needed a purpose that they could use the data for, and then it became useful to them. That contradicts \citet{ackoff_data_1999}'s notion of data ability to produce information and knowledge and wisdom (i.e., knowledge that can be applied in action). Data often in the form that it has been collected and presented is not self evident and straight away becomes useful. Data needs multiple processes, and often social process, to help give it meaning. Furthermore, when data is taken out from a particular context both its place and its community, it often looses its meaning \citep{taylor_data_2014,gitelman_raw_2013,hakken_social_2003}. These are however things that cannot be implemented into an algorithms, these are much more human processes of facilitation and trying to work with people to help them understand what their needs and challenges are. Additionally, the findings of the first two case studies revealed that the key to being able to make use of that data is social capital, and often being able to access gain to capacities and skills outside the community group. However, it was illustrated that the digital tools can still be configured to be generic and robust, but have processes attached that help facilitate or infrastructure the creation of community networks. These findings somewhat contradict the existing research in the design of community technologies \citep{dantec_infrastructuring_2013,ehn_participation_2008}, however have also been proved to be valid by research in community commissioning \citep{garbett_designing_2017,garbett_app_2016}. Contacting research in these spaces and by moving from data access and use (including production) to start looking at what makes data useful for people in communities, the research took a shift to more collective and collaborative approach, and started looking at the wider factors that influence the effective uses of data by communities. One of which was the shift from active citizens to communities, and also looking at the roles and supporting capacities around the uses of data for advocacy and action.

\subsubsection{From Active Citizens to Communities}
The aim of this research from the beginning has been to understand the processes around active citizen participation in the Smart city. The term \textit{citizens} have been used throughout this thesis to refer to \textit{`people who inhabit, work and play in cities'}. The official term of citizen; ``as a legally recognised subject or national of a state or commonwealth, either native or naturalised''; however does not apply to all citizens referred to in this thesis. When embarking on this research around active citizen participation though data, the objective was to support active forms of using data (see Chapter \ref{chap:literature}) by citizens to produce actionable knowledge. However, the context of this research was not about individuals using data and information systems to create value for their own purposes (but can be a part of it, see Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}), it was more about a community sharing and creating knowledge and courses of action. Furthermore, the knowledge that is needed for civic advocacy and for taking collective action is collective knowledge and distributed information of the community. Hence, throughout the research there was a shift of focus from active citizens to communities. Still promoting the agenda of citizens as active users of data, but the focus shifted to the ways communities can make data useful towards activities of civic advocacy and action. The findings related to civic advocacy and action from Case Study I and II, also showed the importance of social capital and building community capacities. For that reason the model of CDI was built around community and focused on the key elements that promoted creation of community networks that benefit towards achieving the \textit{``knowledge society''} \citep{jennex_big_2017,hakken_social_2003}. For \citet{jennex_big_2017} the knowledge society is an approach that uses knowledge and intelligence to create processes and products that better serve citizens. This idea of creating processes and products for citizens to use, aligns well with the research agenda of Social Informatics (SI) and also with CI \citep{stillman_community_2009}. However, thinking about Community-Data Interaction within the CI, the knowledge society is more about the process of facilitation and support through the use of methods and digital technologies to help community to create and to exchange knowledge for purposes of civic action. This aligns more with \citet[ch. 6-7]{hakken_social_2003}'s approach to knowledge networking and what \citet{gurstein_toward_2012} considers (\textit{``development, deployment, and use of ICTs for local benefit''} [p. 36]) should be the central concern of CI. If the focus shifts from just designing and building better SI and CI tools; to access, make sense and use data; to thinking about the interactions that the community is having around the use of data, we can then start incorporating new ways of researching, designing and building tools that support the processes of CDI and the effective use of data by communities. Achieving the `effective use' use of data by communities \citep{gurstein_effective_2003} is far more desirable outcome than a very well built tool for data access, comprehension or visualisation.

This type of thinking and designing was put into practice in the Case Study III (Chapter \ref{chap:cdi_in_action}) where the aim was not to build a perfect CI tool for data (i.e., a perfect version of the Data:In Place platform), but to help communities better understand their challenges, and the resources and capacities needed to use data for activities of local benefit. The inclusion of \textit{Hypothesis Setting} and the \textit{Question Formulation Technique (QFT)} to the processes of inquiry and the \textit{Challenge Creation} methods for taking action, are some of these examples that where trialled in the Case Study III. Whether the community's aim was to improve and optimise their working practices, as was the case for the local charity; or get better service provisioning and facilities for the community, as was the case for the neighbourhood planning group; the indicative findings from Case Study III (Chapter \ref{chap:cdi_in_action}) suggest that the ability to understand the challenges, recognise the capacities needed, and be able to plan out the actions, were far more empowering for people than having access to open datasets. Rather than giving communities products and services, and coming in and trying to solve their problems, the approach taken was more about helping community increase their capacities and resilience to resist economic and social disparities. 

\subsubsection{Dissembling the Role of Data Scientist}
One of these important capacities around making data useful for local benefit is the ability to manipulate the data. That does not only mean having access to data and tools to work with it, but also possessing the skills to be able to use it: being able to ask the right questions, knowing what needs to be found and what to do with it. These are all the skills of an professional data analyst or more widely know now as data scientist. Often the type of data in these contexts, such as the Smart city, are data that are inherently complex (i.e., big data). Hence quite often there is need for a help of a data scientist, because of all of these complexities of the data. Throughout this research, the author acted as a data scientist supporting communities with skills and knowledge needed to work with data. However, the challenge was to figure out ways that the role of the professional can be reduced to truly democratise these processes. This was done for example by using intelligent CI systems such as Data:In Place (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) to interface with expert data accessing systems (i.e., APIs), and through the process of QFT to come up with right questions for data, or using Data Request on the Data:In Place platform to find data needed by the community. As a result the role of the professional did not disappear from the picture, it was reduced and accompanied by automation and also in some extent skills transfer to ordinary citizens. Furthermore, the combination of \textit{Challenge Creation} methods and \textit{Data Challenges} on the Data:In Place platform, trialled in Case Study II (Chapter \ref{chap:cdi_in_action}), opened up new opportunities for capacity building and creation of community links between citizens and data professionals. This illustrated a new way of replacing the role of the researcher (who acted as a data professional for the community) with automated tools to help other data professionals interface with the community.

\subsection{Digital Civics Approaches to Community Informatics}
\begin{quote}
    (\textit{Technical objective}) Design and build tools that enable and promote the use of data in civic participation and advocacy. This involves understanding the context, culture, values and practices of different community groups working with data, in order to adequately respond to their needs and issues they are tackling. This also includes establish processes that will help build capacity within the community to help sustain the support for community groups working with data.
\end{quote}

\noindent
The case studies conducted in this research resulted in the design and development of multiple digital tools (see SenseMyStreet in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} and Data:In Place in Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) for data production, access and use by communities. Some of them were built bespoke platforms and tools such as: the online GIS issue flagging system\footnote{\url{https://sensemystreet.uk/map}}, an Android application\footnote{\url{https://play.google.com/apps/testing/uk.sensemystreet}} for recording GPS and personal reflections, an online proposal collaboration tool IdeaBoard\footnote{\url{https://sensemystreet.ideaboard.co.uk}}, and a sensor data analysis tool\footnote{\url{https://github.com/aarepuu/fidasfrogparser}} for SenseMyStreet toolkit; and the Data:In Place platform\footnote{\url{https://app.data-in.place}} for accessing and using open data. Others however were already existing platforms which were configured to serve different purposes for the SenseMyStreet toolkit: an automatic on-boarding and monitor loan forms\footnote{\url{https://goo.gl/forms/7A9eVtB4KwyuM0AA2}} and an automatic monitor loan scheduler\footnote{\url{https://goo.gl/14wrgM}} configured on Google Apps; and an automatic sensor mapping using Carto\footnote{\url{https://sensemystreet.carto.com}} for mapping hand-held monitor data from community sensing. Furthermore, all these technologies were a result of an iterative design process where the solutions and the features implemented were direct results to community challenges and the issues they were tackling. 

The initial exploration for establishing the SenseMyStreet toolkit (see Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}) was conducted through ethnographic work, engaging with active local community groups, followed by a community workshop to further explored ways to promote usage of data for raising awareness about the issues in local neighbourhoods and for civic action. Toolkit was then set up trough a user-centred integrative design process evolving in attending and taking part of community meetings with local community groups advocating for built infrastructure changes in their local areas. All that resulted in the development of SenseMyStreet, a sensor commissioning toolkit for communities that enables people to use and to commission environmental data from scientific grade monitors from their local streets and neighbourhoods. The research did not only try to provide the needed resources for the communities, but also worked together with the community to understand best practices for utilising the resources. Often designing user friendly manuals to replace the ones accompanied by the scientific grade environmental monitors\footnote{\url{https://sensemystreet.uk/sensors}}. The constant feedback loops with the community provided valuable input for further improving the technologies, but also enabled to understand the key elements of CDI and successful uses of the data in action by communities (see Chapter \ref{chap:cdi}).

Similarly, the Data:In Place platform which was presented in Case Study II (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}), took data access and active use by communities under consideration by exploring the value of Open Governmental Data (OGD) to help inform local decision-making. Although, the the case study was set out by three design principles for accessible data systems based on the current state of the art of open data systems for non-expert users and critiques from the literature, the platform itself was designed and developed through an 18-month iterative co-design process, involving a neighbourhood-planning group and multiple charity organisations from the North East of England. This involved the author attending and taking part in Neighbourhood Planning meetings and having regular meetings with the local charity organisations to understand the context that the communities were operating in. The detailed iterative design process that was described in Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace} showed how the features of the platform (and the data), were direct responses to community's problem solving activities \citep{stillman_community_2009}.

Taking a Digital Civics \citep{olivier_digital_2015,vlachokyriakos_digital_2016} approach to designing and building Community Infomatics (CI) technologies provided an opportunity to expand on the Information Systems Design (ISD) framework of \citet{hirschheim_exploring_1996} to include more social, community and activist agendas, and also enabled to present CI a better theoretical framing through the model of CDI (Chapter \ref{chap:cdi}). Expanding the uses of technology further from just being the instrumentation's of control, as presented by \citet{hirschheim_exploring_1996} and extended by \citet{stillman_community_2009}; to enabling people find uses of technology in other orientations such as \textit{strategizing}, \textit{sense-making} and \textit{argumentation}. The findings from the Case Study III (Chapter \ref{chap:cdi_in_action}) showed that technology can also find use in discussing and deliberating around issues; by using \textit{Ambit} in Issue Mapping activity; or communicating issues to others; by using Data:In Place Data Requests and Data Challenges. Additionally, technology aided in capturing some of that context (e.g., metadata and paradata on Data:In Place platform) needed to understand the challenges of communities in order to adequately respond to them without necessarily engaging into face-to-face talk. Furthermore, according to \citet[ch. 6]{hakken_social_2003}, finding ways to use automated information technologies (AITs) to facilitate interactions that redeem face-to-face talk should be the main task of knowledge engineering by communities. 

\subsubsection{The Sustainability of CDI for Community Informatics Research}

Having the capacities of the researcher in hand to constantly provide support to communities puts the sustainability and longevity of these resources and systems under a risk. This issue was also raised by \citet{garbett_designing_2017} around sustaining community commissioned information resource, emphasising the need for finding a community that will \textit{adopt and sustain} the asset in the future -- keeping the information resources up to date and relevant to the community. However, the research in this thesis went a step further to not only look at how to enable citizens produce community commissioned information resources, but also how they make effective use of those resources. Similarly, the findings of Case Study II evaluation (see Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}) showed that in order to sustain the engagement around the commissioned data resources there needs to be a transfer of ownership to the community, but it also has to be supported by building of capacities in order for the community to make effective use of the data. For some groups involved, these capacities were found through existing links and networks that enabled them to tap into new skills and competences. Such as data science skills for making easily digestible visualisations for the community (see Figure \ref{fig:localair}). One other solution that was proposed in Case Study III (\ref{chap:cdi_in_action}) for cases when the community lacked these links, was through the use of an online platform (i.e., Data:In Place's Data Challenges) that could help interface between communities and data professionals. However there would have always be a reliance on the researcher (or the software developer) to support and sustain these technologies (e.g., Data:In Place and SenseMyStreet toolkit).

The SenseMyStreet toolkit was officially launched in the summer of 2017. Since then over 60 people have signed up, 7 groups of people have done environmental sensing with hand-held monitors loaned from the toolkit in their commutes to explore issues in their local area and four communities have successfully commissioned stationary monitors to be deployed in their neighbourhood. \subsection{Guiding Principles for Community-Data Interaction}
\begin{quote}
    (\textit{Pragmatic objective}) Provide an model of practice for interactions with data in knowledge networking at community level that can be applied when designing and building tools for effective use of data by citizens for civic participation, advocacy and action.
\end{quote}
\noindent

The concept of \textit{``effective use''} of ICT and data put forward by \citet{gurstein_effective_2003,gurstein_open_2011} what later became the main aims for community informatics \citep{gurstein_what_2007} have been around for over a decade now. There have been different theoretical basis proposed to CI over the years. For example \citet{stillman_community_2009} proposed that CI should be adopted as a part of a \textit{``fragmented adhocracy''} of information systems (IS), which enables to extend IS theoretical basis to the field of CI and also helps expand IS design to include more social and community agendas. This aligns with \citet{carroll_participatory_2007}'s views on CI research which looks at the design and management of ISs, and infrastructures for hyper local level entities. However, they put a much bigger emphasis on the participatory design activities for doing this type of research within communities. Another attempt to develop a theoretical basis for CI was done by \citet{gurstein_toward_2012}, through looking at the work and case studies conducted as part of the Alliance for Community Innovation and Networking (CRACIN) in Canadian context. His realisation however was that because the work in CI is applied and case study specific, it is difficult to develop a definitive theoretical framing for the fields. Instead we should use theory as pragmatic practice or process in order to help conceptualise and understand the factors and processes influencing the adoption and use of technologies by communities. Similarly to him, \citet{moor_collaboration_2009} has stated that CI research is a wide-ranging field which is predominantly \textit{``practice built''} having connections to several methodological pillars. For him the connecting element between these pillars are \textit{``collaboration patterns''} that can be applied in CI practice. Furthermore, \citet{stillman_capability_2014} have proposed that because of the activist and social justice nature of CI research, we should adopt more critical social theory and approaches such as \textit{Capability Approach}. This would extend the research from having a narrow technical orientation to having a border critical social lens -- enabling to extend the research agendas to look at the long term affects and adoption of the technologies by communities.

It seems that there is no `one' theoretical framing that would work with all the research agendas surrounding CI. However, most scholars in CI seem to agree that the way to extend the theoretical basis of CI, is through analysing practical approaches. Hence the research in this thesis took an investigative approach through two case studies (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}, \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}, \ref{chap:datainplace}), looking at different aspects and elements of effective use of data by communities, and provided practical examples of design and implementations of technologies. Furthermore, through reflecting and analysing the findings from the first two case studies enabled to extend on the theoretical basis of CI by providing a model of Community-Data Interaction (CDI) (see Chapter \ref{chap:cdi}). The model of CDI provided a set of guiding principles for thinking about and designing technologies and processes for the effective use of data by communities, which were then put into practice in Case Study III (Chapter \ref{chap:cdi_in_action}). Through taking a more holistic view to CI research in practice, and by using a dual approach for the developing of CI tools and methods, enabled to illustrate the use of technologies not only for access and use of data, but also the training, education and organisational support aspects of effective use of data by communities \citep{gurstein_effective_2003,gurstein_toward_2012}. Providing researchers a better understanding of the ways that people interact with data within a complex sociotechnical systems and helping to identify and underline the existing challenges. This enables further research in CI around the effective use of data to understand not only the practical forms that the key elements of CDI can take, but also to give indications how to apply new strategies for supporting communities to make effective use of data. Furthermore, through analysing not only technical aspects, but also social and organisational impacts on CDI, will enable researchers make better design choices for tools and methods to be used for purposeful and effective uses of data in civic participation.

Thus, the research done in this thesis has conducted and documented the practical cases of participatory design processes for effective use of data by communities (Case Study I and II); helped further extend the design and development of ISs for community use (e.g., Data:In Place and SenseMyStreet platforms); provided a wider conceptualisation around effective use of data by communities through the model of CDI (Chapter \ref{chap:cdi}) that extends on theoretical basis of IS, CI and community commissioning; and showed how one would design technologies and methods in the frame of the CDI model and for the purposes of community action (Case Study III in Chapter \ref{chap:cdi_in_action}). The reminder of this chapter is dedicated to looking at new opportunities for designing for CDI -- new ways of thinking around the use of ICT for data discovery and use, and ways that we could truly democratise data science practices.

\section{The Future Research in CDI}

Although we all live in the ``information society'', the way that each individual (or community) can benefit from it differs marginally. There are raising tensions between those who have access and can effectively use the the new technologies and information sources for economic and social benefits, and those who lack such means \citep{gurstein_toward_2012}. The research in this thesis looked at ways we can surface and start identifying this divide in the society by understanding ways to enable better uses of ICT and data for those who might benefit from it the most. However, in order to start addressing these issues in the larger scale across communities, there needs to be an ability to scale up this research and the practical uses that it has produced. That could also mean we need to adopt new ways of thinking about data, data use and re-use.

One approach for future developments of CDI systems could be to design features that promote exploration and curiosity, accompanied by using embedded context, to improve retrieval of useful information from different data sources. This criteria is important, because people do not acquire information in the manner of how machines do (e.g., through semantic links), to satisfy their curiosity and need for information on the web, people posit questions which often are open ended and do not map to any specific structure. With that in mind, \citet{paritosh_what_2018} has theorised what instead of `web of data' or Semantic Web, \textit{``we rethink the web with the primary goal of fostering and satisfying human curiosity?''}. Furthermore, how would that affect the way we retrieve data from the web?

Currently, people need the skills and tools of data scientists who know where to look and can plug into APIs of data publishers, or use automated interfaces that could contextualise that data for people (e.g., Data:In Place platform). In order for non-experts to start re-purposing and using abstract datasets from the web, there is a need for a system that links questions with the data that might bare the answers. Using this criteria and drawing on previous work and experiences of the author, with addition to looking at the available infrastructures on the web, it is possible to start thinking about of how might we reconfigure the way people interact with open data sources on the web, and start compiling and making use of this rich dataset of questions with linked open data sources.

\subsection{Adjacent Possible: State of The Art of Available Infrastructures}
% Unplatforming
It seems that data needs anchors for people to start exploring some of the questions that these datasets answer. Common anchors for open data publishing platforms are things like domains or topics (e.g., transport, health, education, local services), publishing entities (e.g. NHS Digital, local governments, Ordnance Survey, ONS) and data access formats (e.g., machine readable files, APIs, PDF files). Related work has also shows that place can be a valuable anchor for accessing and exploring datasets \citep{taylor_data--place:_2015,puussaar_making_2018}. Case Study II (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) illustrated that place and situated activities that people engage there, provides a valuable context for starting to explore data that might answer questions about those places. It seems that in order to make data \textit{useful} it needs to be somehow contextualised -- made relevant to people and their situation. Anchoring in this sense is just another form of grounding or contextualising the data. Furthermore, Chapter \ref{chap:cdi} presented further practical approaches for contextualising by linking them to \textit{issues} and \textit{inquires}.

Despite that the data can be anchored in place, linked to issues and inquires, because of how it has been generated and archived for access, it still falls short having a direct route for answering questions that people come up every day. In search of information on the web, people often rely on the experiences of others finding out answers to similar questions. The uses of online forums like Quora\footnote{\url{https://www.quora.com}} and Reddit\footnote{\url{https://www.reddit.com}}, problem solving platforms like StackOverflow\footnote{\url{https://stackoverflow.com}} are just few examples of places where people go to look for answers. Often the information or datasets people are seeking are already out there, but someone (often a professional data analyst) needs to make the link between the questions and the abstract datasets. This often requires an understanding of how the data was compiled or collected, and what hidden values it represents. Furthermore, the link between the actual data that answered the question might get lost in the noisy forum thread.

From the software development processes, people might be familiar with issue tagging when they discover a bug or come up with an idea for improving the software. With the increasing popularity of the open source software movement and people having access to open code repositories such as GitHub\footnote{\url{https://github.com}}, issue tagging functionalities are becoming more and more similar to forum or question \& answer (QA) based interactions with the systems. We can already see projects on GitHub that leverage this infrastructure to compile and archive links to open datasets. For example Awesome Public Datasets\footnote{\url{https://github.com/awesomedata/awesome-public-datasets}} project which compile a topic-centric list of open datasets in a public domain. It uses Github issues and pull requests to receive new proposals of dataset links. The project has tens of thousands of followers and majority people who contribute the datasets already seem to know how and what data they are looking for. However, a brief observation reveals that some of the issues are actually more rephrased as questions without any link or routes to data. For example \textit{``How can I apply job for driver in canada/ usa''}. This exact request posted on the \textit{Issues} already has some of the anchors mentioned previously: (1) a place - Canada or USA, (2) a issue or a topic - working as a driver. There has been number of other projects which make use the open source community and the capabilities of platforms such as GitHub. Similar ideas where applied in Case Study III (Chapter \ref{chap:cdi_in_action}) where people used Data:In Place platform's \textit{Data Challenges} to posit questions for data. However, the platform automatically collected some of the contextual data -- the place of the challenge issuer, drawn boundary and the interactions with existing datasets on the platform. Furthermore, the challenge description was automatically translated into Markdown language which is often what these issue tagging and QA systems (e.g., GitHub) use. With these opportunities, we could start leveraging the ideas and infrastructure of the open source ecosystem to organise, archive and make accessible this contextual information which helps people link their questions to datasets which might hold the answers.

\subsection{Human Computation Approach to Organise Data on The Web}

The problem with using already available structured data systems that organise information on the web, is that the questions that people come up with do not obey any laws of structured data sources (e.g., Linked Data and JSON-LD). Questions that people come up with are not some polished queries for exact pieces of information, they are driven more by need and curiosity \citep{paritosh_what_2018} of people. Often these questions need to be understood by someone else with the knowledge of a possible information to address them -- a data scientist. It has also been reported that the majority, about 79\%, of data scientist work is actually `janitorial' -- collecting, untangling, matching and cleaning data\footnote{2016 CrowdFlower Data Science Report. Available at: \url{https://visit.figure-eight.com/rs/416-ZBE-142/images/CrowdFlower_DataScienceReport_2016.pdf}}. Furthermore, having somebody else make descisions what is important can also reveal some further problems around multiplicity of views and biases of people thinking that they have the correct link to information answering the proposed questions. There is no single truth, there is only a matter of perspective corresponding to the values the person or a group looking for answers. Nevertheless the first step of organising this information and making it accessible for re-enquiry is to start documenting these `knowledge enquiries' and routes to data were the answer was found. Having this laid out for others to learn from, might make their discovery of information a bit easier.

\subsubsection{Challenges}

Firstly, the challenge is how to make these links between data and questions useful. The current analysis of previous work suggest that there is a need for new mechanisms for people to posit questions, and have number of previously asked questions generated. That would help link similar questions together and also show the route of discovery and data sources for those that have already answered. For that tagging, merging and hierarchies with combination of HITS or NLP algorithms (see Section \ref{sub:human_machine} in Chapter \ref{chap:cdi}) could be used to make these associations automatically. We can already see this first part happening on Google Search Engine through the \textit{People Also Ask} section. However this needs to be extended to not only linking questions to each other and links to websites, but also links to actual datasets on the web where one might find the answer. Building on that, Google has recently come out with a new Dataset Search\footnote{\url{https://datasetsearch.research.google.com}}. However in order for the Dataset Search to discover a particular dataset on the web, it requires to be described through adding metadata in \href{https://schema.org}{schema.org}. From there, it could be possible to start linking questions to datasets. Furthermore, there is a need for a validation process or feedback loop, that likely involves human intelligence, to assess the usefulness of retrieved datasets for each question. This could take a form of QA or a forum site which connects questions to datasets on the web. Secondly, the challenge is what are the scaling strategies of this solution. Case Study III (Chapter \ref{chap:cdi_in_action}) illustrated through the use of Data:In Place's \textit{Data Challenges} how we might start collecting this corpus of data of questions and linked datasets on a local community level with isolated questions. However, these links often had to be made by professional data scientist (e.i., the author) who knew where to look for data and how to put it into a form that it can be used by communities. Moving forward, there needs to be some type of process that enables and incentives data professionals to start making these links and answering to these questions with datasets. One option could be to connect these challenges into a learning and skills training platform such as \href{https://mooqita.org}{mooqita.org}. This would enable data scientist in training to take up the challenges and provide solutions and datasets, but also if companies who are hiring could steward the challenges, it would offer monetary value for challenge takers in addition for the social credit.

\subsection{Engineering Approach to Organise Data on The Web}

On the other hand, perhaps using big data and building better algorithms and contextually aware artificial intelligence's (AIs) could solve this problem of human-data interaction. The affordances of IoT and sensing technologies that constantly record large quantities of data (e.g., big data) could emulate some of those processes at the level of syntax and associations. Similarly for using the standards of Semantic Web to describe and organise data on the web for better access and use of data, computer algorithms and AI could possibly emulate that and organise it for the people.

In early days, the AI development was built on the assumption that in order for computers to understand natural language they needed to understand grammar and semantics. That meant people were developing semantic, syntactic and pragmatic theories of language and implemented them into computer systems. So that the systems could use those rules of the grammar to parse the syntax and semantics of language, and work out what the pragmatics was. For example people implemented online lexicons such as WordNet\footnote{\url{https://wordnet.princeton.edu/citing-wordnet}} to advance their AI systems to understand context and pragmatics of language. However, with the constant flow of single sentences from people's personal devices such as Google Assistance, Amazon Alexa and Apple Siri, that are accompanied by contextual metadata (e.g., people, places and situations) has accumulated a massive dataset for training and improving responses of AI. Furthermore, the way that these technologies become more personalised (e.g., understanding unique voices and people) and becoming more aware of historical interactions between each individual, enables them to give information and answers unique to a person. This seamless feedback loop between individuals and the devices could potentially emulate the processes of inquiry and replace the work of data scientist (or the Semantic Web) making these connections. However, there is need for a deeper discussion around these technologies and whether they are actually helping people achieve control or giving it away to private hands.
