\begin{abstract}

We live in the age of data, where it is being collected and archived in tremendous volumes and at great velocity. Smart cities are a good example of how we generate and use this data with the aim of improving the lives of citizens. As cities adopt more technologies and embed them in the physical fabric of the city itself, it will drastically change the way decisions are made in the city, but also the way citizens interact with the city. Research to date has predominantly focused on engineering agendas or has had a narrow focus on the citizens' participation as passive producers of data in the smart city. This thesis takes a more holistic approach and focuses on both, simultaneously -- the engineering problem solving agenda and community problem solving activities. Taking a participatory research approach, this thesis explores this context through three case studies which involve the design, development and analysis of two Community Informatics (CI) systems. In addition to producing two open-source CI technologies (SenseMyStreet and Data:In Place) for active citizen participation, this research posits the Citizen Advocacy Framework (CAF) and Community-Data Interaction (CDI) model as novel theoretical framings, which enable researchers to discuss and design for effective use of data by communities. Furthermore, this thesis provides a practical example of the use of CDI for supporting communities taking local action. This improved understanding of the relationship between data and communities demonstrates a better direction for future research and design of CI technologies as they work towards democratising data science and enabling the effective use of data by communities for active civic participation, advocacy and action.

\end{abstract}
