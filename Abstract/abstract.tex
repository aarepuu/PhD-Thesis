\begin{abstract}
We live in the age of data where data is being collected 

Information and knowledge is a powerful thing

There is an emphasis on data-driven and evidence based decision making and knowledge from data can lead to better decisions and actions. Having that resource available for the public creates new opportunities for people to be more involved in creating the spaces they live in larger scale of civic participation

Smart cities are a good example of promotion and use of data for improving the lives of citizens. Data helps understand the impact of cities and complex interactions in it. Opening up that data has potential to innovate public services, increase transparency and empower citizens to take action. 
Efforts of open data movement permits citizen to access Open Governmental Data and other initiatives promote data publishing and openness around data
there's multiple initiatives from Open Data Chapters to ODI and 

in addition citizens accessing these data source they are becoming also the producers of the data through Citizen Science initiatives, helping engage with academic research.

Although there is a huge potential for data to be used for social good and in processes of place-making, community building and civic advocacy, is still being unlocked and there is lack of clear evidence of it having any meaningful or long lasting impact on governance or citizen participation in it. This thesis investigates the barriers for using data in civic advocacy and action, identifying issues with existing tools and models of participation and uses more inclusive and participatory ways for designing data-driven, people-centred technology that promotes sustained engagement around issues of local concern. Thesis contributes an understanding of effective use of data in civic participation and the exploration of how re-positioning the role of data in knowledge creation networks enabling it to provide measurable impacts for citizens. Taking participatory action research approach the thesis explores this context through two case studies which involves the design, development and analysis of two data systems, SenseMyStreet and Data:In Place. First case study presents design and deployment of SenseMyStreet, a commissioning toolkit that helps manage a common resource of environmental sensors for community use, for gathering evidence to inform and rise awareness around local issues, and to advocate for physical infrastructure changes. Second case study presents the design and development of Data:In Place, an open source web tool which supports citizens in accessing, interpreting and making sense of open data to support local decision-making and provide resource for action. Both technologies continue to exist and be used by communities, creating more resources for collective action. Drawing on these case studies and an extended analysis of data-driven participation in them, the thesis provides a framing for data in practice to support its effective use in civic participation and advocacy.

\end{abstract}
