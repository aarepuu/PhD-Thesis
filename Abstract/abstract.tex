\begin{abstract}
We live in the age of data, where it is being collected and archived in tremendous volumes and velocities. Historically data collections were mainly carried out by the state, for example to count the citizens for tax purposes or inform the government about eligible young men for military service. Nowadays new Information and Communication Technologies (ICTs) extend data generation abilities to other contexts. Smart cities are a good example of generation and use of data with an aim of improving the lives of citizen. In addition, the efforts of open data movement promoting publishing of data has enabled citizen to gain access to Open Governmental Data (OGD) and other sources of open data. People have theorised that opening up data for everyone has the potential to innovate public services, increase transparency and empower citizens to take action. Furthermore, some citizens are also becoming producers of data through engaging with academia and Citizen Science initiatives. While the technology to generate and store vast quantities of data is ubiquitous, what remains a significant challenge is how could communities leverage that data for activities of local benefit. Although there might be a huge potential for data to be used for social good and in processes of community decision-making, civic participation, advocacy and action; it is yet to be unlocked and there is lack of clear evidence of it having any meaningful or long lasting impact on governance or citizen participation in democracy. 

This thesis investigates the barriers of using data in civic advocacy and action, identifying issues with existing models of citizen participation through data, and uses a more inclusive and participatory ways for designing data-driven, people-centred technology that promotes sustained engagement and effective use of data by communities. The thesis contributes an understanding of effective use of data by communities for civic participation, advocacy and action, and the exploration of how re-positioning the role of data in knowledge creation networks enables them it to become useful for communities. Taking a participatory research approach the thesis explores this context through three case studies which involves the design, development and analysis of two Community Informatics (CI) systems. First case study presents the design and deployment of SenseMyStreet, a commissioning toolkit that helps manage a common resource of environmental sensors for gathering evidence to inform and raise awareness around local issues, and to advocate for physical infrastructure changes. Second case study presents the design and development of Data:In Place, an open source web platform which supports citizens in accessing, interpreting and making sense of OGD to support local decision-making and provide resource for action. Both technologies continue to exist and be used by communities, creating more resources for collective action. Drawing on these case studies a new model of Community-Data Interaction (CDI) is offered which enables researcher discuss and design for effective use of data by communities for civic participation, advocacy and action. Lastly, the third case study provides an example of practical use of CDI for supporting communities taking action.
