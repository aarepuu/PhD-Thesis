\begin{abstract}
We live in the age of data, where it is being generated, collected and archived in tremendous volumes and velocities. Historically data collections were carried out by the state, for example to count the citizens for tax purposes or inform the government about eligible young men for military service. However nowadays everyone is either generating data or is part of a data generation process run by others. Being able to transform data into information and knowledge is a powerful tool which can lead to better decisions and actions. Smart cities are a good example of promotion and use of data for improving the lives of citizens. The efforts of open data movement promoting data publishing and openness around data has enabled citizen to access Open Governmental Data and other sources of open data. Opening up data for everyone has potential to innovate public services, increase transparency and empower citizens to take action. In addition to citizens accessing data, they are also becoming the producers of data through engaging with academia and Citizen Science initiatives. While the technology to generate and store such vast quantities of data is ubiquitous, what remains a significant challenge is how to leverage that data for the public good.
Although there is a huge potential for data to be used for social good and in processes of place-making, community building and civic advocacy, is still unlocked and there is lack of clear evidence of it having any meaningful or long lasting impact on governance or citizen participation in it. This thesis investigates the barriers for using data in civic advocacy and action, identifying issues with existing tools and models of participation and uses more inclusive and participatory ways for designing data-driven, people-centred technology that promotes sustained engagement around issues of local concern. Thesis contributes an understanding of effective use of data in civic participation and the exploration of how re-positioning the role of data in knowledge creation networks enables it to provide measurable impacts for citizens. Taking participatory research approach, the thesis explores this context through two case studies which involves the design, development and analysis of two data systems, SenseMyStreet and Data:In Place. First case study presents design and deployment of SenseMyStreet, a commissioning toolkit that helps manage a common resource of environmental sensors for community use, for gathering evidence to inform and raise awareness around local issues, and to advocate for physical infrastructure changes. Second case study presents the design and development of Data:In Place, an open source web tool which supports citizens in accessing, interpreting and making sense of open data to support local decision-making and provide resource for action. Both technologies continue to exist and be used by communities, creating more resources for collective action. Drawing on these case studies and an extended analysis of data-driven participation in them, the thesis provides a framing for data in practice to support its effective use in civic participation and advocacy.

\end{abstract}
