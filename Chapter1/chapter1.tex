\epigraph{Thinking is hard. Words must make it as easy as possible.}{--- \textup{Juhan Liiv}}

This chapter outlines the concept of a smart city, which, through the use of information and communication technology (ICT) and data, is changing the way cities operate. Under this model of city governance, the people who inhabit these cities are often perceived as another passive source of data for measurement, control, optimisation and management at scale. The literature review in this chapter explores different modes of civic participation and examines what other roles citizens could have in the smart city. How do we move from smart cities to smart citizens? What is the role of active citizens and the role of data in supporting activities and engagement with decision-making that determines the future of their city? As the creation and use of data in civic life and within communities span a wide range of disciplines, the literature from technical fields such as pervasive computing and information engineering is overlapped with social science, HCI and CSCW. Drawing on a range of critiques, this chapter identifies criteria for a more active idea of the citizen as a user of data (i.e. an active or a smart citizen), which will be the main focus for the research presented in this thesis. Thought the literature review, the research gaps are identified, providing the basis for the thesis' case studies.

\section{Participation in the Smart City}
\label{smart-city}
\subsection{Defining the Smart City}
The vision of a smart city, where information technology is woven into the city infrastructure to help optimise resources and improve the lives of citizens \citep{chourabi_understanding_2012,ratti_city_2016}, has been the focus of heated debate over the past five years\footnote{\url{https://trends.google.com/trends/explore?date=all&q=smart\%20city}}. Perhaps considered fictional at the time, such ideas were echoed in concepts like the \textit{`third wave'} of computing or \textit{`ubiquitous computing'}, as envisioned by \citet{weiser_computer_1991,weiser_computer_1993} in the early 1990s. For many years now, commercial companies -- IBM, Cisco and Siemens, to name but a few -- have been `hooking up' the city with sensors and other information technologies in order to gather all the data about the city in the hopes of solving some of the old and new `wicked' problems of society. For example, intelligent street lighting is in place in many cities to improve efficiency and reduce energy costs; CCTV and traffic counters are used to manage the flow of traffic through traffic signals; and pedestrian counters are utilised to understand the flow of people through the city. In this regard, there seems to be a push for the use of data in all aspects of life in order to optimise and organise resources \citep{batty_big_2013,calvillo_energy_2016}, reduce our carbon footprint \citep{li_optimal_2015,pierangeli_health_2020,zubelzu_urban_2015} and improve sustainability \citep{vilajosana_bootstrapping_2013}, in addition to also facilitating novel modes of participation \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016}, democracy and social interaction \citep{ tan_social-network-sourced_2013,baack_datafication_2015}. Although there are plenty of `smart' applications available, discussion around what makes a city smart is still ongoing \citep{klein_smart_2008,foth_handbook_2009,nam_smart_2011,su_smart_2011,dameri_smart_2013}. However, with the increase of urbanisation and the expansion of cities, this trend will likely continue to increase, and more businesses are developing solutions to supposedly `\textit{compute away local problems}' \citep{townsend_smart_2013}.

Smart cities live off data -- traditionally acquired through a rigorous collection process by government (i.e. conducting censuses) Ð that is increasingly captured through the use of sensor networks located on lampposts, roadsides, trees and rooftops \citep{su_smart_2011,zanella_internet_2014} from humans inhabiting the city (e.g. mobile phone data, passive mobile positioning and `citizens as sensors') \citep{ahas_using_2010,rein_ahas_mobile_2010,berntzen_citizens_2018}, as well as from the web and social media platforms \citep{batty_pulse_2010, puiu_citypulse:_2016}. The vast amount of data accumulated through the smart city, coupled with ideas of `data-driven everything', has resulted in claims that cities are becoming `sentient' in terms of developing an awareness of everything that is going on in the city \citep{thrift_sentient_2014}. Furthermore, interest in data-driven city governance is growing as more technologies are being developed and deployed in the city. In turn, this requires a new skill set relating to \textit{urban informatics} \citep{thakuriah_introduction_2017} from not only city managers but also from private and non-profit organisations (NPOs) that work to improve the life of people in the city \citep{foth_handbook_2009,kontokosta_urban_2017}.

A popular way of showing and using all the accumulated data is through real-time \textit{`data dashboards'} \citep{kitchin_real-time_2014,kitchin_knowing_2015}. Almost all aspiring smart cities have at least one dashboard at hand to create and visualise statistical indicators, benchmark the city against others and drive city governance \citep{kitchin_knowing_2015}. According to \citet{kitchin_knowing_2015}, the utility of these dashboards can be separated into two broad categories, i.e. \textit{managerial} and \textit{contextual}, with the first more linked to the assessment and performance measurement of city governance and the second prioritising the use of indicators for informing policy. Developing statistical indicators and visualising them through dashboards is a necessary and valid approach in city management, especially when trying to condense all that messy data into useful chunks that can be represented and used by decision-makers. However, \citet[p. 24]{kitchin_knowing_2015} points out that these initiatives are often presented as a mirrored model, or an exact \textit{`digital twin`} \citep{bolton_gemini_2018}, of the city through numbers -- \textit{`visualised facts that reflect the truth about the world'}. Instead, what these dashboards actually are is a representation of abstractions that make up a view of the city. Unfortunately, in many cases, these dashboards are representations of the view of the city from the perspective of management and governance, which means that problems and data collection are framed by institutional actors to fit the governance processes of the city, often without engagement from the relevant publics \citep{asad_creating_2017}. Similar concerns are raised by \cite{greenfield_against_2013} and \cite{townsend_smart_2013}, who argue that the power of making educated decisions based on that data is currently in the hands of the big tech giants, whose software works in controlled centralised locations. In addition to concerns relating to transparency and ethics surrounding surveillance and control \citep{van_dijck_datafication_2014,tufekci_engineering_2014}, this lack of inclusivity in smart cities can lead to a narrow representation of views and social desires in governance \citep{tufekci_big_2014,mcmillan_data_2016}.

\subsection{Citizens in the Smart City}
In the smart city literature, the people inhabiting the city are referred to by a variety of generic terms, such as `subjects', `consumers', `users', `stakeholders' and `citizens'. In this thesis, the term \textit{`citizens'} is used for those who inhabit, work and play in cities.

\citet{berntzen_citizens_2018} argue that in addition to political participation -- direct and indirect participation in political decision-making -- the role of the citizen in the smart city is also to participate in helping solve problems and improve services in the city (also \citep{townsend_smart_2013}). However, this requires the city to have the necessary infrastructure and processes to support communication and multiple types of participation \citep{berntzen_regulatory_2010}. New technological infrastructure and sources of data definitely broaden channels of participation. In modern democratic societies, citizens should be actively participating in all matters of civic life \citep{chourabi_understanding_2012}, including political discourse \citep{hoffman_participation_2012}, through civic engagement activities run by the state \citep{berntzen_citizens_2018} as well as citizen initiatives. On the other hand, from the algorithmic smart city perspective, citizens can also be viewed as additional sources of data that could be tapped into to improve the efficiency and functionality of the smart city.

There is enormous potential for technology to enable new ways of participation, engagement and service delivery; however, this comes at a time when the UK, among other countries in the EU, is subjected to acute economic austerity, which has had a significant effect on local governance, resulting in public service delivery having severely suffered and in some cases even being outsourced or privatised. Many of the solutions provided by institutional actors are not relevant to the real issues in the community \citep{boehner_data_2016}, and people living in communities often have a much keener sense of what the real problems are \citep{disalvo_neighborhood_2008}.

With the age of the internet and information technologies becoming more accessible outside of institutional boundaries, digital civic approaches \citep{olivier_digital_2015} have been emerging from HCI-related disciplines, which promote active participation in civic life and engagement in service creation and delivery, with the hope of increasing civic professionalism \citep{boyte_boyte_2010} in society and increasing dialogue between citizens and the government \citet{boyte_everyday_2004}. Examples of these kinds of study areas are community commissioning \citep{taylor_supporting_2009,taylor_creating_2010,balaam_feedfinder:_2015,garbett_app_2016}, democratic discourse \citep{valkanova_myposition:_2014,crivellaro_contesting_2015,johnson_reflections_2016,johnson_community_2017}, situated voting \citep{taylor_viewpoint:_2012,behrens_smart_2014,vlachokyriakos_postervote:_2014,hespanhol_vote_2015,golsteijn_sens-us:_2016} and community infographics \citep{claes_street_2013,koeman_what_2014,lindley_surfacing_2017}. Common to all these studies is that they are moving out from the research facilities and laboratories to conduct investigations in the real world. \citet{rogers_interaction_2011} calls this approach research \textit{`in the wild'} -- carrying out in situ studies and, rather than conducting observations of existing practices, focusing on local issues and designing disruptive technologies to address the concerns and needs of a specific population. Depending on the configuration of participation \citep{vines_configuring_2013}, this could also mean including citizens as co-designers in the process of design.

Although these processes are often messy, unpredictable and uncontrollable, they provide a better insight and understanding of how technology should be designed and how it is used in the real world \citep{suchman_plans_1985,suchman_human-machine_2007}. However, these approaches often stay within HCI- and social science-related disciplines, meaning that the actual technology and systems implemented and supported in the smart city do not extend to community problem-solving activites \citep{stillman_community_2009}. As machines and algorithms are getting better at making decisions and acting on behalf of humans, gaining more `non-human agency', citizens cannot be disconnected from the decision-making processes that affect their lives \citep{graham_too-smart_2014}. Instead, these approaches should overlap with engineering, geography, planning and spacial modelling to tackle inequalities in the smart city\footnote{\url{https://www.turing.ac.uk/research/research-projects/spatial-inequality-and-smart-city}} and respond to the UN's 
Sustainable Development Goals\footnote{\url{https://sustainabledevelopment.un.org/?menu=1300}}.

Indeed, many have recognised \citep{saunders_rethinking_2015,rudolf_giffinge_smart_2007} that using technology for participation is the key to a successful model of a smart city. However, the type of participation enabled by the smart city is determined by how citizens can engage with technological solutions and what roles they take in the use, design, creation and ownership of these solutions.

\section{Data-Driven Participation}
\label{data-driven-participation}
Citizens are not only moving around in this sensed environment (i.e. smart city) but are also contributing some of the data to it by using smart devices in their pockets, i.e. smartphones, and other portable personal computing devices. \citet{townsend_smart_2013} calls them \textit{`smart-city construction kits'} or \textit{`digital Swiss Army knifes'}, which are increasingly becoming sources of data for studying human behaviour and platforms for new bottom-up civic movements \citep{obar_advocacy_2012,eltantawy_social_2011}.

However, not all participation is created equal. In the literature, two distinct modes of data-driven participation can be identified: \textit{passive} and \textit{active}. Passive participation refers to the use of some service or automated system (e.g. a data logger or a service on a personal computing device), or as the result of a `data exhaust', that sends data off to be analysed by algorithms implemented on automated systems. This means that citizens are contributing data either voluntarily by knowingly using a particular application or program or anonymously in the background by using some type of service on their computers or mobile devices. Passive participation is the classical model of smart city participation, where citizens are contributing another dataset for the smart city to use.

On the other hand, active participation is referred to in the literature as the type of participation where citizens take a more active role in data production, with the aim of helping to solve the city's problems. This can manifest itself, for example, in citizens actively contributing data by reporting issues and problems through their personal computing devices or using citizen science devices to collect data about issues concerning them. This model of data-driven participation turns people into data producers and in some cases consumers, which is a more active way of participating in the smart city. Furthermore, some citizens with skills could even become prosumers of data.

Both modes -- \textit{passive} and \textit{active} -- refer to the ways citizens supply data to the smart city and its algorithms, which in turn inform decision-making. However, there is another type of participation -- \textit{the active data user} -- that has generally been neglected by researchers, designers and city officials. This type of participation is the focus of the present thesis, i.e. participation where citizens can \textit{access}, \textit{create}, \textit{understand}, \textit{use} and \textit{make use} of data to actively participate in decision-making processes in the smart city.

\subsection{The Citizen as Passive Data User}
\label{sub:passive-user}
Historically, people were principally connecting by trade, post, telephone or face-to-face interactions. In the digital age, it is becoming more commonplace to use computerised systems and data to mediate communication of ideas and opinions. The internet enables us to establish enormously complex networks, connecting us with different people and helping to engage in numerous activities, both individual and collaborative. Although not always explicit, all participation in cyberspace is driven through data sharing and use, which can manifest itself, for example, in participation on social media platforms like Twitter and Facebook (FB) \citep{crivellaro_pool_2014}, using personal informatics tools\footnote{\url{http://www.personalinformatics.org}} for self-reflection or improvement \citep{li_stage-based_2010,epstein_personal_2015,epstein_lived_2015}, or engaging with collaborative platforms to work towards a common goal \citep{salovaara_collective_2006}. Popular examples of the latter are Wikipedia, a crowd-powered free encyclopedia, and OpenStreetMap, an open-source geographical database compiled by a community of volunteer mappers.

The availability of portable smart devices in almost every citizen's pocket is producing excellent sources of data for the algorithmic smart city to understand how humans use the city. For example, there is a growing body of research into the use of mobile phone call logs for developing near real-time models of spatial interactions \citep{ahas_using_2010,vanhoof_exploring_2017} and deriving early statistical indicators to optimise planning and transport \citep{sonntag_feasibility_2014}. Furthermore, usage of mobile phone data has also shown to be beneficial for developing epidemiological models \citep{finger_mobile_2016,panigutti_assessing_2017}. In addition to using passive mobile positioning, the `smart city' is tapping into the data generated by social media in order to gather opinions and model large-scale human behaviour. There is a growing body of literature around the usage of social media platforms to conduct large-scale content analysis for semantics \citep{sizov_geofolk_2010,golder_diurnal_2011,cranshaw_livehoods_2012, procter_reading_2013,bontcheva_making_2014}. Other applications include the use of social media for crisis and emergency watch \citep{kamel_boulos_crowdsourcing_2011,liu_crisis_2014}, developing predictive models for public health interventions \citep{chourabi_understanding_2012} and indicating political sentiment or even predicting the outcomes of political elections \citep{tumasjan_predicting_2010}. Social media can be a powerful tool for mediating participation, but it can also be used to influence the outcomes of democratic elections \citep{bond_61-million-person_2012}. Although data from this type of passive participation of citizens can be useful for the smart city, there is definitely a discussion to be had around the ethical and privacy concerns surrounding it \citep{lanier_who_2014,van_dijck_datafication_2014}.

In addition to using the `data exhaust' of people's phones or activities on social media, this type of passive data participation can also be voluntarily. Certainly, civic engagement is one domain where such technologies and methods are being introduced, with the aim of increasing participation in democracy through data. In particular, these new approaches have caught the interest of city planners, who see them as an excellent opportunity for large-scale participation through the use of data and pervasive technology, i.e. trying to harness the power of crowds through data science \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016}. In big geography and urban planning, this is often referred to as volunteered geographic information (VGI), which has been argued to increase participation and the representativness of data \citep{goodchild_citizens_2007}. Examples of these types of systems include projects like MetaPos\footnote{\url{https://www.gsa.europa.eu/r-d/gnss-project-portfolio}}, Habits\footnote{\url{https://habitsdata.org}} and Sunset\footnote{\url{http://sunset-project.eu}}, each of which has a mobile application that collects anonymous location data to be used in spatial modelling to help with the organisation of infrastructure and/or traffic in the city. Although these applications have elements of feedback in terms of people seeing their analysed mobility patterns or being able to share travel tips with others on social media, they are still contained within the frame of the research or agendas of city authorities. Furthermore, some of these agendas can also be linked to behavioural change (not necessarily bad), which is administered through incentives\footnote{\url{https://www.betterpoints.ltd}}. This \textit{`citizens as sensors'} \citep{berntzen_citizens_2018} form of participation is often preferred by corporation, busy citizens and city managers because of its low impact on citizens' everyday lives and the reduced workload on city officials to actively engage their citizenry \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016}.

There is a lot of potential for crowdsourced data collection approaches to broaden participation and create alternative sources of data for public use in multiple domains. \citet{salim_urban_2015} developed a taxonomy of interaction and participation to assess different types of urban computing solutions. Their paper surveyed a variety of applications in mobile crowdsensing, urban informatics, urban probes and interventions, and Internet of Things (IoT), categorising them in terms of \textit{`the technologies used, the level of participation they stimulate, the participation scale they support, the manipulation and effects mode they enable, [and] the interaction mode and scale they enable'} \citep[p. 44]{salim_urban_2015}. Although urban computing systems may vary in shapes and sizes, they all aim for maximum participation from the public. Through reflecting on projects, \citet{salim_urban_2015} compiled a list of strategies that put emphasis on identifying needs and tensions through active dialogue with stakeholders and providing open and accessible tools that help citizens construct their own rationales for participating. It seems that the key for successful engagement is not only to provide the best technology for participation (i.e. only working on engineering problem solving) but also to prioritise the social interactions surrounding it (i.e. work on community problem-solving activities).
There are new opportunities emerging around designing tools that successfully facilitate this kind of data collection, which tries to harness crowds and capitalise on the capabilities of big data. However, there is an ongoing discourse around the use of crowdsourced geospatial (not exclusively) data to mediate civic interaction, both in term of its validity for participation in democracy \citep{le_dantec_planning_2015} and as a tool for decision-making \citep{brabham_crowdsourcing_2009,misra_crowdsourcing_2014,tenney_data-driven_2016}. Caution needs to be taken not to fall into the same pitfalls by taking data at face value. As \cite{le_dantec_planning_2015} and \citet{kitchin_knowing_2015} observe, it is often appealing for city managers to base their decisions solely on what the numbers are showing and the maps are identifying rather than on engaging directly with citizens to also consider what the people on the ground are experiencing, including not considering that technology sometimes excludes groups of people who are struggling with low-tech literacy or just do not have the resources to access them. People need to be reminded that big data can only show the `what is' but cannot reveal the `how', `why' and, most importantly, `what is not' \citep{mayer-schonderger_big_2013}.

\subsection{The Citizen as Data Maker and Producer}
\label{sub:data-maker}
Active data-driven participation also leverages the capabilities of modern-day information technologies and the internet but also tries to actively involve and engage in the problem solving, design and reorganisation of urban environments. These active participation approaches, such as Public Participation Geographic Information Systems (PPGIS), are perceived to be more grassroots, enabling citizens to actively contribute to decision-making processes \citep{sieber_public_2006,jankowski_towards_2009}. Examples of such systems include FixMyStreet\footnote{\url{https://www.fixmystreet.com}}, popular in the UK, and its Norwegian clone FiksGataMi\footnote{\url{http://www.fiksgatami.no}}; SeeClickFix\footnote{\url{https://seeclickfix.com}}, PublicStuff\footnote{\url{http://www.publicstuff.com}} and Street Bump\footnote{\url{http://www.streetbump.org}} in the US. Although PPGIS, at its core, is a \textit{`bottom-up'} approach, its technological realisation is often more \textit{`top-down'}, serving governmental interests \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016}. However, the scope for these technologies more often originates from institutional actors who frame the issues from their perspectives. Although helpful for citizens, this can result in a technical solution that generates data specific to the formulation of governmental concerns. In addition, the `datasets' are usually collected by non-expert citizens without any formal training, thus resulting in questionable accuracy, which makes city managers sceptical of the value of the data and opinions coming from non-expert citizens \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016}. Furthermore, there are issues around the interpretation of citizen concerns from data to actual tangible solutions in the city and in sufficient feedback loops to measure the successes of those solutions.

\subsubsection{Citizen Sensing and Citizen Science}
\label{citizen-sensing}
In addition to sensors in the city, i.e. passive and active data-driven participation that supports the algorithmic smart city agenda, there is also citizen sensing, an approach that usually involves (albeit not exclusively) citizens using a sensing technology (or mobile phones) to voluntarily gather data. Citizen sensing is not exactly the same as crowdsourcing, where people are seen as workers or data collectors who complete tasks or collect data for the work `requester', often getting paid for their participation. Furthermore, citizen sensing is not necessarily exclusively linked to citizen science initiatives, in which non-scientist members of the public are recruited to take part in science projects, volunteering their time for data collection and/or analysis \citet{cohn_citizen_2008}. In the same way, simply taking part in a study -- for example, participating in a sleep study or filling in a survey Ð should not be confused with citizen science. In this sense, citizen sensing can be used to refer to any activity where citizens are engaging in voluntarily data collection using sensing technologies, and it can be linked, although not exclusively, to crowdsourcing, participating in a study or a citizen science project.

According to the definition of the network of the National Contact Points for Science with and for Society\footnote{\url{https://www.sisnetwork.eu/sis.net-project}}, citizen science projects are \textit{`projects where citizens contribute to actual science research'} \citep{eitzel_citizen_2017}, examples of which are BioBlitz, a biological surveying project, and British Bird Watching, a bird surveying project. It seems that citizen science has multiple definitions, and it is often down to the `scientists' to decide how to frame it; however, it is not often clear who `the scientist' is \citep{haklay_citizen_2013} in these projects. \citet[p.11]{haklay_citizen_2013} proposes a framework to classify citizen science projects that focuses on the level of engagement and involvement of participants in science activities (Figure \ref{fig:4levels}). This is somewhat similar to \citet{arnstein_ladder_1969}'s \textit{`ladder of participation'} (Figure \ref{fig:ladder}); however, instead of solely focusing on the power relationships between citizens and decision-makers on a fixed ladder, \citet{haklay_citizen_2013} also points out the processes of obtaining new understandings that foster curiosity, promote knowledge creation and are likely to move people up the ladder of participation. In addition to finding an appropriate and sustainable engagement level for the project, there is also the question of `who knows best?' Although there have been successful citizen science projects like Neighbourhood Nest Watch \citep{evans_neighborhood_2005} or volunteer computing projects like \href{climateprediction.net}{climateprediction.net} and Rosetta@home, there is ongoing debate about whether the `science' should be left to the `scientists' or whether we should fully accept citizen contributions to science activities \citep{cohn_citizen_2008,gabrys_just_2018}.

However, there can be other purposes for conducting citizen data collection than advancing science or providing data to the smart city, e.g. for self-knowledge and personalised health interventions \citep{swan_health_2012,huck_mapping_2014,larkin_towards_2017}, to raise public awareness around an issue \citep[ch. 16]{loreto_participatory_2017} \citep{kuznetsov_participatory_2010,hasenfratz_participatory_2012}, to evidence an issue in the community \citep{disalvo_local_2009} or to push for change \citep{balestrini_city_2017}. The latter approaches involve community-led investigations, whereby citizens are actively involved in the definition of the issue, as well as the collection or acquiring of data and its analysis. In relation to the typologies of the ladder of \citet{arnstein_ladder_1969} and the four levels of \citet{haklay_citizen_2013}, these approaches come at the very top in terms of the level of active engagement. \citet{balestrini_city_2017} examines this type of participation through a \textit{City-Commons Framework for Citizen Sensing} (Figure \ref{fig:city_commons}), which utilises a bottom-up approach to orchestrate citizen sensing and community engagement. The types of citizen sensing projects that explore issues related to the environment and place often involve some sort of sensing technologies \citep{thompson_crowd-sourced_2016} and PPGIS or mapping coupled with HCI for geospatial technologies, examples of which are Aircasting\footnote{\url{http://aircasting.org}} and SmartCitizen\footnote{\url{https://smartcitizen.me}} (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}), both of which consist of lightweight sensing equipment and GIS applications for exploring the data. Citizen sensing provides an opportunity to rethink the role of data in civic participation and focus on digital technology and mechanisms that could support such grassroots approaches. More projects like this are now being developed around the world, with community volunteers using sensing equipment and collecting data with the aim of promoting dialogue on environmental issues and getting closer to the next level of participation through data.

\subsection{The Citizen as an Active User of Data}
\label{sub:active-user}
Although citizens are actively involved in data production with active data-driven participation, and they are also more involved in problem definition with citizen sensing approaches, they are not necessarily using the data for activism or changing their communities. Another level (i.e. the third level) of data-driven participation is where citizens not only supply data, either passively or actively, but also have access to such data in a way that they can make use of it. This type of participation is referred to in this thesis as the citizen as \textit{active data user} -- equipped with the tools for accessing and understanding data and sensing technology to create and use data and knowledge so as promote dialogue on issues and push for positive change in the community. 

This shifts the focus from the smart city to smart citizens who are making the city smart. Active citizens not only produce data but also get access to the data and can even commission the kinds of data they need, opening up another level of data-driven participation and empowerment through data. The `new` smart city, which is creating data with the people for the people and enabling them to commission and use data, then has to think about what structural, digital and legal infrastructures are required to support these new forms of data use, which go beyond simply data consumption or enabling the smart city to consume it. This could be done by opening up processes of the smart city to citizens and moving away from the technocratic governance and corporatisation of city management \citep{kitchin_real-time_2014}. \cite{kitchin_knowing_2015} encourages smart city initiatives to openly recognise their flaws, uncertainty, messiness and levels of error and accept the fact that they do not reflect the world as it actually is but actively frame and produce the world through measuring and observations it. In this sense, there is no one true image of the city, and everything depends on the perspective of the observer. While there is great potential in using data in such contexts, and an active effort is being undertaken to integrate sensors into our spaces, meaning that a lot of technology is already in place, it has to be configured and shaped in a way that it serves the needs of citizens and not the technology itself.

\subsubsection{Civic Advocacy}
\label{civic-advocacy}
This type of stance is also activist in nature, promoting activities of civic advocacy and action. Engaging in practices linked to advocacy is not a new concept in democratic societies. For many people, lobbying and grassroots lobbying are considered the main forms of advocacy; however, advocacy is not limited only to lobbying. It utilises protesting, petitions, citizen campaigning and a variety of other information resources to influence decisions within the socioeconomic and political systems that shape the lives of people \citep{christoffel_public_2000} as legitimate means of democratic participation that should be part of a healthy society.
Advocacy efforts can come from individuals, but they are more likely to be associated with some sort of organisation or self-organised group \citep{leroux_nonprofits_2007,suarez_nonprofit_2009,prakash_advocacy_2010,obar_advocacy_2012}. Advocacy can also be categorised into two strategies: insider and outsider strategies \citep{asad_tap_2017}, with the former using more traditional approaches to directly affect policy systems and the latter focusing on informal processes by contributing resources or skills to organisations in order to help address issues \citep{hackler_strategic_2007,erete_storytelling_2016,marshall_accountable:_2016}. Additionally, the advocates are often not the ones with the personal attachments to the issues and are acting as proxies to voice community concerns \citep{leroux_nonprofits_2007,voida_bridging_2012,johnson_community_2017}.

ICT affordances and capabilities are enabling new forms of digital advocacy \citep{brady_utilizing_2015}, where people are taking advantage of digital tools and platforms to affect change in the community \citep{karpf_online_2010}, e.g. using platforms such as Change.org\footnote{\url{https://www.change.org}} and TheyWorkForYou\footnote{\url{https://www.theyworkforyou.com}} to digitalise traditional forms of advocacy (e.g. signing petitions and sending letters to representatives). Research in HCI has started looking into ways that technologies and data can facilitate these new roles of active citizen engagement. For example, \citet{garbett_app_2016} examined a model for the community commissioning of technologies to help communities gather data about local concerns; \citet{balestrini_beyond_2014} explored how low-cost sensing equipment can be used to empower citizens tackling specific sustainability issues; and \citep{kuznetsov_participatory_2010} looked at using low-cost sensors for measuring the outdoor air quality of diverse communities and explored the uses of data for public awareness and activism, in addition to projects where citizens used their mobile phones to collect data about transportation issues to advocate for changes in cycling infrastructure \citep{ dantec_data-based_2014,dantec_advocating_2016,maskell_spokespeople:_2018} or other types of sensor data to bring issues to the public attention and advocate for more accessible city infrastructure \citep{rodger_technology_2016}. All of these examples are part of the model of citizens as active data users. At the other end of the spectrum, citizens themselves have taken control and repurposed technologies to aid in their advocacy efforts. For example, we have already witnessed disruptive social movements through the use of social media platforms \citep{eltantawy_social_2011,howard_opening_2011} and smaller movements aimed at hyper-local concerns \citet{crivellaro_pool_2014} or the coordination of humanitarian action \citep{starbird_chatter_2010,starbird_voluntweeters:_2011}. A significant benefit of online tools is that they do not put geographical restrictions on participation, allowing for participation from other places and also enabling instant access to shared information.

Digital advocacy efforts are similar to the participation of citizens as data producers; however, data-driven advocacy \citep{gray_changing_2016,asad_tap_2017} is taking a more active stance, wherein citizens become the creators of data. While this also involves people using digital technologies to collect data, they then use that data to inform or evidence an issue or advocate for change. In addition, as a more active form of participation, it has greater emphasis on the relational interactions between civic stakeholders \citep{olivier_digital_2015,vlachokyriakos_digital_2016,asad_tap_2017}. Although there might be a multiplicity of stakeholders from different organisation involved, issues take precedence and different communities, or \textit{publics} (Section \ref{sub:com_net}), form around them. Projects like The Bristol Approach\footnote{\url{http://www.bristolapproach.org}} (Figure \ref{fig:city_commons}) provide examples of issue-led investigations where people often co-design the technologies used in advocating. Furthermore, it has been pointed out that the technology and how it is used in advocating for issues play an active role in forming these publics \citep{dantec_infrastructuring_2013,jenkins_object-oriented_2016}. When designing or repurposing digital tools for active citizen (i.e. active data user) engagement, there is an obligation from designers and developers to think about how they support different groups and promote civic participation and advocacy (i.e. infrastructuring) among all citizens \citep{asad_tap_2017}. It seems that the way forward is to open up and democratise data and technology that is already used in decision-making to help integrate a multiplicity of views and data into the smart city.
\newline

\noindent
In his book \textit{Smart Cities: Big Data, Civic Hackers, and the Quest for a New Utopia}, \citet{townsend_smart_2013} states that the answer to the question of what is the smartest city is \textit{`[...] the one you live in'}. People make the city smart and not the technology, although technology can certainly lend a helpful hand in defining and informing people of some of the issues in the city or helping to communicate ideas and knowledge. However, citizens also need to take a more active role in matters of local concern and actively engage in issue exploration and data production and analysis and use that to develop the smart city. In addition to being passive sources of data, the producers and users of data citizens are going to become commissioners of data. However, with these new modes of participating, it might change the nature of what we mean by data and how we use it in knowledge-making processes in such contexts. Hence, there is a need for a better understanding of the implications for data or for what we understand by data. Obviously, as a necessity, data needs to be open for anyone to use it, but the ways it needs to be open and what it needs to be doing for it to be used in a citizen-driven smarter city that support these new modes of participation need further exploration.

\section{Open Movements}
\label{sec:open-movements}
Just as all ideas and opinions were historically shared through face-to-face encounters, all data, information and knowledge was also exchanged orally and spread freely. However, with the rise of the printing press in the 1400s, knowledge started to be seen as individual rather than collective property. Soon, multiple copyright laws were put in place and enforced by governments across the world to protect the property rights of publishers. The coming of the internet, which made sharing and copying information even easier and broader, resulted in copyright laws becoming stricter and more unified around the globe. In response to the prevalence of copyright laws, a new political and social movement -- the \textit{Open Movement} -- was brought into being, which seeks to promote the sharing of content and resources for the good of society. In this regard, the idea of open movement operates in a spirit of transparency, collaboration, reuse and free access. It has been realised in some regards through specific groups, institutions and individuals around the world embracing the benefits of releasing digitised text, data and multi-media content online free of charge, often collaboratively, and free of most copyright and licensing restrictions. Open Movement is seen as an umbrella term that encompasses factors such as open access, open data, open government, open development, open science, open education, open source, open licensing and open content. However, open can mean many things, and what the research in this thesis is examining is how these movements are contributing to making data and knowledge open so as to support active citizen engagement in new modes of participation in the smart city.

\subsection{Open Knowledge}
\label{sub:open-knowledge}
Open movements aim to promote participatory processes through the sharing of data, knowledge and outputs as well as the source code of technical implementations. The openness of these projects and the resources they provide is not always defined the same way. Open data and knowledge is commonly defined by Open Definition\footnote{\url{http://opendefinition.org}} as follows: \textit{`Open data and content can be freely used, modified, and shared by anyone for any purpose'}. The most successful example of open knowledge sharing is Wikipedia, which was founded in 2001, the same year as Creative Commons, which aims to facilitate the sharing of creative works in the public domain and is used by Wikimedia to publish all of its content. Wikipedia is an online encyclopedia where anyone can become a contributor of content that is interlinked and publicly shared. According to internet rankings, it is in the top five most visited sites on the web\footnote{\url{https://www.alexa.com/topsites}}. Although just about anyone can edit and add content to Wikipedia, it has been highlighted that the shared information has value and quality like any other professional source of knowledge \citep{giles_internet_2005}. Other notable projects are Khan Academy, for publishing free educational resources, and the Open Knowledge Foundation, which promotes and shares free information. Open knowledge also extends to publishing scientific research, where people advocate for open access to publications, especially if the research is publicly funded \citep{garcia-penalvo_open_2010}.This also includes publishing of all the data and tools to reproduce the results in the benefit of public knowledge \citep{molloy_open_2011}.

\subsection{Open Source}
\label{sub:open-source}
However, with software, it is not always clear what is freely usable and to what extent there is a difference between `open' and `free' software. The difference between open and free is that open is software people can access the source code of, fork (i.e. download and replicate) and change to work within their contexts. The initial idea of \textit{open source} software was proposed by the members of the Free Software Foundation\footnote{\url{https://www.fsf.org/}}, who promoted sharing software and design documents for reuse and repurposing in other projects. Open-source software are published using open-source licences\footnote{\url{https://opensource.org/licenses}}, which dictates the specific rules for how people can use and modify the source code. Perhaps the most well known and successful open-source project is Linux, created by Linus Tonvalds in 1991, the kernel code for which has been used in multiple popular operating systems, including the UNIX-like GNU operating system initiated by Richard Stallman. Nowadays, sharing and collaborating on software development results in geographically dispersed programmers working together on projects through online collaboration interfaces \citep{west_contrasting_2005} such as GitHub\footnote{\url{https://github.com}}, Bitbuket\footnote{\url{https://bitbucket.org}} and SourceForge\footnote{\url{https://sourceforge.net/}}. Nowadays, models of free and open-source software are becoming more popular, to the point that even commercial companies are publishing their software on these platforms, potentially sharing their intellectual property with competitors \citep{west_challenges_2006}. This extends to the idea that there is `strength in numbers' or, as Linus's Law states (also proven by the development of Linux Kernel), \textit{`given enough eyeballs, all bugs are shallow'} \citep{raymond_cathedral_2001}, meaning that if there are more people working on a project, it will ultimately produce higher quality software. In addition to better quality software, people working on these projects have also reported that they are motivated by the participation and sense of belonging to a collective and the sharing of knowledge and skills \citep{lakhani_why_2003}. People working in the spirit of transparency and openness, sharing skills and knowledge on these new virtual platforms, enables trust and opens up new opportunities for these systems to promote new ways of using them for social good. Open-source software could be a beneficial resource to tap into in order to support active citizen engagement in the smart city; however, working with such software requires software development skills and knowledge.

\section{Open Data}
\label{sec:open-data}
Then, potentially the most transformative branch of the Open Movement is open data, i.e. data that \textit{`can be freely accessed, used, modified and shared by anyone for any purpose -- subject only, at most, to requirements to provide attribution and/or share-alike'}\footnote{\url{http://opendatahandbook.org/glossary/en/terms/open-data}}. As defined by Open Definition, it has to be open both legally (licensed under open licence) and technically (without attached costs) in bulk and machine-readable format. Among such data is OGD \citep{ubaldi_open_2013}, one branch of open government alongside other open governmental initiatives like Open Budgeting, data that is collected or whose collection is commissioned by governmental entities and then published using open licences. The main aims for publishing this data are to serve the transparency and accountability of government, unlock the potential of the social and commercial reuse of the data, and also to promote new ways of democratic participation \citep{jetzek_data-driven_2014}. Governments, including the UK's, are publishing data about land use, the environment, governmental services, citizenry and their health and public spending and expenditure. However, the breadth and depth of the data published differs from country to country and is also constantly changing as new legislation, data collection methods and rules are implemented. Open Movement advocates and organisations are keeping their hand on the pulse by releasing yearly reports about the current state of open governments, the most well known of which are Open Data Barometer\footnote{\url{https://opendatabarometer.org}}, published by the World Wide Web Foundation, and Global Open Data Index\footnote{\url{https://index.okfn.org/}}, published by Open Knowledge International, which aim to document outcomes, share ideas and knowledge, and spark discussion around open data in governments. Despite the popularity of the open data movement, there are still a lot of unanswered questions relating to what it means for active citizens wanting to participate in the smart city and how open data is serving the needs of new modes of participation.

\subsection{Finding and Accessing Open Data}
\label{sub:access-open-data}
One way to make data open is to make it available for others to use. The preferred method of making open data available is usually through open data portals. Leigh Dodds, chair of BathHacked\footnote{\url{https://www.bathhacked.org}} and Director of Advisory at the Open Data Institude\footnote{\url{https://theodi.org}} defines a most basic open data portal as \textit{`a list of datasets with pointers to how those datasets can be accessed'} \citep{dodds_what_2015}. While this definition can be seen as the \textit{minimum viable product (MVP)} for an open data portal, when a data portal needs to cater to a wide range of audiences \citep{davies_open_2010}, it is worth looking into a more sophisticated solution. The usual functions of open data portals can be categorised into two groups: (1) discoverability - functions that help index and organise the metadata in order to make data discoverable and (2) interoperability - functions that help interlink the data from different sources and make it programmatically accessible through an Application Programming Interface (API). Using these technologies, but not exclusively, governments have spin-off portals, such as \href{https://open.canada.ca}{open.canada.ca} and \href{https://data.gov.uk/}{data.gov.uk} for Canada and the UK, respectively, with many others countries following their footsteps (\href{https://opendatabarometer.org}{Open Data Barometer}), enabling people to search and download governmental open data. With the `father of the World Wide Web' Tim Berners-Lee, among others, calling for \textit{`raw data now'}, publishing open data has increased massively over the past ten years, and there are over 170 open data portals in the EU alone, according to the Open Data Monitor\footnote{\url{https://opendatamonitor.eu}}. Although an abundance of data has already been published, there are still datasets not being disclosed by governments. One way that citizens can 
gain access to such datasets, provided it does not violate any restrictions, is by going through a Freedom of Information (FOI) request. However, processes linked to submitting a FOI request can be complex, lengthy and hard to document for reuse. To address these issues, MySociety, a UK-based charity, developed a simple online tool called WhatDoTheyKnow\footnote{\url{https://www.whatdotheyknow.com}} to aid people in submitting FOI requests and sharing the process and results with everyone. Using this approach, people have made over half a million requests, resulting in new datasets being released on the web.
\begin{quote}
    \textit{`[,...] if people put data onto the web [,...] it will be used by other people to do wonderful things'} \citep{berners-lee_year_2010}.
\end{quote}
It is important that data is out there because it promotes transparency and encourages discussion, but it has turned into a `numbers game' with little context. In this regard, there is a lot of ambiguity surrounding what makes a government open and what the purposes of open govermental data are -- is it merely a political statement or a call for technologies that make that data usable \citep{renee_e._sieber_civic_2015}. People have critiqued that the \textit{`measure of openness'} for governments is flawed as it only takes into account the number datasets and repositories published online \citep{davies_open_2010} and that there are more nuances to the effective reuse of open data than simply the ability to access (while not even the case for everyone) the generated data files \citep{gurstein_effective_2003,gurstein_open_2011}. Similarly, Open Data Barometer's latest report points out that there is a lack of work being undertaken around inclusivity surrounding open data \citep{world_wide_web_foundation_open_2018}. 
Clearly, making data available is not the same as making it accessible and usable for everybody, especially for citizens who want to engage in active forms of civic participation through data. One of these necessities for what data needs to become in order to make this vision of fully engaged citizens or data engaged citizens a reality is to get data from \textit{available} to \textit{usable}. A similar point is made by \citet{gurstein_open_2011}, who proposes a model of the \textit{`effective use of data'}, as opposed to simply open data, that includes far more steps than just making data available in order to tackle the `digital divide' around data.

Open data from governments and private companies could potentially provide valuable input for citizens to leverage so as to participate in democracy and help improve their communities. Working with data, however, requires specialist know-how, tools and understanding of data science techniques -- skills that community groups and citizens often lack. Even with tools like WhatDoTheyKnow, which simplify requests for data, there may be problems with not knowing what data would be useful in particular contexts, especially when the request consists of having to explain what data one is looking for in a `free text' field, with limited guidance and ways for pulling in context. 

Perhaps the biggest misconception around open data is that it comes in a `raw' state. In fact, data is always \textit{`influenced by potential uses, expectations, contexts, and theoretical constructs'} \citep{tuomi_data_1999}. \citet{heidegger_what_1976} and \citet{polanyi_tacit_2009}, among other philosophers of knowledge, have criticised the empirical model of epistemology, arguing that an object's being and our interpretation of it exist in an entwinded state, meaning that we can not observe an object without it being part of its current interpretation. Hence, it is impossible for humans to take data as raw material and obtain knowledge from data through empirical observations. 

In the form that open data is made available to citizens, it does not fully lend itself to being used in supporting new forms of civic participation. Citizens are more likely to gain from data exploration tools that wrap data in some narrative that positions data in context and helps to tell a story. \cite{taylor_data--place:_2015} have emphasised the importance of data production and use being bounded to a place with geographical and social dimensions, giving it context and making it usable. There are already examples of systems such as MapIT\footnote{\url{https://mapit.mysociety.org}} and TheyWorkForYou\footnote{\url{https://www.theyworkforyou.com}}, which enable people to access open data linked to a specific postcode, or Know My Neighbourhood\footnote{\url{https://neighbourhood.knowmyarea.org}} and Local Insight\footnote{\url{https://local.communityinsight.org}}, which enable community organisations to link different open governmental datasets to an area of interest in order to aid local decision-making or improve service delivery (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}).
\newline

\noindent
Openly sharing data and knowledge, whether originating from official sources or being shared by Wikipedians on the web, takes it closer to becoming knowledge that is understood and accessible by everyone. In addition, if knowledge is shared, it can be questioned, confirmed or contested -- either through public critique or systematic reviews and meta-analyses -- ultimately leading to an understanding of different perspectives and a better quality of focal knowledge. Not openly sharing data and knowledge or deliberately making access obscure and difficult for reasons such as the data being \textit{too complex to understand} or \textit{too difficult to interpret}, or that it \textit{might be misunderstood}, are not valid reasons for not putting it out there. The issue is not that the data is complicated but that the presentation of data is complicated or missing crucial context to make sense of it, meaning that there is a representation issue rather than a data issue, which is more related to the information-systems problem-solving agenda; however, this can be approached from the community problem-solving angle \citep{stillman_community_2009}. Furthermore, if something is hidden from people, it cannot be discussed, and its representations cannot be worked on.

\subsection{Working with Open Data}
\label{sub:data-viz}
In order for people to start making sense of data and working with data it needs to be condensed into something that is comprehensible through human senses. This is where visual communication of data or data visualisation comes into play aiming to communicate information that data contains. There is often an assumption that data visualising is just reflecting the information that data holds. However, as there is no such thing as `raw data', there is also no such thing as direct or `raw visualisation'. Data visualisations, although based on data, are already analysed and edited forms of data projections which convey the message of the data analyst or an editor. The aim of data visualisation is to support the analytical process, formal procedure used to solve any type of problem, and help with information discovery and knowledge creation. That means it is also important to identify the type of audiences that will use the data representation, to better contextualise and present information aiding them in knowledge creation \citep{chi_operator_1998}.

People might theoretically have access to the same data, however not everyone has the know-how, skills and resources to turn data into something useful. Manipulation of data in a spreadsheet can be manageable for vast majority of people, however when data requires some cleaning, merging or reducing, more specialised tools such as Matlab\footnote{\url{https://www.mathworks.com/products/matlab.html}}, Python\footnote{\url{https://www.python.org}} or R\footnote{\url{https://www.r-project.org}} are needed. Although still considered a professional trade the advances in information technologies and the open source movement have produced multiple visualisation libraries such as Matplotlib\footnote{\url{https://matplotlib.org}}, ggplot2\footnote{\url{https://ggplot2.tidyverse.org}}, D3.js\footnote{\url{https://d3js.org}} enabling more people to experiment with data visualisation. However, data visualisation tools often require software development skills in order to manipulate the data and produce visual outputs. Software like Tableau and Gephi however are trying to break down those barriers and use graphical user interfaces (GUI) to help users visualise data. Advances in web technologies also permit data manipulation on web platforms in an easy `drag and drop' manner. Examples of such tools are Carto\footnote{\url{https://carto.com/}}, for manipulating geographic data and making interactive maps; Charted \citep{sall_using_2014} and RAWGraphs \citep{mauri_rawgraphs:_2017} for automatic chart creation from files, online spreadsheets, web APIs or other data sources, which can be easily shared with others. Although there are open source web tools for making graphs, people still have to choose the right visualisation for particular data to convey the information. DataVizProject\footnote{\url{https://datavizproject.com}} from Ferdio makes good headway towards helping people make the right choice for their data, by having simple visual representation of different types of charts and categorised by family, input, functionality and shape.

Even if data is made accessible, visible, and formatted nicely, or put it into `pretty' graphs, it does not necessarily mean that the average person (or citizen) can use it. Presenting data effectively requires both artistic and technical skills for identifying the message or insight it needs to convey and choosing the correct representation guided by visualisation techniques \citep{chi_taxonomy_2000}. Working with data is a complex task requiring both technical and cognitive skills to that people spend years training to achieve. Working on asking the right questions; figuring out where to look for data and how to access it; improving their visualisation craft; experimenting and figuring out the best practices for different types of data in that suit different contexts; ultimately becoming professional data analysts and visualisers. Sites like InformationIsBeautiful\footnote{\url{https://informationisbeautiful.net}}, Visualising Data\footnote{\url{http://www.visualisingdata.com/}} and Flowing Data\footnote{\url{https://flowingdata.com}} are good examples of people achieving that level of professionalism. Furthermore the data in question, the data that smart city uses and the citizens produce, is data at scale and complexity (i.e., big data), involving quite complex constructs such as demographics, geospacial patterns, trends etc., and is inherently complex to process, analyse and to understand, which makes it far more challenging to make it work for ordinary citizens.
\newline

\noindent
Indeed, it appears at the moment that citizens need these professional analysts or information engineers to mediate these processes that make data work for citizens actively participating through data. Going forward, there needs to be a better understanding established of the role of professional data analyst in these processes. Furthermore, perhaps there is a process that makes citizens smarter and able to participate directly in decision making that involves data at the city, country or even global level. To make it truly democratic, the challenge is in how can data science skills become transferable to ordinary citizens to do it for themselves, or built into information systems that can automate those processes for them. Whether or not the professional expertise could be replaced, or perhaps the solution is a mix of all three things: professional, automation, and smarter citizens. These are the challenges that the research in this thesis is looking to explore and address.

\subsection{Stakeholder Groups}
\label{sub:stakeholders}
From smart city management to citizen sensing, data is the epicentre of our worlds, communities, and research. Despite the pervasiveness of the term, there is still little critical understanding of what data might mean for different people and in different contexts (e.g., smart cities, business, research, community). Whether the term might be used to refer to `big data' analytics and statistics, or to the much broader collection of diverse ‘things’ like images, texts, audio etc. Interpretations of data and the meaning people draw from it is intrinsically liked to the motivations, contexts and situations that people find themselves in. Such diversity and ambiguity raises tensions particularly in contexts in which professionals and non-professionals are required to collaborate around data to communicate ideas and produce knowledge. From a Foucauldian perspective there is need to identify a common language, understandings and representations of data to promote discourse \citep{foucault_archaeology_1982,foucault_subject_1982}. That could aid people in knowledge production and help balance the power relationships between citizens and decision-makers, moving towards citizens becoming active users of data and part of the decision-making processes in the smart city.

Understanding relationships and the roles in this network helps identify barriers and opportunities to reconfigure these roles, and to make data more inclusive by increase participation through use of it with an aiming to support the vision of citizens as active users of data.


\subsubsection{Government}
From ancient Egypt and China to modern day countries, governments have held censuses to gather information about their citizenry to inform decisions and understand or avoid risks. Historically censuses were carried out to count the citizens to tax and inform the government about eligible young men for military service. After collecting the data, it took about a year to add it up and provide the statistics. Although the techniques have changed and efficiency has increase when moving to the digital age of computers, the aims of a census have remained pretty much the same. Governments are well established data collectors and the processes in place for polling their citizens are perfected over the years. As mentioned before, opening up that data is becoming a common practice for governments (local and national) and is being integrated into the way they operate. Additionally to data generated through censuses, governments are also releasing data about how they operate and spend public money \citep{marshall_accountable:_2016}. Introducing open data initiatives puts pressure on governments to `reform' current processes of governance to guarantee the continuance of these initiatives as public services go through a digital transformation. Data portals, currently being the main channels for publishing OGD are still supply-driven and lack engagement and tools for effective use of data to help reach all the stakeholder groups \citep[sec. 3]{davies_state_2019}.

\subsubsection{Business/Private Sector}
Data is probably the biggest business for the private sector companies operating in ICT. In addition to the data that private sector companies generate themselves, having the tools and the know-how to tap in to open data, they are also benefiting from data published by other institution. It has been suggested that open data has the potential to help generate \$3 to \$5 trillion of economic value annually in the U.S\footnote{\url{https://www.mckinsey.com/business-functions/mckinsey-digital/our-insights/open-data-unlocking-innovation-and-performance-with-liquid-information}}. This huge potential for economic growth has also become a driver, alongside transparency and accountability, for some governments to open up their datasets for re-use. The private sector has been an avid user of open data for years now and has shown its value to help understand demand, use it in strategic planning and produce useful products and services (Open Data 500\footnote{\url{https://www.opendata500.com}} and Open Data Impact Map\footnote{\url{https://opendataimpactmap.org}}). The value of open data for the private sector is clearly illustrated by the Open Data Impact Map, which provide a search-able catalogue of open data project, where two-thirds of organisations on that list are for-profit companies \citep{gurin_open_2019}.

In addition to private sector leveraging open data to improve their products and services, there are number of companies have also become `data intermediaries' \citep{gurin_open_2019} by helping governments publish their data or repackaging existing data for public and business use. There are companies who are providing data portal software for governmental and other institutions (Section \ref{sub:access-open-data}) based on data-as-a-service model to help publish their data, but recently there are more companies coming out with data-broker tools catering for consumers and the general public. Tools such as LG Inform Plus\footnote{\url{https://about.esd.org.uk}} from the Local Government Association which combine multiple sources of open data and make them available through Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) and file downloads. There are also companies who package data and try visualise data better by using GIS, reporting and graphs. These include companies like OCSI and HACT who provide software for understanding trends in housing market\footnote{\url{https://ocsi.uk/community-insight}} through OGD, Porism\footnote{\url{https://porism.com}} who package OGD for informing neighbourhood plans and MySociety\footnote{\url{https://www.mysociety.org}} who has developed multiple tools for making open data more accessible and promoting new routes for democratic participation from the public.

Although private sector are mainly data users and generators, they are also increasingly becoming data publishers by opening up anonymised user datasets or accumulated data sources for researchers and developers alike. Companies like AirBnB, Uber, Strava and Zoopla have recognised the potential benefits of opening up data and have published some of their data in order to improve the quality or find new business value for their data, but also to promote collaborations to help understand and tackle some societal problems. This is enabling companies to collaborate with one and other and with research facilities. GovLab’s Data Collaboratives Explorer\footnote{\url{http://datacollaboratives.org/explorer.html}} categorises data collaboratives into six types: \textit{Trusted Intermediaries}, \textit{Prizes and Challenges}, \textit{Research Partnership}, \textit{Intelligence Products}, \textit{API}, \textit{Data Pooling}. The Alan Turing Institute\footnote{\url{https://www.turing.ac.uk}} in London is a good example of such collaborative approaches where often resources are shared between private companies, governments and research. Another example of this is Positium LBS\footnote{\url{https://www.positium.com}}, a university spin off company which collaborates with telecommunication companies, government and research facilities to provide statistical indicators for public institutions about the population derived from big mobile data.

\subsubsection{Journalists/Media}
The role of a journalists have always been to keep the public informed about governments accountability, business developments and academic and scientific research. In the digital era, \textit{Data-Driven Journalism} \citep{ausserhofer_datafication_2017} is becoming a term that is widely recognised as part of journalistic agenda which uses data as a resource to investigate and/or shed light on matters of public interest. Becoming an effective journalist nowadays requires the skills of a data scientist, which has resulted in traditional media outlets dying out and being replaced with online ones. Computer-assisted reporting is becoming mainstream in journalism and major news outlets have their dedicated data visualisation and data science departments solely focused on storytelling with data (New York Times, Guardian). These new `programmer-journalists' \citep{parasie_data-driven_2013} are equipped with new skills and tools to obtain, analyse, visualise and even open up some of the data for citizens. Like initiatives such as Fatal Force where journalists have created a database of unreported killings by the police to highlight racial injustices or making governments responsible by releasing data about chemical attacks and human rights violations in Syria\footnote{\url{https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2018/04/13/world/middleeast/syria-chemical-attacks-maps-history.html?mtrref=www.nytimes.com}}.

Through automated tools developed by `programmer-journalist', who are often also part of open movements, some of that data can be potentially reported automatically when new data is collected or released, making way to `automated journalism' \citep[ch. 27]{davies_state_2019}. The interdisciplinary nature of data journalism promotes collaborations between different people and organisations to help address social issues. However it has been reported that the working groups are usually small and enclosed, focusing on providing descriptive statistics or visualisations to exploratory questions \citep{choi_characteristics_2017}. Also the majority of the stories in data journalism are focusing on political rather than social issues and mainly using the existing open data \citep{loosen_data-driven_2017}. With the increasing popularity of big social media platforms becoming the new news outlets and politicians constantly calling `fake news' \citep{carson_fake_2017} it is even harder to spark up collaborative efforts between diverse groups of journalist, data scientists and communities of practice to use data for social good.

\subsubsection{Multilateral Organisations}
Multilateral organisations \citep[ch. 28]{davies_open_2010} like the United Nations (UN), World Bank, World Trade Organization and the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD) have a role to play as promoters of open data and intermediaries to connect different stakeholders to work together and use data to improve peoples lives. Having the reach across different countries and continents, these organisations have also a key role to play in disseminating knowledge through consortiums and conferences. However a critical lens need to be used to assess whether the successes of open government and open data can be applied in developing countries to achieve the desired social impact \citep{gigler_realizing_2011}. Additionally these organisations also publish data about world economics \footnote{\url{https://data.worldbank.org/}} and demographics \footnote{\url{http://data.un.org/}}. Multilaterals often have the ability to tap into resources that are far greater than any one government and use new techniques and methods to require, process and report on data. In addition to the world organisations there are smaller non profits who promote and use data, aiming to create better standards and a more cohesive data ecosystem. Bodies such as the Open Data Institute who helps develop and promote open data tools and initiatives, and Open Knowledge International who are advocating for knowledge sharing, are also responsible for keeping their finger on the pulse of the developments around OGD through publishing Global Open Data Index reports \footnote{\url{https://index.okfn.org}}.

\subsubsection{Civil Society}
Civil society -- community organisations or groups of citizens with common interests -- who often mediates data to the rest of the community is perhaps the most important link in realising the effective use of data within communities. Civil society is predominantly a data user, through conducting research with it and often publishing its findings to shed light on different matters. Although citizens are mainly data generators, there is a particular set of enthusiast among the civil society often called data activists, `hacktivists' or civic hackers, who use civic data to surface concerns and contest decisions through enquiries and visualisations of data \citep{schrock_civic_2016,taylor_hackers_2005}. They often take strong political statements and act as watchdogs for governments and big corporations. In addition to individuals there are also groups of people who operate through Meetups\footnote{\url{https://www.meetup.com}} and collaborative projects. They consist of mostly professionals working with data or researchers who want to contribute their time and skills to for the good of society. Examples of such groups are Open Oakland\footnote{\url{https://openoakland.org}} in San Francisco, US and NE Data\footnote{\url{https://www.nedata.uk/}} in Newcastle, UK. There are also networks such as OpenGov\footnote{\url{https://www.opengovernment.org.uk/}} in UK that combine active citizens and civil society organisations to help make government work better for people through transparency, participation and accountability.

However when it comes to pushing the open data agenda civil society has far more obligations to uphold. In addition to being the bridge between the governmental institutions and community groups, it also helps raise awareness around open data, set the standards and course for data publishing, consult and train people on how access and use open data, publish tools to realise open data needs and advocate for new datasets to be released as open data \citep[ch. 24]{davies_open_2010}. Members of the civil society often even help gather data and openly publish it to promote advocacy and push for change. Being the driving force behind pushing the use of open data for social good, civil society has the biggest responsibility to citizens but also the most challenging role to play. However there is a not always clear where the civil society ends and other stakeholders begin, clear there is an overlap with the research community, data donors and the private sector companies \citep[sec. 3]{davies_open_2010}.

Although civil society is taken as this big force that can achieve anything it sets out, the reality is that it often does not possess the resources it needs to help communities with their data agendas, especially smaller groups who are struggling with lack of financial support or other resources to further their cause. Problematic also is the unwelcome competition between civil society groups trying to do the same thing and competing for the same pots of funding \citep{johnson_neighbourhood_2018}. This also makes it harder for the funding bodies to figure out which one of them would potentially have the greatest impact on the society, ending up often un-evenly or unfairly distributing the resources. At the same the gap widens between the people who are already engaged and making use of data, and people who might be gain from it the most but do not have any routes to engagement. Instead of competing, non-profits could collaborate with each other, be more transparent and use data and technology to secure more funding \citep{goecks_charitable_2008,johnson_neighbourhood_2018}. However great the potential of data to aid community organisations in their agendas is, it is still yet to be unlocked by everyone.

\subsubsection{(Academic) Research}
Researchers -- from academic, policy and non-profit -- have been engaging with open data from its early days of existence. Academic research has been turning to open data as a resource to understand populations (using data from censuses) and improve scientific value of the research, but also to study the impacts of open data for society \citep{hossain_state---art_2016,van_schalkwyk_state_2017}. Furthermore, initiatives like ODDC and Open Data Research Symposium are dedicated to looking at open data research and the impact of it. However, the research is still heavily focused on the measurable things in open data, quantity of published datasets and how it is made available, often failing to look at the social impacts of data in research. \citet{davies_state_2019} stress that researchers need to go further to make sure that the work they do is relevant to communities who have been identified as potential beneficiaries of open data.

Fortunately, more and more researchers are engaging in not only looking at the impacts of open data to communities, but trying to help communities use open data to instrument social change. Becoming these data professionals who are trying to make the vision of actively engaged or citizens as active users of data into reality. There has been individual research agendas which seek to understand the current open data practices amongst NGOs and design new tools that would unlock open data for these organisations \citep{erete_storytelling_2016,marshall_accountable:_2016}, but often driven through bigger initiatives that pair up researchers with civil society groups or local and transnational NGOs. These initiatives that are operating under the banner of \textit{``Data Science for Social Good''} are usually part of a universities or research facilities who run fellowships and mentoring programs to aid community organisations harness the power of data science on open data. Examples of such are Data Science for Social Good Foundation\footnote{\url{https://dssg.uchicago.edu}} and Turing's DSSG program\footnote{\url{https://www.turing.ac.uk/collaborate-turing/data-science-social-good}}. In addition there are also volunteering initiatives such as DataKind for experts who want to contribute their skills for common good of humanity.

Researchers are also publishing data accumulated through their research projects as Open Datasets. For example the the National Urban Observatory facility which combines six urban observatories in the UK under one umbrella, have accumulated and openly published over a billion observation about the city environment. Furthermore, researchers are also helping citizens collect data through citizen sensing \citep{balestrini_beyond_2014}, new media and content creation tools \citep{bartindale_our_2019,schofield_bootlegger_2015,taylor_creating_2010,crivellaro_re-making_2016} and community commissioning technology \citep{garbett_app_2016}. All of these activities are contributing to the vision of citizens as active data users engaged in production and use of data. Although researchers often work alongside communities and citizens to develop tools for access, use and production of data, the solutions they offer are often highly dependent on the researchers; hence lacking longevity and sustainability. The challenge here is to explore models that researchers could apply to support citizens actively participating in the smart city in a long term basis.

\subsubsection{Citizens}
\label{sub:stake_citizens}
\citet{davies_state_2019} did not include citizens as one of the stakeholder (or it was meshed together with civil society) in the open data ecosystem. Perhaps the hope is that other stakeholders will do the work for the citizens and towards unlocking potential of open data for everyone. However for focus of the research in this thesis \textit{citizens} as stakeholders deserve particular attention. The aim of this thesis is to explore ways to support citizens becoming more active participants in the smart city using data -- become smart citizens.

There is often an assumption that opening up data makes it accessible for everyone \citep{berners-lee_year_2010}, however most of the population does not possess the skills and tools to access and use the data. In most cases citizens are actually generating all those data or data are generated about them, without them having any say how and what those data should be concerning (Section \ref{sub:passive-user}). However, that is a rather impoverished notion of the citizens who live in cities, we should think about citizens of how they might be more actively engaged in decision making. How they might be more than sources of information for the smart city, how they can be witting makers, producers and consumers of data. Furthermore, they might even want to use data for their own civic purposes, and they might even want to commission data from other people that they currently cannot get access to. Some people strongly believe that open data is not for the public and \textit{``raw data is for people who know how to use raw data''} \citep{whong_thoughts_2015}. Although as mentioned before (Section \ref{sub:access-open-data}) and a view shared by others \citep{gitelman_raw_2013,tuomi_data_1999}, data is never raw, it always represents the bias of the collector and the observer. It is not contradicting the idea of open data is not for the public per-se, it is just highlighting that the current representation of data -- whether it is coined as `raw data' or `open data' -- is not accessible and usable in this form to everyone. Data needs to go from open $\rightarrow$ accessible $\rightarrow$ comprehensible $\rightarrow$ usable, to be used in active citizen engagement. There is no one size fits all solutions or representation format of data. When designing platforms to support communities and institutions working together with data to address problems in society, significant challenges -- both technical and social -- arise around translation and representations of data that have not yet been addressed.
\newline

\noindent
Considerations of what data is valuable to whom and in what form needs to be taken into account to better support communities collaborating around data. There is an obvious lack of resources in terms of funding and access to tools, however if there are existing competencies in the community it needs to be linked up through developing discourse. Because essentially discourse is about about production of knowledge through language, and through sharing of practices \citep{foucault_archaeology_1982}. Developing discourse and relationships between stakeholders has the potential to increase the social capital within the community. In the time of austerity \citep{lowndes_local_2012} there is a lot relaying on local initiatives and the availability of social capital. This also includes human capital \citep{coleman_social_1988} - combined knowledge and skills of individuals in the community - which is perhaps the most important resource for building up community resilience. Exploring relationships and roles of stakeholders surrounding maintaining, accumulating, sharing and using social capital, or as \citep{granovetter_strength_1983} puts it \textit{bonding}, \textit{bridging} and \textit{linking}, could help understand the important variables that contribute to successful active citizen participation and action. There is empirical evidence that social capital is far more effective at time of crisis \citep{morton_community_2013,aldrich_social_2015} than outside support. Enabling to establish connections and increase shared knowledge, local organisations and citizen groups are able to respond quicker and adapt to changing situations. However this does not only apply when disaster strikes, at the time of economic disparity, strong knowledge society and social capital are key for tackling inequalities \citep[ch. 7]{lin_social_2004} and moving towards the active citizen participation in the smart city. There is a need for a better understanding of the impacts for the different actors involved and the social capital surrounding the active use of data by citizens. This is also related to the role of expert professionals and looking at the ways their expertise can be replaced: either handed down to the citizens themselves, or can it be pushed into smart interfaces that allow the do the intelligent processing for them, or finding a middle ground or balance between those two things. More work is needed to understand how much can be done by computers and information systems, and how much training of citizens is required.

\section{Knowledge Society}
\label{knowledge-society}

\subsection{Communities, Publics and Networks}
\label{sub:com_net}
The usage of social capital -- the exchange and sharing of skills and other resources in order to produce knowledge \citet{coleman_social_1988} -- is usually contained within the a specific group or community that share an interest, issue or are working towards a common goal. In the literature, may have identified these communities differently and there is a bit of confusion around it. \citet[Orig. 1927]{dewey_public_1954} refers to these communities as \textit{publics}, and breaks that down by saying that the reason why publics form is because individuals are all affected in some way by an issue or social condition which makes them come together. However, these people might be complete strangers and they also might be affected in different ways by an issue. Building on Dewey and on \citet{warner_publics_2005} (`publics' and 'counterpublics'), more modern takes of publics have manifested such as \citet{mccarthy_taking_2015}, and \citet{le_dantec_designing_2016}. For \citet{le_dantec_designing_2016} the design of publics are looked from a point of attachments to issues and linked to infrastructures \citep[ch. 2]{le_dantec_designing_2016}. \citet{mccarthy_taking_2015} conception of publics is more linked to \textit{media publics} -- strangers who come together and share things through different medias and do it because they got a common interest, although they are quite diverse and different people. \textit{Community of Practice} (CoP) however is a group of people who share a common set of ways of doing things, common practices and a common set of values. They might have different levels and types of capability and capacity, but they all working together to address and achieve common goals. According to \citet{lave_situated_1991}, the important structural elements of CoPs are the \textit{domain} of knowledge, the notion of a \textit{community} and its \textit{practice}. Domain is some form of common denominator that defines a group of people, notion of a community are the connections and interactions that individuals share in the domain and the practice is the focus and activities that engage in to produce, share and maintain the knowledge. A classic example of a CoP are healthcare professionals -- hospital staff, nurses, doctors etc -- who aim to work together to deliver a good healthcare service to people. On the other hand \textit{Community of interest} can simply be referred to as individuals who have a common interest in something. They can be however completely different people from different backgrounds and do not have to share any common practices. In some cases they do not even have to practice the thing they are interested in.

In the case of the research conducted in this thesis, the groups of people, \textit{`communities or publics'} are referred to as individuals who are connected by the proximity, interest, issue or any combination of that. This can be individuals living in close proximity working together to improve the neighbourhood and tackle local issues, but can also be a virtual community \citep{rheingold_virtual_2000} that is present on online platforms such as Facebook, Reddit, GitHub or Quora or mediated through some other medium \citep{disalvo_neighborhood_2008}. However, one could say that it can also be a combination of publics and CoPs, because we have citizens, data professionals, charity organisations, activists, people with different skill-sets and capabilities that can be utilised and used as a resource, all working together to make use of data for civic participation. This combination or mix of different publics and CoPs is actually forming \textit{Community Networks}. The emphasis here is on the use of technologies and information systems to leverage data for community problem solving. It aligns with ideas of \citet{carroll_trajectory_2003} and has the characteristics of \citet[ch. 2]{clement_connecting_2012}'s proposed \textit{networked communities}. Also similar concept echo from \citet{varnelis_networked_2008} where they use the term \textit{networked publics} to refer to how individuals and groups engage with each-other and communicate ideas and knowledge using the capabilities of modern ICT and media. The use of publics here emphases a more engaged population, as opposed to passive and consumptive. \cite{varnelis_networked_2008} also points out that networked publics are far more complex in the way people are using capabilities of ICT to communicate ideas, produce knowledge and take action. Furthermore, these networks are \textit{``bottom-up, top-down, as well as side-to-side''} \citep[p. 3]{varnelis_networked_2008}, bridging together public, professional, institutional and personal.

\subsection{Community Informatics and Information Systems}
\label{sub:ci_is}
Information systems (IS) and information system design (ISD) have strong theoretical and technical background more often rooted in engineering and computing and with a strong focus to business and commercial endeavours. The aim for ISD is to build a best solution that solves an engineering problem and results in an artefact -- an information system. So the focus in highly on the design of the artefact to address that engineering issue. Other issues, also pointed out by many researchers \citep{carroll_participatory_2007,stillman_community_2009,hirschheim_exploring_1996,leonardi_materiality_2008,hakken_social_2003}, such as user groups, interfaces, usability, contexts, choice of technology, indented uses; become unimportant without the artefact. These issues however relate to the social aspects of IS which scholars involved in the ISD started calling \textit{social informatics} (SI) \citep{bowker_social_1997,kling_learning_2000}. SI puts the users of the system in the centre of the design processes of information systems and looks at how to address those non-engineering problems in an organisational context. For \citet{lamb_reconceptualizing_2003} it is important that we re-conceptualise `users as social actors' in these contexts.

However, working with communities the main aim is to address the issues that people have or the things they want to change in the community rather than the problems of technology. Similarly they are not bounded to an organisational structures and uses of particular technologies is a specific context. As a result scholars such as \citep{hakken_social_2003}, \citet{bradley_social_2006}, \citet{gurstein_introduction_2000} and \citet{carroll_participatory_2007} have expanded on these ideas of SI to go beyond the context of organisations and users. A new field of \textit{community informatics} (CI) \citet{gurstein_introduction_2000} has emerged that is focused on the design and implementation of technologies that help local communities address their issues. Later definitions of CI have also taken account other fields that influence the CI in practice and expanded to things like equal opportunities; recognition of importance of ``lived physical community''; user-focused underestanding of ICT; applied social leadership entrepreneurship and creativity \citep[p. 12]{gurstein_what_2007}. The overarching aim of CI is to overcome the `digital divides' through the design and implementation of ICT that enable and empower citizens.

If the focus is on the uses of data and information system, CI has actually a dual focus: \textit{``first, the conduct of research about the relationship between the design of ICTs and local communities, and second, the implementation of ICT projects in local communities''} \citep[p. 255]{stillman_community_2009}. Pointing out the still present engineering agenda or \textit{information systems problem solving} on top of the \textit{community problem solving} one. In the critical approach to IS provided by \citet{stillman_community_2009} they state that despite the differences of IS and CI, it can still be looked at a part of ``fragmented adhocracy'' of IS research, helping to provide CI a theoretical basis. They draw on \citet[p. 17]{hirschheim_exploring_1996}'s federated framework for ISD, and state that it can be adopted to CI. The fact that \citet{hirschheim_exploring_1996} used social action theory and change as lens to analyse the framework makes it more relevant to CI \citep{stillman_community_2009}. Figure \ref{fig:object_system_classes} shows the original and the adapted object system classes framework. It is presented as a two axis matrix where one axis shows the \textit{domains of change}, the objects that are changed by ISD; and the other axis presents the orientations that show the purpose of change instrumented by ISD. Importantly, the framework also distinguishes between two control objects -- humans and artefacts. However, there are two issues with this framework from the perspective of CI: firstly, its one dimensional perspective, also noted by \citet{stillman_community_2009}, that lends itself to have repetition and overlap in concepts across domains; and secondly, the role of technology only being utilised and being prevalent at instrumentation of control. To address this literal gap in the framework and to contribute to the field of CI, this thesis presents research that explores the role of data and technology in all the orientations of the framework and towards active citizen engagement, participation and instrumentation of change.
\newline

\noindent
Although there have been work done exploring technologies and mechanisms for citizens as active data users, they are limited to working on one aspect of interactions with data (e.g., production, access, use) and not taking a holistic approach needed \citep[cp. 2]{clement_connecting_2012} to address the dual focus of this research \citep{stillman_community_2009}. Additionally, the research done, has not looked at it thought the lens of digital civics \citep{olivier_digital_2015}, civic advocacy, activism and action. Furthermore, a lot of the work done around the design and engagement of data with citizens in a smart city has been done by researchers with social science or humanities background, and not done from a perspective of an information engineer working with citizens trying to address the community problem solving activities. According to \citet{bradley_social_2006} this type of research requires a \textit{``new type of engineer''} to be educated who has a much broader grasp on social issues and their importance towards the design and uses of technologies; however, at the same time have the technological know-how and background to not resist ICT and think more about the aspects of HCI (or HDI) and interpretation in CI \citep{stillman_community_2009}. 

\section{Summary}
\label{literature-summary}

This literature review has provided an overview of the concept of the smart city and the role of citizens in it, as well as providing a thorough review of the different ways that citizens can participate in the smart city through data -- either passively or actively. The literature review has help put forward a concept of more actively engaged citizen (i.e., smart citizen) of the smart city who participates in producing, accessing, understanding, using and making use of data to participate in decision making processes. Furthermore, the chapter has given an overview of the current state of open data; the way its made available, accessible, comprehensible and usable to citizens; and the stakeholders involved in it. Thus allowing to understand what it means to make data usable to citizens in civic context and for advocating. Additionally, highlighting the current role that expert professionals would have to play in this, because the data that the smart city concerns is large at scale and complex at nature; weather the expertise can be handed down to citizens or pushed into intelligent information systems. Although, there is an overlap in critiques of participatory design (or social design) and ISD for active civic engagement and participation, the tools implemented and the research conducted for the use of data for purposes of civic advocacy has had a narrow focus with little empirical research. Additionally, the research in this space tends to stay either in humanitarian fields or in systems engineering and there is little crossovers. Therefore, there is a need for empirical studies with citizens to investigate the active use of data in complex civic processes to find a middle ground between technological determinism and social constructionism, providing a holistic model and pragmatic practice that responds to critiques and helps guide this type of research in the future.
