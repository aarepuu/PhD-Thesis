\epigraph{Thinking is hard. Words must make it as easy as possible.}{--- \textup{Juhan Liiv}}

This chapter presents the concept of a Smart city which, through the use of information and communication technology (ICT) and data, is changing the way cities operate. Under this model of city governance, the people who inhabit these cities are often perceived as another passive source of data for measuring, control, optimisation and management at scale. This literature review explores different modes of civic participation and deposits a question of what other roles could citizens have in the Smart city? How do we go from smart cities to smart citizens? What is the role of active citizens and the role of data in supporting activities and engagement with decision making that determine the future of their city? As the use and creation of data in civic life and within communities spans across a wide range of disciplines, the literature from technical fields like pervasive computing and information engineering are intersected with social science, human computer interaction (HCI) and computer-supported collaborative work (CSCW). Drawing on critiques, this chapter identifies a criteria for a more active idea of the citizen as a user of data (e.i, an active or a smart citizen), which will be the main focus for the research presented in this thesis. Thought the literature review research gaps are identified which will be responded by the case studies of this thesis. This literature review gives an overview of the underlying concepts touched in this thesis, however for each case study a more in depth and context specific body of research is reviewed and presented.

This chapter describes related research around the use of data in knowledge creation and citizen participation in democracy. The use of data in civic life and within communities spans across a wide range of disciplines from technical fields like sensing networks, pervasive computing and information engineering to human computer interaction (HCI) and computer-supported collaborative work (CSCW). The first two sections of the literature review give an overview of the increasing use of data in decision-making through the lens of smart city and looks at the ways that citizens are involved or not involved in these data driver processes. Subsequent section describe the new ways of creating knowledge sources and opening them up for everyone to use and re-purpose. This also includes opportunities for new interpretations of open data sources published by the governments or other data generators. Fourth section describes the research around data production and different ways citizens can generate their own data sources, whether it is to contribute to science or to evidence local issues and contest decisions. Fifth section describes the current state-of the-art of data visualisation and storytelling through data which is essential for data science practices and the effective us of data. Drawing on work done by State of Open Data\footnote{\url{https://www.od4d.net/stateofopendata}} project and its contributors, section six tries to map out the relevant stakeholder groups around data production, use and dissemination of derived knowledge from it. It is essential to understand these complex relationships and roles of stakeholders, as we are increasingly coming to contend with significant challenges and questions relating to what data may be relevant and for whom; how to translate and represent it; as well as how different representations might be combined to better support communication and sense-making of complex information that sits at the boundaries between what may be private, shared and public. Building from that, section seven reviews the relevant literature about communities of practice and the knowledge networking within these communities. Finally the last section looks at the research into civic advocacy and action with the support of data and technology, including the existing models of successful and unsuccessful citizen decision-making. This literature review gives an overview of the underlying concepts touched in this thesis, however for each case study a more detailed and specific body of research is reviewed and presented.

\section{Participation in Smart City}
\label{smart-city}
\subsection{Defining the Smart City}
A vision of a smart city, where information technology is woven into the city infrastructure helping to optimise resources and improve the lives of citizens \citep{chourabi_understanding_2012,ratti_city_2016}, has been greatly discussed over the past five years\footnote{\url{https://trends.google.com/trends/explore?date=all&q=smart\%20city}}. Perhaps considered fictional at the time, but these ideas where echoed from concepts like \textit{`third wave'} of computing or \textit{`ubiquitous computing'} as it was envisioned by \citet{weiser_computer_1991,weiser_computer_1993} in the early 90s. For many years now commercial companies -- IBM, Cisco and Siemens, to name a few -- have been `hooking up' the city with sensors and other information technologies to gather all the data about the city in the hopes of solving some of the old and new `wicked' problems of society. For example intelligent street lighting is instrumented in many cities to improve efficiency and reduce energy costs; CCTV and traffic counters are used to manage the flow of traffic through traffic signals; and pedestrian counters are used to understand flow of people trough the city. There is a push for using data in all aspects of life -- to optimise and organise resources \citep{calvillo_energy_2016,batty_big_2013}, to reduce carbon footprint \citep{li_optimal_2015,pierangeli_health_2020,zubelzu_urban_2015} and improve sustainability \citep{vilajosana_bootstrapping_2013}, but also to facilitate novel modes of participation \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016}, democracy and social interaction \citep{baack_datafication_2015,tan_social-network-sourced_2013}. Despite of all the applications available, the discussion of what makes the city smart is still ongoing \citep{nam_smart_2011,foth_handbook_2009,su_smart_2011,klein_smart_2008,dameri_smart_2013}. However, with the increase of urbanisation and expansion of cities, this trend is more that likely to grow and more businesses are coming up solutions that can supposedly ``\textit{compute away local problems}'' \citep{townsend_smart_2013}.

Smart cities live off data -- traditionally acquired through a rigorous collection process ran by the government (i.e., conducting censuses) -- increasingly now captured using sensor networks located on lampposts, roadsides, tree and rooftops \citep{zanella_internet_2014,su_smart_2011}, and on humans inhabiting the city (e.g., mobile phone data, passive mobile positioning, `citizens as sensors') \citep{ahas_using_2010,rein_ahas_mobile_2010,berntzen_citizens_2018}; but also scraped from the web and social media platforms \citep{puiu_citypulse:_2016,batty_pulse_2010}. That vast amount of data accumulated through the smart city coupled with the ideas of `data-driven everything' have surfaced claims that cities are becoming `sentient', in terms of developing an awareness of everything that is going on in the city \citep{thrift_sentient_2014}. Furthermore, the interest in the data-driven city governance is only growing as more technologies are being developed and deployed in the city. This in turn requires a new skill-set of \textit{urban informatics} \citep{thakuriah_introduction_2017} from the city managers, but also from private and non-profit organisations that work to improve the life of people in the city \citep{kontokosta_urban_2017,foth_handbook_2009}.

A popular way of showing and using all that accumulated data is through real time \textit{`data dashboards'} \citep{kitchin_real-time_2014,kitchin_knowing_2015}. Almost all aspiring smart cities have at least one dashboard in hand to create and visualise statistical indicators, benchmark the city against others and drive the governance of the city \citep{kitchin_knowing_2015}. \citet{kitchin_knowing_2015} broadly categorises the utilisation of these dashboards into two -- managerial and contextual. First is more linked to assessment and performance measurement of the city governance and second prioritises the use of indicators for informing policy. Developing statistical indicators and visualising them through dashboards is a necessary and valid approach in city management, especially when trying to condense down all that messy data into some useful chunks that could be represented and used by decision-makers. However, \citet[p. 24]{kitchin_knowing_2015} points out the issue that often these initiatives are presented as the mirrored model or an exact \textit{`digital twin`} \citep{bolton_gemini_2018} of the city through numbers -- \textit{``visualised facts; that reflect the truth about the world''}. What these dashboards actually are, is a representation of abstractions that make up a view of the city. Unfortunately, in many cases these dashboards are representations of the view of the city from the perspective of management and governance. This means that the data collection and the problems are framed by institutional actors to fit into the governance processes of the city and often done without engaging the concerned publics \citep{asad_creating_2017}. Similar cirques have echoed from 
\cite{greenfield_against_2013} and \cite{townsend_smart_2013}, arguing that currently the power of making educated decisions based on that data is in the hands of the big tech giants, whose software works in controlled centralised locations. In addition to concerns around transparency and ethics surrounding surveillance and control \citep{van_dijck_datafication_2014,tufekci_engineering_2014}, this lack of inclusivity in smart cities can lead to narrow representation of views and desires of society in governance \citep{mcmillan_data_2016,tufekci_big_2014}.

\subsection{Citizens in the Smart City}
In Smart city literature the people inhabiting the city are referred to in a variety of generic terms, such as `subjects', `consumers', `users',  `stakeholders' and `citizens'. In this thesis the term \textit{`citizens'} is used for the people who inhabit, work and play in cities. 

 \citet{berntzen_citizens_2018} argues that in addition to political participation -- direct and indirect participation in political decision-making, the role of citizen in the smart city is also to participate in helping solve the problems and improve services in the city. That entails that city also has the needed infrastructure and processes to support communication and these multiple types of participation \citep{berntzen_regulatory_2010}. New technological infrastructure and sources of data definitely broaden the channels of participation. In the modern democratic society, citizens should be actively participating in all matters of civic life \citep{chourabi_understanding_2012}, including political discourse \citet{hoffman_participation_2012}, through civic engagement activities run by the state \citep{berntzen_citizens_2018} but also citizen initiatives. On the other hand, from the algorithmic smart city perspective, citizens can be also viewed as additional source of data that could be tapped into to improve the efficiency and functionality of the smart city. 

There is an enormous potential for technology to enable new ways of participation, engagement and service delivery, however it comes at the time when the UK, among other countries in the EU, is subjected to acute economic austerity which has significant effects to local governance. Meaning that public services delivery has severely suffered and in some cases even outsourced or privatised. A lot of the solutions provided by institutional actors are not relevant to the real issues in the community \citep{boehner_data_2016}. People living in the communities often have a much keener sense of what the real problems are \citep{disalvo_neighborhood_2008}. 

With the age of internet and information technologies becoming more accessible outside institutional boundaries, digital civics approaches \citep{olivier_digital_2015} have been emerging from HCI related disciplines, which promote active participation in civic life and engagement in service creation and delivery, with hopes of increasing civic professionalism \citep{boyte_boyte_2010} within the society and increasing the dialog between citizens and the government \citet{boyte_everyday_2004}. Examples of these kind of study areas are, but not exclusively; community commissioning \citep{garbett_app_2016,balaam_feedfinder:_2015,taylor_creating_2010,taylor_supporting_2009}, democratic discourse \citep{johnson_community_2017,johnson_reflections_2016,valkanova_myposition:_2014,crivellaro_contesting_2015} situated voting \citep{vlachokyriakos_postervote:_2014,golsteijn_sens-us:_2016,taylor_viewpoint:_2012,behrens_smart_2014,hespanhol_vote_2015} and community infographics \citep{koeman_what_2014,claes_street_2013,lindley_surfacing_2017}. What is common with these studies is that they are moving out from the research facilities and labs to conduct investigations in the real world. \citet{rogers_interaction_2011} calls this approach research \textit{`in the wild'} -- carrying out in situ studies, and rather than conducting observations of existing practices, focusing on local issues and designing disruptive technologies to address concerns and needs of specific population. That could also mean, but not inclusively \citep{vines_configuring_2013}, including citizens as co-designers in the process of design. 

Although these processes are often messy, unpredictable and uncontrollable, they provide a better insight and understanding of how technology should be designed and is used in the real world \citep{suchman_plans_1985,suchman_human-machine_2007}. However, these approaches often stay in the HCI and social science related disciplines which means that the actual technology and systems implemented and supported in the smart city does not extend to the community problem solving activites \citep{stillman_community_2009}. As machines and algorithms are getting better at making decisions and acting in behalf of humans, gaining more `nonhuman agency', citizens cannot be disconnected from the decision-making processes that effect their lives \citep{graham_too-smart_2014}. These approaches should overlap to engineering, geography, planning and spacial modelling to tackle inequalities in the smart city\footnote{\url{https://www.turing.ac.uk/research/research-projects/spatial-inequality-and-smart-city}} and respond to the UN's 
Sustainable Development Goals \footnote{\url{https://sustainabledevelopment.un.org/?menu=1300}}.

Indeed, many have recognised \citep{saunders_rethinking_2015,rudolf_giffinge_smart_2007} that using technology for participation is the key to a successful model of a smart city. However, the type of participation enabled by the smart city is determined by how citizens can engage with technological solutions and what roles they take in the use, design, creation and ownership of these solutions.

\section{Data Driven Participation}
\label{data-driven-participation}
% Passive and active participation
% Review and critique on literature on passive and active participation using data and case studies which used either techniques and technologies.
\petenote{What is data and who is it for? Passive and active par. Draw a picture.}
Citizens are not only moving around in this sensed environment (i.e., smart city), they are also contributing some of the data to it by using smart devices in their pockets -- smartphones and other portable personal computing devices. \citet{townsend_smart_2013} calls them \textit{``smart-city construction kits''} or \textit{``digital Swiss Army knifes''} which are increasingly becoming the sources of data for studying human behaviour, but they are also becoming a platform for new bottom up civic movements. 

However, not all participation is created equal. In the literature there can be identified two distinct modes of data driven participation: \textit{passive} and \textit{active}. Passive participation refers to the use of some service or automated system (e.g., data logger or a service on a personal computing device), or as a result of a `data exhaust', that sends off data to be analysed by algorithm implemented on automated systems. This means that citizens are contributing data either voluntarily by knowingly using a particular app or program or anonymously in the background by using some type of service on their computers or mobile devices. Passive participation is the classical model of smart city participation where citizens are adding another dataset for the smart city to use.

Active participation on the other hand is referred in the literature, as type of participation where citizens take a more active role in data production and towards helping to solve the problem of the city. This can manifest for example in citizens actively contributing data by reporting issues and problems through their personal computing devices or using citizen science devices to collect data about issues concerning them. This model of data driven participation makes people into data producers and in some cases consumers which is more active way of participating in the smart city.

Both modes -- \textit{passive} and \textit{active} -- refer to the ways that citizens supply data to the smart city an its algorithms, being the data that informs the decision making. However there is another type of participation -- \textit{active data user} -- that is often been neglected by researchers, designers and city officials. This type of participation is what the research in this thesis is exploring. Participation where citizens can \textit{access}, \textit{create}, \textit{understand}, \textit{use} and \textit{make use} of data to actively participate in decision making processes in the smart city.

\subsection{Citizen as Passive Data User}
\label{sub:passive-user}
Historically people were mainly connecting by trade, post, telephone or face-to-face interactions. In the digital age it is becoming more commonplace to use computerised systems and data to mediate communication of ideas and opinions. Internet enables us to establish enormously complex networks, connecting us with different people and helping to engage in number of activities -- individual and collaborative. Although not always explicit, all participation in the cyberspace is driven through data sharing and use. This can manifest for example in participation on social media platforms like Twitter and Facebook \citep{crivellaro_pool_2014}, using personal informatics tools\footnote{\url{http://www.personalinformatics.org/}} for self reflection or improvement \citep{li_stage-based_2010,epstein_personal_2015,epstein_lived_2015}, or engaging with collaborative platforms to work towards a common goal \citep{salovaara_collective_2006}.
% Talk about project to discover enzymes (CSCW) or space objects
Popular examples of latter are Wikipedia, a crowd powered free encyclopedia and OpenStreetMap, an open source geographical database compiled by community of volunteer mappers.

Availability of portable smart devices in almost every citizen's pocket are becoming excellent sources of data for the algorithmic smart city for understanding how humans use the city. For example there is a growing body or research into usage of mobile phone call logs for coming up with near-real time models of spatial interactions \citep{ahas_using_2010,vanhoof_exploring_2017} and deriving early statistical indicators to optimise planning and transport \citep{sonntag_feasibility_2014}. Furthermore, usage of mobile phone data has also shown to be beneficial for coming up epidemiological models \citep{finger_mobile_2016,panigutti_assessing_2017}. Additional to using passive mobile positioning, `smart city' is tapping into data generated by social media to gather opinions and model large scale human behaviours. There is growing body of literature around usage of social media platforms to conduct large scale content analysis for semantics \citep{bontcheva_making_2014,procter_reading_2013,sizov_geofolk:_2010,golder_diurnal_2011,cranshaw_livehoods_2012}. Other applications include usage of social media for crisis and emergency watch \citep{liu_crisis_2014,kamel_boulos_crowdsourcing_2011}, coming up with predictive models for public health interventions \citep{chourabi_understanding_2012} and using social media as an indicator for political sentiment and even predicting outcomes of political elections \citep{tumasjan_predicting_2010}. Social media can be a powerful tool for mediating participation, but as demonstrated by \citep{bond_61-million-person_2012} it can be also used to influence the outcome of democratic elections. Although data from this type of passive participation of citizens can be useful for the smart city, there is definitely a discussion to be had about the ethical and privacy concerns surrounding it \citep{van_dijck_datafication_2014,lanier_who_2014}.

In addition to using the `data exhaust' of people's phones or activities on social media, this type of passive data participation can also be voluntarily. Certainly, civic engagement is one domain where these technologies and methods are being introduced with an aim to increase participation in democracy through data. These new approaches have particularly peaked interest of city planners, who see this as a great opportunity for large scale participation through usage of data and pervasive technology. In other words, trying to harness the power of crowds through data science \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016}. In big geography and urban planning it is often referred to as volunteered geographic information (VGI) and it has been argued that it increases participation and the representativness of data \citep{goodchild_citizens_2007}. Examples of these type of systems include projects like MetaPos\footnote{\url{https://www.gsa.europa.eu/r-d/gnss-project-portfolio}}, Habits\footnote{\url{https://habitsdata.org}} and Sunset\footnote{\url{http://sunset-project.eu}}, each having a mobile application which collects anonymous location data to be used in spatial modelling that aim to aid organisation of infrastructure and/or traffic in the city. Although these applications have elements of feedback in terms of people seeing their analysed mobility patterns or being able to share travel tips with others on social media, they are still contained within the frame of the research or agendas of city authorities. Furthermore, some of these agendas can also be linked to behavioural change (not necessarily bad), which is administered through incentives\footnote{\url{https://www.betterpoints.ltd}}. This \textit{`citizens as sensors'} \citep{berntzen_citizens_2018} form of participation is often preferred by corporation, busy citizens and city managers, because of its low impact on citizens everyday lives and reduced workload on city officials to actively engage their citizenry \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016}.

There is a lot potential for crowdsourced data collection approaches to broaden the participation and create alternative sources of data for public use in multiple domains. \citet{salim_urban_2015} came up with a taxonomy of interaction and participation to assess different types of urban computing solutions. Their paper surveyed a variety of applications in mobile crowdsensing, urban informatics, urban probes and interventions, and Internet of Things (IoT), categorising them in terms of \textit{``the technologies used, the level of participation they stimulate, the participation scale they support, the manipulation and effects mode they enable, the interaction mode and scale they enable''} \citep[p. 44]{salim_urban_2015}. Although urban computing systems may vary in shapes and sizes, they all aim for maximum participation from the public. Through the reflection on projects \citet{salim_urban_2015} came up with list of strategies that put emphasis on identification of needs and tensions through active dialog with stakeholders, and providing open and accessible tools that help citizens construct their own rationals for participating.
\mynote{Conclusion: Clearly the key for success is not to provide the best technology for participation, but to prioritise the social interactions surrounding it.}
There are differently new opportunities that emerge around designing tools that successfully facilitate this kind of data collection which tries to harness crowds and capitalise on capabilities of big data. However, there is a ongoing discourse around usage of crowdsourced geospatial (not exclusively) data to mediate civic interaction, both in term of its validity for participation in democracy \citep{le_dantec_planning_2015} and as a tool for decision-making \citep{brabham_crowdsourcing_2009,tenney_data-driven_2016,misra_crowdsourcing_2014}. Caution needs to be taken, not to fall into the same pitfalls again by taking data at face value. As \cite{le_dantec_planning_2015} and \citet{kitchin_knowing_2015} observed, it is often appealing for city managers to base their decisions solely on what numbers are showing and maps are identifying, rather than engaging directly with the citizens to also considering what people on the ground are experiencing. Including not considering that sometimes technology excludes groups of people who are snuggling with low tech literacy or just does not have resources to access them. People need to be reminded that big data can only show the `what is', but can not reveal the `how', `why' and most importantly `what is not' \citep{mayer-schonderger_big_2013}.

\subsection{Citizen as Data Maker and Producer}
\label{sub:data-maker}
% more active model
% asking people to contribute to the problem solving
Active data driven participation also leverages capabilities of modern day information technologies and internet, however it tries to actively involve and engage in the problem solving, design and reorganisation of urban environments. These active partition approaches such as Public Participation Geographic Information Systems (PPGIS) as perceived to be more grassroots, enabling citizens to actively contribute to the decision-making processes \citep{sieber_public_2006,jankowski_towards_2009}. Examples of such systems include FixMyStreet\footnote{\url{https://www.fixmystreet.com}} popular in the UK and its Norwegian clone FiksGataMi\footnote{\url{http://www.fiksgatami.no}}; SeeClickFix\footnote{\url{https://seeclickfix.com}}, PublicStuff\footnote{\url{http://www.publicstuff.com}} and Street Bump\footnote{\url{http://www.streetbump.org}} in the US. Although PPGIS in its core is \textit{`bottom-up'} approach, its technological realisation is often more \textit{`top-down'} and serves government interests \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016}. Issue here is that the scope for these technologies originate from institutional actors, who phrase the issue from their perspective. Although helpful for citizens as well, this can end up with a technical solution that helps generate data that is specific to the formulation of governmental concerns. In addition, the `datasets' are usually collected by non-expert citizens without any formal training around how to run rigorous data collection and using devices with questionable accuracy. This makes city managers sceptical of the value of that data and opinions coming from non-expert citizens \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016}. Furthermore, there are issues around interpretation of citizen concerns from data to actual tangible solutions in the city and in sufficient feedback loops to measure the successes of those solutions.

\subsubsection{Citizen Sensing and Citizen Science}
\label{citizen-sensing}
In addition to sensors in the city, passive and active data-driven participation that supports the algorithmic smart city agenda, there is also citizen sensing. An approach that usually involves, but not exclusively, citizens using a sensing technology (or mobile phones) to voluntarily gather data. Citizen sensing is not exactly the same as crowdsourcing -- where people are seen as workers or data collectors who complete tasks or collect data for the work `requester', often getting payed for their participation. Furthermore, citizen sensing in not necessarily exclusively linked to citizen science initiatives, which recruits non-scientist members of the public to take part of science projects volunteering their time for data collection and/or analysis \citet{cohn_citizen_2008}. Nor should not simply taking part of study -- for example participating in a sleep study or filling in a survey -- be confused with citizen science. Citizen sensing can be then used to refer to any activity where citizens are engaging in voluntarily data collection using sensing technologies; and it can be linked, but not exclusively, to crowdsourcing, participating in a study or to a citizen science project.

According to the network of National Contact Points for Science with and for Society\footnote{\url{https://www.sisnetwork.eu/sis.net-project}} definition, citizen science projects are \textit{`projects where citizens contribute to actual science research'} \citep{eitzel_citizen_2017}. Examples of these kind of initiatives are BioBlitz, a biological surveying project; and British Bird Watching, a bird surveying project. It seems that citizen science has multiple definitions and often it is down to the `scientists' to decide how they are framing it, however it is not often clear who `is the scientist' \citep{haklay_citizen_2013} in these project. \citet[p.11]{haklay_citizen_2013} proposes a framework to classify citizen science projects which focuses on the level of engagement and involvement of participant in science activities (see Figure \ref{fig:4levels}). It is somewhat similar to \citet{arnstein_ladder_1969}'s \textit{`ladder of participation'} (see Figure \ref{fig:ladder}) however instead of solely focusing on the power relationships between citizens and decision-makers on a fixed ladder, \citet{haklay_citizen_2013} also points out the processes of obtaining new understandings which fosters curiosity and promotes knowledge creation, and is likely to move people up the ladder of participation. In addition to finding appropriate and sustainable engagement level for the project there is also the question of `who knows best'? Although there have been successful citizen science projects like Neighbourhood Nest Watch \citep{evans_neighborhood_2005} or volunteer computing projects like climateprediction.net and Rosetta@home, there is an ongoing debate on should the `science' be left to the `scientists' should we fully accept citizen contribution to science activities \citep{gabrys_just_2018,cohn_citizen_2008}.

However there can be other purposes than advancing science or providing data to the smart city, for doing citizen data collection. For example for self knowledge and personalised health interventions \citep{huck_mapping_2014,swan_health_2012,larkin_towards_2017}, to raise public awareness around an issue \citep[ch. 16]{loreto_participatory_2017} \citep{hasenfratz_participatory_2012,kuznetsov_participatory_2010}, evidence an issue in the community \citep{disalvo_local_2009} or push for change \citep{balestrini_city_2017}. Latter approaches involve community-led investigation where citizens are actively involved in definition of the issue, collection or acquiring of data and the analysis of it. In relation to typologies of \citet{arnstein_ladder_1969}'s latter and \citet{haklay_citizen_2013}'s 4 levels, these approaches come on the very top in terms of level of active engagement. \citet{balestrini_city_2017} looks at this type of participation through a \textit{City-Commons Framework for Citizen Sensing} (see Figure \ref{fig:city_commons}) which nurtures a bottom-up approach to orchestrate citizen sensing and community engagement. These type of citizen sensing projects that explore issues related to environment and place, often involve some sort of sensing technologies \citep{thompson_crowd-sourced_2016} and PPGIS or mapping coupled with HCI for geospatial technologies. Examples of such platforms are Aircasting\footnote{\url{http://aircasting.org}} and SmartCitizen\footnote{\url{https://smartcitizen.me}} (see more Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}) which both consist of lightweight sensing equipment and GIS application for exploring the data. Citizen sensing provides an opportunity to rethink the role of data in civic participation and focus on digital technology and mechanisms that could support these grassroots approaches. More projects like this are now being developed around the world with community volunteers using sensing equipment and collecting data with an aim to promote dialogue on environmental issues getting closer to the next level of participation through data.

\subsection{Citizen as Active User of Data}
\label{sub:active-user}
% How does this change the relationship of data
Although with active data-driven participation citizens are actively involved in data production, and with citizen sensing approaches also more involved in problem definition, they are not necessarily using the data for activism and for chaining their community. Another level (i.e., the third level) data-driven participation is where citizens not only supply data, either passively or actively, but they also have access to those data, in a way that they can use and make use of it. This type of participation is referred in this thesis as citizen as \textit{active data user} -- equipped with tools for accessing and understanding data, sensing technology to create data and knowledge to make use of data to promote dialogue on issues and push for positive change in the community. 

This shifts the focus from the smart city to smart citizens who are making the city smart. Active citizens not only produce data, they also get access to the data, and can even commission the kinds of data they need, opening up another level of data-driven participation and empowerment through data. The `new` smart city, which is creating data with the people for the people and enabling them to commission and use data, then has to think about what are the structural, digital and legal infrastructures to support these new forms of data use, which go beyond just data consumption or only enabling the smart city to consume it. This could be done by opening up processes of smart city to citizens and moving away from technocratic governance and corporatisation of city management \citep{kitchin_real-time_2014}. \cite{kitchin_knowing_2015} encourages smart city initiatives to openly recognise their flaws, uncertainty, messiness and levels of error, and accept the fact that they do not reflect the world as it actually is, but actively frame and produce the world through measuring and observations of it. Meaning there is no one true image of the city and everything depends on the perspective of the observer. Whilst there is great potential in using data in these contexts, and there is an active effort being done to integrate sensors into our spaces, meaning that lot of technology is already in place, it has to be configured and shaped in a way that it serves the needs of citizens, not technology itself.

\subsubsection{Civic Advocacy}
\label{civic-advocacy}
This type of stance is also activistic in nature, promoting activities of civic advocacy and action. Engaging in practices linked to advocacy is not a new concept in democratic societies. For many people lobbying and grassroots lobbying (e.g., regulated by US Federal Law) are considered the main forms of advocacy, however advocacy does not limit itself to only lobbying. It uses protesting, petitions, citizen campaigning and instrumentation of variety of other information resources to influence decisions withing the socioeconomic and political systems that shape the lives of people \citep{christoffel_public_2000} are legitimate means of democratic participation and should be part of a healthy society.
Advocacy efforts can come from individuals, but they are more likely to be associated with some sort of organisation or self-organised group \citep{obar_advocacy_2012,suarez_nonprofit_2009,leroux_nonprofits_2007,prakash_advocacy_2010}. Advocacy can also be characterised into two strategies - insider and outsider strategy \citep{asad_tap_2017}. Where the former uses more traditional approaches to directly effect policy systems and the latter focuses on informal processes by contributing resources or skills to organisations to help address issues \citep{hackler_strategic_2007,marshall_accountable:_2016,erete_storytelling_2016}. Additionally, often the advocates are not the ones with the personal attachments for the issues, they are acting as proxies to voice community concerns \citep{leroux_nonprofits_2007,johnson_community_2017,voida_bridging_2012}.

Affordances and capabilities of ICT are enabling new forms of digital advocacy \citep{brady_utilizing_2015} where people are taking advantage of digital tools and platforms to affect change in the community \citep{karpf_online_2010}. For example using platforms such as Change.org\footnote{\url{https://www.change.org}} and TheyWorkForYou\footnote{\url{https://www.theyworkforyou.com}} to digitalise traditional forms of advocacy (e.g., singing petitions, sending letters to representatives). Research in HCI has started looking into ways that technologies and data can facilitate these new roles of the active citizen engagement. For example, \citet{garbett_app_2016} looked at a model for community commissioning of technologies to help communities gather data about local concerns; \citet{balestrini_beyond_2014} explored at how low cost sensing equipment can be used to empower citizens tackling specific sustainability issues; \citep{kuznetsov_participatory_2010} looked at usage of low cost sensors for measuring outdoor air quality with diverse communities, and explored the uses of data for public awareness and activism; projects where citizens used their mobile phones to collect data about transportation issues to advocate for changes in cycling infrastructure \citep{maskell_spokespeople:_2018,dantec_advocating_2016,dantec_data-based_2014}; or other types of sensor data to surface issues and to advocate for more accessible city infrastructure \citep{rodger_technology_2016}, all which are part of the a model of citizens as active data users. At the other end, citizens themselves have taken control and re-purposed technologies to aid them in their advocacy efforts. For example we have already witnessed disruptive social movements through the use of social media platforms \citep{howard_opening_2011,eltantawy_social_2011} and smaller movements aimed at hyper-local concerns \citet{crivellaro_pool_2014}; or to coordinate humanitarian action \citep{starbird_voluntweeters:_2011,starbird_chatter_2010}. Benefits of online tools are that they do not put geographical restrictions on participation, allowing participation from other places and also enabling instant access to the information shared. 

Digital advocacy efforts are similar to citizens as data producers participation, however data-driven advocacy \citep{asad_tap_2017,gray_changing_2016} is taking a more active stance where citizen as becoming witting makers of data. Similarly, it involves people using digital technologies to collect data, however they then use that data to inform or evidence an issue or advocate for change. Also, being a more active form of participation, it has greater embassies on the relational interactions between civic stakeholders \citep{olivier_digital_2015,vlachokyriakos_digital_2016,asad_tap_2017}. Although there might be multiplicity of stakeholders from different organisation involved, issues take precedence and different communities, or \textit{publics} (see Section \ref{sub:com_net}) form around them. Projects like The Bristol Approach\footnote{\url{http://www.bristolapproach.org}} (see also Figure \ref{fig:city_commons}) provide examples of issue-led investigations where people often co-design the technologies used in advocating. Furthermore, it has been pointed out that the technology, and how it is being used in issue advocating plays an active role in forming these publics \citep{jenkins_object-oriented_2016,dantec_infrastructuring_2013}. When designing or re-purposing digital tools for active citizen (i.e., active data user) engagement, there is an obligation from designers and developers to think about how they support different groups and promote civic participation and advocacy (i.e., infrastructuring) among all citizens \citep{asad_tap_2017}.

\citet{townsend_smart_2013} wrote in his book \textit{Smart Cities: Big Data, Civic Hackers, and the Quest for a New Utopia} that the answer to the question of what is the smartest city, is \textit{"...the one you live in"}. \section{Open Movements}
\label{sec:open-movements}
Just like all ideas and opinions where historically shared through face-to-face encounters, all data, information and knowledge was also exchanged orally and spread freely. However with rise of the printing press in the 1400s, knowledge started to be seen as individual rather than a collective property. Soon multiple copyright laws were put in place and enforced by governments across the world to protect the property rights of the publishers. With the coming of the Internet, which made sharing and copying of information even easier and more broader, resulted in copyright laws to become stricter and more unified around the globe. In response to the prevalence of the copyright laws a new political an social movement -- \textit{Open Movement} -- was forced that seeks to promote sharing of content and resources for the good of the society. Movement operates in a spirit of transparency, collaboration, re-use and free access. Movement as been realised in some regards through specific groups, institutions and individuals around the world embracing the benefits of releasing digitised text, data and multi-media content that is online, free of charge, often collaborative, and free of most copyright and licensing restrictions. Open Movement is seen as an umbrella term that encompasses things like open access, open data, open government, open development, open science, open education, open source, open licensing and open content. However open can mean many things, what the research in this thesis is seeking is how are these movements contributing to making data and knowledge open to support active citizens engaging in new modes of participation in the smart city?

\subsection{Open Knowledge}
\label{sub:open-knowledge}
Open movements aim to promote participatory processes through sharing of data, knowledge and outputs as well as source code of technical implementations. The openness of these projects and the resources they provide is not always defined just the same. Open Data and knowledge is commonly defined by the Open Definition\footnote{\url{http://opendefinition.org}}, which states: \textit{``Open data and content can be freely used, modified, and shared by anyone for any purpose''}. Most successful example of open knowledge sharing is Wikipedia, founded in 2001, the same year as Creative Commons, which aims to facilitate sharing of creative works in public domain and is used by Wikimedia to publish all its content. Wikipedia is an online encyclopedia where anyone can become a contributor of content which is interlinked and publicly shared. According to internet rankings it is in the top five most visited sites on the web\footnote{\url{https://www.alexa.com/topsites}}. Although just about anyone can edit and add content on Wikipedia it has been illustrated that the information shared has value and quality like any other professional source of knowledge \citep{giles_internet_2005}. Other notable project are Khan Academy, for publishing free educational resources; and Open Knowledge Foundation which promotes and shares free information. Open knowledge also extends itself to publishing scientific research, where people are advocating for open access to publications, especially if the research is publicly funded \citep{garcia-penalvo_open_2010}.

\subsection{Open Source}
\label{sub:open-source}
However with software it is not always clear what is freely usable and to what extent, there is difference between `open' and `free' software. The difference between open and free is that, open is software people can access the source code of, fork (i.e., download and replicate) and change it to work within the context they have. The initial idea of \textit{open source} software was proposed by the members of Free Software Foundation\footnote{\url{https://www.fsf.org/}} who promoted sharing of software and design documents for re-use and re-purposing in other projects. Open source software are published using open source licences\footnote{\url{https://opensource.org/licenses}} which dictates the specific rules of how people can use and modify the source code. Perhaps the most well known and successful open source project is Linux, created by Linus Tonvalds in 1991, which kernel code has being used in multiple popular operating systems, including UNIX-like GNU operating system which was initiated by Richard Stallman. Nowadays sharing and collaborating on software development manifests in geographically dispersed programmers working together on projects using online collaboration interfaces \citep{west_contrasting_2005} such as GitHub\footnote{\url{https://github.com}}, Bitbuket\footnote{\url{https://bitbucket.org}} and SourceForge\footnote{\url{https://sourceforge.net/}}. Nowadays models of free and open source software are becoming more popular to the point that even commercial companies are publishing their software on these platforms, potentially sharing their intellectual property with their competitors \citep{west_challenges_2006}. This extends itself to the idea that there is `strength in numbers' or as Linus's Law states (also proven by the development of Linux Kernel) \textit{``given enough eyeballs, all bugs are shallow''} \citep{raymond_cathedral_2001}, meaning that if there is more people working on the a project it will ultimately produce higher quality software. In addition better quality software however, people working on these projects have also reported that they are motivated by participation and sense of belonging to a collective, and sharing of knowledge and skills with them \citep{lakhani_why_2003}. People working in the spirit of transparency and openness, sharing of skills and knowledge on these new virtual platforms, inserts trust and opens up new opportunities for these systems to promote new ways of using them for social good. Open source software could be one of beneficial resources to tap into to support active citizen engagement in the smart city, however it requires software development skills and knowledge to work with.

Then there are Open Data, data that \textit{``can be freely accessed, used, modified and shared by anyone for any purpose -- subject only, at most, to requirements to provide attribution and/or share-alike''}\footnote{\url{http://opendatahandbook.org/glossary/en/terms/open-data}}, potentially the most transformative branch of Open Movement. When defined by the Open Definition it has to be open both; legally, licensed under open licence; and technically, without attached costs, in bulk and machine-readable format. Among that data are Open Governmental Data (OGD) \citep{ubaldi_open_2013}, one branch of open government alongside with other open governmental initiatives like Open Budgeting, data that is collected or which collection is commissioned by governmental entities and then published using open licences. Using these technologies, but not exclusively, governments have spin up portals like \url{open.canada.ca} and \url{data.gov.uk}, Canada and UK respectively, with many others countries following their footsteps (see \href{https://opendatabarometer.org}{Open Data Barometer}), enabling people to search and download governmental open data. With `father of the World Wide Web' Tim Berners-Lee, among others, calling for \textit{``raw data now''}, publishing of open data has increased massively over the past ten years and there are over 170 open data portals in the EU alone according to Open Data Monitor\footnote{\url{https://opendatamonitor.eu}}. Althought there is abundance of data already published there are still datasets that are disclosed by governments. One way citizens can 
gain access to those datasets, provided it does not violate any restrictions, is by going through Freedom of Information (FOI) request. However processes linked to submitting a FOI request can be complex, lengthy and hard to document for re-use. To address these issues MySociety, a UK-based charity, came up with a simple online tool called WhatDoTheyKnow\footnote{\url{https://www.whatdotheyknow.com}} to aid people in submitting FOI request and sharing the process and the results with everyone. Using this approach, people have done over half a million requests resulting in new datasets being released onto the web.
\begin{quote}
\textit{``…if people put data onto the web […] it will be used by other people to do wonderful things.''} \citep{berners-lee_year_2010}
\end{quote}
It is great that data is out there, because it promotes transparency and encourages discussion, however it has turned into a `numbers game' with little context. There is a lot of ambiguity around what makes a government open and what are the purposes of Open Govermental data -- is it merely political statement or call for technologies that make that data usable \citep{renee_e._sieber_civic_2015}. People have critiqued that the \textit{`measure of openness'} for governments is flawed, only taking account the number datasets and repositories published online \citep{davies_open_2010} and that there are more nuances to the effective re-use of open data than simply the ability to access (not even being the case for everyone) the generated data files \citep{gurstein_open_2011,gurstein_effective_2003}. Similarly Open Data Barometer's latest report points out that there is a lack of work being done around inclusivity surrounding open data \citep{world_wide_web_foundation_open_2018}. 
Clearly making data available is not the same as making it accessible and usable for everybody, especially the citizens who want to engage in active forms of civic participation through data. One of these necessities for what data needs to become in order to make this vision of fully engaged citizen or data engaged citizen a reality, is to get data from \textit{available} to \textit{usable}. Similar critiques echo from \citet{gurstein_effective_2003} who proposes a model of \textit{`effective use of data'} as apposed to open data, that includes far more than steps than just making data available in order to tackle the `digital divide' around data.

Open data from governments and private companies could potentially provide valuable input for citizens to leverage to participate in democracy and help improve their communities. Working with data however requires specialist know-how, tools and understanding of data science techniques -- trades that community groups and citizens often lack. Even with tools like WhatDoTheyKnow, which simplify the requests for data, there might be problems with not knowing what data would be useful in particular context, especially when the request consists of having to explain what data one's looking for in a `free text' field, with limited guidance and ways for pulling in context. 

Perhaps the biggest misconception around open data are that it comes in a `raw' state. In fact data is always \textit{``influenced by potential uses, expectations, context, and theoretical constructs''} \citep{tuomi_data_1999}. \citet{heidegger_what_1976} and \citet{polanyi_tacit_2009}, among other philosophers of knowledge, have criticised the empirical model of epistemology, with an underlining principle that an object's being and our interpretation of it is in an entangled state, meaning that we can not observe an object without it being part of its current interpretation. Hence it is impossible for humans to take data as raw material and to obtain knowledge from data through empirical observations of it. 

In the form it is made available for citizens does not lend itself to be used in supporting new forms of civic participation. Citizens are more likely to gain from a data exploration tools that wrap data in some narrative that position data into context helping to tell a story. \cite{taylor_data--place:_2015} has emphasised the importance of data production and use being bounded to a place with geographical and social dimensions, giving it context and making it usable. There are already examples of systems like MapIT\footnote{\url{https://mapit.mysociety.org}} and TheyWorkForYou\footnote{\url{https://www.theyworkforyou.com}} which enable people to access open data linked to specific postcode; or Know My Neighbourhood\footnote{\url{https://neighbourhood.knowmyarea.org}} and Local Insight\footnote{\url{https://local.communityinsight.org}} which enables community organisations to link different open governmental datasets to an area of interest to aid local-decision making or improve service delivery (see more in Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}).

Openly sharing data and knowledge, whether it originated from official sources or shared by Wikipedians on the web, takes it closer to becoming knowledge that is understood and accessible by everyone. But not only that, if knowledge is shared it can be questioned, confirmed or contested  -- either through public critique or even systematic reviews and meta-analyses -- which ultimately lead to understanding of different perspectives and a better quality of focal knowledge. Not openly sharing data and knowledge, or deliberately making accessing obscure and difficult for reasons such as the data is \textit{too complex to understand}, \textit{too difficult to interpret} or \textit{might be misunderstood} are not valid reasons for not putting it out there. The issue is not that the data is complicated, the issue is that the presentation of data is complicated or missing crucial context to make sense of it. Meaning that there is a representation issue rather than data issue, which is more related to information-systems problem solving agenda, however this can be approached through the community problem solving angle \citep{stillman_community_2009}. Furthermore, if things are hidden from people, they can not be discussed and their representations can not be worked on.

\subsection{Working with Open Data}
\label{data-viz}
% Review and critique of different visualisation techniques and reviewing the current state of the art of data visualisation tools.
% Data visualisation ≠ comprehension
In order for people to start making sense of data and working with data it needs to be condensed into something that is comprehensible through human senses. This is where visual communication of data or data visualisation comes into play aiming to communicate information that data contains. There is often an assumption that data visualising is just reflecting the information that data holds. However, as there is no such thing as `raw data', there is also no such thing as direct or `raw visualisation'. Data visualisations, although based on data, are already analysed and edited forms of data projections which convey the message of the data analyst or an editor. The aim of data visualisation is to support the analytical process, formal procedure used to solve any type of problem, and help with information discovery and knowledge creation. That means it is also important to identify the type of audiences that will use the data representation, to better contextualise and present information aiding them in knowledge creation \citep{chi_operator_1998}.
% https://www.d3-graph-gallery.com/index.html
% https://blog.bitsrc.io/11-javascript-charts-and-data-visualization-libraries-for-2018-f01a283a5727

People might theoretically have access to the same data, however not everyone has the know-how, skills and resources to turn data into something useful. Manipulation of data in a spreadsheet can be manageable for vast majority of people, however when data requires some cleaning, merging or reducing, more specialised tools such as Matlab\footnote{\url{https://www.mathworks.com/products/matlab.html}}, Python\footnote{\url{https://www.python.org}} or R\footnote{\url{https://www.r-project.org}} are needed. Although still considered a professional trade the advances in information technologies and the open source movement have produced multiple visualisation libraries such as Matplotlib\footnote{\url{https://matplotlib.org}}, ggplot2\footnote{\url{https://ggplot2.tidyverse.org}}, D3.js\footnote{\url{https://d3js.org}} enabling more people experiment with data visualisation. However, data visualisation tools often require software development skills in order to manipulate the data and produce visual outputs. Software like Tableau and Gephi however are trying to break down those barriers and use graphical user interfaces (GUI) to help users visualise data. Advances in web technologies also permit data manipulation on web platforms in an easy `drag and drop' manner. Examples of such tools are Carto\footnote{\url{https://carto.com/}}, for manipulating geographic data and making interactive maps; Charted \citep{sall_using_2014} and RAWGraphs \citep{mauri_rawgraphs:_2017} for automatic chart creation from files, online spreadsheets, web APIs or other data sources, which can be easily shared with others. Although there are open source web tools for making graphs, people still have to choose the right visualisation for particular data to convey the information. DataVizProject\footnote{\url{https://datavizproject.com}} from Ferdio makes good headway towards helping people make the right choice for their data, by having simple visual representation of different types of charts and categorised by family, input, functionality and shape.

Even if data is made accessible, visible, and formatted nicely, or put it into `pretty' graphs, it does not necessarily mean that the average person (or citizen) can use it. Presenting data effectively requires both artistic and technical skills for identifying the message or insight it needs to convey and choosing the correct representation guided by visualisation techniques \citep{chi_taxonomy_2000}. Working with data is a complex task requiring both technical and cognitive skills to that people spend years training to achieve. Working on asking the right questions; figuring out where to look for data and how to access it; improving their visualisation craft; experimenting and figuring out the best practices for different types of data in that suit different contexts; ultimately becoming professional data analysts and visualisers. Sites like InformationIsBeautiful\footnote{\url{https://informationisbeautiful.net}}, Visualising Data\footnote{\url{http://www.visualisingdata.com/}} and Flowing Data\footnote{\url{https://flowingdata.com}} are good examples of people achieving that level of professionalism. Furthermore the data in question, the data that smart city uses and the citizens produce, is data at scale and complexity (i.e., big data), involving quite complex constructs such as demographics, geospacial patterns, trends etc., and is inherently complex to process, analyse and to understand, which makes it far more challenging to make it work for ordinary citizens.

Indeed, it appears at the moment that citizens need these professional analysts or information engineers to mediate these processes that make data work for citizens actively participating through data. Going forward, what is the role of a professional data analyst in these processes? Is there a process that makes citizens smarter and able to participate directly in decision making that involves data at the city, country or even global level? To make it truly democratic, the challenge is in how can those skill become transferable to ordinary citizens to do it for themselves or built into information systems that can automate those processes for them. How much of can professional expertise be
replaced? Or perhaps the solution is a mix of all three things: professional, automation, and smarter citizens? These are the challenges that the research in this thesis is looking to explore and address.

\subsection{Stakeholder Groups}
\label{sub:stakeholders}
From smart city management to citizen sensing, data is the epicentre of our worlds, communities, and research. Despite the pervasiveness of the term, there is still little critical understanding of what data might mean for different people and in different contexts (e.g., smart cities, business, research, community). Whether the term might be used to refer to `big data' analytics and statistics, or to the much broader collection of diverse ‘things’ like images, texts, audio etc. Interpretations of data and the meaning people draw from it is intrinsically liked to the motivations, contexts and situations that people find themselves in. Such diversity and ambiguity raises tensions particularly in contexts in which professionals and non-professionals are required to collaborate around data to communicate ideas and produce knowledge. From a Foucauldian perspective there is need to identify a common language, understandings and representations of data to promote discourse \citep{foucault_archaeology_1982,foucault_subject_1982}. That could aid people in knowledge production and help balance the power relationships between citizens and decision-makers, moving towards citizens becoming active users of data and part of the decision-making processes in the smart city.

\subsubsection{Government}
% Government (data generator, data provider, policy maker, data user)
From ancient Egypt and China to modern day countries, governments have held censuses to gather information about their citizenry to inform decisions and understand or avoid risks. Historically censuses were carried out to count the citizens to tax and inform the government about eligible young men for military service. After collecting the data, it took about a year to add it up and provide the statistics. Although the techniques have changed and efficiency has increase when moving to the digital age of computers, the aims of a census have remained pretty much the same. Governments are well established data collectors and the processes in place for polling their citizens are perfected over the years. As mentioned before, opening up that data is becoming a common practice for governments (local and national) and is being integrated into the way they operate. Additionally to data generated through censuses, governments are also releasing data about how they operate and spend public money \citep{marshall_accountable:_2016}. Introducing open data initiatives puts pressure on governments to `reform' current processes of governance to guarantee the continuance of these initiatives as public services go through a digital transformation. Data portals, currently being the main channels for publishing OGD are still supply-driven and lack engagement and tools for effective use of data to help reach all the stakeholder groups \citep[sec. 3]{davies_state_2019}.

\subsubsection{Business/Private Sector}
% Business/Private Sector (data generator, data provider, data user, ?intermediaries?)
Data is probably the biggest business for the private sector companies operating in ICT. In addition to the data that private sector companies generate themselves, having the tools and the know-how how to tap in to open data, they are also benefiting from data published by other institution. It has been suggested that open data has the potential to help generate \$3 to \$5 trillion of economic value annually in the U.S\footnote{\url{https://www.mckinsey.com/business-functions/mckinsey-digital/our-insights/open-data-unlocking-innovation-and-performance-with-liquid-information}}. This huge potential for economic growth has also become a driver, alongside transparency and accountability, for some governments to open up their datasets for re-use. The private sector has been an avid user of open data for years now and has shown its value to help understand demand, use it in strategic planning and produce useful products and services (Open Data 500\footnote{\url{https://www.opendata500.com}} and Open Data Impact Map\footnote{\url{https://opendataimpactmap.org}}). The value of open data for the private sector is clearly illustrated by the Open Data Impact Map, which provide a search-able catalogue of open data project, where two-thirds of organisations on that list are for-profit companies \citep{gurin_open_2019}.

% intermediary
In addition to private sector leveraging open data to improve their products and services, there are number of companies have also become `data intermediaries' \citep{gurin_open_2019} by helping governments publish their data or repackaging existing data for public and business use. There are companies who are providing data portal software for governmental and other institutions (see Section \ref{sub:access-open-data}) based on data-as-a-service model to help publish their data, but recently there are more companies coming out with data-broker tools catering for consumers and the general public. Tools such as LG Inform Plus\footnote{\url{https://about.esd.org.uk}} from the Local Government Association which combine multiple sources of open data and make them available through Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) and file downloads. There are also companies who package data and try visualise data better by using GIS, reporting and graphs. These include companies like OCSI and HACT who provide software for understanding trends in housing market\footnote{\url{https://ocsi.uk/community-insight}} through OGD, Porism\footnote{\url{https://porism.com}} who package OGD for informing neighbourhood plans and MySociety\footnote{\url{https://www.mysociety.org}} who has developed multiple tools for making open data more accessible and promoting new routes for democratic participation from the public.

% competitions, APIs, app developers
% Uber data, zoopla, mobile phone data, AirBnb
Although private sector are mainly data users and generators, they are also increasingly becoming data publishers by opening up anonymised user datasets or accumulated data sources for researchers and developers alike. Companies like AirBnB, Uber, Strava and Zoopla have recognized the potential benefits of opening up data and have published some of their data in order to improve the quality or find new business value for their data, but also to promote collaborations to help understand and tackle some societal problems. This is enabling companies to collaborate with one and other and with research facilities. GovLab’s Data Collaboratives Explorer\footnote{\url{http://datacollaboratives.org/explorer.html}} categorises data collaboratives into six types: \textit{Trusted Intermediaries}, \textit{Prizes and Challenges}, \textit{Research Partnership}, \textit{Intelligence Products}, \textit{API}, \textit{Data Pooling}. The Alan Turing Institute\footnote{\url{https://www.turing.ac.uk}} in London is a good example of such collaborative approaches where often resources are shared between private companies, governments and research. Another example of this is Positium LBS\footnote{\url{https://www.positium.com}}, a university spin off company which collaborates with telecommunication companies, government and research facilities to provide statistical indicators for public institutions about the population derived from big mobile data.

\subsubsection{Journalists/Media}
% Journalists/Media (data user, data generator)
% Choi 2017
The role of a journalists have always been to keep the public informed about governments accountability, business developments and academic and scientific research. In the digital era, \textit{Data-Driven Journalism} \citep{ausserhofer_datafication_2017} is becoming a term that is widely recognised as part of journalistic agenda which uses data as a resource to investigate and/or shed light on matters of public interest. Becoming an effective journalist nowadays requires the skills of a data scientist which has resulted in traditional media outlets dying out and being replaced with online ones. Computer-assisted reporting is becoming mainstream in journalism and major news outlets have their dedicated data visualisation and data science departments solely focused on storytelling with data (New York Times, Guardian). These new `programmer-journalists' \citet{parasie_data-driven_2013} are equipped with new skills and tools to obtain, analyse, visualise and even open up some of the data for citizens. Like initiatives such as Fatal Force where journalists have created a database of unreported killings by the police to highlight racial injustices or making governments responsible by releasing data about chemical attacks and human rights violations in Syria.
% https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2018/04/13/world/middleeast/syria-chemical-attacks-maps-history.html?mtrref=www.nytimes.com
Through automated tools developed by `programmer-journalist', who are often also part of open movements, some of that data can be potentially reported automatically when new data is collected or released, making way to `automated journalism' \citep[ch. 27]{davies_state_2019}. The interdisciplinary nature of data journalism promotes collaborations between different people and organisations to help address social issues. However it has been reported that the working groups are usually small and enclosed, focusing on providing descriptive statistics or visualisations to exploratory questions \citep{choi_characteristics_2017}. Also the majority of the stories in data journalism are focusing on political rather than social issues and mainly using the existing open data \citep{loosen_data-driven_2017}. With the increasing popularity of big social media platforms becoming the new news outlets and politicians constantly calling `fake news' \citep{carson_fake_2017} it is even harder to spark up collaborative efforts between diverse groups of journalist, data scientists and communities of practice to use data for social good.

\subsubsection{Multilateral Organisations}
% Multilateral Organisations (data user, data publisher)
Multilateral organisations \citep[ch. 28]{davies_open_2010} like the United Nations (UN), World Bank, World Trade Organization and the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD) have a role to play as promoters of open data and intermediaries to connect different stakeholders to work together and use data to improve peoples lives. Having the reach across different countries and continents, these organisations have also a key role to play in disseminating knowledge through consortiums and conferences. However a critical lens need to be used to assess whether the successes of open government and open data can be applied in developing countries to achieve the desired social impact \citep{gigler_realizing_2011}. Additionally these organisations also publish data about world economics \footnote{\url{https://data.worldbank.org/}} and demographics \footnote{\url{http://data.un.org/}}. Multilaterals often have the ability to tap into resources that are far greater than any one government and use new techniques and methods to require, process and report on data. In addition to the world organisations there are smaller non profits who promote and use data, aiming to create better standards and a more cohesive data ecosystem. Bodies such as the Open Data Institute who helps develop and promote open data tools and initiatives, and Open Knowledge International who are advocating for knowledge sharing, are also responsible for keeping their finger on the pulse of the developments around OGD through publishing Global Open Data Index reports \footnote{\url{https://index.okfn.org}}.

\subsubsection{Civil Society}
% Civil Society (data user, ?data provider?)
Civil society -- community organisations or groups of citizens with common interests -- who often mediates data to the rest of the community is perhaps the most important link in realising the effective use of data within communities. Civil society is predominantly a data user, through conducting research with it and often publishing its findings to shed light on different matters. Although citizens are mainly data generators, there is particular set of enthusiast among the civil society often called data activists, `hacktivists' or civic hackers, who use civic data to surface concerns and contest decisions through enquiries and visualisations of data \citep{schrock_civic_2016,taylor_hackers_2005}. They often take strong political statements and act as watchdogs for governments and big corporations. In addition to individuals there are also groups of people who operate through Meetups\footnote{\url{https://www.meetup.com}} and collaborative projects. They consist of mostly professionals working with data or researchers who want to contribute their time and skills to for the good of society. Examples of such groups are Open Oakland\footnote{\url{https://openoakland.org}} in San Francisco, US and NE Data\footnote{\url{https://www.nedata.uk/}} in Newcastle, UK. There are also networks such as OpenGov\footnote{\url{https://www.opengovernment.org.uk/}} in UK that combine active citizens and civil society organisations to help make government work better for people through transparency, participation and accountability.

However when it comes to pushing the open data agenda civil society has far more shoes to fill. In addition to being the bridge between the governmental institutions and community groups, it also helps raise awareness around open data, set the standards and course for data publishing, consult and train people on how access and use open data, publish tools to realise open data needs and advocate for new datasets to be released as open data \citep[ch. 24]{davies_open_2010}. It even helps gather data and openly publish it to promote advocacy and push for change. Being the driving force behind pushing the use of open data for social good, civil society has the biggest responsibility to citizens but also the most challenging role to play. However there is a not always clear where the civil society ends and other stakeholders begin, clear there is an overlap with the research community, data donors and the private sector companies \citep[sec. 3]{davies_open_2010}.

Although civil society is taken as this big force that can achieve anything it sets out, the reality is that it often does not possess the resources it needs to help communities with their data agendas, especially smaller groups who are struggling with lack of financial support or other resources to further their cause. Problematic also is the unwelcome competition between civil society groups trying to do the same thing and applying for the same pots of funding \citep{johnson_neighbourhood_2018}. This also makes it harder for the funding bodies to figure out which one of them would potentially have the greatest impact on the society, ending up often un-evenly or unfairly distributing the resources.

At the same the gap widens between the people who are already engaged and making use of data, and people who might be gain from it the most but do not have any routes to engagement. Instead of competing, non-profits could collaborate with each other, be more transparent and use data and technology to secure more funding \citep{goecks_charitable_2008,johnson_neighbourhood_2018}. Academic research have been turning to open data as a resource to understand populations (using data from censuses) and improve scientific value of the research, but also to study the impacts of open data for society \citep{hossain_state---art_2016,van_schalkwyk_state_2017}.

Initatives like ODDC and Open Data Research Symposium are dedicated to looking at open data research and the impact of it.

Research is still heavily focused on the measurable things in open data, quantity of published datasets and how it is made available, often failing to look at the social impacts of data in research. \citet{davies_state_2019} stress that researchers need to go further to make sure that the work they do is relevant to communities who have been identified as potential beneficiaries of open data.

Fortunately, more and more researchers are engaging in not only looking at the impacts of open data to communities, but trying to help communities use open data to instrument social change. Becoming these data professionals who are trying to make the vision of actively engaged or citizens as active users of data into reality. There has been individual research agendas which seek to understand the current open data practices amongst NGOs and design new tools that would unlock open data for these organisations \citet{erete_storytelling_2016,marshall_accountable:_2016}, but often driven through bigger initiatives that pair up researchers with civil society groups or local and transnational NGOs. These initiatives that are operating under the banner of \textit{``Data Science for Social Good''} are usually part of a universities or research facilities who run fellowships and mentoring programs to aid community organisations harness the power of data science on open data. Examples of such are Data Science for Social Good Foundation\footnote{\url{https://dssg.uchicago.edu}} and Turing's DSSG program\footnote{https://www.turing.ac.uk/collaborate-turing/data-science-social-good} In addition there are also volunteering initiatives such as DataKind for experts who want to contribute their skills for common good of humanity.

Furthermore, researchers are also helping citizens collect data through citizen sensing \citep{balestrini_beyond_2014}, new media and content creation tools \citep{bartindale_our_2019,schofield_bootlegger:_2015,taylor_creating_2010,crivellaro_re-making_2016} and community commissioning technology \citep{garbett_app_2016}. \subsubsection{Citizens}
\label{sub:stake_citizens}
% People/The Public/Citizens (data generator) - neighbourhood data
% Come back to smart citizens
\citet{davies_state_2019} did not include citizens as one of the stakeholder (or it was meshed together with civil society) in open data ecosystem. Perhaps the hope is that other stakeholders will do the work for the citizens and towards unlocking potential of open data for everyone. However for focus of the research in this thesis \textit{citizens} as stakeholders deserve particular attention. The aim of this thesis is to explore ways to support citizens becoming more active participants in the smart city using data -- become smart citizens.

There is often an assumption that opening up data makes it accessible for everyone \citep{berners-lee_year_2010}, however most of the population does not possess the skills and tools to access and use the data. In most cases citizens are actually generating all those data or data are generated about them, without them having any say how and what those data should be concerning (see Section \ref{sub:passive-user}). However that is a rather impoverished notion of the citizens who live in cities, we should think about citizens of how they might be more actively engaged in decision making. How they might be more than sources of information for the smart city, how they can be witting makers, producers and consumers of data. Furthermore, they might even want to use data for their own civic purposes, and they might even want to commission data from other people that they currently cannot get access to. Some people strongly believe that open data is not for the public and \textit{``raw data is for people who know how to use raw data''} \citep{whong_thoughts_2015}. Although as mentioned before (see Section \ref{sub:access-open-data}) and a view shared by others \citep{gitelman_raw_2013,tuomi_data_1999}, data is never raw, it always represents the bias of the collector and the observer. It is not contradicting the idea of open data is not for the public per-se, it is just highlighting that the current representation of data -- whether it is coined as `raw data' or `open data' -- is not accessible and usable in this form to everyone. Data needs to go from open to accessible to usable to be used in active citizen engagement. There is no one size fits all solutions or representation format of data. When designing platforms to support communities and institutions working together with data to address problems in society, significant challenges -- both technical and social -- arise around translation and representations of data that have not yet been addressed.
\newline

Considerations of what data is valuable to whom and in what form needs to be taken into account to better support communities collaborating around data. There are obvious lack of resources in terms of funding and access to tools, however if there are existing competencies in the community it needs to be linked up through developing discourse. Because essentially discourse is about about production of knowledge through language, and through sharing of practices \citep{foucault_archaeology_1982}. Developing discourse and relationships between stakeholders has the potential to increase the social capital within the community. In the time of austerity \citep{lowndes_local_2012} there is a lot relaying on local initiatives and the availability of social capital. This also includes human capital \citep{coleman_social_1988} - combined knowledge and skills of individuals in the community - which is perhaps the most important resource for building up community resilience. Exploring relationships and roles of stakeholders surrounding maintaining, accumulating, sharing and using social capital, or as \citep{granovetter_strength_1983} puts it \textit{bonding}, \textit{bridging} and \textit{linking}, could help understand the important variables that contribute to successful active citizen participation and action. There is empirical evidence that social capital is far more effective at time of crisis \citep{morton_community_2013,aldrich_social_2015} than outside support. Having good connections and shared local knowledge, local organisations and citizen groups are able to respond quicker and better adapt to changing situations. However this does not only apply when disaster strikes, at the time of economic disparity, strong knowledge society and social capital are key for tackling inequalities \citep[ch. 7]{lin_social_2004} and moving towards the active citizen participation in the smart city. However, what are the new roles of different actors involved and the social capital surrounding the active use of data by citizens? This is coming back to the question of what is the role of expert professionals and how much of their expertise can be replaced? Either can it be handed down to the citizens themselves, or can it be pushed into smart interfaces that allow the do the intelligent processing for them? Or is there a middle ground or balance between those two things? How much can be done by computer and information systems and how much training of citizens is required?

\subsection{Communites, Publics and Networks}
\label{sub:com_net}
% Le Dantec communities = publics
The usage of social capital -- the exchange and sharing of skills and other resources in order to produce knowledge \citet{coleman_social_1988} -- is usually contained within the a specific group or community that share an interest, issue or are working towards a common goal. In the literature, may have identified these communities differently and there is a bit of confusion around it. \citet[Orig. 1927]{dewey_public_1954} refers to these communities as \textit{publics}, and breaks that down by saying that the reason why publics form is because individuals are all affected in some way by an issue or social condition which makes them come together. However, these people might be complete strangers and they also might be affected in different ways by an issue. Building on Dewey and on \citet{warner_publics_2005} (`publics' and 'counterpublics'), more modern takes of publics have manifested such as \citet{mccarthy_taking_2015}, and \citet{le_dantec_designing_2016}. For \citet{le_dantec_designing_2016} the design of publics are looked from a point of attachments to issues and linked to infrastructures \citep[ch. 2]{le_dantec_designing_2016}. \citet{mccarthy_taking_2015} conception of publics is more linked to \textit{media publics} -- strangers who come together and share things through different medias and do it because they got a common interest, although they are quite diverse and different people. \textit{Community of Practice} (CoP) however is a group of people who share a common set of ways of doing things, common practices and a common set of values. They might have different levels and types of capability and capacity, but they all working together to address and achieve common goals. According to \citet{lave_situated_1991} the important structural elements of CoPs are the \textit{domain} of knowledge, the notion of a \textit{community} and its \textit{practice}. Domain is some form of common denominator that defines a group of people, notion of a community are the connections and interactions that individuals share in the domain and the practice is the focus and activities that engage in to produce, share and maintain the knowledge. A classic example of a CoP are healthcare professionals -- hospital staff, nurses, doctors etc -- who aim to work together to deliver a good healthcare service to people. On the other hand \textit{Community of interest} can simply be referred to as individuals who have a common interest in something. They can be however completely different people from different backgrounds and do not have to share any common practices. In some cases they do not even have to practice the thing they are interested in.

In the case of the research conducted in this thesis, the groups of people, communities or publics are referred to as individuals who are connected by the proximity, interest, issue or any combination of that. This can be individuals living in close proximity working together to improve the neighbourhood and tackle local issues, but can also be a virtual community \citep{rheingold_virtual_2000} that is present on online platforms such as Facebook, Reddit, GitHub or Quora or mediated through some other medium \citep{disalvo_neighborhood_2008}. However, one could say that it can also be a combination of publics and CoPs, because we have citizens, data professionals, charity organisations, activists, people with different skill-sets and capabilities that can be utilised and used as a resource, all working together to make use of data in civic participation. This combination or mix of different publics and CoPs is actually forming \textit{Community Networks}. The emphasis here is on the use of technologies and information systems to leverage data for community problem solving. It aligns with ideas of \citet{carroll_trajectory_2003} and has the characteristics of \citet[ch. 2]{clement_connecting_2012}'s proposed \textit{networked communities}. Also similar concept echo from \citet{varnelis_networked_2008} where they use the term \textit{networked publics} to refer to how individuals and groups engage with each-other and communicate ideas and knowledge using the capabilities of modern ICT and media. The use of publics here emphases a more engaged population, as opposed to passive and consumptive. \cite{varnelis_networked_2008} also points out that networked publics are far more complex in the way people are using capabilities of ICT to communicate ideas, produce knowledge and take action. Furthermore, these networks are \textit{``bottom-up, top-down, as well as side-to-side''} \citep[p. 3]{varnelis_networked_2008}, bridging together public, professional, institutional and personal.

\subsection{Community Informatics and Information Systems}
\label{sub:ci_is}
Information systems (IS) and information system design (ISD) have strong theoretical and technical background more often rooted in engineering and computing and with a strong focus to business and commercial endeavours. The aim for ISD is to build a best solution that solves an engineering problem and comes up with an artefact -- an information system. So the focus in highly on the design of the artefact to address that engineering issue. Other issues, also pointed out by many researchers \citep{carroll_participatory_2007,stillman_community_2009,hirschheim_exploring_1996,leonardi_materiality_2008,hakken_social_2003}, such as user groups, interfaces, usability, contexts, choice of technology, indented uses; become unimportant without the artefact. These issues however relate to the social aspects of IS which scholars involved in the ISD started calling \textit{social informatics} (SI) \citep{bowker_social_1997,kling_learning_2000}. SI puts the users of the system in the centre of the design processes of information systems and looks at how to address those non-engineering problems in an organisational context. For \citet{lamb_reconceptualizing_2003} it is important that we re-conceptualise `users as social actors' in these contexts.

However, working with communities the main aim is to address on the issues that people have or the things they want to change in the community rather than the problems of technology. Similarly they are not bounded to an organisational structures and uses of particular technologies is a specific context. As a result scholars such as \citep[ch. 6]{hakken_social_2003}, \citet{bradley_social_2006}, \citet{gurstein_introduction_2000} and \citet{carroll_participatory_2007} have expanded on these ideas of SI to go beyond the context of organisations and users. A new field of \textit{community informatics} (CI) \citet{gurstein_introduction_2000} has emerged that is focused on the design and implementation of technologies that help local communities address their issues. Later definitions of CI have also taken account other fields that influence the CI in practice and expanded to things like equal opportunities; recognition of importance of ``lived physical community''; user-focused underestanding of ICT; applied social leadership entrepreneurship and creativity \citep[p. 12]{gurstein_what_2007}. The overarching aim of CI is to overcome the `digital divides' through the design and implementation of ICT that enable and empower citizens.

If the focus is on the uses of data and information system, CI has actually a dual focus: \textit{``first, the conduct of research about the relationship between the design of ICTs and local communities, and second, the implementation of ICT projects in local communities''} \citep[p. 255]{stillman_community_2009}. Pointing out the still present engineering agenda or \textit{information systems problem solving} on top of the \textit{community problem solving} one. In the critical approach to IS provided by \citet{stillman_capability_2014} they state that despite the differences of IS and CI, it can still be looked at a part of ``fragmented adhocracy'' of IS research, helping to provide CI a theoretical basis. They draw on \citet[p. 17]{hirschheim_exploring_1996}'s federated framework for ISD, and state that it can be adopted to CI. The fact that \citet{hirschheim_exploring_1996} used social action theory and change as lens to analyse the framework makes it more relevant to CI \citep{stillman_capability_2014}. Figure \ref{fig:object_system_classes} shows the original and the adapted object system classes framework. It is presented as a two axes matrix where one axes shows the \textit{domains of change}, the objects that are changed by ISD; and the other axes presents the orientations that show the purpose of change instrumented by ISD. Importantly, the framework also distinguishes between two control objects -- humans and artefacts. However, there are two issues with this framework from the perspective of CI: firstly, its one dimensional perspective, also noted by \citet{stillman_capability_2014}, that lends itself to have repetition and overlap in concepts across domains; and secondly, the role of technology only being utilised and being prevalent at instrumentation of control. To address this literal gap in the framework and to contribute to the field of CI, this thesis presents research that explores the role of data and technology in all the orientations of the framework and towards active citizen engagement, participation and instrumentation of change.
\newline

Although there have been worked done exploring technologies and mechanisms for citizens as active data users, they are limited to working on one aspect of interactions with data (e.g., production, access, use) and not taking a holistic approach needed \citep[cp. 2]{clement_connecting_2012} to address the dual focus of this research \citep{stillman_capability_2014}. Additionally, the research done, has not looked at it thought the lens of digital civics \citep{olivier_digital_2015}, civic advocacy, activism and action. Furthermore, a lot of the work done around the design and engagement of data with citizens in a smart city has been done by researchers with social science or humanities background, and not done from a perspective of an information engineer working with citizens trying to address the community problem solving activities. According to \citet{bradley_social_2006} this type of research requires a \textit{``new type of engineer''} to be educated who has a much broader grasp on social issues and their importance towards the design and uses of technologies; however, at the same time have the technological know-how and background to not resist ICT and think more about the aspects of human-computer interaction (or human-data interaction) and interpretation in CI \citep{stillman_capability_2014}.

This literature review has provided an overview of the concept of the smart city and the role of citizens in it, as well as providing a thorough review of the different ways that citizens can participate in the smart city through data -- either passively or actively. The literature review has help put forward a concept of more actively engaged citizen (i.e., smart citizen) of the smart city who participates in producing, accessing, understanding, using and making use of data to participate in decision making processes. Furthermore, the chapter has given an overview of the current state of open data; the way its made available, accessible, comprehensible and usable to citizens; and the stakeholders involved in it. Thus allowing to understand what it means to make data usable to citizens in civic context and in advocating. Additionally, highlighting the current role that expert professionals would have to play in this, because the data that the smart city concerns is large at scale and complex at nature; weather the expertise can be handed down to citizens or pushed into intelligent information systems. Although, there is an overlap in critiques of participatory design (or social design) and information system design for active civic engagement and participation, the tools implemented and the research conducted for the use of data for purposes of civic advocacy has had a narrow focus with little empirical research. Additionally, the research in this space tends to stay either in humanitarian fields or in systems engineering and there is little crossovers. Therefore, there is a need for empirical studies with citizens to investigate the active use of data in complex civic processes to find a middle ground between technological determinism and social constructionism, providing a holistic model and pragmatic practice that responds to critiques and helps guides this type of research in the future.
\newline

This thesis presents research conducted with citizens in a `smart city' that aimed to explore the role of technologies in supporting active citizen participation and advocacy through data. In order to investigate the implications of active citizen participation to data, an participatory action design research approach was taken which resulted in the design of tools for data in community contexts. This thesis supports an activist approach and presents three case studies to look at different modes of active citizen participation using data: data generation or making (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}); data access, interpretation and comprehension (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}); and data use in civic advocacy (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval} and Chapter \ref{chap:cdi_in_action}). Throughout the thesis a dual agenda was considered, where both engineering problem solving agenda and community problem solving activities was given focus, resulting in new challenges for community data interaction and a holistic approach towards use of data by local communities (Chapter \ref{chap:cdi}). In Chapter \ref{chap:cdi_in_action} the approach is trialled through the use of technologies and methods of inquiries. The discussion Chapter (Chapter \ref{chap:discussion}) of the thesis will show how the findings of the studies respond to the critiques of active civic participation and effective use of data by citizens, and how the model of Community-Data Interaction supports the future agendas of active public engagement and democratisation of data science.

Finally, there is the question of what makes people participate and whether these new digital tools guarantee increase in participation? Multiple researchers have studied the way participation is affected when digital tools are implemented \citep{conroy_e-participation_2006,macintosh_e-democracy_2008,kim_e-participation_2012,macintosh_characterizing_2004,kitsing_online_2011}. Perhaps the most influential digital participation to governance is internet voting (I-voting) which is gaining popularity countries like Estonia, Switzerland, US, Canada and France. One of the pioneers in the area Estonia, has been using I-voting for all of its elections (local, parliamentary and EU parliamentary) since 2005. Although the participation through e-voting has increased, the overall voter turnout has stayed pretty much the same\footnote{\url{https://www.valimised.ee/en/archive/statistics-about-internet-voting-estonia}}. \citet{kitsing_online_2011} argues that though internet voting has a quicker turnover and is cheaper for the government, it does not necessarily make the process more inclusive. People who are already activity engaged will have another channel for participation, however the people who have never engaged will not necessarily start now because of a new technologically superior system. \citet{kitsing_online_2011} adds however, that having insights from a real world use case helps us understand these processes, and the heterogeneous nature of them, a bit better which opens up a space for reconfigurement of these processes.

Data however does not always have to refer to something that simply can be counted and represented as quantifiable values (e.g., `big data') \citep{michael_toward_2016,mcmillan_data_2016}, it can be much broader and diverse set of `things' often characterised as contemporary media such as 3D printed physical fabrications of data \citep{nissen_data-things:_2015}; travelling suitcases filled with stories and memories \citep{crivellaro_re-making_2016}; interactive physical graphs \citep{sweeney_pixel_2019}. Often a good way to engage people with data is through installations and art practices that use technology coupled with engineering practices to show data under a critical lens. Examples of these kind of projects are Take a Bullet for The City\footnote{\url{http://www.digiart21.org/art/take-a-bullet-for-the-city}}, which uses data from New Orleans Police Department to fire a blank shot every time when a report of a firearm discharge occurs and Invisible Airs\footnote{\url{http://yoha.co.uk/invisible}}, which uses different `machines' to visualise the public spending of Bristol City Council. In may cases this way of `releasing data' is far more effective than a flat machine readable file on some data portal and gets people more engaged with the issue in hand. In addition to the benefits of being able to combine digital and non-digital tools to visualise data, situated urban displays also enables people to keep the data in the place it was collected from and with the community it belongs to. For example using chalk and tape to visualise locally collected data to raise awareness around local issues and promote dialog between residents \citep{koeman_what_2014} or using public spaces to display private household data in order to promote behavioural change around energy usage \citep{moere_comparative_2011,bird_pulse_2010}. It is known however that the way in which different data comes to be interpreted is always entangled with and dependent upon both contexts and situations \citep{suchman_plans_1985,taylor_data--place:_2015}. While, there is a tendency for civic technology projects to place emphasis on quantitative data -- on the assumption that quantitative data may be relevant to people -- qualitative data describing things that cannot be easily quantified or measured are not less important. Indeed, it is often this type of `data' that helps people making sense of what matters and help generate actionable knowledge.
