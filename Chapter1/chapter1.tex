This chapter describes related research around the use of data in knowledge creation and citizen participation in democracy. The use of data in civic life and within communities spans across a wide range of disciplines from technical fields like sensing networks, pervasive computing and information engineering to human computer interaction (HCI) and computer-supported collaborative work (CSCW). First two sections of the literature review give an overview of the increasing use of data in decision-making through the lens of smart city and looks at the ways that citizens are involved or not involved in these data driver processes. Subsequent section describe the new ways of creating knowledge sources and opening them up for everyone to use and re-purpose. This also includes opportunities for new interpretation of open data sources published by the governments or other data generators. Forth section describes the research around data production and different ways citizens can generate their own data sources, whether it is to contribute to science or to evidence local issues and contest decisions. Fifth section describes the current state-of the-art of data visualisation and storytelling through data which is essential for data science practices and the effective us of data. 

Drawing on work done by State of Open Data\footnote{\url{https://www.od4d.net/stateofopendata}} project and its contributors, section six tries to map out the relevant stakeholder groups around data production, use and dissemination of derived knowledge from it. It is essential to understand these complex relationships and roles of stakeholders, as we are increasingly coming to contend with significant challenges and questions relating to what data may be relevant and for whom; how to translate and represent it; as well as how different representations might be combined to better support communication and sense-making of complex information that sits at the boundaries between what may be private, shared and public. Building from that, section seven reviews the relevant literature about communities of practice and the knowledge networking within these communities. 

Finally the last section looks at the research into civic advocacy and action with the support of data and technology, including the existing models of successful and unsuccessful citizen decision-making. This literature review gives an overview of the underlying concepts touched in this thesis, however for each case study a more detailed and specific body of research is reviewed and presented.

\section{Participation in Smart City}
\label{smart-city}
A vision of a smart city, where information technology is woven into the city infrastructure helping to optimise resources and improve the lives of citizens \citep{chourabi_understanding_2012}, has been greatly discussed over the past five years\footnote{\url{https://trends.google.com/trends/explore?date=all&q=smart\%20city}}. Perhaps considered fictional at the time, but these ideas where echoed from concepts like \textit{`third wave'} of computing or \textit{`ubiquitous computing'} as it was envisioned by \citet{weiser_computer_1991,weiser_computer_1993} in the early 90s. For many years now commercial companies -- IBM, Cisco and Siemens, to name a few -- have been `hooking up’ the city with sensors and other information technologies to gather all the data about the city in the hopes of solving some of the old and new `wicked’ problems of society. There is a push for using data in all aspects of life -- to optimise and organise resources [REF], to reduce carbon footprint and improve sustainability [REF], but also to facilitate novel modes of participation [REF], democracy and social interaction [REF]. The discussion of what makes the city smart is still ongoing \citep{nam_smart_2011,foth_handbook_2009,su_smart_2011,klein_smart_2008}. However, with the increase of urbanisation and expansion of cities, this trend is more that likely to grow and more businesses are coming up solutions that can supposedly ``\textit{compute away local problems}'' \citep{townsend_smart_2013}.

% (Thrift 2014)
Smart cities live off data -- traditionally acquired through a rigorous collection process ran by the government (i.e. conducting censuses), increasingly now captured using sensor networks located on lampposts, roadsides, tree and rooftops \citep{zanella_internet_2014,su_smart_2011}, but also scraped from the web and social media platforms... \citep{puiu_citypulse:_2016,batty_pulse_2010}. 
% Data driven decision making
% (Kontokosta 2017 - Urban Informatics for Social Good)
% Forth 2009

A popular way of showing and using all that accumulated data is through real time \textit{`data dashboards'} \citep{kitchin_real-time_2014-1,kitchin_knowing_2015}. Almost all aspiring smart cities have at least one dashboard in hand to create and visualise statistical indicators, benchmark the city against others and drive the governance of the city \citep{kitchin_knowing_2015}. \citet{kitchin_knowing_2015} broadly categorises the utilisation of these dashboards into two -- managerial and contextual. First is more linked to assessment and performance measurement of the city governance and second prioritises the use of indicators for informing policy. Developing statistical indicators and visualising them through dashboards is a necessary and valid approach in city management, specially when trying to condense down all that messy data into some useful chunks that could be represented and used by decision-makers. However, \citet[p. 24]{kitchin_knowing_2015} points out the issue that often these initiatives are presented as the mirrored model or an exact digital twin \citep{bolton_gemini_2018} of the city through numbers -- ``\textit{visualised facts; that reflect the truth about the world}''. What these dashboards actually are, is a representation of abstractions that make up a view of the city. Unfortunately, in many cases these dashboards are representations of the view of the city from the perspective of management. This means that the data collection and the problems are framed by institutional actors to fit into the governance processes of the city and often done without engaging the concerned publics \citep{asad_creating_2017}. Similar cirques have come from 
\cite{greenfield_against_2013} and \cite{townsend_smart_2013}, arguing that currently the power of making educated decisions based on that data is in the hands of the big tech giants, whose software works in controlled centralised locations. In addition to concerns around transparency and ethics surrounding surveillance and control, this lack of inclusivity in smart cities can lead to narrow representation of views and desires of society in governance \citep{mcmillan_data_2016}.

In the modern democratic society, citizens should be also actively participating in all matters of civic life, including political discourse \citet{hoffman_participation_2012}, through civic engagement activities run by the state but also citizen initiatives. All of this potential however comes at the time when the UK, among other countries in the EU, is subjected to acute economic austerity which has tragic effects to local governance. Meaning that public services delivery has severely suffered and in some cases even outsourced or privatised. However, a lot of the solutions provided by institutional actors are not relevant to the real issues in the community \citep{boehner_data_2016}. People living in the communities often have a much keener sense of what the real problems are \citep{disalvo_neighborhood_2008}. With the age of internet and information technologies becoming more accessible, digital civics approaches \citep{olivier_digital_2015} have been emerging from HCI related disciplines, which promote active participation in civic life and engagement in service creation and delivery, with hopes of increasing civic professionalism \citep{boyte_boyte_2010} within the society and increasing the dialog between citizens and the government \citet{boyte_everyday_2004}. Examples of these kind of study areas are, but not exclusively; community commissioning \citep{garbett_app_2016,balaam_feedfinder:_2015,taylor_creating_2010,taylor_supporting_2009}, democratic discourse \citep{johnson_community_2017,johnson_reflections_2016,valkanova_myposition:_2014,crivellaro_contesting_2015} situated voting \citep{vlachokyriakos_postervote:_2014,golsteijn_sens-us:_2016,taylor_viewpoint:_2012,behrens_smart_2014,hespanhol_vote_2015} and community infographics \citep{koeman_what_2014,claes_street_2013,hutchison_comparative_2011,lindley_surfacing_2017}. What is common with these studies is that they are moving out from the research facilities and labs to conduct investigations in the real world. \citet{rogers_interaction_2011} calls this approach research \textit{`in the wild'} -- carrying out in situ studies, and rather than conducting observations of existing practices, focusing on local issues and designing disruptive technologies to address concerns and needs of specific population.

Citizens are not only moving around in this sensed environment (i.e. smart city), they are also contributing some of the data to it by using smart devices in their pockets -- smart-phones. \citet{townsend_smart_2013} calls them \textit{"smart-city construction kits"} or \textit{"digital Swiss Army knifes"} which are increasingly becoming the sources of data for studying human behaviour, but they are also becoming a platform for new bottom up civic movements. The method of data collection from these devices can be either voluntarily or done passively by using some type of service on the them (see more in Section \ref{data-driven-participation}). For example there is a growing body or research into usage of mobile phone call logs for coming up with near-real time models of spatial interactions [REF] and deriving early statistical indicators [REFs]. Also people are tapping into data generated by social media to gather opinions and model large scale human behaviours.

At the other end of perspective, we have already witnessed disruptive social movements through the use of social media platforms \citep{howard_opening_2011,eltantawy_social_2011} and seen uses of these devices in urban planning by people contributing their movement data while cycling in the city to improve the infrastructure \citep{le_dantec_planning_2015}.

There are differently new opportunities that emerge around designing tools that successfully facilitate this kind of data collection which tries to foster collective intelligence. However caution needs to be taken, not to fall into the same pitfalls again by taking data at face value. As \cite{le_dantec_planning_2015} and \citet{kitchin_knowing_2015} observed, it is often appealing for city managers to base their decisions solely on what numbers are showing and maps are identifying, rather than engaging directly with the citizens to also considering what people on the ground are experiencing. Including not considering that sometimes technology excludes groups of people who are snuggling with low tech literacy or just does not have resources to access them. People need to be reminded that big data can only show the `what is', but can not reveal the `how', `why' and most importantly `what is not' \citep{mayer-schonderger_big_2013}.

The promises from tech giants to `wire up' the city to solve some of the `wicked' problems might have been overstated or perhaps a bit premature. Many fancy dashboards and data silos later, society is starting to realise that data is not an end in itself. Having all the numbers does not give all the answers, to make sense and use the data, we must ask the right questions or possess the knowledge relevant to the issue at hand which can only obtained through a collaborative effort. This means we are not relaying on one piece of knowledge, but multiple and obtained from different sources -- smart city sensors, experts, city managers, researchers, decision-makers and citizens. 
% need some literature here

This can be only done by opening up these processes and moving away from technocratic governance and corporatisation of city management \citep{kitchin_real-time_2014-1}. \cite{kitchin_knowing_2015} encourages smart city initiatives to openly recognise their flaws, uncertainty and levels of error, and accept the fact that they do not reflect the world as it actually is, but actively frame and produce the world through measuring and observations of it. Meaning there is no one true image of the city and everything depends on the perspective of the observer. Clearly, the way forward is to open up and democratise data and technology to help integrate a multiplicity of views, and data, into the smart city.

\citet{townsend_smart_2013} wrote in his book \textit{Smart Cities: Big Data, Civic Hackers, and the Quest for a New Utopia} that the answer to the question of what is the smartest city, is \textit{"...the one you live in"}. People make the city smart not the technology, although technology can certainly lend a helpful hand of defining and informing people of some of the issues in the city, or helping to communicate ideas and knowledge. Whilst there is great potential in using data in these contexts, and there is an active effort being done to integrate sensors into our spaces, meaning that lot of technology is already in place, it has to be configured and shaped in a way that it serves the needs of citizens, not technology itself. There is an important role for designers and technologist to play here, working together with citizens to develop smart city tools for data collection, access, sensemaking that promote civic participation and aim to democratise data science practices in the smart city. Similar design principles have guided the development of case studies in this thesis, where the developed tools and technology are direct responses to the needs of citizens.

Historically people were mainly connecting by trade, post, telephone or face-to-face interactions. In the digital age it is becoming more commonplace to use computerised systems and data to mediate communication of ideas and opinions. Internet enables us to establish enormously complex networks, connecting us with different people and helping to engage in number of activities -- individual and collaborative. Although not always explicit, all participation in the cyberspace is driven through data sharing and use. This can manifest for example in participation on social media platforms like Twitter and Facebook \citep{crivellaro_pool_2014}, using personal informatics tools\footnote{\url{http://www.personalinformatics.org/}} for self reflection or improvement [REF], or engaging with collaborative platforms to work towards a common goal \citep{salovaara_collective_2006}.

Popular examples of latter are Wikipedia, a crowd powered free encyclopedia and OpenStreetMap, an open source geographical database compiled by community of volunteer mappers.

There is a lot potential for crowdsourced data collection approaches to broaden the participation and create alternative sources of data for public use in multiple domains. \citet{salim_urban_2015} came up with a taxonomy of interaction and participation to assess different types of urban computing solutions. Their paper surveyed a variety of applications in mobile crowdsensing, urban informatics, urban probes and interventions, and Internet of Things, categorising them in terms of \textit{``the technologies used, the level of participation they stimulate, the participation scale they support, the manipulation and effects mode they enable, the interaction mode and scale they enable''} \citep[p. 44]{salim_urban_2015}. Although urban computing systems my vary in shapes and sizes, they all aim for maximum participation from the public. Through the reflection on projects \citet{salim_urban_2015} came up with list of strategies that put emphasis on identification of needs and tensions through active dialog with stakeholders, and providing open and accessible tools that help citizens construct their own rationals for participating. Clearly the key for success is not to provide the best technology for participation, but to prioritise the social interactions surrounding it.

Certainly, civic engagement is one domain where these technologies are being introduced with an aim to increase participation in democracy through data. These new approaches have particularly peaked interest of city planners, who see this as a great opportunity for large scale participation through usage of data and pervasive technology. In other words, trying to harness the power of crowds through data science \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016}. There is a ongoing discourse around usage of crowdsourced geospatial data to mediate civic interaction, both in term of its validity for participation in democracy \citep{le_dantec_planning_2015} and as a tool for decision-making \citep{brabham_crowdsourcing_2009,tenney_data-driven_2016}.

However not all participation is created equal. There are two distinct modes of data driven participation: passive and active. Passive participation refers to the use of some automated system (e.g., data logger or a service on a phone) or as a result of a `data exhaust', that sends off anonymous data to be analysed by algorithm implemented on automated systems. In big geography and urban planning it is often referred to as volunteered geographic information (VGI) and it has been argued that it increases participation and the representativness of data \citep{goodchild_citizens_2007}. 

% https://www.betterpoints.ltd/ - behavioural change platform

Examples of these type of systems include projects like MetaPos\footnote{\url{https://www.gsa.europa.eu/r-d/gnss-project-portfolio}}, Habits\footnote{\url{https://habitsdata.org}} and Sunset\footnote{\url{http://sunset-project.eu}}, each having a mobile application which collects anonymous location data to be used in spatial modelling that aim to aid organisation of infrastructure and/or traffic in the city. Although these applications have elements of feedback in terms of people seeing their analysed mobility patterns or being able to share travel tips with others on social media, they are still contained within the frame of the research or agendas of city authorities. Furthermore, some of these agendas can also be linked to behavioural change (not necessarily bad), which is administered through incentives. This \textit{`citizens as sensors'} form of participation is often preferred by corporation, busy citizens and city managers, because of its low impact on citizens everyday lives and reduced workload on city officials to actively engage their citizenry \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016}. 

Active data driven participation also leverages capabilities of modern day information technologies and internet, however it tries to actively involve and engage in the design and reorganisation of urban environments. These active partition approaches such as Public Participation Geographic Information Systems (PPGIS) as perceived to be more grassroots, enabling citizens to actively influence decision-making processes \citep{sieber_public_2006,jankowski_towards_2009}. Examples of such systems include FixMyStreet\footnote{\url{https://www.fixmystreet.com}} popular in the UK and its Norwegian clone FiksGataMi\footnote{\url{http://www.fiksgatami.no}}; SeeClickFix\footnote{\url{https://seeclickfix.com}}, PublicStuff\footnote{\url{http://www.publicstuff.com}} and Street Bump\footnote{\url{http://www.streetbump.org}} in the US. Although PPGIS in its core is \textit{`bottom-up'} approach, its technological realisation is often more \textit{`top-down'} and serves government interests \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016}. Issue here is that the scope for these technologies originate from institutional actors, who phrase the issue from their perspective. Although helpful for citizens as well, this can end up with a product that helps generate data that is specific to the formulation of governmental concerns.

Although these methods might appear to be an easy win for doing large-scale participation, they come with a lot of challenges.

\section{Citizen Sensing}
\label{citizen-sensing}
In addition to sensors in the city, passive and active data-driven participation that supports the algorithmic smart city agenda, there is also citizen sensing. An approach that usually involves, but not exclusively, citizens using a sensing technology (or mobile phones) to voluntarily gather data. Citizen sensing is not exactly the same as crowdsourcing -- where people are seen as workers who complete tasks or collect data for the work `requester', often getting payed for their participation. Citizen sensing should not also be taken as exactly the same as Citizen Science, which recruits non-scientist members of the public to take part of science projects volunteering their time for data collection and/or analysis \citet{cohn_citizen_2008}. Nor should not simply taking part of study -- for example participating in a sleep study or filling in a survey -- be confused with Citizen Science. According to the network of National Contact Points for Science with and for Society (SiS.net) definition, Citizen Science projects are projects where citizens contribute to actual science research \citep{eitzel_citizen_2017}. Examples of these kind of projects are BioBlitz and Brittish Bird Watching. It seems that Citizen Science has multiple definitions and often it is down to the `scientists' to decide how they are framing it, however it is not often clear who `is the scientist' \citep{haklay_citizen_2013} in the project. \citet[p. 11]{haklay_citizen_2013} proposes a framework to classify Citizen Science projects which focuses on the level of engagement and involvement of participant in  science activities. It is somewhat similar to \citet{arnstein_ladder_1969}'s \textit{`ladder of participation'} however instead of solely focusing on the power relationships between citizens and decision-makers on a fixed ladder, \citet{haklay_citizen_2013} also points out the processes of obtaining new understandings which fosters curiosity and promotes knowledge creation, and is likely to move people up the ladder of participation. In addition to finding appropriate and sustainable engagement level for the project there is also the question of `who knows best'? Although there have been successful Citizen Science projects like Neighbourhood Nest Watch and Y, there is an ongoing debate on should the `science' be left to the `scientists' should we fully accept citizen contribution to science activities \citep{gabrys_just_2018,cohn_citizen_2008}.

% Maybe add table of different examples through Haklay's model

However there can be other purposes than advancing science for doing citizen data collection. For example for self knowledge (Huck 2014), to raise public awareness around an issue \citep{hasenfratz_participatory_2012,loreto_participatory_2017}(Chapter 16), evidence an issue in the community [REF] or push for change \citep{balestrini_city_2017}. Latter approaches involve community-led investigation where citizens are actively involved in definition of the issue, collection or acquiring of data and the analysis of it. \citet{balestrini_city_2017} looks at this process through a \textit{City-Commons Framework} which nurtures a bottom-up approach to orchestrate citizen sensing and community engagement. Citizen sensing projects that explore issues related to environment and place, often involve some sort of sensing technologies and PPGIS or mapping coupled with HCI for geospatial technologies. Examples of such platforms are Aircasting\footnote{\url{http://aircasting.org}} and SmartCitizen\footnote{\url{https://smartcitizen.me}} which both consist of lightweight sensing equipment and GIS application for exploring the data. More projects like this have been developed around the world now with community volunteers using sensing equipment and collecting data to promote dialogue on environmental issues and push for positive change in the community. Citizen sensing provides an opportunity to rethink the role of data in civic participation and focus on digital technology and mechanisms that could support these grassroots approaches.


\section{Open Movements, Data and Knowledge}
\label{open-data}
Just like all ideas and opinions where historically shared through face-to-face encounters, all data, information and knowledge was also exchanged orally and spread freely. However with rise of the printing press in the 1400s, knowledge started to be seen as individual rather than a collective property. Soon multiple copyright laws were put in place and enforced by governments across the world to protect the property rights of the publishers. With the coming of the Internet, which made sharing and copying of information even easier and more broader, resulted in copyright laws to become stricter and more unified around the globe. In response to the prevalence of the copyright laws a new political an social movement was forced that seeks to promote sharing of content and resources for the good of the society. Movement operates in a spirit of transparency, collaboration, re-use and free access. Open Movement is seen as an umbrella term that encompasses things like open data, open government, open development, open science.

Open movements aim to promote participatory processes through sharing of data, knowledge and outputs as well as source code of technical implementations. The openness of these projects and the resources they provide is not always defined just the same. Open Data and knowledge is commonly defined by the Open Definition\footnote{\url{http://opendefinition.org}}, which states: \textit{``Open data and content can be freely used, modified, and shared by anyone for any purpose''}. Most successful example of open knowledge sharing is Wikipedia, founded in 2001, the same year as Creative Commons, which aims to facilitate sharing of creative works in public domain and is used by Wikimedia to publish all its content. Wikipedia is an online encyclopedia where anyone can become a contributor of content which is interlinked and publicly shared. According to internet rankings it is in the top five most visited sites on the web\footnote{\url{https://www.alexa.com/topsites}}. Although just about anyone can edit and add content on Wikipedia it has been illustrated that the information shared has value and quality like any other professional source of knowledge \citep{giles_internet_2005}. Open knowledge also extends itself to publishing scientific research, where people are advocating for open access to publications, specially if the research is publicly funded \citep{garciapenalvo_open_2010}.

This also includes publishing of all the data and tools to reproduce the results in the benefits of public knowledge \citep{molloy_open_2011}.

However with software it is not always clear what is freely usable and to what extent, there is difference between `open' and `free' software. The initial idea of \textit{open source} software was proposed by the members of Free Software Foundation\footnote{\url{https://www.fsf.org/}} who promoted sharing of software and design documents for re-use and re-purposing in other projects. Open source software are published using open source licences\footnote{\url{https://opensource.org/licenses}} which dictates the specific rules of how people can use and modify the source code. Perhaps the most well known and successful open source project is Linux, created by Linus Tonvalds in 1991, which kernel code has being used in multiple popular operating systems, including UNIX-like GNU operating system which was initiated by Richard Stallman. Nowadays sharing and collaborating on software development manifests in geographically dispersed programmers working together on projects using online collaboration interfaces \citep{west_contrasting_2005} such as GitHub\footnote{\url{https://github.com}}, Bitbuket\footnote{\url{https://bitbucket.org}} and SourceForge\footnote{\url{https://sourceforge.net/}}. Nowadays models of free and open source software are becoming more popular to the point that even commercial companies are publishing their software on these platforms, potentially sharing their intellectual property with their competitors \citep{west_challenges_2006}. This extends itself to the idea that there is `strength in numbers' or as Linus's Law states (also proven by the development of Linux Kernel) \textit{''given enough eyeballs, all bugs are shallow''} \citep{raymond_cathedral_2001}, meaning that if there is more people working on the a project it will ultimately produce higher quality software. In addition better quality software however, people working on these projects have also reported that they are motivated by participation and sense of belonging to a collective, and sharing of knowledge and skills with them \citep{lakhani_why_2003}. People working in the spirit of transparency and openness, sharing of skills and knowledge on these new virtual platforms, inserts trust and opens up new opportunities for these systems to promote new ways of using them for social good.

Then there are Open Data, data that \textit{``can be freely accessed, used, modified and shared by anyone for any purpose -- subject only, at most, to requirements to provide attribution and/or share-alike''}\footnote{\url{http://opendatahandbook.org/glossary/en/terms/open-data}}, potentially the most transformative branch of Open Movement. When defined by the Open Definition it has to be open both; legally, licensed under open licence; and technically, without attached costs, in bulk and machine-readable format. Among that data are Open Governmental Data (OGD) \citep{ubaldi_open_2013}, one branch of open government alongside with other open governmental initiatives like Open Budgeting, data that is collected or which collection is commissioned by governmental entities and then published using open licences. Main aims for publishing this data are to serve the means of transparency and accountability of the government, unlock the potentials of social and commercial re-use of that data, but also to promote new ways of democratic participation. Governments, including UK's, are publishing data about land use, environment, governmental services, citizenry and their health and also public spending and expenditure. However the breadth and depth of data published differs from country to country, and are also constantly changing as new legislation's, data collection methods and rules are implemented. Open Movement advocates and organisations are keeping their hand on the pulse by releasing yearly reports of the current state of open governments. Most well known of them are Open Data Barometer\footnote{\url{https://opendatabarometer.org}} published by World Wide Web Foundation and Global Open Data Index\footnote{\url{https://index.okfn.org/}} published by Open Knowledge International which aim to document outcomes, share ideas and knowledge, and spark discussion around open data in governments.

Open data are made `open' usually through open data portals. Leigh Dodds, chair of BathHacked\footnote{\url{https://www.bathhacked.org}} and the Director of Advisory at the Open Data Institude\footnote{\url{https://theodi.org}} defines a most basic open data portal as: \textit{``a list of datasets with pointers to how those datasets can be accessed''} \cite{dodds_what_2015}. Saying that this definition is the \textit{minimum viable product (MVP)} for open data portal, however when data portal needs to cater to a wide range of audiences \citep{davies_open_2010} it is worth looking into some more sophisticated solution. The usual functions of open data portals are categorised into two groups: (1) discoverability - functions which help index and organise the metadata in order to make data discoverable, (2) interoperability - functions that help interlink the data from different sources and make it programmatically accessible through Application Programming Interface (API). Using these technologies, but not exclusively, governments have spin up portals like open.canada.ca and data.gov.uk, Canada and UK respectively, with many others countries following their footsteps (see \href{https://opendatabarometer.org}{Open Data Barometer}), enabling people to search and download governmental open data. With `father of the World Wide Web' Tim Berners-Lee, among others, calling for \textit{``raw data now''}, publishing of open data has increased massively over the past ten years and there are over 170 open data portals in the EU alone according to Open Data Monitor\footnote{\url{https://opendatamonitor.eu}}. Althought there is abundance of data already published there are still datasets that are disclosed by governments. One way citizens can 
gain access to those datasets, provided it does not violate any restrictions, is by going through Freedom of Information (FOI) request. However processes linked to submitting a FOI request can be complex, lengthy and hard to document for re-use. To address these issues MySociety, a UK-based charity, came up with a simple online tool called WhatDoTheyKnow\footnote{\url{https://www.whatdotheyknow.com}} to aid people in submitting FOI request and sharing the process and the results with everyone. Using this approach, people have done over half a million requests resulting in new datasets being released onto the web.
\begin{quote}
\textit{``…if people put data onto the web […] it will be used by other people to do wonderful things.''} \citep{berners-lee_year_2010}
\end{quote}
It is great that data is out there, because it promotes transparency and encourages discussion, however it has turned into a `numbers game' with little context. There is a lot of ambiguity around what makes a government open and what are the purposes of Open Govermental data -- is it merely political statement or call for technologies that make that data usable \citep{renee_e._sieber_civic_2015}? People have critiqued that the \textit{`measure of openness'} for governments is flawed, only taking account the number datasets and repositories published online \citep{davies_open_2010} and that there are more nuances to the effective re-use of open data than simply the ability to access (not even being the case for everyone) the generated data files \citep{gurstein_open_2011}. Similarly Open Data Barometer's latest report points out that there is lack of work being done around inclusivity surrounding open data \citep{world_wide_web_foundation_open_2018}. Clearly making data available is not the same as making it accessible and usable for everybody.

Open data from governments and private companies could potentially provide valuable input for citizens to leverage to participate in democracy and help improve their communities. Working with data however requires specialist know-how, tools and understanding of data science techniques -- trades that community groups and citizens often lack. Even with tools like WhatDoTheyKnow, which simplify the requests for data, there might be problems with not knowing what data would be useful in particular context. Specially when request consists of having to explain what data one's looking for in a `free text' field, with limited guidance and ways for pulling in context. 

Perhaps the biggest misconception around open data are that it comes in a `raw' state. In fact data is always \textit{``influenced by potential uses, expectations, context, and theoretical constructs''} \citep{tuomi_data_1999}. \citet{heidegger_what_1976} and \citet{polanyi_tacit_2009}, among other philosophers of knowledge, have criticised the empirical model of epistemology, with an underlining principle that an object's being and our interpretation of it is in an entangled state, meaning that we can not observe an object without it being part of its current interpretation. Hence it is impossible for humans to take data as raw material and to obtain knowledge from data through empirical observations of it.

Citizens are more likely to gain from a data exploration tools that wrap data in some narrative that position data into context helping to tell a story. \cite{taylor_data--place:_2015} has emphasised the importance of data production and use being bounded to a place with geographical and social dimensions, giving it context and making it usable. There are already examples of systems like MapIT\footnote{\url{https://mapit.mysociety.org}} and TheyWorkForYou\footnote{\url{https://www.theyworkforyou.com}} which enable people to access open data linked to specific postcode; or Know My Neighbourhood\footnote{\url{https://neighbourhood.knowmyarea.org}} and Local Insight\footnote{\url{https://local.communityinsight.org}} which enables community organisations to link different open govermental datasets to an area of interest to aid local-decision making or improve service delivery (see more in Chapter \ref{chapter:data-in-place}).

Openly sharing data and knowledge, whether it is originated from official sources or shared by Wikipedians on the web, takes it closer to becoming knowledge that is understood and accessible by everyone. But not only that, if knowledge is shared it can be questioned, confirmed or contested  -- either through public critique or even systematic reviews and meta-analyses -- which ultimately lead to understanding of different perspectives and a better quality of focal knowledge. Not openly sharing data and knowledge, or deliberately making accessing obscure and difficult for reasons such as the data is \textit{too complex to understand}, \textit{too difficult to interpret} or \textit{might be misunderstood} are not valid reasons for not putting it out there. The issue is not that the data is complicated, the issue is that the presentation of data is complicated or missing crucial context to make sense of it. Meaning that there is a representation issue rather than data issue. If things are hidden from people, they can not be discussed and their representations can not be worked on.

\section{Data Visualisation}
\label{data-viz}
% Review and critique of different visualisation techniques and reviewing the current state of the art of data visualisation tools.
In order for people to start making sense of data it needs to be condensed to something that is comprehensible through human senses. This is where visual communication of data or data visualisation comes into play aiming to communicate information that data contains. There is often an assumption that data visualising is just reflecting the information that data holds. However, as there is no such thing as `raw data', there is also no such thing as direct or `raw visualisation'. Data visualisations, although based on data, are already analysed and edited forms of data projections which convey the message of the data analyst or an editor. The aim of data visualisation is to support the analytical process, formal procedure used to solve any type of problem, and help with information discovery and knowledge creation. That means it is also important to identify the type of audiences that will use the data representation, to better contextualise and present information aiding them in knowledge creation \citep{chi_operator_1998}. 

% https://www.d3-graph-gallery.com/index.html
% https://blog.bitsrc.io/11-javascript-charts-and-data-visualization-libraries-for-2018-f01a283a5727

People might theoretically have access to the same data, however not everyone has the know-how, skills and resources to turn it into something useful. Manipulation of data in a spreadsheet can be manageable for vast majority of people, however when data requires some cleaning, merging or reducing, more specialised tools such as Matlab\footnote{\url{https://www.mathworks.com/products/matlab.html}}, Python\footnote{\url{https://www.python.org}} or R\footnote{\url{https://www.r-project.org}} are needed. Although still considered a professional trade the advances in information technologies and the open source movement have produced multiple visualisation libraries such as Matplotlib\footnote{\url{https://matplotlib.org}}, ggplot2\footnote{\url{https://ggplot2.tidyverse.org}}, D3.js\footnote{\url{https://d3js.org}} enabling more people experiment with data visualisation. However, data visualisation tools often require software development skills in order to manipulate the data and produce visual outputs. Software like Tableau and Gephi however are trying to break down those barriers and use graphical user interfaces (GUI) to help users visualise data. Advances in web technologies also permit data manipulation on web platforms in an easy `drag and drop' manner. Examples of such tools are Carto\footnote{\url{https://carto.com/}}, for manipulating geographic data and making interactive maps; Charted \citep{sall_using_2014} and RAWGraphs \citep{mauri_rawgraphs:_2017} for automatic chart creation from files, online spreadsheets, web APIs or other data sources, which can be easily shared with others. Although there are open source web tools for making graphs, people still have to choose the right visualisation for particular data to convey the information. DataVizProject\footnote{\url{https://datavizproject.com}} from Ferdio makes good headway towards helping people make the right choice for their data, by having simple visual representation of different types of charts and categorised by family, input, functionality and shape.

Presenting data effectively however requires both artistic and technical skills for identifying the message or insight it needs to convey and choosing the correct representation guided by visualisation techniques \citep{chi_taxonomy_2000}. People spend years on working on their visualisation craft, experimenting and figuring out the best practices for different types of data, ultimately become professional data visualisers. Sites like InformationIsBeautiful\footnote{\url{https://informationisbeautiful.net}}, Visualising Data\footnote{\url{http://www.visualisingdata.com/}} and Flowing Data\footnote{\url{https://flowingdata.com}} are good examples of people achieving a that level of professionalism.

Data however does not always have to refer to something that simply can be counted and represented as quantifiable values (e.g., `big data') \citep{michael_toward_2016,mcmillan_data_2016}, it can be much broader and diverse set of `things' often characterised as contemporary media such as 3D printed physical fabrications of data \citep{nissen_data-things:_2015}; travelling suitcases filled with stories and memories \citep{crivellaro_re-making_2016}; interactive physical graphs \citep{sweeney_pixel_2019}. Often a good way to engage people with data is through installations and art practices that use technology coupled with engineering practices to show data under a critical lens. Examples of these kind of projects are Take a Bullet for The City\footnote{\url{http://www.digiart21.org/art/take-a-bullet-for-the-city}}, which uses data from New Orleans Police Department to fire a blank shot every time when a report of a firearm discharge occurs and Invisible Airs\footnote{\url{http://yoha.co.uk/invisible}}, which uses different `machines' to visualise the public spending of Bristol City Council. In may cases this way of `releasing data' is far more effective than a flat machine readable file on some data portal and gets people more engaged with the issue in hand. In addition to the benefits of being able to combine digital and non-digital tools to visualise data, situated urban displays also enable to keep the data in the place it was collected from and with the community it belongs to. For example using chalk and tape to visualise locally collected data to raise awareness around local issues and promote dialog between residents \citep{koeman_what_2014} or using public spaces to display private household data in order to promote behavioural change around energy usage \citep{moere_comparative_2011,bird_pulse_2010}. It is known however that the way in which different data comes to be interpreted is always entangled with and dependent upon both contexts and situations \citep{suchman_plans_1985,taylor_data--place:_2015}.

While, there is a tendency for civic technology projects to place emphasis on quantitative data -- on the assumption that quantitative data may be relevant to people -- qualitative data describing things that cannot be easily quantified or measured are not less important. Indeed, it is often this type of `data' that helps people making sense of what matters and help generate actionable knowledge.

\section{Stakeholder Groups}
\label{stakeholders}
From citizen sensing to smart city management, data is the epicentre of our worlds, communities, and research. Despite the pervasiveness of the term, there is still little critical understanding of what data might mean for different people and in different contexts (e.g., smart cities, business, research, community). Whether the term might be used to refer to `big data' analytics and statistics, or to the much broader collection of diverse ‘things’ like images, texts, audio etc. Interpretations of data and the meaning people draw from it is intrinsically liked to the motivations, contexts and situations that people find themselves in. Such diversity and ambiguity raises tensions particularly in contexts in which professionals and non-professionals are required to collaborate around data to communicate ideas and produce knowledge. From a Foucauldian perspective there is need to identify a common language and representations of data to promote discourse which produces knowledge and helps balance the power relationships between citizens and decision-makers (Foucault, 1969). 

When designing civic technology that support communities and institutions working together with data it is important to understand different stakeholder groups -- their roles and motivations regarding generating, providing and using data. This classification of stakeholder groups is drawing on work done by State of Open Data\footnote{\url{https://www.od4d.net/stateofopendata}} project and its contributors.

Links between different stakeholders clearly illustrate the transactional relationships that current state of data enforces between the ones in power and the ones who are the subjects of it. Understanding relationships and the roles in this network helps identify barriers and opportunities to reconfigure these roles to make data more inclusive and increase participation through use of it.

From ancient Egypt and China to modern day countries, governments have held censuses to gather information about their citizenry to inform educated decisions and understand or avoid risks. Historically censuses were carried out to count the citizens to tax and inform the government about eligible young men for military service. After collecting the data, it took about a year to add it up and provide the statistics. Although the techniques have changed and efficiency has increase when moving to the digital age of computers, the aims of census have remained pretty much the same. Governments are well established data collectors and the processes in place for polling their citizens are perfected over the years. As mentioned before, opening up that data is becoming a common practice for governments (local and national) and is being integrated into the way they operate. Additionally to data generated through censuses, governments are also releasing data about how they operate and spend public money [REF]. Introducing open data initiatives puts pressure on governments to `reform' current processes of governance to guarantee the continuance of these initiatives as public services go through a digital transformation. Data portals, currently being the main channels for publishing OGD are still supply-driven and lack engagement and tools for effective use of data to help reach all the stakeholder groups (https://www.stateofopendata.od4d.net).

Data is probably the biggest business for the private sector companies operating in ICT. In addition to the data that private sector companies generate themselves, having the tools and the know-how how to tap in to open data, they are also benefiting from data published by other institution. It has been suggested that open data has the potential to help generate \$3 to \$5 trillion of economic value annually in the U.S (Mckinsey 2013). This huge potential for economic growth has also become a driver, alongside with transparency and accountability, for some governments to open up their datasets for re-use.

Private sector has been an avid user of open data for years now and has shown its value to help understand demand, use it in strategic planning and produce useful products and services (Open Data 500 and Open Data Impact Map). The value of open data for private sector is clearly illustrated by Open Data Impact Map, which provide a search-able catalogue of open data project, where two-thirds of organisations on that list are for-profit companies \citep{gurin_open_2019}.

In addition to private sector leveraging open data to improve their products and services, there are number of companies have also become `data intermediaries' \citep{gurin_open_2019} by helping governments publish their data or repackaging existing data for public and business use. There are companies who are providing data portal software for governmental and other institutions (see Section \ref{open-data}) based on data-as-a-service model to help publish their data, but recently there are more companies coming out with data-broker tools catered for consumers and the general public. Tools such as LG Inform Plus\footnote{\url{https://about.esd.org.uk}} from Local Government Association which combine multiple sources of open data and make them available through Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) and file downloads. There are also companies who package data and try visualise data better by using GIS, reporting and graphs. These include companies like OCSI and HACT who provide software for understanding trends in housing market\footnote{\url{https://ocsi.uk/community-insight}} through OGD, Porism\footnote{\url{https://porism.com}} who package OGD for informing neighbourhood plans and MySociety\footnote{\url{https://www.mysociety.org}} who has developed multiple tools for making open data more accessible and promoting new routes for democratic participation from the public.

The role of a journalists have always been to keep the public informed about governments accountability, business developments and academic and scientific research. In the digital era, \textit{Data-Driven Journalism} \citep{ausserhofer_datafication_2017} is becoming a term that is widely recognised as part of journalistic agenda which uses data as a resource to investigate and/or shed light on matters of public interest. Becoming an effective journalist nowadays requires the skills of a data scientist which has resulted in traditional media outlets dying out and being replaced with online ones. Computer-assisted reporting is coming mainstream in journalism and major news outlets have their dedicated data visualisation and data science departments solely focused on storytelling with data (New York Times, Guardian). These new `programmer-journalists' \citet{parasie_data-driven_2013} are equipped with new skills and tools to obtain, analyse, visualise and even open up some of the data for citizens. With increasing popularity of big social media platforms becoming the new news outlets and politicians constantly calling `fake news' \citep{carson_fake_2017} it is even harder to spark up collaborative efforts between diverse groups of journalist, data scientists and communities of practice to use data for social good.

However critical lens need to be used to assess whether the successes of open government and open data can be applied in developing countries to achieve the desired social impact \citep{gigler_realizing_2011}. Additionally these organisations also publish data about world economics \footnote{\url{https://data.worldbank.org/}} and demographics \footnote{\url{http://data.un.org/}}. Multilaterals often have the ability to tap into resources that are far greater than any one government and use new techniques and methods to require, process and report on data. In addition to the world organisations there are smaller non profits who  promote and use data, aiming to create better standards and more cohesive data ecosystem. Such as Open Data Institute who helps develop and promote open data tools and initiatives, and Open Knowledge International who are advocating for knowledge sharing, also responsible for keeping their hand on the pulse of the developments around OGD through publishing Global Open Data Index reports \footnote{\url{https://index.okfn.org}}.

Civil society -- community organisations or groups of citizens with common interests -- who often mediates data to the rest of the community is perhaps the most important link in realising the effective-use of data within communities. Civil society is predominantly a data user, through conducting research with it and often publishing its findings to shed light on different matters. Although citizens are mainly data generators, there is particular set of enthusiast among the civil society often called data activists, `hacktivists' or civic hackers, who use civic data to surface concerns and contest decisions through enquiries and visualisations of data \citet{schrock_civic_2016,taylor_hackers_2005}. They often take strong political statements and act as watchdogs for governments and big corporations. In addition to individuals there are also groups of people who operate through Meetups\footnote{\url{https://www.meetup.com}} and collaborative projects. They consist of mostly professionals working with data or researchers who want to contribute their time and skills to for the good of society. Examples of such groups are Open Oakland\footnote{\url{https://openoakland.org}} and NE Data\footnote{\url{https://www.nedata.uk/}}.

However when it comes to pushing the open data agenda it has far more shoes to fill. In addition to being the bridge between the governmental institutions and community groups, it also helps raise awareness around open data, set the standards and course for data publishing, consult and train people on how access and use open data, publish tools to realise open data needs and advocate for new datasets to be released as open data (Wilson, Christopher (2018) “Public draft: Open Data and Civil Society”). It even helps gather data and openly publish it to promote advocacy and push for change. 

Being the driving force behind pushing the use of open data for social good, civil society has the biggest responsibility to citizens but also the most challenging role to play. However there is a not always clear where the civil society ends and other stakeholders begin, clear there is an overlap with the research community, donors and the private sector companies (State of Open Data).

Although civil society is taken as this big force that can achieve anything it sets out, the reality is that it often does not possess the resources it needs to help communities with their data agendas. Specially smaller groups who are struggling with lack of financial support or other resources to further their cause. Problematic also is the unwelcoming competition between civil society groups trying to do the same thing and applying for the same pots of funding. This also makes it harder for the funding bodies to figure out which one of them would potentially have the greatest impact on the society, ending up often un-evenly or unfairly distributing the resources. Civil society groups are too busy competing with each other and even with start-up companies, and trying to please the founders, that they are forgetting the mission they set out to do -- help improve the lives of people in the community (Ian's paper). At the same the gap widens between the people who are already engaged and making use of data, and people who might be gain from it the most but do not have any routes to engagement. Instead of competing, non-profits could collaborate with each other, be more transparent and use data and technology to secure more funding \citep{goecks_charitable_2008}. However great the potential of data to aid community organisations in their agendas is, it is still yet to be unlocked by everyone.

There is often an assumption that opening up data makes it accessible for everyone \citep{berners-lee_year_2010}, however most of the population does not possess the skills and tools to access and use the data. In most cases however people are actually generating all those data or data are generated about them, without them having no say how and what those data should be concerning. Some people strongly believe that open data is not for the public and \textit{``raw data is for people who know how to use raw data''} \citep{whong_thoughts_2015}. Although data is never raw, it always represents the bias of the collector and the observer \citet{gitelman_raw_2013}. It is not contradicting the idea of open data is not for the public per say, it is just saying that the current representation of data -- whether it is coined as `raw data' or `open data' -- is not accessible in this form to everyone.

When designing platforms to support communities and institutions working together with data to address problems in society, significant challenges arise around translation and representations of data. Considerations of what data is valuable to whom and in what form needs to be taken into account to better support communities collaborating around data. Because essentially discourse is about about production of knowledge through language, and through sharing of practices (Foucault). 

Developing a common language is critical in order to open spaces where common understandings of what data may be important for civic life can be developed for common purposes. Furthermore, a deeper examination of issues and tensions arising from processes of data classification, representation and interpretation becomes necessary in order to develop technologies and processes to better support collaborations around data capture, analysis, and sense-making, envisaged for a range of purposes from personal data management to community, civic and political engagement.

\section{Knowledge Society}
\label{knowledge-society}
% Lave, Jean, and Etienne Wenger. 1994. Situated Learning: Legitimate Peripheral Participation, Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.
% Wenger, Etienne (1998). Communities of Practice: Learning, Meaning, and Identity. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.
% https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Community_of_practice
% Review of forms of community organising and creation of community networks through evaluation existing platforms and related case studies.
The usage of social capital -- the exchange and sharing of skills and other resources in order to produce knowledge -- which is contained within the community around a common concern is referred to as Community of Practice (CoP) \citep{lave_situated_1991}. This can be individuals living in close proximity working together to improve the neighbourhood and tackle local issues, but can also refer to a virtual community (Rheingold 1993) that is present on online platforms such as Facebook, Reddit or Quora. Accoring to \citet{lave_situated_1991} the important structural elements of CoPs are the \textit{domain} of knowledge, the notion of a \textit{community} and its \textit{practice}. Domain is some form of common denominator that defines a group of people, notion of a community are the connections and interactions that individuals share in the domain and the practice is the focus and activities that engage in to produce, share and maintain the knowledge. All of these can be taken as context needed to produce and understand knowledge, because these cases knowledge is always situated in activities engaged in the context of collective consciousness \citep{winograd_understanding_1986,hakken_social_2003}. In these situations knowledge mostly takes a tacit form \citep{polanyi_tacit_2009} which makes it difficult for individuals and organisations from outside the CoP to obtain any information. In order to understand the information it conveys, there is need to understand what the context is and what does it mean \citep{hakken_social_2003}. At the same time this makes it difficult for people outside the domain to contribute to the knowledge base or identify additional resources needed that would benefit the practices.

In cyberspace people are increasingly relying on different data sources to derive knowledge. Often generated by somebody other than individuals involved in the CoPs and pushed out as they are, missing crucial bits of context to really understand and use those data in knowledge creation. An example of this are datasets published as Open Government Data which represent the information collected for the purpose of governance and depict the viewpoint of the government. However that data is connected to organisational practices, which share some similarities with CoPs in terms of having a knowledge base in which it operates, but in reality it is inherently different from the organically created CoPs which do not possess the fixed hierarchical structures and tasked roles within (REF). This does not imply that that data is useless in community practices, it means that data needs to be contextualised by the community related to their practises. It is known from situation theory that information is in fact situated data and knowledge is information whose situation or context is known \citep{hakken_social_2003}. Case studies in this thesis explore ways that available data sources can be made into information that is useful for the communities and their practices. However, there should not be a confusion between \textit{information society} and \textit{knowledge society}. When information society is more focused on generating and publishing data, knowledge society is more concerned with creating mechanisms and processes for effective use of those datasets and putting it into action towards improving the society as a whole. By designing tools with and for CoPs to collect data (SenseMyStreet) or help connect data to concerns (Data:In Place) enable people build stronger community networks, helping create and share knowledge for the community to act upon.

Engaging in practices linked to advocacy is not a new concept in democratic societies. For many people lobbying and grassroots lobbying (e.g., regulated by US Federal Law) are considered the main forms of advocacy, however advocacy does not limit itself to only lobbying. It uses protesting, petitions, citizen campaigning and instrumentation of variety of other information resources to influence decisions withing the socioeconomic and political systems that shape the lives of people \citep{christoffel_public_2000} are legitimate means of democratic participation and should be part of a healthy society.
Advocacy efforts can come from individuals, but they are more likely to be associated with some sort of organisation or self-organised group \citep{obar_advocacy_2012,suarez_nonprofit_2009,leroux_nonprofits_2007,prakash_advocacy_2010}. Advocacy can also be characterised into two strategies - insider and outsider strategy \citep{asad_tap_2017}. Where former uses more traditional approaches to directly effect policy systems and latter focuses on informal processes by contributing resources or skills to organisations to help address issues \citep{hackler_strategic_2007,marshall_accountable:_2016,erete_storytelling_2016}. Additionally, often the advocates are not the ones with the issues, they are acting as proxies to voice community concerns \citep{leroux_nonprofits_2007,johnson_community_2017,voida_bridging_2012}. 

Affordances and capabilities of ICT are enabling new forms of digital advocacy \citep{brady_utilizing_2015} where people are taking advantage of digital tools and platforms  to affect change in the community \citep{karpf_online_2010}. For example using platforms such as Change.org\footnote{\url{https://www.change.org}} and TheyWorkForYou\footnote{\url{https://www.theyworkforyou.com}} to digitalise traditional forms of advocacy (e.g., singing petitions, sending letters to representatives), or usage of existing social media platform such as Facebook and Twitter to engage in activism \citep{crivellaro_pool_2014,howard_opening_2011} or to coordinate humanitarian action \citep{starbird_voluntweeters:_2011,starbird_chatter_2010}. Benefits of online tools are that they do not put geographical restrictions on participation, allowing participation from other places and also enabling instant access to the information shared. Digital tools can also be used for feedback loops, and increasing transparency and accountability of governmental processes. Apps such as Street Bump\footnote{\url{http://www.streetbump.org/}} and FixMyStreet are focused creating these types of feedback loops where information is available for everyone and all reported issues have a record of responses and resolutions from the government. It is very common nowadays to use digital platforms such as  Twitter to directly interact with institutions using \textit{@mentiones}. \citet{asad_tap_2017} pointed out the issues around the expectation of people to get immediate response from digital systems and the realities of governmental processes they are interact with. Another form of digital advocacy is data-driven advocacy \citep{asad_tap_2017,gray_changing_2016} which is a form of data-driven participation \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016} where people use digital technologies to collect data to inform or evidence an issue and advocate for change. Data helps increase the visibility of issues in the eyes of decision-makers, but also helps raise public awareness and gives people a stronger voice for advocating.
