\section{Introduction}
\kylenote{New challenges of community data interaction}
\mynote{Make sure to define accessible data somewhere in the introduction or maybe chapter 3 (Data:In Place) to have a segway for this.}
This chapter reflects on the previous case studies in this thesis, which aimed to support the `accessible data' agenda through active engagements, from the perspective of an experienced data scientist and information engineer designing and building accessible data systems for community use. Drawing on case studies and wider research, chapter starts off by analysing the technical challenges of \textit{accessing}, \textit{making sense} and \textit{effectively using} to support civic participation and advocacy. Additionally chapter provides an analysis of the successes and failures of each project and initiative engaged within the context of the research. Through this, it is possible to chart the process in which accessible data systems are formed and what are the main barriers with technologies for \textit{community data interaction}.

Open data advocates often paint a romantic picture of the data being available for everyone to consume like the air that in the atmosphere, and making it ready to leverage its powers in aiding decision-making, improving public services, and fighting injustices and improving equality for all people. However in closer inspection things are not as they have been advertised. Depending on where a particular person sits in the society they may think that current systems for data access and use are either fantastic or are under-serving their needs. In addition to the clear need of data science skills to interact with these data rich systems, there are also number of barriers to using that data for purposes other than their intended collection: published data is often not standardised; there is a lack of machine readable metadata; tools and systems for data access are disperse and often not fully functional; there is lack of clear documentation how to leverage the existing infrastructures.

Call for open data has been echoing from technology and policy advocates for while now. In some respect it has been answered by institutions publishing their data, however the hopes and promises of open data empowering everyone has been short lived. Data, often represented as machine readable files with abstract descriptions and column headers, holds the values of those specific purposes of data generation or production. There is an abundance of data (or metadata) available on the web and more is being published daily, but there is actually a paucity of data and technology that would help understand what kind of questions could be answered with particular datasets. By supporting the semantic web agenda (Berners-Lee 2011) those data can be standardised and described for querying and retrieval by machines. A great step in a right direction for helping to remove ambiguity around understanding what particular dataset represents and reducing development costs on data accessing, combining and merging. Although Linked Data standards for the web have been around for over a decade now, we are still far off achieving the goal of having a `Web of Data' as opposed to a sheer collection of datasets on the web. As a result, data science skills are becoming increasingly more important to aid exploration of data and support evidence based practices in decision-making. Furthermore organising and linking data on the is only the first step towards its effective use by communities and for civic participation and advocacy.

\section{Conclusions}

Understanding how people where interacting with data through the built digital systems provided a deeper contextualised knowledge of shared data interactions/community data interactions. Future developments of accessible data systems should aim to design features that promote exploration, curiosity and improve retrieval of useful information from different data sources using embedded context. This criteria is important because people do not acquire information in the manner of how machines do, to satisfy their curiosity and need for information on the web, people posit questions which often are open ended and do not map to any specific structure (Paritosh 2018). In order for humans to re-purpose and use abstract datasets from the web, there is a need for a system that links questions with the data that might bare the answers. Reflecting on the previous case studies and the wider research, enabled to set criteria for accessible data systems that needs to be satisfied in order for the re-purposing of open data to be successful. Although the existence of `Web of Data' is prerequisite for enabling the linking of data, the criteria suggests that there needs to be a larger emphasis on the `shared data interactions' and linking questions with data on the web.
