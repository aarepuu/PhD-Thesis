\epigraph{A novel solution to a social problem that is more effective, efficient, sustainable, or just than existing solutions and for which the value created accrues primarily to society as a whole rather than private individuals.}{--- \textup{James Phills et al. 2008}}

This chapter reflects on previous two case studies in this thesis, which aimed to support the \textit{effective use} of data by citizens through active engagements. Up to this point, the thesis has looked at ways to engage citizens in exploration of local issues through data production (Case Study I in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} and \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}) and through accessing and making sense of publicly available datasets (Case Study II in Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}). Both of these case studies, although from a community perspective, had a sharper focus on the engineering problem solving agenda by responding to the issues regarding the lack of specific technical tools, expertise or access to data. That included setting up a community commissioning toolkit to enable citizens to access resources for collecting or commissioning environmental data (i.e., SenseMyStreet); and designing and building an open data accessing and visualisation platform (i.e., Data:In Place) for communities. Drawing on these case studies and the wider research in Ubiquitous Computing (UC), Human Data Interaction (HDI) and Community Informatics (CI), the chapter starts off by analysing the technical challenges of \textit{accessing}, \textit{making sense} and \textit{effectively using} data to support civic participation and advocacy. Additionally, this chapter starts off by analysing the successes and failures of each project and initiative engaged within the context of the research. It is important to note that the analysis of the case studies was done from the perspective of an experienced data scientist and information engineer designing and building usable data systems for communities. Furthermore, throughout the case studies there was a constant dissembling of the role of the professional data analyst, exploring how far expert's knowledge can be removed from processes of active data use by citizens. 

From there, the chapter goes on to discuss the important aspects of human interactions with data and systems facilitating these interactions. However these are not limited to only interactions between humans and machines, but also the `human friction` which is often rooted in particular contexts and the social dynamics within the communities. Through this analysis and the reflection on the previous case studies, it is possible to outline the current open challenges that still exist for shared data interactions at a hyper-local community level. Based on these challenges, this chapter underlines the methods, strategies and practices, outlining a holistic approach through which usable data systems could be formed. Building on the critical approach in CI \citep{stillman_community_2009}, the conceptual framework of CI \citep{gurstein_toward_2012}, commissioning model \citep{garbett_designing_2017}, effective use framework \citep{gurstein_effective_2003}, and extending the Citizen Advocacy Framework (CAF) presented in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}, a model of shared data interaction as Community-Data Interaction (CDI) is offered. The model provides a definition of CDI and ontology within the context of effective use of data for community action in relation to \textit{roles}, \textit{infrastructure}, \textit{resources}, \textit{constraints} and \textit{capacities}. Going forward, the model provides an abstract set of considerations when designing systems of shared data interaction for effective use of data by communities for civic advocacy and action.

\section{Data on the Web}
\label{sec:data_web}
The call for open data has been echoing from technology and policy advocates for a while. In some respect it has been answered by institutions publishing their data, however the hopes and promises of open data empowering everyone has been short lived \citep{davies_state_2019}. Data, often represented as machine readable files with abstract descriptions and column headers, holds the values of those specific purposes of data generation or production. There is an abundance of data (or in some case metadata) available on the web and more is being published daily, but there is actually a paucity of data and technology that would help understand what kind of questions could be answered with particular datasets. By supporting the Semantic Web agenda \citep{berners-lee_semantic_2001} those data can be standardised and described for querying and retrieval by machines. A great step in a right direction for helping to remove ambiguity around understanding what particular dataset represents and reducing development costs on data accessing, combining and merging. Although Linked Data standards\footnote{\url{https://www.w3.org/standards/semanticweb/data}} for the web have been around for over a decade now, we have not yet achieved the goal of having a `Web of Data' as opposed to a sheer collection of datasets on the web. As a result, data science skills are becoming increasingly more important to aid exploration of data and support evidence based practices in decision-making and action. However, organising and linking data on the web is only the first step towards its effective use by communities and for civic participation and advocacy.

\subsection{Organising Data on the Web}
\label{sub:org_data}
There are multiplicity of ways that data and information is organised on the web and can be shared between people. In the corporate world it is essential to keep track of the organisational memory (OM) (or knowledge) \citep{tuomi_data_1999} in a way that it could be accessible and usable to all the members of the organisation. In addition to linking data, knowledge graphs are often used to connect that data together and make the retrieval of data easier from a variety of different sources. Most notable of the knowledge graphs is used by Google to enchants its search engine. Another well know technique is archival and indexing of resources. The retrieval of information from those archives follow a system catalogues which are identified or tagged by topics or other common identifiers. Examples of such systems are the Archives Portal Europe\footnote{\url{https://www.archivesportaleurope.net/}} and probably most well known in the academic field the Digital Object Identifier (DOI) system \footnote{\url{https://www.doi.org/}}. In addition to formal ways of organising data and information on the web there are also systems for community curated `linked data'. Most popular source of that is unquestionably Wikipedia free online encyclopedia, which enables anyone who has an internet access to use that resource and to contribute to it. However, in community problem solving activities that are trying to use data through information systems, the attention to these practices often fall on the background.

The vast number of connections and links between individuals, that are enabled by modern technologies and can be harnessed through the web, have opened up new ways of information and knowledge sharing. On top of media sharing platforms such as Facebook, Instagram, YouTube, to name a few, there are multiple forum based Q\&A systems that aim to connect and exchange information between individuals and groups. Online platforms such as Stack Overflow for any question related to software development, or websites like Reddit and Quora which encourage discussions on a variety of topics. Although there is an abundance of different tools available to retrieve information from digital systems, they often require interactions that are confined in the boundaries of specific technology or contextualised in particular way. This is especially evident in interactions with systems that provide numerical big data which inherently complex in its nature. Such data is organised and accessed in ways that people would not organise and retrieve information themselves in their everyday lives, hence the expression data in `machine readable' format. Furthermore, what motivates the work in this thesis is how to enable the effective use of data by communities for civic advocacy and action. Indeed, it is essential to have data stored in a way that it could be accessed in standardised formats to aid creation of building tools that would interface with data archives, but these tools also need to be able to guide citizens in contextualising and translating abstract datasets into helpful resources for civic participation.

\subsection{Making a Formal Data Request}
\label{sub:data_req}
To investigate the potential uses of data in context of civic participation and action, there is a need to look at the current ways citizens can gain access to open data from official sources. With the Freedom of Information Act \footnote{\url{https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/guide-to-freedom-of-information/what-is-the-foi-act}}, a new mechanism was set up for people to access data held by public authorities. Enabling anyone who wanted, to request data from public authorities by submitting a Freedom of Information (FOI) request. FOI can be looked as a formal query for data, where a person or an organisation deposits a question to the data provider (i.e., public authority). Submitting an FOI request can be a lengthy and complicated process often requiring knowledge regarding legal processes. In order to streamline these processes MySociety came up with a online platform called What They Know\footnote{\url{https://www.whatdotheyknow.com}}, that helps people to submit their requests through a web form. This has made the process of submission much easier, however the actual requesting of data has not improved. In order to get the data needed, one has to know about the existence of some unpublished document or dataset that is being requested. Apart from using the platform to explore previous FOI requests, there is no way one can make sure that a particular data is not yet published somewhere else. Thus, as long as there is no unified `Web of Data` (more Section \ref{sub:linkeddata}), that will describe all the existing data sources on the internet and makes them universally accessible trough standards to any service or client, this remains an issue.

Another scenario which can be encountered, is when a request is formulated as a direct question without an indication to particular requested resource. In that case somebody has to make the association between the question and a particular dataset (i.e., describe the relationship). Now we have reached a fundamental problem with data available on the web: \textit{the lack of links between questions and abstract datasets that might bear the answer}. The ways that these connections are made are largely unknown and often require specialist skills with combination of tacit knowledge. This means either understanding the organisational structures of a public authority or knowing the initial purpose for the data generation. It is unlikely that this knowledge transfer can occur from organisations to citizens, however by contextualising data in different environments, it might help discover new uses and knowledge enabling to re-purpose these datasets. Data publisher and designers of information systems should not only aim to achieve the open data and linked data standards, but also look to achieve the \textit{`FAIR Data Principles'} \citep{wilkinson_fair_2016} and the model of \textit{`Effective Use'} \citep{gurstein_effective_2003,gurstein_open_2011} to promote uses of data by different user groups and within communities.
\newline

Underpinning the current state of the art of web technologies for data use, drawing on related works and examples from the Case Study I and II, the following two chapters provide a reflection of use of data by communities. Through this, a better understanding can be formed of ways that people interact with data within a complex sociotechnical systems to underline and identify existing challenges. By analysing not only technical aspects, but also social and organisational impacts, will enable to make better design choices for tools and methods to be used for purposeful and effective uses of data in civic participation, advocacy and action.

\section[Technical Challenges]{Technical Challenges: The Information Engineering Problem Solving Agenda}
\label{sec:tech_chall}
The promise of the third wave of computing or `calm computing' envisioned by \cite{weiser_computer_1991} has only realised partly over the past 25 years. Indeed, there is Ubicomp devices everywhere and sensors are being integrated into the fabrics of our cities and homes -- as so that it often times seem without a physical presence -- however it has not solved the problems of information overload and has not enabled humans to have seamless interactions with these interconnected computer systems. People have also argued that the vision has not been fully realised because of the loose boundaries of the paradigm and lack of 'killer apps' that would make it clearer and set the focus \citep{sorce_human--human_2017}. On the other hand, wide availability of computing devices, internet and IoT promotes a ``do-it-yourself'' and a ``hacking in the real world'' mentality \citep{abowd_what_2012} which means that research is moving out of the lab setting to the real world and into the hands of non `tech-savvy' people. A similar shift happened in personal computing by the mid-1990’s when HyperCard was introduced and enabled more people to interact with computing systems, however UC is still searching for its own `killer app'.
\newline

\noindent
Despite the pervasiveness of computer systems and availability of different information and data sources, the possible ways of interacting with data are still limited to specificities of individual computing system or proprietary protocol, which require humans to know the `lingo' of these systems. Meaning in order for one to make use of these systems and the data that they hold, one needs to know how they are build and operate, or at least how to use technology to interact with them. This section examines challenges related to information engineering from the technical aspect of purposing data from the web. Additionally these challenges are also related to barriers of the dissembling of the expert data professional. By looking at the current state of open data standards and technologies, this section reflects on issues that surfaced from the previous two case studies. Issues such as missing links between datasets, lack of machine readable metadata, non standardised querying methods or lack of following the gold standards of publishing open data. Through building systems for open data and trying to make data more accessible and usable for people, enabled the author to take an in depth look at how data is currently projected to people and help identify the barriers for its use.

\subsection{Open Data Standards}
\begin{figure}[!ht]
\centering
 \includegraphics[scale=0.5]{odi_badges}
  
  \centering
    \footnotesize\emph{Source: https://certificates.theodi.org/en/about/badgelevels}
  \caption[Levels of Open Data]{Levels of commitment for open data}
  \label{fig:badges}
\end{figure}

Open data refers to data is freely available to everyone to use, re-purpose and republish without any copyright restrictions (Chapter \ref{chap:literature}). By the Open Definition \footnote{\url{http://opendatahandbook.org/glossary/en/terms/open-definition}} it requires open data to be open both \textit{legally} and \textit{technically}. The latter refers to having data available in machine readable form and bulk form. To help keep open data consistent on the web, a set of agreements called open data standards have been developed that would help people and organisations publish, access and use data \footnote{\url{https://standards.theodi.org/}}. Additionally there is a distinction of different levels of open data publishing -- \textit{bronze}, \textit{silver}, \textit{gold} and \textit{platinum} -- which specify how much commitment 
the publishers have made to make their data accessible to others (Figure \ref{fig:badges}).

Developing Data:In Place's map based querying system (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) for open data, the author was faced with the reality of having to adopt the code to each data publisher individually. Having to use specific types of geographical representation for retrieving specific data and different query languages, which often did not conform to open standards of web querying\footnote{\url{https://www.w3.org/standards/semanticweb/query.html}}. Additionally when geographies or data were updated or aggregated, it often lacked links that could map the changes to existing or historical datasets. This was the case with geographies published by the Office of National Statistics UK\footnote{\url{http://geoportal.statistics.gov.uk/}}. This meant that data converters, translators and adaptors had to be built to create these links between different datasets. However in order to create these links, there was a need for an in depth knowledge of how the data was collected, aggregated, stored and published. Ideally this information could be described alongside the data and could be also retrieved in a machine readable form, however this information was often lacking in detail even from the documentations on the data portals. These barriers make the integration of open data (i.e., available data) to \textit{accessible} and \textit{usable} data for communities far more difficult and labour intensive for developers of these systems. 

\subsection{State of Linked Data}
\label{sub:linkeddata}
When people talk about Open Data there is always a mention of Linked Data which is one of the building block of the Semantic Web or `Web of Data'. Originally proposed by Tim Berners-Lee, director of the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C), Linked Data refers to structured data that is interlinked and described by relationships (or vocabularies) between data and `things'.  Lets illustrate this through an example:
\begin{quote}
    There are six cities in the UK which have Urban Observatories, all connected through a The City Observatory Research platfOrm for iNnovation and Analytics (CORONA) project\footnote{\url{https://urbanobservatory.ac.uk/corona}}. All six are doing some type of environmental monitoring and data collection. That means that they all have different ways of deploying sensors and collecting, storing, and publishing data. However, if that data would be described and made available through standard descriptions and links (i.e., linked data), then if one for example wanted to know the current outside temperature of the UK cities, one could write a single query which would return all the temperatures from each city observatory's data portal in a preferable format.     
\end{quote}
This is only possible when all the data and relationships are presented through standardised links \footnote{\url{https://urbanobservatory.github.io/standards}}. In practice Linked Data itself can be looked up and represented using many different open standards such as URIs, HTTP, RDF, SPARQL, OWL, JSON-LD etc (see more \footnote{\url{https://www.w3.org/TR/?tag=data}}).

In theory, if all the data is described and interlinked using Linked Data standards, one should be able to build a single query that returns data from multiple data providers in a format that is useful in a particular context. Whether building a report to surface and understand the most pressing issues in the neighbourhood or gathering evidence to advocate for change in the community, this capability to request, access and merge datasets from different publishers is almost always needed when building an accessible and usable data system for local communities. However, will this be also through in practice?
Lets give an example of such query: 
\begin{quote}
    ``What are the current \colorbox{blue!30}{demographics}, \colorbox{green!30}{health statistics} and \colorbox{orange!30}{powers of governance} in a particular hyper-local community defined by a specified \colorbox{yellow!30}{geographic area}?''
\end{quote} 
In the UK this query spans across four different governmental institutions - \colorbox{yellow!30}{Ordnance Survey}, \colorbox{blue!30}{Office of National Statistics}, \colorbox{green!30}{The National Health Service} and \colorbox{orange!30}{The House of Commons}. Although some of their open data systems have capabilities to request data using linked data standards they are often experimental and do not interact well with other linked data systems. Example provided here is a real life example of building an usable data system for public health interventions around breastfeeding \footnote{\url{https://feed-finder.co.uk}}. This project is especially interesting because it aimed to intersect community generated data \citep{balaam_feedfinder:_2015} through a commissioned technology \citep{garbett_app_2016} with official open datasets and make it useful for local communities. After multiple failed attempts to leverage Linked Data to achieve this, the research team\footnote{The research team consisted of Dr. Andrew Garbett, Dr. Emma Simpson and the author. Together a system was designed based on the design requirements obtained through engagement work of the two collaborators; between Dr. Garbett and the author a platform was built; and with the whole team deployed `in the wild', which was lunched via a stakeholder workshop as part of Fuse - Centre for Translational Research in Public Health.} had to build a bespoke system that makes these links between different data providers and combine them into a visualisation interface. In the end, Linked Data was leveraged only to some extent, not in a way that would be envisioned by the Semantic Web community. This also meant that with any updates to the structure of the data, the links had to be rebuilt. This puts the long term sustainability of these systems under a question because of the effort required by the researchers to provide technical support to keep the system running and updated. Similar issues where raised in the second case study (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) while aiming to make data more usable and helping people discover new available datasets that might be useful for evidencing or exploring a local issue.

Finally, the support offered by the data publishers is not sufficient to fully leverage the capabilities of the APIs. There are very few good examples of advanced usages of data and rarely a forum for maintained for the data users to submit issues. Looking at levels of commitment for publishing open data (Figure \ref{fig:badges}) from the perspective of effective use \citep{gurstein_open_2011} in civic participation, only \textit{gold} and \textit{platinum} start focusing on engaging with communities to enable better use of their data. From experience, there are only a handful of data providers (Chapter \ref{chap:literature} Section \ref{sub:stakeholders}) that could be considered to have gold, and even less, platinum standard open data. The current state of Linked Data amongst the official open data providers does not extend itself to the ideals of Tim Berners-Lee's `Web of Data'. This in turn again puts local communities in a disadvantaged position -- not getting the data they need in a form that it would be beneficial for them without the technical know-how and the interference from a professional data scientist. Furthermore, when there is additional data that has been collected by the community (as in the case of the platforms: SenseMystreet, FeedFinder, and also Data:In Place to some extent) it is becoming increasingly difficult to intersect and contrast that data with official open data without the existence of the `Web of Data'.

\subsection{Human-to-Human vs Human-to-Machine}
\label{sub:human_machine}
This section describes the differences of human-to-human and human-to-machine interactions and identifies the challenges for human data interaction in relation to social and cultural constraints that govern the interactions. Subsequent, these constraints have a major role to play in how we design human-system interactions for information systems and guiding strategies we apply to enable people to effectively use them.
\newline

Human-to-human communication via speech is built on pragmatic linguistics and the principle of charity which can also be explained through \textit{`Gricean Maxims'} named after philosopher \citet{herbert_paul_grice_logic_1975}. What it means is that the way that humans communicate and understand each other does not solely relay on grammatical structures (i.e., written text) and the things that are being said. Humans also pick up on utterances in the speech, take in count the contextual and social dynamics of the situation and the relationships, and operate on a principle of charity trying to interpret the speakers intentions to the best of their abilities. All these trades illustrate the human intelligence which helps us to create conditions for an adequate interchange between each other through conversation.

However, upon human-to-machine interaction these rules do not apply and that creates asymmetry between humans and machines. From early research conducted in human-machine communication at Xerox PARC illustrated how these asymmetries will lead to breakdowns in communication \citep{suchman_plans_1985}. The example given with `intelligent' photocopier revealed that the level of abstraction drawn by computer system about the human plans did not convey the intended actions and without computers understanding human trades of communication the breakdown is inevitable. Now, more than 30 years have passed from these first attempts to understand human-machine communications from a behavioural and human centred angle, making way for numerous communication environments and devices being built that interface interactions between humans and machines. On top of traditional interfaces such as graphical user interfaces (GUI), there are now interfaces that use natural speech (e.i., spoken voice), tangible computing, gestures recorded by cameras or other sensors, using wearable computing, or a combination of them, delivering a multimodal interaction interfaces for communication. 

We are now seeing multimodal interfaces that are connected to the internet, relaying user's interactions to a artificial intelligence systems which can take advantage of an immense computing power and make use of big data and machine learning. Enabling them to understand more about context and learn about the environment they are in. Such interfaces are called virtual assistants with examples like Apple's Siri, Google's Assistant, Amazon's Alexa or Echo and Microsoft's Cortana do name a few. Assistant technology has now been embedded to most of our `smart' personal and home electronics -- smartphones, smartwatches and other personal computing, home media systems, hubs and multiple home monitoring devices. The assistant software can perform tasks like schedule calendar events, set alarms, search the internet, pull up information and adjust settings on a particular device, and respond to individual based commands and questions -- enabling even to engage in two-way conversations between human and their computer. Giving more personal data to these systems and enabling the artificial intelligence to `profile' an individual can help these systems to potentially learn about an individual and adapt their responses in a manner that is truly personalised. This could mean that computers now are able to mimic some of these trades that make human-to-human up communication. However, what has started happening is that rather than waiting for the machines to learn the context and the particularities of each individual to respond to them in an adequate manner, humans have started to preempt machines giving inaccurate responses. People are trying to learn how to interact with the machines to get them to do what humans want. In other words people are learning and adapting to `talk' to the machines rather than vice versa, showing again the intelligence of humans and how we operate on the principle of charity when communicating \citet{herbert_paul_grice_logic_1975}. This principle could also be used as a tactic for improving the responses of machines. For example, assessing the usefulness of the information retrieved by the machine as a response of a human requests. By doing that millions of times using a diverse sample, we could start building links between requests and relevant responses. 

In some regards, this is what Google's PageRank has been doing for websites for years now -- counting the number and quality of (hyper)links to a page to determine their relevance. It is one of the tactics used by Google to respond to people's search queries on their platform. The more people reference (i.e., hyperlink) particular websites for source of information, the more relevant it becomes. The know-how in this method is obtained through automation and crawling the the World Wide Web for these links. This not only makes it difficult to assess the real value of the links to a individual use case, but also means that it can be manipulated \citep{gyongyi_link_2006}. Following PageRank algorithm, there have been more sophisticated algorithms implemented such the HITS (first proposed by Jon Kleinberg) \citep{kleinberg_hubs_1999}, CLEVER from IBM \footnote{\url{https://www.ibm.com/blogs/research/2018/05/clever-adversarial-attack/}}, and Google's own algorithm updates such as Penguin, Panda and Hummingbird, which better deal with link spamming and assessing the quality of the links. Specially interesting is the Hummingbird algorithm, which Google claims to focus more on the natural language, trying to understand context and meaning, rather than pure links and keywords to reference things. In a way this is closer to the ideas of the Semantic Web (Section \ref{sub:linkeddata}) that aims to describe the concepts and relationships between `things' on the web. In addition to algorithms that are deployed to work autonomously scraping the web and providing relevant information to people, we need to look at cases where more human approaches are needed. Discovery of relevant datasets is one such case where feedback loops from humans could be beneficial for helping others understand and make use of the data. 

The first obstacle of human-computer interaction has traditionally been to get the machine to do the things that `you' want. In the context of information systems these barriers of using the machine still exist, but can be overcome by applying methods such as user-centred design. The design and development of Data:In Place platform (Case Study II in Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) is a good example of how one might tackle this human-machine communication problem. However, the focus then shifts to how can humans learn from the data retrieved by the machine, and more specifically what types of data could be useful and relevant to particular people and their situation.

\section[Social Informatics Challenges]{Social Informatics Challenges: Community Problem Solving Activities}
\label{sec:social_chall}
Coming back to \cite{weiser_computer_1991}'s initial vision of ``calm computing'', although computers can be found in just about any context of modern life, they still lack the contextual and social awareness of situations and people who are using them. According to Weiser's vision, computers of the future are always on hand and augment out human actions and interactions in everyday life. To some extent we are starting to see this in context aware or location-aware applications (Section \ref{sub:human_machine}), however the challenge still appears to be understanding the human intent, i.e. the `activity' that the human is trying to accomplish. This is also echoing \citet{ackoff_data_1999}'s stance on computers and their inability to produce wisdom (i.e., actionable knowledge) from data, `something` that can be put into action.

Furthermore, what is the use of ``computers in the background'' that collect vast amounts of data, if there is nobody who interacts, understands and acts on that data? In this case that data just becomes noise. Data needs to go from \textit{available} (i.e., open) to \textit{usable} to \textit{useful}. This agenda central challenge for research in human centred computing and prevalent in, HCI and HDI fields. Low engagement with data is often fully credited to lack of or access to technical know-how, skills and tools, however there are additional challenges involved that relate to social ergonomics \citep{ferreira_social_2020,brown_issues_1985} of using these complex systems in different contexts. Some of these challenges come from the fact that communities are diverse, they all have unique cultural peculiarities and imperfect organic ways of doing things, which are hard to interpret into intelligent machines and processes. 

\subsection{The Importance of Community Links}
\label{sub:comm_links}
Case studies in this thesis engaged with communities which all had different economic and cultural backgrounds; social capital (i.e, skills and resources to produce knowledge), existing proficiency to use data and the existing community links. Already without any engagement, this puts each community group on a different level in terms of their capabilities to effectively use data for actions for local benefit. Many have noted that the way to increase the capabilities of the community is to actively include them in the design process or practice co-design \citep{bernarda_design_2017,ferreira_social_2020,dantec_infrastructuring_2013,disalvo_neighborhood_2008}. Both of the previous case studies in this thesis where conducted through user-centred design approach where communities where actively involved and drove the design of the technical solution. The SenseMyStreet toolkit (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}) and the Data:In Place platform (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) where both a direct results of a longitudinal engagement with community groups. However, as the findings of both case studies show, the tools and data they provided could only get people so far. The groups that could effectively use data had either grater social capital or larger community networks. For example the advocacy group in Case Study I (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}) consisted of self-motivated (ex-)professionals who had connections in local government, local universities and public health services. Additionally, they where already involved with multiple groups and they knew people outside their group who had similar goals to them but in different hyper-local areas. That meant that their connections extended further than their own community. This goes back to the ideas around social capital \citep{coleman_social_1988} discussed in the literature review's Stakeholder Section (Section \ref{sub:stake_citizens}). These were the `ties' that \citet{granovetter_strength_1983} referred to as \textit{linking} connections. \citet{granovetter_strength_1983} stressed the importance of an individual having multiple `weak' \textit{linking} and \textit{bridging} ties, allowing them to tap into skills and resources outside their social network, close friends and colleagues. In practical terms, this means that communities are more likely to get access to skills and resources that are out of their own capabilities. Skills such as community outreach and most importantly data science skills in the context of effective use of data. An example of this was the Twitter bot developed by one of the community members, that published easily digestible daily graphs about the air quality from commissioned monitors. It was developed by one group at first, but then shared amongst and adapted for other groups. However, Case Study I (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}) also included groups that lacked these links, which meant that data was collected or commissioned by them, but not acted upon. Similarly, in Case Study II (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) the fact that open data was made available and usable for people through the Data:In Place platform, did not mean that people found it useful and could straight away use it in activities for local benefit. That needed more capacities, effort, skills and resources from local people and also from outsiders. 

\subsection{Taking Ownership}
An important aspect of working with communities around complex socio-technical issues is the importance of community resilience \citet{ferreira_social_2020,bernarda_design_2017,aldrich_social_2015,mark_resilience_2008}. There are multiple definitions of community resilience \citet{mark_resilience_2008,aldrich_social_2015}, however in the this context it can be referred to as a community's ability or strength to adapt to changes and take action towards positive changes. Designing technologies with communities are also about people \textit{archiving control} \citep{vines_configuring_2013}, which can be seen as a part of activities that contribute to community resilience. \citet{bernarda_design_2017} states that the designer has the ability to act as an `enabler` and facilitator to help towards \textit{``empowerment''}. However, people themselves need to take control and ownership of the the \textit{``factors and decisions that shape their lives''} \citep[p. 903]{bernarda_design_2017}. 

This is something that came through across the two previous case studies of this thesis. For example, in Case Study I (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}) a toolkit was built which enabled citizens and community groups collect and commission environmental data about neighbourhoods. However, people needed to take ownership of those datasets to inform their actions, otherwise the data just became another source of available data on the web. That does not mean that that data is not useful for anybody, it just means that it is not used for the purposes it was generated or commissioned. Lack of ownership and control over decisions and factors can also effect and stall progress towards positive changes. Here is an example of lack of control achieved by the citizens that happened in the local area where the author was operating:
\begin{quote}
    A community group was trying, for several years, to get traffic calming measures to be implemented around their local school. Further down the line a local charity secured funding for a project to do exactly that. However, the local community group got agitated by the charity, because they were not consulted on the project and it came as a surprise for them. Soon after a local councillor got involved and the project was postponed by another year to go though a formal consultation process.
\end{quote}
What this story illustrates is the importance of engaging people early and including them into the design and implementations of solutions that affect their community.

\subsection{The Usefulness of Data}
\label{sub:useful_data}

They aim of the two previous case studies was to data \textit{usable} for citizens, as opposed to its current form of being \textit{available}, to use in civic action. Previous case studies have looked at how to enable people to collect and commission the right data (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}) and enable to access and interpret already available data (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) to understand what data they need. What was learned along the way was that data is not self evident \citep{gitelman_raw_2013,tuomi_data_1999} to people; it needs processes, often social processes to help give those data meaning. Data is often particularly situated, in terms of the community that is trying to make sense of it; the place where it was generated; and the kinds of questions that people want answers to. This echos \citet{gitelman_raw_2013}'s arguments that data is an \textit{``oxymoron''}. The same piece of data can be used and interpreted differently in different contexts and places. Data does not always make sense when it is taken out, or put into for that matter, of its strong context of both its place and its community actually working with it. There are more steps involved in going from \textit{usable} data to \textit{useful} data. In fact, \textit{useful} is not actually a property of the data, because all data is useful, it is actually a property of the person or the interaction or activity a person is doing. Citizens need a purpose for what they use the data for, and then it becomes useful to them. That means having data in the right formats is not enough, people need to have skills to be able to use it. Citizens need to be asking the right questions and planning out the actions, sometimes even before there is any data available.

\section{New Challenges of Community-Data Interaction}
\label{sec:new_challenges}
Open data advocates often paint a romantic picture of the data being available for everyone to consume; like the air that is in the atmosphere, and making it ready to leverage its powers in aiding decision-making, improving public services, and fighting injustices and improving equality for all people. However in closer inspection, and from findings of Case Study I and II, things are not as they have been advertised. Depending on where a particular person sits in the society they may think that current systems for data access and use are either fantastic or are under-serving their needs. In addition to the clear need of data science skills to interact with abundance of data rich systems, there are also number of barriers to using that data for purposes other than their intended collection. Published data is often not standardised; there is a lack of machine readable metadata; tools and systems for data access are disperse and often not fully functional; there is lack of clear documentation how to leverage the existing infrastructures.

On top of the barriers related technical capabilities data systems and skills and resources needed to leverage them (i.e, the engineering problems), there are also multiple challenges around the human aspect of information engineering and production of actionable knowledge from data (i.e., community problems). These relate to social processes of finding ways that data can be made relevant and useful in different contexts of everyday lives of citizens on individual, community, city and state level. All this makes up a wide set of systems and processes that all link to processes of Community-Data Interaction (CDI).

\section{Human Data Interaction as Shared Data Interaction}
Digital technology and the availability of data has created new opportunities to engage in civic participation. New channels of communication could potentially provide a stronger (and more diverse) voice to citizens and support them in active participation in democracy. Appropriation of data as a means of engagement and participation can take many different forms (Chapter \ref{chap:literature}). Similarly, information engineering has examples of big data analytics systems built to algorithmically harness data from citizens to streamline civic participation \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016}. The tendencies are that these two modes -- active and passive, or direct and indirect -- are kept separately from each other because their origination from different disciplines, difficulties around mathematical representations of context or deeper ontological conflicts. Furthermore, there have been criticism towards big data analytics and the way open data advocates portray big data as providing citizens information and resources for capacity building and empowerment, but actually sifting towards datafication , that instead serving the needs of citizens (particularly marginal and vulnerable) they are catering to agendas driven by large industry \citep{tenney_data-driven_2016,chandler_world_2015,michael_toward_2016} and increase in `digital divide' \citep{gurstein_effective_2003}.

Along increases in the capabilities of technology and data, and new modes of participation, new research fields are emerging from HCI and other disciplines that aim to understand the impacts of these developments. For example, Human Data Interaction (HDI), a research field that is interested in understanding how humans interact with data in this complex ecosystem involving ubiquitous and pervasive computing, big data and internet of things. For example \cite{mortier_human-data_2014} places the individual in the centre stage and focused on issues around \textit{legibility}, \textit{agency}, \textit{negotiability} in the context of rapidly growing data-driven society. Looking at how individuals can understand what data about them is being used and for what; what can be done to give people better mechanisms to control their data; and how would the management of dynamic relationships that arise from data use look like. HDI focuses on analysing ``individual and collective decisions we make and the actions we take, whether as users of online systems or as subjects of data collection'' \citep{morton_community_2013}. Without a doubt, these issues are becoming increasingly important in a world where data about people can be used to influence behaviour of individuals \citep{bond_61-million-person_2012}.

However, HDI in the context of community data use does not only apply to interactions with one's own data that is being exchanged through pervasive computing devices. The interactions with data, that are of subject of this thesis, moves away from only looking at personal data and also considers data that has been collected about communities and cities at large -- \textit{civic data}. For example data collected about the environment during a school run at the local community level. This interaction may include data about the individual (e.g., location traces), however it also includes data recorded about the local air quality. Although the data might be valuable for an individual, it comes much more valuable to the particular collective trying to instrument change in their neighbourhood. Such example of shared HDI played out in the first case study of the thesis (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}). In addition, HDI for shared interactions concerns the data that has been already generated by other organisations and the government, which can also turn out to be valuable to citizens advocating for positive change in their local community.

Through the course of the previous two case studies the aim of the research was to explore ways data can be made usable to support civic advocacy and action. This was reflected by the designed digital tools and systems, the interactions that supported this type of \textit{active engagement} around data and the ways that citizens could use that data to take local action (the CAF model in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}). In addition to the technical and conceptual issues around big data analytics systems, there is a fundamental issue that inhibits their effective use in civic advocacy. They mainly support interactions that are individual, rather than group or community focused. It can be noted, that when people talk about public engagement or interactions within the wider concept of data and smart cities, it is often referred to as citizen engagement or citizen participation in the city, however these interactions are always connected to (either directly or indirectly) a wider social context. The ways people learn, form understandings, make decisions and decide to act on them, are always linked to the environmental and social contexts \citet{suchman_human-machine_2007,dourish_where_2004}. This also applies to interacting, interpreting and making use of data and information. Hence interactions with data and systems that mediate them, should be taken as \textit{shared interactions} instead of individual, specially in the context of communities using data to support civic advocacy and action. That means providing resources and support that fosters the community and provides conditions sufficient for translating data into activities for local benefit:

\begin{quote}
    \textit{``The capacity and opportunity to successfully integrate ICTs into the accomplishment of self or collaboratively identified goals.''} \citep{gurstein_effective_2003}.
\end{quote}

To extend research in HDI to incorporate the \textit{shared} and \textit{social} element of interacting with data in community context requires a formal definition of what constitutes as \textit{shared data interaction} and understanding of the influential components and factors need to be considered when designing technologies and processes for Community-Data Interaction (CDI). The following two sections extend the Citizen Action Framework presented in Chapter Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}, and formally define CDI and present a model of shared data interaction, which helps designers discuss and understand how to design for effective use of data by communities. The concept of CDI and the terminology is derived from the findings of previous two case studies presented in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}, Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval} and Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}; and the existing gaps and challenges set out in the literature review in Chapter \ref{chap:literature} and this chapter (Section \ref{sec:tech_chall} and \ref{sec:social_chall}); as well as drawing on the critiques in ISD, SI, CI, social design \citep{ferreira_social_2020} and community commissioning \citep{garbett_app_2016}.

\section{Defining Community-Data Interaction}
The concept of CDI merges from fields such as Information System Design (ISD), Social Informatics (SI), and Community Informatics (CI) focusing on the interaction between people and data in a complex sociotechnical network. In a way CDI can also be considered to be a `fragmented adhocracy' of IS \citep{stillman_community_2009} with a more focused agenda. Similarly to CI, CDI seeks to address both technological and social issues around effective use of data. The aim for CDI is not to create a new theory, but to provide a process or design practice for shared data interactions for effective use of data in community contexts and for purposes of civic advocacy and action.

In this thesis `the search' is for the \textit{social value of data} that have been collected previously or will be collected in the future. Seeking to understanding the influential factors that dictate shared data interaction and the methods and tools that could improve interactions with data around complex social and environmental issues. What has been evident from the previous case studies is when the social ergonomics \citep{brown_issues_1985} (e.g., how the community operates, how can the systems adapt to different contexts, what are the values and norms of the community, what is the relevance of data, what are the aims of people's actions) around the use of data are not addressed properly or ignored, the value of data and the ability for citizens to use and make use of it decreases marginally.

\subsection{Models in Community Informatics}
\citet{hirschheim_exploring_1996} proposed a object system class framework to the design of IS, which was adopted by \citet{stillman_community_2009} for guiding the design of CI systems. As pointed out in the literature review of the thesis (Chapter \ref{chap:literature}) this model has flaws in terms of its one-dimensionality and the inability to recognise the role of technology in other orientations except \textit{instrumentation of control}. Firstly, the findings of previous two case studies in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}, Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval} and Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace} illustrate that technology can not only enable community to achieve control, it can also help organise and coordinate; help make sense of situations and build knowledge; and argument and communicate ideas and issues. In the case of SenseMyStreet toolkit (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}), it had almost all the steps (i.e., orientations) \citep{hirschheim_exploring_1996,stillman_community_2009} supported by technical processes or artefacts. Or in the case of Data:In Place platform (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}), it provided technical scaffolding for citizens to access and interpret (i.e, sensemaking) data from expert systems, and also add their own opinions (i.e., argumentation) of that data and issues to the platform through geo-indexed audio snippets. Furthermore, technology is never neutral and present in a particular isolation. It acts as an \textit{``instrumentation of power''} across domains of change \citep[p. 55]{gurstein_toward_2012}.

Secondly, because of the overlap of similar orientations across the domains of change \citep[also]{stillman_community_2009}, the model should not be taken strictly as cross-relational. According to \citet{gurstein_toward_2012}, who has been credited as the one of the founders of the CI field, the framework for CI is more of an iterative, dialectic and multi faceted, acquiring a more \textit{``linkages, feedback and `feed-forward' mechanism''} \citep[p. 52]{gurstein_toward_2012} between stakeholders. Constantly moving from one orientation to another across domains depending on the context and situations currently present. These views are shared also by \citet{moor_using_2007} who adds that because of the ever-changing nature of the communities, it is very hard to have a definitive and replicable framework for designing SI systems for community use. Finally, the model for designing CI tools needs to incorporate critical social theory and aid in the constant work on the dual agenda of solving engineering problems in relation to community activities \citet{stillman_community_2009}, borrowing concepts from IS and SI \citep{kling_learning_2000,stillman_community_2009} to aim to achieve this holistic approach.


\subsection{Community Commissioning}
Another relevant framework to effective use of data is \citet{garbett_designing_2017}'s proposed Community Commissioning Model. It is a linear model that consists of a five stages: \textit{express needs}, \textit{establish demand}, \textit{design and resource}, \textit{produce} and \textit{adopt and sustain}. This is highly relevant and could be applied also to the Case Study I (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}) in this thesis, where citizens where commissioning environmental sensors to collect data about the local areas. Although, the process has a bottom-up approach, this model only extend to the act of commissioning the infrastructures. Once the data is collected the framework is exhausted. One could argue that the last step of \citet{garbett_designing_2017} commissioning model, \textit{adopt and sustain}, would guarantee the extended uses of the commissioned technologies. However, the act of `sustaining' something often requires rethinking, redesign and even more cycles of development or commissioning. When the processes stops supporting the improving and changing of things to cope with the changes of the community (and the outside changes), the technology is not sustained any more, it is degrading. In the last step of the model, \textit{adopt and sustain}, \citep{garbett_designing_2017} does say that the model continues beyond, putting emphasis the importance of engaging supporters in the adoption of the commissioned infrastructure to establish a community that would sustains it. What that is pointing out is the importance of building \textit{capacity}; new skills, resources and links, which would successfully transfer of ownership of the technologies and solutions to the community. With this in mind the model for community commissioning, and for effective use of data in that matter, should not be a flat and linear driven by a particular process. It should take in count of the changing nature of communities, issues and situations, and incorporate building capacities in every step of the process. Furthermore, these findings also merged from the evaluation of the Case Study I where a CAF framework was posited to discuss and understand the factors that influence citizens taking civic action.

\subsection{Social Innovation Models}
Participatory design, participatory action research (PAR) (and also its extension PADRE) have all links to social design and innovation \citep{haj-bolouri_introducing_2015,ferreira_social_2020,bjorgvinsson_participatory_2010,bilandzic_towards_2011,bradley_social_2006}. Designers working with communities usually applying any, or mix of, these design principles with an aim to promote social change. Whether through small participatory efforts that could be scaled through networks or whether it is to do with building trust and capacities within the community \citet{ferreira_social_2020}. However, within the social design its often up to the designer or the social innovator to be the `hero', have the skills and knowledge to `wield' the technology; be able to engage in critical thinking; have the awareness of social, environmental and personal factors and be able to engage people in the design process \citet{ferreira_social_2020}. This is what \citet{bradley_social_2006} called \textit{``a new type of engineer''} or \citet{bernarda_design_2017} \textit{``designabler''}, the ones who have all the skills of an engineers and also the possess ones of a social scientist. Figure \ref{fig:si_skills} illustrates these skills of the designer and Figure \ref{fig:siframe} shows some of the factors, roles and contexts these skills fit in the social economy. Although, a designer can certainly be an `enabler', the change has to come from the community itself in order it to be sustainable \citep{bernarda_design_2017}. However, the designer can become part of the network; either supporting, connecting, enabling, communities to instrument positive change. Furthermore, there should not be fixed roles in the new model of social change for CI, it should be an ever-chaining mix of different types of collaboration patterns \citep{moor_using_2007} that help build the capacities of community.

\subsection{Model of CDI}
Analysing the models in CI, community commissioning and social innovation, and correlating them with the findings from the previous two case studies in this thesis (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}, Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval} and Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) has enabled to identify the shortcoming of these models to effectively address the communities needs. Therefore building on the CAF framework presented in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}, the model of CDI moves away from a having a linear process or being one-dimensional; not having fixed roles or stakeholders, constantly changing orientations and adopting like the community itself, and is at the same time influenced by the three factors (i.e., personal, behavioural, environmental) of influence (and social learning as posited by \citet{bandura_social_1977}), which will also become the domains of change that the community wants influence and take action towards (Figure \ref{fig:cdi}). To translate this model through CI object class model \citep{stillman_community_2009,hirschheim_exploring_1996} the objects are \textit{roles}, \textit{infrastructures}, \textit{constraints}, \textit{resources}, \textit{capacities}; and \textit{personal}, \textit{behavioural} and \textit{environmental} are the same time the domains of desired chance and also the orientations (i.e., influencing factors) of change. However the importance of each object and its role is defined by the particular context that the community is in (i.e., orientation) and the changes that community wants to implement. Furthermore, technology is not only present in just one orientation, it is omnipresent across the CDI model, helping citizens achieve control and empowerment.

\subsection{Roles}
Chapter \ref{chap:literature} outlined the the different stakeholder groups involved in the development of open data ecosystems. However, when looking at the development of effective use of data within communities in the wider context of CDI, a more focused look of different stakeholders, their importance and the roles they take is needed. Although these roles are defined explicitly, individuals can adopt multiple roles and even transition from one role to another depending on the state of the developments, the actions of other stakeholders and people, outside developments and also the influences of ongoing trends. For example a member of community can become a volunteer or an activist if they find a issue they think needs addressing or a cause worth fighting for in the community. \subsubsection{Professional Data Analyst, Researcher, Instructional Designer}
\label{sec:data_prof}
An underlining agenda of this thesis is to look closely at the role of the data professional in the context of CDI and effective use of data by communities. In this context, the notion of a professional data analyst is a person who effectively makes data usable for people beyond just data visualisation, although it might involve visualisations, but also comes up with the right questions, knows where to look for data and presents data in a way that is comprehensible and actionable. This involves a lot of technical expertise that relate to information processing, however it also requires cognitive skills which take years of training. This is the reason why data science skills are in high demand and companies are willing to pay large amount of money for it.

Through the case studies of the PhD the author has acted as a data professional for these communities providing technical support and skill for working with data. This often also involved making an initial analysis of the data and building a story around it to illustrate the power of using data. Such an example can be seen on Figure \ref{fig:nuf_app} where the author used aggregated client data from a local charity to illustrate how they tackle inequality in the community by doing targeted engagements. In order to create this application, the author had to develop a hypothesis, find the relevant data, process and merge it with the data form the local charity and use visualisation technologies to create the data rich narrative. Tasks that require skills both in software engineering and information processing (i.e., skills of a data scientist).

\subsection{Infrastructures}
In the context of CDI, infrastructures include the building and configuring of technologies and/or services to enable and support communities to make use of data. The obvious examples of infrastructures are \textbf{system} a \textbf{service} which are usually developed to specific purpose and aim. For example Data:In Place can be (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) considered as a system and SenseMyStreet (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}) can be considered as a service for environmental sensor commissioning. Furthermore, SenseMyStreet is made up of multiple systems, but also other smaller services and different \textbf{configurations} of existing services. In some sense, the tools that SenseMyStreet used where already adopted by communities and how they operate, the toolkit just added them as additional resources. This way of configuring has also been coined as \textit{``unplatforming''} \citep{lambton-howard_whatfutures_2019} or design \textit{``appropriation''} \citep{dix_designing_2007} -- identifying an existing community on a particular platform or tool and re-configuring the system to serve the agendas and actions of the community. For example SenseMyStreet initially used people's smartphone's GPS (or any GPS) to attach locations to hand-held monitor readings and used Google Forms and Calendar to automatically schedule senor loans. Although, later a bespoke Android application was developed (and phones added to the toolkit) to record GPS locations for the hand-held monitor, it still supported any type of GPS devices to attach locations. \cite{gurstein_effective_2003} also calls this type service infrastructure a \textit{``carriage facilities''} which refers to making sure people have the needed supporting infrastructure to take up the use of applications. Similarly there needs to be availability of \textbf{``content services''} to support the application use. This type of infrastructures refer to the \textit{``usability and locally contextual requirements''} \citet{gurstein_effective_2003}. This means working with the community to understand how the local context would imply to data and tools. An example of this work was the user-centred and iterative design process of Data:In Place \ref{chap:datainplace}) to make open data available and usable for the community. Finally there is a need for a \textbf{governance}, the legal, regulatory and policy infrastructure that would enable citizens to become active data users. An example of this kind of structure is the Neighbourhood Planning law part of the Localism Act 2011\footnote{\url{https://www.gov.uk/guidance/neighbourhood-planning--2}} in the context of what the community engaged in Case Study II (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) was working in.

\subsection{Resources}
Resources involved in CDI and effective use of data by communities skills, knowledge, time and physical and monetary assets to deploy, use and sustain community technologies or services. \textbf{Skills} are communities abilities to perform particular tasks or master the use of some type of technology or process. \textbf{Assets} are physical and non-physical items and resources that the community owns. Both skills and assets can be individually owned and maintained, however if they become part of the community agenda they can be categorised (but often not transferable) also as community owned. A skill might be, for example, operating a hand-held monitor and developing a website; and assets could include personal smartphones, environmental monitors, money for printing flyers, community meeting facilities, webservers (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} and \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}). \textbf{Knowledge} can refer to the tacit knowledge of each member of a community and the focal knowledge of a community group within the context in which it works.


\subsection{Constraints}
All the other elements are bounded by constraints to enable the effective use of data by community. In the context of CDI, constraints can be discussed in terms of: the community is trying to make use of data by leveraging resources, to address a particular \textbf{agenda} and take \textbf{actions} towards civic \textbf{advocacy} (i.e., translating into activities or changes of local benefit). However the community is always developing and has to adapt with constant \textbf{changes} and at same time bear in mind the community's other \textbf{commitments} that are not linked to this specific agenda. The constraints are also applied to all the actions that people take what are the \textbf{complexities} of those actions. For example a community group organising a `stand' and drop in session at a community festival to engage wider community with the data and issues (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}).

\subsection{Capacities}
Capacities in the context of CDI are probably the most important in terms of adaptability (availability), sustainability, longevity of the technologies and solutions for effective use of data by communities. Both of the previous case studies in this thesis illustrated the importance of \textbf{community links} and \textbf{connections} to making use of data in civic action. For example knowing people in the local council or university to better organise efforts and target the use of data (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}) or ability to together multiple charity organisations and local councillors to coherently (\textbf{cohesion}) work on a combined neighbourhood plan for the community (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}). However, there was also examples of people not being able to leverage data or sustain engagements due to lack of \textbf{social capital} within the community and links to skills and knowledge needed. Such examples of community groups can be found from Case Study II where after the stationary monitor was commissioned and data collected, the engagement did not continue due to the lack of resources or actionable plan for the data (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}). Additionally its important to note that \textbf{mobility} and \textbf{diversity} are also important factors that contribute to increasing the capacities of community and their ability to reach different links and connections, in order to tab into outside resources.

\subsection{Overlapping Components}
As mentioned before there are components or objects that span across multiple elements of CDI. These objects can be seen as things that have the most value to communities trying to make effective use of data.
\newline

\textbf{Time} is probably the most precious \textit{resource} and the biggest \textit{constraint}. Community advocacy work is mostly done my highly motivated individuals who volunteer their free time to have a positive change for the community. Although the aim is to establish effective use of data by communities, advocacy efforts often fail or succeed because of an individual efforts (and combined) of community volunteers and activists.
\newline

\textbf{Social facilitation} - in the context of CDI, it can be referred to \textit{capacities} and \textit{infrastructures} that support the adoption or failure to adopt technologies and solutions to effectively use data by communities. Social facilitation means having the infrastructure for training, skills transfer and increasing of social capital for the community. In terms of the dual agenda of CI and CDI, social facilitation aims to improve the community problem solving agenda by facilitating, supporting, training, linking, enabling community to archive control and empowerment.
\newline

\textbf{Service access/provisioning} spans across all the elements of CDI and both social and technical infrastructure. It links to access to appropriate tools (digital and physical) and training facilities; physical infrastructure; links to networks and other communities, often professional in nature; and sometimes even organisational structures. However lack of access to these services can become the constraints of the community-data interaction and restrict the effective use of data by communities.

\section{Practical Approaches for CDI}
\label{sec:pract_cdi}
The remaining of this chapter builds on the existing challenges outlined, and discusses ways that they could potentially be addressed using the model of CDI and through design of tools and technology, and the use of transdisciplinary approaches and practices which come together to help unlock the social value of data for citizens and communities.
\newline

In the case of CDI, there is a need to take a more holistic approach to implementing strategies that would create a system capable of providing social value from data and enabling people to enact positive change in their community. As research in CI already has a dual agenda \citep{stillman_community_2009}, there is a need for a dual approach. Furthermore, being a transdisciplinary research effort in the interdisciplinary field that is HCI, a more suitable approach could perhaps be found by looking to a discipline outside of engineering and computing science. Looking at practices and methods of implementing strategies for influencing systemic and complex social issues led the author to the \textit{Dual Approach}. The Dual Approach is widely adopted in the context of public health interventions \citep{bauer_prevention_2014} and increasing health equity \citep{graham_population_2016}. The Dual Approach uses a model where strategies are applied to improve the health of general population while the same time using targeted interventions to address issues encountered by a specific targeted population. It follows the principle of \textit{targeted universalism}, which states that alleviating disparities of marginalised populations is essential for developing contextually relevant strategies for achieving universal goals and improving the well-being of everyone \citep{national_collaborating_centre_for_determinants_of_health_lets_2013}. 

In the context of CDI and effective use of data for community action, dual approach means using contextualising; to improve the ways how people make requests, access, and interpret data using computing systems; and implementing strategies to help local communities deal with specific isolated issues. However, it is not impossible to work within the context of one group without working on the interrelated issues and groups. Interventions are affected by other groups, actors, solutions, stakeholders, trends etc. Hence, when working on `wicked problems' there needs to be a holistic approach that considers and bridges different disciplines, stakeholders, issues or as \citet{asad_tap_2017} put this: 
\begin{quote}
    \textit{``Designers and researchers then need to identify where and how to bridge those diverging interests. One strategy, as described above, is through designing added transparency and feedback into advocacy support practices—not just the tools—so that different affected groups develop their own capacities to act.''} - \citet{asad_tap_2017} 
\end{quote}
This also relates back to building capacities through \textit{bridging} and \textit{linking} of social ties discussed in Section \ref{sub:comm_links} that are vital for the community problem solving agenda of CI.

\subsection[Contextualising Data]{Contextualising Data: Making Data Accessible and Usable}
The biggest technical challenge of CDI from the perspective of effective use of data is making data relevant to people and their situation. Most of the available open data is collected to provide higher level statistics for governing and for administrative purposes. Although context-aware computing exist it does not extend itself to humans interacting with abstract datasets though computer interfaces yet. Data needs to be anchored, grounded and situated in every facet of life, in other words without contextualising structure there is no value in data. According to \cite{hakken_social_2003}, in order to create or transfer knowledge through information systems, they should aim to embed as much context as possible to counterbalance the abstractions needed to represent data through computer systems. Linking data with issues of importance and places of interest for local communities, however sets out series of challenging technical tasks. The following sections explore ways data can be contextualised in information systems by anchoring it in \textit{place}, to \textit{issues} and to \textit{inquires}.

\subsubsection{Linking to Place}
Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace} focused on the ideas of making data accessible and usable through places identified by communities. That became the main premise of the Data:In Place platform and the \textit{map based querying system} that was developed to interface between people and abstract open datasets. Geographical data is most cases represented in computer systems using the ISO 6709\footnote{\url{https://www.iso.org/standard/39242.html}} standard expressing it in coordinates describing geographic point location. Linking data to place is done by including metadata to (i.e., other data that provides information about this data) to coordinates or in the case of semantic web described through the relationship of that collected data to geographies (e.g., for example UK official administrative geographies\footnote{\url{https://www.ons.gov.uk/methodology/geography/ukgeographies}}). For example census data in the UK is often published on the output area (OA) level (at least 40 households and 100 persons, the target size being 125 households), which is the smallest official geography unit used by the Office of National Statistics (ONS). Table \ref{tab:geo_standards} illustrate different Standards, techniques and methods of linking data to place. Although there multiple different standards to represent a particular place, they need to be associated with something that communities can relate to in order to be effectively used buy them. Working with different communities in the Case Study II (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) it came apparent that things people relate to are often related to personal stories or locations that have particular memories to them (e.g., streets, points of interest (POIs), Post Codes) \citep[also]{manuel_participatory_2017}. The challenge is to translate these locations that people refer to something that the computers can store and reference against available datasets. This can be done through: \textit{conversions} which just converts one format to another, \textit{best fit} which tries to find the closest official geographical representation that it fits into, or translators which to the conversions on the fly. Data:In Place platform (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) was using all of these methods when matching boundaries to ONS Codes, conversing them into ISO 6709 and mapping Post Code data on the map by translating them using Javascript mapping libraries on the fly. Additionally having data in standardised format enabled people to use Data:In Place platform also to discover data from the commissioned sensors from the SenseMyStreet (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}) toolkit. Data:In Place used these standards, techniques and methods to contextualise data and make it available and useful, however these techniques could also be used to these techniques could also be used to improve the discovery and linking of data.

\subsubsection{Linking to Issues}
Another way of contextualising or anchoring data is to link it to particular issue that the community is facing. Issue mapping was used as the first step of SenseMyStreet toolkit (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}) to have a purpose for the data once it is collected. In the case of Data:In Place platform Data:In Place platform (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) the first set of datasets incorporated into the platform directly translated from their relations to the issues pointed out at the community meeting. These links however were made by professional data scientist (i.e., the author) who had knowledge of available datasets and knew where to look for them. Future developments of issue anchoring should also consider ways these links can be made automatically or through the help of extended community links.

\subsubsection{Linking to Inquires}
Finally, data can be anchored with inquires. These can be linked to concrete questions or even to curiosities that people have. For example when building the data narrative application (Section \ref{sec:data_prof}), there was need for a hypothesis that would dictate what data is needed and in what form its needed. Additionally, there was also a need for an understanding of the context where the local charity worked in (i.e., and the data they collect) to produce something that would be of value to them. However, this part of the process of inquiry was done by the author by translating the needs of the charity into hypothesis for data. In the context of CDI and building capacities, new ways and processes of inquiry should be developed that would actively engage citizens in these processes and enable them to set these agendas for data use.


\subsection[Actioning Data]{Actioning Data: Making Data Useful}
What was learned from the previous two case studies (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet}, \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval} and \ref{chap:datainplace}) of this thesis is that data itself, regardless of its form, is not particularly useful for the community if it does not find a purpose. Without a actionable plan for data, the commissioned data was not utilised by a community (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}) and without next steps of action the open data accessible for people did not translate to any activities for the community (Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}). On the other hand when a community has a actionable plan the data finds purpose and becomes useful (Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}). This was also expressed through the analysis and presenting of the Citizen Advocacy Framework (CAF) in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}. The model of CDI illustrates that there is need for moving away from the assumption that data is the `silver bullet', meaning if there is data information, knowledge and wisdom (i.e., knowledge that can by applied in action) reveals itself without additional steps; naturally, automatically. Forevermore, CDI has identified the key elements of effective use of data that might lead to action and activities for local benefit. The model of CDI for effective use of data could also be used to help understand the gaps, needed resources and capacities to improve the usefulness of data for the communities, and aid in coming up with strategies for actively participating in decision-making processes.

\subsection{Applying the Model of Community-Data Interaction}
This chapter has discussed the dual agenda of CI and looked at it through the model of Community-Data Interaction, also extending it to examples of practical applications. Understanding how people where interacting with data through the built digital systems provided a deeper contextualised knowledge of CDI. Reflecting on the previous case studies in this thesis, enabled to highlighted new challenges for the CDI that surface the importance of targeted work on the community problem solving agenda. There is a clear need for not only technologies that would work for communities, but also processes that would help build the social capital and capacities of the community. Currently there is still a major reliance on the work of the expert data professional to make effective use of data by communities. The model of CDI has been presented in terms of roles, resources, constrains, infrastructures and capacities that are all influenced by the factors (e.g., personal, behavioural, environmental) which are also the domains of change. The model of CDI however has identified the that there are far more social processes needed to guarantee the extended engagement by communities and use of data for local benefit. Rather than building a new system or tool, researchers might consider focusing more on the ways that we think about `data as a resource' in general, and its use and re-use, to enable a paradigm shifts to happen. Furthermore, through applying the model of CDI on the design of Community Informatics projects enables us to further understand these concepts and configure the project to achieve the desired outcomes for the community.

\section{Summary}
This chapter has presented a model of Community-Data Interaction (CDI) for effective use of data by communities for civic advocacy and action by reflecting on the previous two case studies of the thesis from the perspective of dual agenda of Community Informatics (CI) (engineering problem solving agenda and community problem solving activities) as well as drawing on the literature of CI and social innovation. The model of CDI is presented through five key elements that are influenced by factors which are also the domains of change within the community. Extending on the conceptual model of CDI and the key elements of it, the chapter provides an ontology of components, discussing them through the examples from the previous two case studies of this thesis. Lastly, the chapter provides examples of practical approaches for the CDI model that can be applied when designing for effective use of data by communities. Although, examples of components of CDI in action can already be identified from the previous case studies of this thesis, the next chapter presents the Case Study III, which was conducted to apply the CDI model in full to provide empirical evidence of its usefulness and potential issues that might arise from it.
