This chapter reflects on previous two case studies in this thesis, which aimed to support the `accessible data' agenda through active engagements. Up to this point, the thesis has looked at ways to engage citizens in exploration of local issues through data production (case study 1 in Chapter \ref{chap:sensemystreet} and \ref{chap:sensemystreet-eval}) and through making sense of publicly available datasets (case study 2 in Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) from a more engineering point of view by responding to the issues regarding the lack of technical tools or access to data. That included setting up a community commissioning toolkit to access resources for collecting environmental data (i.e., SenseMyStreet) and designing and building a open data access and visualisation platform (i.e., Data:In Place). Drawing on the case studies and wider research in ubiquitous computing (UC) and human data interaction (HDI), chapter starts off by analysing the technical challenges of \textit{accessing}, \textit{making sense} and \textit{effectively using} data to support civic participation and advocacy. Additionally, the chapter provides an analysis of the successes and failures of each project and initiative engaged within the context of the research. It is important to note that the analysis of the case studies was done from the perspective of an experienced data scientist and information engineer designing and building accessible data systems for community use. From there, the chapter goes on by discussing the important aspects of human interactions with data and systems facilitating these interactions. However these are not limited to only interactions between humans and machines, but also the `human friction` which is often rooted in particular contexts and the social dynamics within the communities. Through this analysis and reflection on the previous case studies, it is possible to outline the current open challenges that still exist for shared data interactions in a hyper-local community level. Based on these challenges, this chapter underlines the methods, strategies and practices, outlining a holistic approach through which accessible data systems could be formed. As a result a formal framework of shared data interaction as Community Data Interaction (CDI) is offered. The framework provides a definition of CDI and ontology within the context of effective use data for communities in relation to \textit{roles, infrastructure, resources, constraints and capacity}. Going forward, the framework provides an abstract set of considerations when designing systems of shared data interaction for accessible data and effective use of data for civic advocacy and action.

\section{Related Work/Background}

Call for open data has been echoing from technology and policy advocates for while now. In some respect it has been answered by institutions publishing their data, however the hopes and promises of open data empowering everyone has been short lived. Data, often represented as machine readable files with abstract descriptions and column headers, holds the values of those specific purposes of data generation or production. There is an abundance of data (or metadata) available on the web and more is being published daily, but there is actually a paucity of data and technology that would help understand what kind of questions could be answered with particular datasets. By supporting the Semantic Web agenda (Berners-Lee 2011) those data can be standardised and described for querying and retrieval by machines. A great step in a right direction for helping to remove ambiguity around understanding what particular dataset represents and reducing development costs on data accessing, combining and merging. Although Linked Data standards for the web have been around for over a decade now, we are still far off achieving the goal of having a `Web of Data' as opposed to a sheer collection of datasets on the web. As a result, data science skills are becoming increasingly more important to aid exploration of data and support evidence based practices in decision-making. Furthermore organising and linking data on the is only the first step towards its effective use by communities and for civic participation and advocacy.

\subsection{Organising Data On the Web}

There are multiplicity of ways that data and information is organised on the web and can be shared between people. In the corporate world it is essential to keep track of the organisational memory (OM) in a way that it could be accessible and usable to all the members of the organisation. In addition to Linked Data, Knowledge Graphs are often used to connect that data together and make the retrieval of data easier from variety of different sources. Most notable of the knowledge graphs is used by Google to enchants its search engine. Another well know techniques are archival and indexing of resources. The retrieval of information from archives follows a system catalogues which are identified or tagged by topics or other common identifiers. Examples of such systems are the Archives Portal Europe\footnote{\url{https://www.archivesportaleurope.net/}} and probably most well know in the academic field the Digital Object Identifier (DOI) system \footnote{\url{https://www.doi.org/}}.
In addition to formal ways of organising data and information on the web there are also systems for community curated `linked data'. Most popular source of that is unquestionably Wikipedia free online encyclopedia, which enables anyone who has an internet access to use that resource and to contribute to it. 

The vast number of connections and links between individuals that are enabled by modern technologies and can be harnessed through the web, have opened up new ways of information and knowledge sharing. On top of media sharing platforms such as Facebook, Instagram, YouTube, to name few, there are multiple forum based Q\&A systems that aim to connect and exchange information between individuals and groups. Online platforms such as Stack Overflow for any question related to software development, or websites like Reddit and Quora which encourage discussions on variety of topics. Although there are availability of different tools to retrieve information from digital systems, they often require interactions that are confined in the boundaries of specific technology or contextualised in particular way. This is specially evident in interactions with systems that provide numerical big data. They are often organised and accessed in ways that people would not organise and retrieve information themselves in their everyday lives, hence the expression data in `machine readable' format. Furthermore, what motivates the work in this thesis is how to effectively use these available datasets in civic advocacy and action. Indeed, it is essential to have data stored in a way that it could be accessed in standardised ways to aid creation of building tools that would interface with data archives, but these tools also need to be able to guide people in contextualising and translating abstract datasets into helpful resources for civic participation.

\subsection{Making A Formal Data Request}
To investigate the potential uses of data in context of civic participation and action, there is a need to look at the current ways citizens can gain access open data from official sources. With the Freedom of Information Act, a new mechanism was set up for people to access data held by public authorities. Enabling everyone who wanted, to request data from public authorities by submitting a Freedom of Information (FOI) request. FOI can be looked as a formal query for data, where a person or an organisation deposits a question to the data provider (i.e., public authority). Submitting an FOI request can be a lengthy and complicated process often requiring knowledge regarding legal processes. In order to streamline these processes MySociety came up with a online platform called What They Know\footnote{\url{https://www.whatdotheyknow.com}}, that helps people to submit their requests through a web form. This has made the process of submission much easier, however the actual requesting of data has not improved. In order to get the data needed, one needs to know about the existence of some unpublished document or dataset that is being requested. Apart form using the platform to explore previous FOI requests, there is no way one can make sure that particular data is not yet published somewhere else. Thus, as long as there is no unified `Web of Data` (see more Section \ref{sub:linkeddata}), that will describe all the existing data sources on the internet and makes them universally accessible trough standards to any service or client, this remains an issue.

Another scenario which can be encountered, is when a request is formulated as a direct question without an indication to particular requested resource. In that case somebody has to make the association between the question and a particular dataset (i.e., describe the relationship). Now we have reached a fundamental problem with data available on the web: \textit{the lack of links between questions and abstract datasets that might bear the answer}. The ways that these connections are made are largely unknown and often require specialist skills with combination of tacit knowledge. This means either understanding the organisational structures of a public authority or knowing the initial purpose for the data generation. It is unlikely that this knowledge transfer can occur from organisations to citizens, however by contextualising data in different environments, it might help discover new uses and knowledge enabling to re-purpose these datasets.


Underpinning the current state of the art of web technologies for data use, drawing on related works and examples from case study 1 and 2, the following two chapters provide a reflection of use of data in communities. Through this a better understanding can be formed of ways that people interact with data within a complex sociotechnical systems to underline and identify existing challenges. By analysing not only technical aspects, but also social and organisational impacts, will enable to make better design choices for tools and methods to be used for purposeful and effective uses of data in civic participation.

Open data refers to data is freely available to everyone to use, re-purpose and republish without any copyright restrictions (see Chapter \ref{chap:literature}). By the Open Definition \footnote{\url{http://opendatahandbook.org/glossary/en/terms/open-definition}} it requires open data to be open both \textit{legally} and \textit{technically}. The latter refers to having data available in machine readable form and bulk form. To help keep open data consistent on the web, a set of agreements called open data standards have been developed that would help people and organisations publish, access and use data \footnote{\url{https://standards.theodi.org/}}. Additionally there is a distinction of different levels of open data publishing -- bronze, silver, gold and platinum -- which specify how much commitment have the publishers made to make their data accessible (see Figure \ref{fig:badges}).

Developing Data:In Place's map querying system (see Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}), the author was faced with the reality of having to adopt the code to each data publisher individually. Having to use specific types geographies for retrieving specific data and different query languages, which often did not conform to open standards. Additionally when geographies or data were updated or aggregated, it often lacked links that could map the changing to existing or historical datasets. This meant that data converters, translators and adaptors needed to be built to create these links between datasets. However in order to create these links, there was a need for an in dept knowledge of how the data was collected, aggregated, stored and published. Ideally this information could be described alongside with the data and could be also retrieved in a machine readable form, however this information was often lacking in detail even from the documentations on the data portals. These barriers make the integration of open data to accessible data more difficult and labour intensive for developers of these systems. 

\subsubsection{State of Linked Data}
\label{sub:linkeddata}
%This section describes the potential and state of Linked Data to achieve the `Web of Data' goal for organising information on the web.

When people talk about open data there is always a mention of Linked Data which is one of the building block of the Semantic Web or `Web of Data'. Originally proposed by Tim Berners-Lee, director of the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C), Linked Data refers to structured data that is interlinked and described by relationships (or vocabularies) between data and `things'. Linked Data itself can be looked up and represented using open standards such as URIs, HTTP, RDF, SPARQL, OWL, JSON-LD etc. (see more \url{https://www.w3.org/TR/?tag=data}).
\mynote{have an example of data linked such way. Maybe an example of the observatories data}
In theory, if all the data is described and interlinked using Linked Data standards, one should be able to build a single query that returns data from multiple data providers in a format that is useful in a particular context. Whether building a report to surface and understand the most pressing issues in the neighbourhood or gathering evidence to advocate for change in the community, this capability to request, access and merge datasets from different publishers is almost always needed when building an accessible data system for local communities. 
Lets give an example of such query: 
\begin{quote}
    ``What are the current demographics, health statistics and powers of governance in a particular hyper-local community defined by a specified geographic area?''
\end{quote} 
In the UK this query spans across four different governmental institutions - Ordnance Survey, Office of National Statistics, The National Health Service and The House of Commons. Although some of their open data systems have capabilities to request data using linked data standards they are often experimental and do not interact well with other linked data systems. Example provided here is a real life example of building a accessible data system for public health interventions around breastfeeding (see more https://feed-finder.co.uk). This project is especially interesting because it aimed to merge community generated data \cite{balaam_feedfinder:_2015} with official open datasets. After multiple failed attempts to leverage Linked Data to achieve this, the author had to build a bespoke system that makes these links between different data providers and combine them into a visualisation interface. In the end Linked data was leveraged only to some extent, not in a way that would be envisioned by the Semantic Web community. This also meant that with any updates to the structure of the data, the links had to be rebuilt. This puts the long term sustainability of these systems under a question because of the effort required by the researchers to provide technical support to keep the system running and updated. Similar issues raised in the second case study (see Chapter \ref{chap:datainplace}) while trying to make data more accessible and helping people discover new available datasets that might useful for evidencing or exploring a local issue.

Finally, the support offered by the data publishers is not sufficient to fully leverage the capabilities of the APIs. There are very few good examples of advanced usages of data and rarely a forum for maintained for the data users to submit issues. Looking at levels of commitment for publishing open data (see Figure \ref{fig:badges}) from the perspective of effective use in civic participation, only \textit{gold} and \textit{platinum} start focusing on engaging with communities to enable better use of their data. From experience, there are only a handful of data providers (see Section \ref{stakeholders}) that could be considered to have a gold, and even less platinum standard open data. The current state of Linked Data amongst the official open data providers does not extend itself to the ideals of Tim Berners-Lee's `Web of Data'. This in turn again puts local communities in a disadvantaged position -- not getting the data they need in a form that it would be beneficial for them without the technical know how and the interference form a professional data scientist. Furthermore, when there is additional data that has been collected by the community (as in the case of SenseMystreet, FeedFinder, and also Data:In Place project to some extent) it is becoming increasingly difficult to intersect and contrast that data with official open data without the existence of the `Web of Data'.

However, upon human-to-machine interaction these rules do not apply and that creates asymmetry between humans and machines. From early research conducted in human-machine communication at Xerox PARC illustrated how these asymmetries will lead to breakdowns in communication \citep{suchman_plans_1985}. The example given with `intelligent' photocopier revealed that the level of abstraction drawn by computer system about the human plans did not convey the intended actions and without computers understanding human trades of communication the breakdown is inevitable. Now, more than 30 years have passed from these first attempts to understand human-machine communications from a behavioural and human centred angle, making way for numerous communication environments and devices being built that interface interactions between humans and machines. On top of traditional interfaces such as graphical user interfaces (GUI), there are now interfaces that use natural speech (e.i., spoken voice), tangible computing, gestures recorded by cameras or other sensors, using wearable computing, or combination of them, delivering a multimodal interaction interfaces for communication. We are now seeing multimodal interfaces that are connected to the internet, relaying user's interactions to a artificial intelligence systems which can take advantage of an immense computing power and make use of big data and machine learning. Enabling them to understand more about context and learn about the environment they are in. Such interfaces are called virtual assistants with examples like Apple's Siri, Google's Assistant, Amazon's Alexa or Echo and Microsoft's Cortana do name a few. Assistant technology has now been embedded to most of our `smart' personal and home electronics -- smartphones, smartwatches and other personal computing devices, home media systems, hubs and multiple home monitoring devices. The assistant software can perform tasks like schedule calendar events, set alarms, search the internet, pull up information and adjust settings on a particular device, and respond to individual based commands and questions -- enabling even to engage in two-way conversations. Giving more personal data to these systems and enabling the artificial intelligence to `profile' an individual can help these systems to potentially learn about an individual and adapt their responses in a manner that is truly personalised. This could mean that computers now are able to mimic some of these trades that make human-to-human up communication. However what has started happening is that rather than waiting for the machines to learn the context and the particularities of each individual to respond to them in an adequate manner, humans have started to preempt this and are trying to learn how to interact with the machines to get them to do what humans want. In other words people are learning and adapting to `talk' to the machines rather than vice versa, showing again the intelligence of humans and how we operate on the principle of charity when communicating. This principle could also be used as a tactic for improving the responses of machines. For example assessing the usefulness of the information retrieved by the machine as a response of a human requests. By doing that millions of times using a diverse sample, we could start building links between requests and relevant responses.

In some regards, this is what Google's PageRank has been doing for websites for years now -- counting the number and quality of (hyper)links to a page to determine their relevance. \section{Defining Community Data Interaction}
%https://blogs.lt.vt.edu/grad5104/multiintertrans-disciplinary-whats-the-difference/
The concept of CDI merges from fields such as information systems (IS), social informatics (SI) and community informatics (CI) focusing on the interaction between people and data in a complex sociotechnical network. In a way CDI can be also considered to be a `fragmented adhocracy' of IS \citep{stillman_community_2009} with a more focused agenda. Similarly to CI, community data interaction seeks to address both technological and social issues around effective use of data.

The aim for CDI is not to create a new theory, but to provide a process or design practice for shared data interactions in community context.

In this thesis `the search' is for the \textit{social value of data} that have been collected previously or will be collected in the future. Open data advocates often paint a romantic picture of the data being available for everyone to consume like the air that is in the atmosphere, and making it ready to leverage its powers in aiding decision-making, improving public services, and fighting injustices and improving equality for all people. However in closer inspection, and from findings of case study 1 and 2, things are not as they have been advertised. Depending on where a particular person sits in the society they may think that current systems for data access and use are either fantastic or are under-serving their needs. In addition to the clear need of data science skills to interact with abundance of data rich systems, there are also number of barriers to using that data for purposes other than their intended collection: published data is often not standardised; there is a lack of machine readable metadata; tools and systems for data access are disperse and often not fully functional; there is lack of clear documentation how to leverage the existing infrastructures.

On top of the barriers related technical capabilities data systems and skills and resources needed to leverage them, there are also multiple challenges around the human aspect of information engineering and production of actionable knowledge from data. The biggest technical challenge of HDI from the perspective of effective use of data is making data relevant to people and their situation. Most of the available open data is collected to provide higher level statistics for governing and for administrative purposes. Although context aware computing exist it does not extend itself to humans interacting with abstract datasets though computer interfaces yet. Data needs to be anchored, grounded and situated in everyday/every facet of life, in other words without contextualising structure there is no value in data. According to \cite{hakken_social_2003}, in order to create or transfer knowledge through information systems, they should aim to embed as much context as possible to counterbalance the abstractions needed to represent data through computer systems. Linking data with issues of importance and places of interest for local communities, however sets out series of challenging technical tasks. The following sections explore ways data can be contextualised in information systems by anchoring it in \textit{place}, to \textit{issues} and to \textit{questions}.

\section{Conclusions}

Understanding how people where interacting with data through the built digital systems provided a deeper contextualised knowledge of shared data interactions/community data interactions. Future developments of accessible data systems should aim to design features that promote exploration, curiosity and improve retrieval of useful information from different data sources using embedded context. This criteria is important because people do not acquire information in the manner of how machines do, to satisfy their curiosity and need for information on the web, people posit questions which often are open ended and do not map to any specific structure (Paritosh 2018). In order for humans to re-purpose and use abstract datasets from the web, there is a need for a system that links questions with the data that might bare the answers. Reflecting on the previous case studies and the wider research, enabled to set criteria for accessible data systems that needs to be satisfied in order for the re-purposing of open data to be successful. Although the existence of `Web of Data' is prerequisite for enabling the linking of data, the criteria suggests that there needs to be a larger emphasis on the `shared data interactions' and linking questions with data on the web.
